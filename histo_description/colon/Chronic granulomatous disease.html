Microscopic (histologic) description
  • Acute colitis
    • Cryptitis, crypt abscesses, ulceration
  • Chronic colitis
    • Lymphoplasmacytic infiltrate, basal cell plasmacytosis, Paneth cell metaplasia and crypt distortion (Am J Surg Pathol 2013;37:1365)
  • Increased number of eosinophils or eosinophilic microabscesses
  • Granulomas are frequent (50%)
    • Mostly as microgranulomas composed of 5 - 15 epithelioid histiocytes
    • Poorly or well formed
    • With or without Langhans or foreign body type giant cells
    • No necrosis
    • Seen in both superficial and deep mucosa as well as submucosa
  • Enlarged, pigmented macrophages (Am J Surg Pathol 2013;37:1365)
    • 50 - 100 micrometers
    • Seen in the majority of colon and small biopsies
    • Not described in the upper gastrointestinal tract
    • Seen in superficial and deep mucosa, submucosa, muscularis propria and around Auerbach plexus
    • Cytoplasmic, golden brown, granular or eosinophilic crystalline and refractile under the light microscope
  • Small bowel biopsies could show similar acute and chronic inflammation features, including villous shortening, although less common compared to the colon
  • Biopsies from the stomach could harbor acute and chronic gastritis (focal or diffuse), increased number of eosinophils and granuloma formations
  • Esophagus is the least commonly involved part of the gastrointestinal (GI) tract; acute esophagitis or increased intraepithelial lymphocytes have been described
  • No dysplasia has been described in gastrointestinal biopsies of CGD patients
  • Liver biopsies show clusters of portal pigmented macrophages as well as portal plasma cells and lymphocytes, parenchymal granulomas and mild to moderate fibrosis
  • Discoid or subacute lupus erythematosus-like lesions are described in skin biopsies of female X linked carriers
