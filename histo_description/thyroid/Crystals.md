# Crystals

Microscopic (histologic) description

* Anisotropic crystals appear as small transparent pale or colorless refractile foci variable in size and shape (polygonal, sand-like, needle shaped, rhomboid or irregular)
* Large round or oval vacuoles within the colloid may contain calcium oxalate crystals
* Crystals are strongly birefringent under polarized light microscopy
* Intrathyroidal crystals are exclusively found within colloid and do not appear within cytoplasm of the follicular epithelial cells or in stroma ([Wenig: Atlas of Head and Neck Pathology, Third Edition, 2015](http://www.amazon.com/exec/obidos/ASIN/1455733822/pathologyoutl-20))
* Background thyroid ([Am J Surg Pathol 1993;17:698](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/8317610)):
  + There is no association with any specific thyroid condition
  + Highest prevalence of crystals was found in nodular goiters and follicular adenomas (up to 80%)
  + Rarely associated with malignant tumors, 33% prevalence in follicular carcinoma and 5% in papillary carcinoma
  + One study found low prevalence in Graves' disease and Hashimoto thyroiditis but a large amount of crystals in subacute thyroiditis, which were also located in granulomas ([Mills: Histology for Pathologists, Fourth Edition, 2012](http://www.amazon.com/exec/obidos/ASIN/145111303X/pathologyoutl-20))
* Crystals found at autopsy tend to disappear within hours after death ([Virchows Arch A Pathol Anat Histopathol 1993;422:301](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/8506623))
