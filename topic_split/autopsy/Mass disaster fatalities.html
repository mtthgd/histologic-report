Definition / general
  • Mass disaster fatalities are multiple deaths that occur due to the same source event
  • No hard criteria, such as the number of deaths or type of incident, are established to define a mass fatality incident (MFI) as they will vary from jurisdiction to jurisdiction
Essential features
  • Mass fatality incident is an event that results in the deaths of more persons than a local medical examiner / coroner jurisdiction can reasonably accommodate with typical resources
  • Mass fatality preparedness involves preplanning exercises with required partners
  • Mass fatality response will involve a number of partner organizations with individual roles that are best outlined in a mass fatality plan prepared prior to a true response
  • Main responsibilities of the medical examiner / coroner in mass fatality response will be recovery and identification, death certification and morgue operations
Terminology
  • Mass fatality incident
  • Mass casualty incident
Mass fatality incident
  • MFI is an event that results in (or has the potential to result in) more decedents to be recovered and examined than can be managed in the local medical examiner / coroner's (ME / C) jurisdiction (NAME: NAME Position Statement on Disaster Victim Identification in Mass Fatality Incidents [Accessed 24 June 2024])
    • Definition of an MFI will vary from jurisdiction to jurisdiction depending on several factors but largely the size and available resources of that jurisdiction
  • Mass casualty incident (MCI) is an event that results in (or has the potential to result in) more injured or deceased than can be managed by local resources (Mo Med 2018;115:451)
    • MCI is a broader term that may also encompass an MFI
    • May be more complex due to the need for both rescue of injured survivors and recovery of remains
  • MFI can be divided into a variety of categories based on the source of the incident
    • Several different classification systems have been described but the 2 broadest categories can be described as natural MFI and manmade MFI (Acad Forensic Pathol 2017;7:318)
    • Natural MFIs include
      • Weather related incidents (including tornadoes / hurricanes, flooding, etc.)
        • Based on epidemiologic data, the rate of weather related MFI has been increasing with warmer global temperatures (UNDRR: Economic Losses, Poverty & Disasters - 1998-2017 [Accessed 24 June 2024])
      • Non-weather related natural disasters (including earthquakes, tsunamis and volcanic eruptions)
      • Infectious disease incidents (pandemic and epidemic type events)
    • Manmade MFIs include
      • Transportation incidents (including air travel, rail, motor vehicle and shipping / maritime transportation)
      • Infrastructure / industrial incidents (including building collapses, fire and industrial exposure events such as chemical, radioactive and nuclear incidents)
      • Inflicted injury incidents (including mass shootings, mass suicides, war and explosive device incidents)
    • MFIs may be characterized under more than 1 category / subcategory
  • MFIs involve a number of different partner organizations that will interface for a successful incident response
    • National standards recommend the utilization of the National Incident Management System (NIMS) (FEMA: National Incident Management System [Accessed 24 June 2024])
    • In addition to ME / C representatives, an MFI response will include the local emergency management team (EMT), representatives of involved health care organizations, first responders (law enforcement, fire and emergency medical personnel) and members of local volunteer and faith based organizations
    • It is unlikely that the ME / C will be the main agency coordinating the entire MFI response but they will be a main partner
    • Additional agencies may be involved depending on size and degree of response and if additional resources are needed (including state or federal)
Mass fatality response
  • Response to an MFI should result in a systematic implementation of a disaster plan with the ME / C responsible for the jurisdiction (NAME: Standard Operating Procedures for Mass Fatality Management [Accessed 25 June 2024])
    • Documentation, recovery, examination, identification and post-identification disposition of remains
    • Death certification
    • Morgue operations
  • Some of these processes will involve multiple agencies or partners depending on the capabilities of the ME / C and the scale of response
  • First step to response is evaluation by team leads to include
    • Number of fatalities (including additional expected fatalities)
    • Population
      • Closed population: identities known or suspected (i.e., air disaster with a known passenger manifest)
      • Open population: unknown identities (i.e., natural disasters)
    • Condition of remains
      • Intact, fragmented, charred or decomposition related changes
      • Separate individuals or commingled remains
    • Estimated recovery rate
    • Recovery site accessibility
      • Transportation needs
    • Anticipated equipment, storage and supply needs
    • Additional recovery hazards (biological, chemical, physical or weather related)
      • Will decontamination need to occur prior to processing?
    • Morgue establishment
      • Onsite holding / storage
      • Current ME / C facilities versus the need for temporary morgue facilities
    • Personnel needs
      • Additional considerations for food, lodging and transportation
    • Media, communications and security
  • Incident command and organization structure implementation and setup
    • Includes incident command, operations, planning, logistics, finance / administration and communications
  • Site search and recovery
    • Always secondary to life saving operations
    • Joint effort involving law enforcement, ME / C personnel, photography, evidence collection and scribe
    • Each set of remains should be given a unique identifier
    • Data to gather should include the location recovered, personal effects / objects of evidentiary value (with inventory), date / time of collection and any identifying information (with appropriate photographs)
    • Remains are then placed into a body bag with the unique identifier and transferred to the site holding area for transportation to the morgue facility (maintaining chain of custody)
  • Incident morgue facility (Forensic Sci Med Pathol 2017;13:67)
    • Main goals are disaster victim identification (DVI), death certification and final disposition
    • Site should be located away from primary incident site, family assistance center and media / communications
    • Considerations include: size, utilities (water and electricity), drainage, HVAC, administrative / communications space, restrooms and respite areas for personnel, hazardous material and waste collection / storage, parking / transportation, refrigerated storage and security
    • Establishment of a workflow with work stations for collection of postmortem data including
      • Decontamination (if needed)
      • Triage: identification of human / nonhuman remains and separation of commingled remains
      • Admitting: recording of all available information including site information and start of victim identification process
      • Photography
      • Personal effects
      • Radiology
      • Odontology
      • Fingerprinting
      • Anthropology
      • Pathology
      • DNA sample collection
      • Final processing
    • Personal protective equipment for standard biological precautions should be used at all times except in the administrative / respite areas
      • Specialty protective equipment and decontamination areas may be utilized depending on the type of MFI
  • Family assistance center
    • Separate from the main incident site, morgue facilities and media / communications
    • Provide assistance to families attempting to locate missing persons
    • Provide a site for collection of antemortem data and evidence that can be utilized for identification purposes
    • Single site for communication with families (both individual and group)
  • Media relations / communications
    • Media presence is often significant at MFI
    • Organized dissemination of information is key to an orderly response
    • Separate from incident site, morgue facilities and family assistance center to allow for privacy and respect to personnel, the deceased and their families
  • The National Disaster Medical System provides federal resources in the event of MCI / MFI which require greater response than local and state ME / C systems can accommodate (ASPR: Disaster Mortuary Operational Response Teams [Accessed 9 July 2024])
    • Disaster mortuary operational response teams (DMORTs) are regionally available to provide assistance
    • Response capabilities can range from limited (such as resources and logistics support) to full response (including personnel and specialists)
    • Activation requires an emergency declaration and requests from the local and state levels
Disaster victim identification
  • Disaster victim identification is a multidisciplinary process involving detailed data recording and multifaceted personnel utilization
    • Forensic odontologists can assist in identification through comparison or antemortem and postmortem dental radiographs or records (Forensic Sci Res 2019;4:316)
    • Forensic anthropologists can assist in (Forensic Sci Res 2018;4:303)
      • Separating osseous from nonosseous materials
      • Separating human from nonhuman remains
      • Determining and separation of elements in potentially commingled remains
      • Providing biological profile and other potentially identifying features
      • Injury reconstruction
    • Forensic pathologists can assist in identification through body characteristics (identifying marks or features) or implanted medical devices
    • Additional personnel involved in identification efforts may include radiologic technologists, fingerprint specialists and DNA experts
  • See Autopsy & forensics - Identification
Mass disaster planning
  • All ME / C offices should have a developed mass disaster plan (Forensic Sci Int Synerg 2024;8:100462)
  • Preincident planning should involve the ME / C and partner organizations
    • Key areas include
      • Agency / cross agency protocols and procedures
      • Resources availability, procurement and storage
      • Communications plans
      • Identification of factors that may challenge response capabilities (Forensic Sci Res 2023;8:107)
  • Preparedness is highly variable within different sectors and is generally thought to be suboptimal, especially in the event of a large scale MFI (Disaster Med Public Health Prep 2016;10:87)
  • Deathcare sector (including funeral homes and suppliers) should also be engaged in planning as they are a potential bottleneck (J Occup Environ Med 2011;53:1179)
  • Regular exercises involving observers and partner organizations are needed for effective MFI responses (Forensic Sci Med Pathol 2012;8:88)
    • Observers, planners and participants should know the goals of the exercise and the intended outcomes, which may include
      • Teams integration and familiarization
      • Organization and preparedness (including resource identification)
      • Addressing potential psychological and emotional impacts
      • Testing infrastructure, equipment and command control structure
    • Exercises may take many forms and each has particular strengths and weaknesses
      • Seminar, tabletop, post control and live simulation
    • Well managed exercise should identify both strengths and capability gaps

Board review style question #1
During a mass fatality incident (MFI), which of the following aspects of disaster response would the medical examiner / coroner (ME / C) be responsible for?

  1. Debriefing of the event
  2. Establishment of an incident command center
  3. Identification and collection of forensic evidence related to a terrorist act
  4. Planning a field exercise for MFI
  5. Victim recovery, identification and death certification
Board review style answer #1
E. Victim recovery, identification and death certification. Recovery, identification and death certification would all be the responsibility of the local ME / C regardless of who is involved in the MFI response. Answer A is incorrect because debriefing should occur after the incident has been resolved and would not be the sole responsibility of the ME / C. Answer B is incorrect because establishing the incident command center would be the responsibility of the emergency operations management team. Answer C is incorrect because forensic evidence should be identified and collected by appropriate law enforcement agencies. An ME / C representative may incidentally identify evidence but it would not be their responsibility to catalog or collect it. Answer D is incorrect because planning for a field exercise would not be appropriate during the course of an active MFI and would divert needed resources.

Board review style question #2
During a mass fatality incident, which of the following should be located directly at the site of the incident?

  1. Family assistance center
  2. Incident command center
  3. Media relations
  4. Morgue facilities
  5. Recovered deceased and evidence storage
Board review style answer #2
E. Recovered deceased and evidence storage. Of the listed options, only temporary storage for material collected onsite should be located at the incident site (in addition to storage of supplies and personnel needs). Answer A is incorrect because the family assistance center should be located at a secondary site so family members are not subject to potential ongoing trauma from visiting the incident site. Answer B is incorrect because the incident command center may require significant space that could disrupt recovery efforts. When possible, the incident command center should be located near the primary incident site for fast and reliable movement of information / personnel. Answer C is incorrect because the media relations area should be separate from the incident site to maintain respect and dignity for the site and those who died there. Answer D is incorrect because the size and requirements for morgue facilities would usually not permit placement at the main incident site as well as potentially disrupting the incident site recovery efforts.
