Definition / general
  • An excellent autopsy report is the cornerstone of forensic pathology, providing the critical details that elucidate the causes and circumstances of death, helping both the investigation and the courtroom reach the truth
  • In forensic pathology, court testimony transforms scientific findings from the autopsy into compelling narratives that uphold justice, bridging the gap between medical evidence and legal resolution
Essential features
  • Summary of findings must be a comprehensive list of all the pathologic diagnoses and ancillary study results
  • Opinion section must include the cause and manner of death
    • In selected cases, a detailed opinion can be added to explain the determination of cause and manner of death
  • Forensic experts can be called to testify in court
    • Can testify on own cases or autopsies performed by other forensic pathologists
      • If the reviewed material is enough to allow for an independent and impartial opinion
  • Court testimony includes several steps, from appointing the forensic pathologist as an expert to a thorough cross examination by the defense attorney
Terminology
  • Summary of findings / final anatomical diagnosis (FAD): summarizes the pertinent autopsy findings
  • Opinion: the forensic pathologist's professional interpretation and conclusion regarding the cause and manner of death, based on the findings and evidence gathered during the examination
  • Forensic testimony: the testimony given by scientific experts to aid in the resolution of a lawsuit or prosecution
  • Voir dire examination: process to establish expert's competency in profession and field
  • Direct examination: initial questioning of a witness by the party that called them to the stand
  • Cross examination: formal questioning of a witness called by the other party in a trial to challenge the testimony the witness has already given
Summary of findings
  • Summary of findings / final anatomic diagnosis usually written in a list format and may include
    • Cause of death
    • Natural disease or pre-existing conditions
    • Evidence of medical / surgical therapy
    • Other minor traumatic injury
    • Postmortem changes
    • Identifying body marks
    • Toxicology and microbiology results
    • Other ancillary studies results
  • Reference: Virchows Arch 2022;480:509
Opinion
  • The most crucial section of the autopsy report, likely to be read by lawyers, judges, law enforcement, insurance companies, the general public and most importantly, the deceased's family members
  • First and foremost, the opinion must include the cause and manner of death
  • In selected cases, the opinion may include a paragraph with the interpretation of the findings pertinent to the cause and manner of death
  • Opinion section may be a few short sentences or several pages, depending on the complexity of the case
  • Differing opinions can occur regarding cause and manner of death but the opinion must be precise, factual and logical (Tidsskr Nor Laegeforen 2021;141:11)
Witness testimony
  • Types of witnesses
    • Lay witness: testimony is admissible if it is based on the witness’s perceptions (use of their senses) and is pertinent to the case (J Forensic Sci Med 2018;4:221)
    • Professional witness: fact witness is similar to a lay witness; this testimony is given by a professional that relates facts of what was seen or heard in the context of their profession (Surg Clin North Am 2007;87:889)
      • Example: a surgeon relating the facts of the case during a surgery
    • Expert testimony: testimony given by a witness who is qualified as an expert (StatPearls: Expert Witness [Accessed 11 July 2024])
      • Expert opinion may be based on
        • Facts or data seen or heard by the expert
        • Facts or data communicated to the expert before the hearing
        • Admissibility of facts or data not essential if typically relied on in the field
      • Expert opinion on the ultimate issue
        • Expert may express an opinion on the ultimate issue of the facts using their training and experience
        • Expert is not required to be present at the scene or to have performed any procedures
  • Opinions or inferences regarding the mental state of the accused are reserved for when the mental state is an element of the crime charged or a defense to that crime
  • Disclosure of facts or data underlying expert opinion: an expert does not need to provide facts supporting the reason for their opinion unless it is required by the court or the witness is asked to provide the facts upon cross examination
Expert witness competency
  • Competency is determined by knowledge, skill, experience, training, education or a combination thereof
  • Expert must know underlying methodology and procedures for their opinion
  • Background knowledge includes technology, literature review and experience leading to an opinion with scientific certainty
  • No absolute rule for knowledge degree; affects weight and credibility of testimony once competency is established
  • Reference: Forensic Sci Int 2007;168:89
Voir dire examination
  • Used to establish expert's competency in profession and field (Camb Q Healthc Ethics 2021;30:702)
  • Conducted to speak the truth, revealing qualifications
  • Opposing attorney can challenge qualifications
  • Court determines expert status (Forensic Magazine: Qualifying the Expert Witness - A Practical Voir Dire [Accessed 11 July 2024])
  • Importance of voir dire (Med Sci Law 2022;62:206)
    • Sets standard for expert testimony and credibility
    • First impression for the judge and jury
    • Neither party should assume qualification
    • Stipulations should reflect the witness's biographical statement
Expert witness discovery
  • Controlled by Federal Rule of Civil Procedure (FRCP) 26 and Daubert standards (Am J Public Health 2005;95:S59)
  • Expert must provide written summary opinion, qualifications, previous testimony and compensation details
  • Continuing duty to provide updated information under FRCP 26(e)(1)
  • Salaries and compensation affect weight and credibility, not qualifications
  • Compensation does not inherently affect testimony reliability but is relevant for cross examination
  • Prior judicial recognition of qualifications is not sole determinant; deposition testimony is not equivalent to court recognition
Governmental imprimatur and expert trustworthiness
  • Competency must be established before opinion evidence is provided (Front Psychol 2021;12:785677)
  • Governmental association does not guarantee reliability
  • Job titles, like drug recognition expert, can imply authority but may not reflect true expertise
  • Trial tactics can influence expert selection, sometimes compromising reliability
  • Competence does not guarantee credibility; improper qualification harms litigants (Int J Law Psychiatry 2015;41:58)
  • Examples of flawed science and false testimony exist
  • Witnesses must not have an interest in trial outcomes
  • Fraudulent claims of professional status can lead to legal consequences
Qualifying an expert witness
  • List of qualifying questions will be asked to qualify the forensic pathologist as an expert witness (Can Fam Physician 2007;53:65, SAGE Open Nurs 2018;4:2377960818757097)
  • Questions may include
    • Full name (with spelling)
    • Occupation
      • Definition of assistant medical examiner / forensic pathologist
      • Duties
    • Place of employment
    • Position(s) currently held
      • Length of time at current position(s)
    • Educational background
      • Medical school, residency and fellowship
      • Board certification(s)
      • Definitions (pathology, forensic pathology)
    • Academic background (if any)
      • Research activity (publications, etc.)
      • Scientific meetings and conferences
      • Teaching experience(s)
      • Membership in professional societies
      • Honors, awards or acknowledgments
    • State(s) and year of medical license
    • Professional experience
      • Number of autopsies
      • Previous experience(s) as an expert witness
      • Activity as a private consultant
      • Testimony on autopsy cases performed by other pathologists
  • After the qualifying questions are asked, the lawyer will request that the forensic pathologist be qualified as an expert in forensic pathology
  • Opposing council is given the opportunity to object to the request of qualifying the forensic pathologist as an expert
Direct examination of expert witness
  • The party will have the opportunity to ask questions of the expert witness in regard to the facts of the case (Can Fam Physician 2007;53:65)
  • Witness should wait to answer questions until it is clear that the opposing party does not object to the question
  • If an autopsy report was generated, the party may request that the forensic pathologist be allowed to refer to the report if it will help aid in the testimony
    • Copy of the autopsy report will be available and shown to the expert witness
    • They will be asked to confirm that the report corresponds to the one generated
    • Typically, the autopsy report is referred to as protocol
  • Pathologist may be asked to explain what an external examination and an autopsy is to the court
  • Pathologist may be asked about the facts surrounding the case and the autopsy
Cross examination of expert witness
  • Opposing party will have the chance to assess the credibility and reliability of the expert's testimony (J R Soc Med 2002;95:431)
  • Attorneys may
    • Ask technical questions to test the expert's knowledge and expertise in forensic pathology
    • Questions may focus on the expert's qualifications, experience and training in forensic pathology
    • Point questions to probe the expert's methodologies and conclusions
    • Expose any conflict of interests or limitations in the expert's understanding or proficiency in the field (Clin Orthop Relat Res 2009;467:383)
    • Attempt to discredit the expert's opinions by highlighting any biases, conflicts of interest or errors in their analysis
      • Even minor mistakes on the autopsy report (e.g., wrong autopsy date or wrong height, weight or race) may result in loss of credibility
Tips for a good expert witness court testimony
  • Wear professional attire
  • Remain calm, even under pressure
  • Always wait for the question to be asked before starting to answer
    • If the question is not clear, ask for it to be repeated; if it is still unclear, ask for it to be rephrased
  • Look at the interviewer when being asked questions
    • Although there is no definitive rule, it is suggested to look at the jury while answering questions
    • Looking at the judge is also acceptable
  • Always use easy, nonmedical terms when answering questions
    • Judge, attorneys and jury may be unfamiliar with medical terms
    • Using extremely technical language may jeopardize an otherwise unremarkable postmortem examination and related testimony
  • Reference: SAGE Open Nurs 2018;4:2377960818757097
Additional references

Board review style question #1
Which statement characterizes the opinion testimony provided by an expert witness in forensic pathology?

  1. An expert witness can express an opinion on the ultimate issue of the case using their specialized training and experience
  2. An expert witness is required to disclose all underlying facts and data supporting their opinion during direct examination
  3. An expert witness's opinion is only admissible if it aligns with the facts testified to by lay witnesses
  4. An expert witness's opinion must be accepted by the court without any challenge once their qualifications are established
  5. An expert witness's opinion must be based solely on their personal observations at the scene of the incident
Board review style answer #1
A. An expert witness can express an opinion on the ultimate issue of the case, drawing on their specialized training, experience and expertise to interpret the facts and provide conclusions that assist the court. Answer E is incorrect because an expert witness's opinion does not have to be based solely on their personal observations at the scene of the incident. They can form opinions based on various data sources, including information communicated to them before the hearing. Answer C is incorrect because an expert witness's opinion is not limited to aligning with the facts testified to by lay witnesses. Expert opinions can include interpretations of data that lay witnesses might not be qualified to understand or testify about. Answer B is incorrect because an expert witness is not required to disclose all underlying facts and data supporting their opinion during direct examination unless specifically asked to do so by the court or during cross examination. Answer D is incorrect because an expert witness's opinion is not automatically accepted by the court without challenge. Even after their qualifications are established, their testimony can still be subjected to cross examination and the opposing party can challenge the credibility and reliability of their opinion.

Board review style question #2
When qualifying a forensic pathologist as an expert witness, which of the following aspects is most crucial for demonstrating their expertise in forensic pathology?

  1. Academic background, including medical school, residency and board certifications
  2. Membership in professional societies
  3. Number of scientific meetings attended
  4. The definition and duties of a forensic pathologist
  5. The forensic pathologist’s full name and place of employment
Board review style answer #2
A. Academic background, including medical school, residency and board certifications, is crucial for demonstrating the pathologist's formal qualifications and expertise in forensic pathology. Answer D is incorrect because while understanding the definition and duties is important, it is more fundamental and does not directly demonstrate the pathologist’s expertise. Answer B is incorrect because membership in professional societies is relevant but not as crucial as demonstrating formal education and certification for establishing expertise. Answer E is incorrect because these details are basic identifiers and do not specifically demonstrate expertise in forensic pathology. Answer C is incorrect because attendance at scientific meetings is beneficial for keeping current but does not alone demonstrate expertise compared to formal education and certification.
