Definition / general

* Carcinoma derived from the urothelium and showing pure glandular differentiation
* Not including urachal adenocarcinoma or urothelial carcinoma with glandular differentiation

Essential features

* Rare, derived from the urothelium of the bladder with pure glandular differentiation
* Histology: intestinal (enteric) type, mucinous type, signet ring cell type, NOS and mixed
* Immunohistochemistry: often CK7+ and CK20+ or CK7- and CK20+, no nuclear staining for beta catenin
* Diagnosis made after excluding secondary adenocarcinoma either by metastasis or by direct extension

Terminology

* Primary adenocarcinoma of the bladder
* Primary adenocarcinoma of the ureter
* Primary adenocarcinoma of the renal pelvis

ICD coding

* ICD-10:
  + [C65](https://www.icd10data.com/ICD10CM/Codes/C00-D49/C64-C68/C65-/C65) - malignant neoplasm of renal pelvis
  + [C66](https://www.icd10data.com/ICD10CM/Codes/C00-D49/C64-C68/C66-/C66) - malignant neoplasm of ureter
  + [C67](https://www.icd10data.com/ICD10CM/Codes/C00-D49/C64-C68/C67-/C67) - malignant neoplasm of bladder

Epidemiology

* Rare primary neoplasm, 0.5 - 2% of bladder malignancies ([J Urol 2010;183:915](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/20083267))
  + 0.7% of upper urinary tract tumors ([Clin Genitourin Cancer 2021;19:117](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/33358490))
* M:F = 3:1
* Mean age 68 years ([J Urol 2010;183:915](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/20083267), [Cancer 1991;67:2165](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/1706216))

Sites

* Usually lateral, posterior wall or trigone of bladder (if dome, should consider urachal adenocarcinoma by default)
* Second most common site in urethra; rare in ureters and renal pelvis

Etiology

* Some cases may be due to progression of extensive intestinal metaplasia (cystitis glandularis) or villous adenoma; these cases arise at trigone and are usually enteric
* Exstrophy: diffuse intestinalization; 4 - 7% risk of developing adenocarcinoma, even after repair ([J Urol 1970;104:699](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/5486851))
* Diverticula: usually develop urothelial carcinoma, occasionally adenocarcinoma
* Also *Schistosoma haematobium* ([Urol Oncol 2006;24:13](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/16414487))
* Chronic irritation of bladder, including nonfunctioning bladder or obstruction ([Urol Oncol 2006;24:13](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/16414487))
* Urethral adenocarcinomas may arise from metaplastic surface mucosa or from periurethral glands secondary to chronic inflammation, stricture, diverticula, fistula and infections

Clinical features

* Usually presents with hematuria, rarely with mucusuria, dysuria ([Cancer 1991;67:2165](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/1706216))
* Patients are older and mucusuria is more common than in urachal adenocarcinoma
* 5 year disease free survival rate is 40 - 50% ([Urol Oncol 2006;24:13](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/16414487))

Diagnosis

* Screen test: urine cytology
* Imaging modalities include ultrasound, computerized tomography (CT) urogram, retrograde pyelogram or magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) urogram
* Cystoscopic examination of the entire urethra and bladder, with diagnostic specimens collected by either biopsy or transurethral resection of bladder tumor (TURBT) ([Eur Urol Focus 2021 [Epub ahead of print]](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/33867307))

Prognostic factors

* Stage is most important prognostic factor
* Compared with urothelial carcinoma, patients with bladder adenocarcinoma have a worse prognosis, as they commonly present at a more advanced stage
* It is controversial whether it is more aggressive if controlled for tumor stage
* After adjusting pathologic stage, the presence of lymphovascular invasion and lymph node status, a study demonstrated that the primary adenocarcinoma has a worse prognosis than conventional urothelial carcinoma ([J Urol 2006;175:2048](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/16697800))

Treatment

* Radical cystectomy and pelvic lymph node dissection
* Adjuvant therapy (radiation or chemotherapy) may be given in some cases

Microscopic (histologic) description

* Intestinal type (or enteric type):
  + Resembles colonic adenocarcinoma ([Cancer 1991;67:2165](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/1706216))
  + Neoplastic glands are lined by pleomorphic mucin producing pseudostratified columnar epithelium
  + Central dirty necrosis is commonly seen
* Mucinous (colloid) type:
  + Nests of neoplastic cells floating in abundant extravasated mucin
  + Singly dispersed or groups of signet ring cells can be seen in the mucin pools
  + Mucin usually deeply invades muscularis propria
* Signet ring cell type (diffuse poorly differentiated adenocarcinoma):
  + Discohesive round cells with large intracellular mucin vacuoles displacing nuclei to the periphery without extracellular mucin
  + Diffusely infiltrating stromal tissue
* Association with in situ component is an important clue to differentiate primary adenocarcinoma of the bladder from secondary adenocarcinoma involving the bladder
* [Intestinal metaplasia](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/bladderintestinalmetaplasia.html) or [villous adenoma](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/bladdervillousadenoma.html) may be seen

Cytology description

* Clusters of tumor cells, intracytoplasmic vacuoles, eccentrically located nuclei, hyperchromatic irregular nuclear contours, conspicuous to prominent nucleoli ([Cancer 1998;84:335](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/9915134))
* Tumor cells of signet ring cell carcinoma show large intracytoplasmic mucin vacuole, hyperchromatic crescent shaped nuclei
* Background necrosis or mucin may be present, depending on the subtype

Molecular / cytogenetics description

* Harbors recurrent mutations in *TP53*, *KRAS*, *PIK3CA*, *CTNNB1*, *APC* and the promoter of *TERT* ([Adv Anat Pathol 2020;27:303](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/32520749), [Mod Pathol 2017;30:1133](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/28548125), [Proc Natl Acad Sci U S A 2013;110:17426](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/24101484))
* Most common abnormal FISH finding is 9p21 homozygous loss, followed by polysomy pattern ([Diagn Pathol 2012;7:151](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/23121893))

Sample pathology report

* Bladder and prostate, radical cystoprostatectomy:
  + Bladder, invasive adenocarcinoma, mixed mucinous type with signet ring cell component (see synoptic report)
  + Prostate, negative for carcinoma

Differential diagnosis

* [Urothelial carcinoma with predominantly glandular features / differentiation](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/kidneytumormalignanturothelialcarcinomasubtypesglandulardiff.html):
  + Glandular differentiation in urothelial carcinoma is second most common after squamous differentiation
  + Composed of enteric type glands with columnar epithelium and mucin production with typical urothelial carcinoma components present
    - However, typical urothelial carcinoma components, such as invasive or noninvasive urothelial carcinoma, are present
  + Examine the tumor specimen carefully to rule out any urothelial component
* [Metastatic or local extension of colonic cancer](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/colontumoradenocarcinoma.html):

  [![Metastatic colonic adenocarcinoma](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/imgau/bladderadenoZuo12.jpg)](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/imgau/bladderadenoZuo12.jpg)

  [![Nuclear beta catenin](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/imgau/bladderadenoZuo13.jpg)](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/imgau/bladderadenoZuo13.jpg)

  + No in situ adenocarcinoma component or precursor lesion like [villous adenoma](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/bladdervillousadenoma.html)
  + [Beta catenin](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsbetacatenin.html) nuclear staining (81%), [CK7](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsck7.html)-, [thrombomodulin](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsthrombomodulin.html), also [CK20](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsck20.html)+ ([Am J Surg Pathol 2001;25:1380](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/11684954), [Am J Surg Pathol 1993;17:171](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/8422112))
* [Metastatic or local extension of prostatic adenocarcinoma (particularly ductal type)](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/prostateadenoNOS.html):
  + [PSA](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainspsa.html)+, [PSAP](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainspap.html)+, [PSMA](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainspsma.html)+, [NKX3.1](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsnkx3.html)+, [prostein (P501S)](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsprostein.html)+, [CDX2](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainscdx2.html)-, [thrombomodulin](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsthrombomodulin.html)-
  + Nuclear [NKX3.1](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsnkx3.html) expression is specific for prostatic origin and retains even after heavily treated with androgen deprivation therapy, while hormonally treated prostate cancer often loses expression of [PSA](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainspsa.html) and [PSAP](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainspap.html)
* [Metastatic or local extension of endometrial](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/uterusendometrialcarc.html) and [cervical adenocarcinoma](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/cervixhpvadenocarcinoma.html):
  + [Endometrioid adenocarcinoma](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/uterusendometrioid.html): [ER](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainser.html)+, [PR](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsprog.html)+ and [vimentin](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsvimentin.html)+
  + [Cervical adenocarcinoma](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/cervixhpvadenocarcinoma.html): [p16](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsp16.html) strong nuclear and cytoplasmic immunoreactivity (some primary bladder adenocarcinoma can express [p16](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsp16.html)), high risk [HPV](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainshpv.html)+ by chromogenic in situ hybridization (CISH)
* [Other metastatic disease](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/bladdermets.html):
  + [Ovarian serous carcinoma](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/ovarytumorserouscarcinomahg.html): [PAX8](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainspax8.html)+, [p53](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsp53.html)+ (or null type), [WT1](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainswt1.html)+, [ER](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainser.html)+, [PR](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsprog.html)+
  + **Breast carcinoma**: [GATA3](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsgata3.html)+, panel of [ER](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainser.html) variable, [PR](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsprog.html) variable, [HER2](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsbreasther2.html) variable, [GCDFP-15](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsgcdfp15.html) variable, [mammaglobin](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsmammaglobin.html) variable, [uroplakin II](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsUroplakinii.html)
  + [Ovarian](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/ovarytumorclearcell.html) and [endometrial clear cell adenocarcinoma](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/uterusclearcell.html): [napsin A](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsnapsina.html)+, [HNF-1B](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsHNF1B.html)+, [p53](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsp53.html)+ (or null type)
  + [Lung adenocarcinoma](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/lungtumoradenocarcinoma.html): [TTF1](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsttf1.html)+
* [Urachal adenocarcinoma](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/bladderadenourachal.html):
  + Located in urachus / dome
  + Epicenter of the mass is usually in the muscular wall of the bladder
  + [CK7](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsck7.html)+ (variable), [CK20](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsck20.html)+ (variable), [CDX2](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainscdx2.html) (variable), [high molecular cytokeratin](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsck34be12.html) (variable), [beta catenin](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsbetacatenin.html) negative for nuclear staining
* [Müllerian type carcinoma of the bladder and the urethra](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/bladderclearcell.html):
  + Most commonly arise from [endometriosis](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/bladderendometriosis.html)
  + [Clear cell adenocarcinoma](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/bladderclearcell.html): [PAX8](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainspax8.html)+, [HNF-1B](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsHNF1B.html)+, [AMACR](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsamacr.html)+
* [Villous adenoma](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/bladdervillousadenoma.html):
  + Rare benign neoplasm of the urinary tract and urachus
  + No invasion
* [Intestinal metaplasia](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/bladderintestinalmetaplasia.html):
  + May mimic well differentiated adenocarcinoma due to widespread involvement with dissecting mucin pools
  + However, minimal atypia, no mitoses, no signet ring cells, noninfiltrative, no muscle invasion ([Hum Pathol 1997;28:1152](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/9343322))
* [Florid cystitis glandularis](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/bladdercystitiscystica.html):
  + No nuclear anaplasia, rarely invades muscularis propria

A bladder TURBT is shown. Which of the following immunohistochemistry stains is most helpful in distinguishing the metastatic colon cancer to the bladder from the primary adenocarcinoma of the bladder, intestinal type?

1. Beta catenin
2. CDX2
3. CK7
4. CK20
5. STAB2

Board review style answer #1

**A.** Beta catenin. Up to 80% of metastatic colon cancers are positive for nuclear beta catenin (in addition to cytoplasmic stains), an indicator of WNT pathway activation. Primary adenocarcinoma of the bladder, intestinal type can express CK20, CDX2 and STAB2, however, lacks nuclear beta catenin staining.
