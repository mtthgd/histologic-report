Definition / general
  • An inverted growth pattern can be observed in urothelial carcinoma, although it is not recognized as a distinct subtype by the WHO
  • It has to be distinguished from the inverted pattern associated with papilloma urothelial neoplasm of low malignant potential, low / high grade tumors that are noninvasive
Essential features
  • Inverted (endophytic) growth pattern can be associated with papilloma, urothelial neoplasm of low malignant potential, low / high grade tumors that are noninvasive or invasive; this topic discusses invasive tumors
  • Anastomosing cords and columns of urothelium resembling inverted papilloma or broad pushing bulbous invaginations into lamina propria (broad front pattern) but with definitive evidence of invasion
  • Not a WHO diagnosis
Terminology
  • Inverted papillary urothelial carcinoma
ICD coding
  • ICD-O: 8120/3 - urothelial carcinoma, NOS
Epidemiology
  • Mean age: 68 years
  • 75% male
  • Reference: Hum Pathol 2019;92:18
Sites
  • Bladder, ureter, renal pelvis
Pathophysiology
  • Usually exophytic papillary urothelial carcinoma is also present
Etiology
  • Smoking is the main risk factor

Microscopic (histologic) description
  • Anastomosing cords and columns of urothelium resembling inverted papilloma or broad pushing bulbous invaginations into lamina propria (broad front pattern)
  • Trabeculae are wider and more irregular than those of inverted papilloma
  • Invasive if there are irregularity of broad front of bulbous invaginations, retraction artifact, desmoplasia or other features of destructive invasion
  • Usually exophytic papillary urothelial carcinoma is also present
  • Substantial nuclear pleomorphism, readily apparent mitotic figures and architectural abnormalities (Ann Diagn Pathol 2020;44:151433)
  • At least 25% of the tumor should have an inverted component to be considered as inverted urothelial carcinoma (BJU Int 2011;107:532)
  • Findings suggestive of invasion: irregularly shaped nests with disruption or absence of the basement membrane, desmoplasia or fibrotic stromal response (note that microinvasion usually does not elicit a stromal response, which makes its identification more difficult)
  • Pattern is associated with invasion in 50% of papillary urothelial carcinomas (Am J Surg Pathol 1997;21:1057)
  • Urothelial carcinoma in situ, if present in the surface urothelium, provides support for a diagnosis of inverted urothelial carcinoma

Sample pathology report
  • Bladder, cystectomy:
    • Urothelial carcinoma, invasive, inverted growth pattern
    • WHO grade: high grade
    • Histological subtype features: inverted growth
Differential diagnosis
  • Inverted papilloma:
    • May have focal papillary pattern; normal bland urothelium with occasional umbrella cells, no invasion (Ann Diagn Pathol 2009;13:158)
    • Negative for Ki67, CK20, p53; normal fluorescence in situ hybridization (FISH) (Am J Surg Pathol 2007;31:1861)
    • Shows HRAS mutations in particular and seems to lack pathway mutations in TERT promoter, FGFR3 and TP53 / RB1 genes (J Pathol 2019;249:3)
    • p53 is not typically altered in low grade / benign inverted papilloma but is associated with higher grade / malignant subtypes (Ann Diagn Pathol 2020;44:151433)
  • Inverted papilloma with atypia:
    • Rare mitotic figures and very low proliferation rate as estimated by Ki67 immunostaining

Board review style question #1
Which of the following immunohistochemical stains could help to identify malignant components in an inverted papillary lesion of the urinary bladder?

  1. CK14
  2. CK20
  3. CD44
  4. p53
Board review style answer #1
D. p53. Due to its association with invasiveness and tumor aggression, p53 is useful in assessing the presence of malignant characteristics in an inverted papillary lesion of the bladder. p53 can be useful for distinguishing between benign / low grade lesions (like inverted papilloma, which generally do not show p53 alterations) and malignant components. Answer A is incorrect because CK14 is not a marker specifically used to detect invasive characteristics in bladder lesions, particularly in an inverted papillary lesion, where squamous differentiation is not the main focus. Answer B is incorrect because, while CK20 can help confirm a urothelial carcinoma, it does not distinguish between invasive and noninvasive lesions, making it less useful for identifying invasion in this context. Answer C is incorrect because, although CD44 plays a role in the biology of urothelial carcinoma, it is not specific enough to be a marker for assessing invasion directly in bladder lesions.
