Definition / general

* Malignant epithelial neoplasm arising in the urinary bladder, renal pelvis or ureter demonstrating a pure squamous cell phenotype
* This topic describes classic squamous cell carcinoma; please see separate topic on [verrucous squamous cell carcinoma](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/bladdersquamousverrucous.html)

Essential features

* Uncommon subtype of carcinoma showing pure squamous morphology without any component of conventional urothelial carcinoma
* Predisposing factors include *Schistosoma* infection, smoking and conditions resulting in chronic irritation for bladder tumors and nephrolithiasis, anatomic anomalies of the kidney and chronic recurrent infections for upper urinary tract tumors
* May be associated with keratinizing squamous metaplasia / dysplasia of the mucosa
* Must be distinguished from urothelial carcinoma with squamous differentiation, secondary spread of squamous cell carcinoma primary to another site, e.g. cervix, penis, anus and metastasis

Terminology

* Classic / typical squamous cell carcinoma

ICD coding

* ICD-O: [8070/3](https://apps.who.int/iris/bitstream/handle/10665/96612/9789241548496_eng.pdf?sequence=1#page=63) - squamous cell carcinoma, NOS
* ICD-10
  + [C67](https://www.icd10data.com/ICD10CM/Codes/C00-D49/C64-C68/C67-/C67) - malignant neoplasm of bladder
  + [C65](https://www.icd10data.com/ICD10CM/Codes/C00-D49/C64-C68/C65-/C65) - malignant neoplasm of renal pelvis
  + [C66](https://www.icd10data.com/ICD10CM/Codes/C00-D49/C64-C68/C66-/C66) - malignant neoplasm of ureter

Epidemiology

* Bladder
  + Incidence varies by geographic region
  + 2 - 5% of bladder tumors in Western countries (nonbilharzial) ([Arab J Urol 2016;14:183](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/27547458))
    - Most common nonurothelial subtype of bladder cancer
  + 20 - 30% of bladder tumors in geographic regions with high prevalence of schistosomiasis (bilharzial) including Egypt and other parts of Africa ([Arab J Urol 2016;14:183](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/27547458))
    - Frequency of squamous cell carcinoma is falling due to declining rates of *Schistosoma* infection ([Cancer Causes Control 2008;19:421](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/18188671))
  + Slight male predominance
* Renal pelvis / ureter
  + 1 - 8% of malignancies involving the renal pelvis and ureter ([J Urol 1987;138:269](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/3599235), [J Urol 2007;178:51](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/17574059))

Sites

* Bladder: most commonly involving posterior and lateral walls ([Am J Surg Pathol 2007;31:1777](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/18043032))
* Renal pelvis
* Ureter

Pathophysiology / etiology

* Bladder
  + Schistosomiasis
    - *Schistosoma haematobium*
    - Major risk factor for bladder squamous cell carcinoma in geographic regions with high prevalence of schistosomiasis including Egypt and other parts of Africa
  + Smoking: 5x increased risk compared with nonsmokers ([JAMA 2011;306:737](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/21846855))
  + Chronic bladder irritation / inflammation associated with:
    - Long term catheterization
    - Bladder calculi or foreign bodies
    - Neurogenic bladder
    - Bladder exstrophy ([Urology 2006;67:199](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/16413365))
  + Renal pelvis / ureter
    - Nephrolithiasis, such as staghorn calculi ([J Urol 1987;138:269](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/3599235))
    - Anatomic anomalies of the kidney, such as horseshoe kidney, duplicated renal pelvis ([Int J Urol 2004;11:782](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/15379945), [Int J Clin Exp Pathol 2014;7:7957](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/25550838))
    - Chronic recurrent infections

Clinical features

* Typically presents with hematuria
* May also present with dysuria, urgency and frequent urination, recurrent urinary tract infection, urinary obstruction and flank or suprapubic pain

Diagnosis

* Urine cytology
* Cystoscopy and biopsy / transurethral resection of bladder tumor

Radiology description

* Bladder: tumors may appear plaque-like, polypoid, papillary or circumferential bladder wall thickening ([Radiographics 2004;24:855](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/15143234))
* Renal pelvis / ureter: solid mass may be seen, along with other nonspecific findings, such as hydronephrosis, calcifications and nephrolithiasis ([J Urol 1987;138:269](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/3599235), [Urology 1992;40:458](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/1441047), [Int J Urol 2000;7:316](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/10976822))
* CT or MRI may be used for local staging to delineate suspected depth of tumor invasion ([J Natl Compr Canc Netw 2020;18:329](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/32135513))

Prognostic factors

* Prognosis determined by tumor stage (staged in the same way as urothelial carcinoma), lymph node involvement and tumor grade ([Urology 1999;53:1005](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/10223497), [J Urol 2012;187:74](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/22088332))
* Often advanced stage at diagnosis resulting in poor prognosis ([Am J Surg Pathol 2007;31:1777](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/18043032), [J Urol 2010;183:915](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/20083267))
* Upper urinary tract squamous cell carcinoma presents with higher stage (and with frequent renal parenchyma invasion) than bladder squamous cell carcinoma ([J Urol 2007;178:51](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/17574059), [Clin Genitourin Cancer 2020 Dec 2 [Epub ahead of print]](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/33358490), [J Urol 2012;187:74](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/22088332))
* Schistosomiasis associated squamous cell carcinoma appears to have better prognosis than non-schistosomiasis associated ([BJU Int 2004;93:216](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/14690486))

* 80 year old woman with squamous cell carcinoma in the renal pelvis of a horseshoe kidney ([Int J Urol 2004;11:782](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/15379945))

Treatment

* Radical cystectomy / nephrectomy / nephroureterectomy
* Neoadjuvant radiation may improve outcome ([J Urol 2012;187:74](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/22088332), [Arab J Urol 2016;14:183](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/27547458))
* Insufficient evidence to support the use of chemotherapy

Microscopic (histologic) description

* May be well, moderately or poorly differentiated
  + Moderately or poor differentiated tumors are most common
* Irregular infiltrating nests or sheets of malignant squamous cells with destructive stromal invasion
* Squamous differentiation in the form of keratin pearls, individual cell keratinization or intercellular bridges; findings may be focal in poorly differentiated tumors
* Often associated with surface keratinizing squamous metaplasia and dysplasia / squamous carcinoma in situ ([Am J Surg Pathol 2007;31:1777](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/18043032))
* Must not show any component of conventional urothelial carcinoma or urothelial carcinoma in situ
  + If present, even focally, tumor is designated as urothelial carcinoma with squamous differentiation

* Often necroinflammatory background

Squamous cell carcinoma

Positive stains

* Positive for squamous markers [CK5/6](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsck5and6.html), [p63](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsp63.html), [p40](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsdeltaNp63.html), [CK14](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsck14.html) and **desmoglein 3**
* [CK7](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsck7.html) variable

Negative stains

* Generally negative or reduced expression of urothelial markers [GATA3](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsgata3.html), [uroplakin II](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsUroplakinii.html), [S100P](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainss100p.html) ([Hum Pathol 2013;44:164](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/22995333))
* [p16](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsp16.html) expression in a proportion of cases (25 - 31%); does not correlate with HPV infection ([Mod Pathol 2012;25:1526](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/22684221), [Urology 2020;144:158](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/32681917), [Pathol Res Pract 2020;216:153084](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/32825952))
* [CK20](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsck20.html)

Molecular / cytogenetics description

* HPV DNA in situ hybridization negative; HPV positivity may be seen in association with condyloma or chronic injury ([Am J Surg Pathol 2019;43:1547](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/31368913), [Urology 2020;144:158](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/32681917))
* Basal / squamous molecular subgroup of muscle invasive bladder cancer show frequent mutations in *TP53* and *RB1* ([Eur Urol 2020;77:420](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/31563503))

Sample pathology report

* Bladder, tumor left lateral wall, transurethral resection:
  + Invasive poorly differentiated carcinoma with extensive squamous differentiation
  + No evidence of in situ carcinoma (urothelial or squamous) in this biopsy material (see comment)
  + Comment: The differential includes pure squamous cell carcinoma and urothelial carcinoma with extensive squamous differentiation. This distinction requires evaluation of the entire resected tumor. In addition, clinical / radiological correlation is advised to ensure the tumor is compatible with a bladder primary.
* Bladder and prostate, robotic radical cystoprostatectomy:
  + Bladder with invasive squamous cell carcinoma, moderately differentiated (see synoptic report)
  + Prostate, negative for carcinoma

Differential diagnosis

* [Urothelial carcinoma with squamous differentiation](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/bladdersquamousdiff.html):
  + Squamous differentiation is common in urothelial carcinoma and may be extensive
  + Careful search for conventional urothelial carcinoma component or urothelial carcinoma in situ (may be focal)
  + Immunohistochemistry is of limited utility in this differential due to overlapping profiles; a panel approach may aid diagnosis in some cases: (squamous markers [CK14](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsck14.html) / **desmoglein 3** with urothelial markers [GATA3](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsgata3.html) / [uroplakin](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsuroplakiniii.html) / [S100P](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainss100p.html)) ([Hum Pathol 2013;44:164](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/22995333))
* [Metastasis](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/bladdermets.html) or direct extension of squamous cell carcinoma primary to another site, e.g. cervix, penis, anus:
  + Requires clinical and radiological correlation
  + [p16](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsp16.html) expression not of use in this differential
  + In situ hybridization for HPV DNA supports cervical primary (expressed in majority of cervical but not bladder squamous cell carcinoma) ([Am J Surg Pathol 2014;38:e20](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/25029121))
* [Verrucous squamous cell carcinoma](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/bladdersquamousverrucous.html):
  + Well differentiated tumor showing minimal cytologic atypia
  + Hyperkeratotic papillary or undulating projections with a pushing type of invasion at the base comprising rounded, broad based tongues of epithelium
  + Absence of irregular infiltrating nests
