Definition / general

* Urothelial carcinoma with variable amounts of squamous differentiation (intracellular keratin, intercellular bridges or keratin pearls)

Essential features

* Squamous differentiation is the most frequent line of divergent histology seen in high grade urothelial carcinoma (HGUC)
* Approximate percentage of the squamous component should be noted in the report
* Not human papillomavirus (HPV) associated
* Squamous differentiation may be seen in the context of chronic irritation such as with stones, *Schistosoma* infection and neurogenic bladder with indwelling catheters ([Front Med (Lausanne) 2018;5:223](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/30159314), [World J Urol 2021;39:1531](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/32632554))

Terminology

* Urothelial carcinoma with squamous differentiation

ICD coding

* ICD-O: [8120/3](https://portal.ct.gov/-/media/Departments-and-Agencies/DPH/Tumor-Registry/ICD-O-3-numeric-table_Jan-10-2018.pdf#page=2) - urothelial carcinoma with squamous differentiation
* ICD-10
  + [C67.9](https://www.icd10data.com/ICD10CM/Codes/C00-D49/C64-C68/C67-/C67.9) - malignant neoplasm of bladder, unspecified
  + [C66.9](https://www.icd10data.com/ICD10CM/Codes/C00-D49/C64-C68/C67-/C66.9) - malignant neoplasm of unspecified ureter
  + [C68.0](https://www.icd10data.com/ICD10CM/Codes/C00-D49/C64-C68/C67-/C68.0) - malignant neoplasm of urethra
  + [C65.1](https://www.icd10data.com/ICD10CM/Codes/C00-D49/C64-C68/C67-/C65.1) - malignant neoplasm of right renal pelvis
  + [C65.2](https://www.icd10data.com/ICD10CM/Codes/C00-D49/C64-C68/C67-/C65.2) - malignant neoplasm of left renal pelvis

Epidemiology

* Occurs in 30 - 40% of urothelial carcinomas of bladder ([J Clin Pathol 2007;60:332](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/16882698), [Urology 2007;70:69](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/17656211))

Sites

* Genitourinary tract (renal pelvis, ureter, bladder and urethra)

Etiology

* Smoking: smoking may increase the risk due to harmful chemicals accumulating in the urine ([BJU Int 2008;101:11](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/17971176))
* Aging: urothelial carcinoma can occur at any age; however, most patients diagnosed with urothelial carcinoma are older than 55
* Male gender: men are more likely to develop bladder cancer than women
* Chemical exposure: including arsenic and chemicals used in the manufacture of dyes, rubber, leather, textiles and paint products ([Lab Invest 2008;88:686](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/18475256))
* Previous cancer treatment: anticancer drug cyclophosphamide and pelvic radiation treatment ([Eur Urol 2019;75:319](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/30293908))
* Longstanding bladder irritation: chronic or repeated urinary infections or inflammations (cystitis), long term use of a urinary catheter ([World J Urol 2021;39:1531](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/32632554))
* Family history of cancer: Lynch syndrome with mismatch repair gene mutations ([Eur Urol 2013;63:379](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/22883484))
* Neurogenic bladder and HPV infection ([Mod Pathol 2012;25:1534](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/22766788))

Clinical features

* Hematuria, dysuria and frequent urination ([Urology 2006;67:3](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/16530066))

Diagnosis

* Imaging studies including plain radiograph, ultrasound, computed tomography (CT), magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) ([Urol Clin North Am 2018;45:389](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/30031461))
* Cystoscopy
* Biopsy (ultrasound guided biopsy)
* Transurethral resection of bladder tumor (TURBT) ([Urology 2006;67:3](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/16530066), [Eur Urol Focus 2022;8:457](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/33867307))
* Urine cytology and surgical pathology ([Cytopathology 2024;35:199](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/37919868), [Urology 2006;67:3](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/16530066))

Laboratory

* Hematuria (red blood cells are identified in urine) is most common

Radiology description

* Contrast filling defect ([Cancers (Basel) 2023;15:5040](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/37894407), [Urol Clin North Am 2018;45:389](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/30031461))
* Calyces showing distortion / obliteration / amputation ([Cancers (Basel) 2023;15:5040](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/37894407))
* Other findings: hydronephrosis or nonfunctioning kidney ([Cancers (Basel) 2023;15:5040](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/37894407), [Radiographics 2010;30:1553](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/21071375))

Prognostic factors

* Invasive urothelial carcinoma with divergent differentiation has poor prognosis because of common presentation at high stage
* Uncommonly noninvasive urothelial carcinoma showing divergent patterns may not have worse prognosis by limited data
* Stage by stage, no significant association with worse cancer specific survival in comparison with conventional urothelial carcinoma ([Urology 2007;70:69](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/17656211), [J Urol 2012;188:405](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/22704101))

Case reports

* 62 year old man with differentiation of urothelial carcinoma into 2 distinct subtypes, glandular and squamous, in 3 different organs ([Cureus 2020;12:e7280](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/32300500))
* 69 year old White woman with invasive urothelial carcinoma with squamous differentiation and associated high risk HPV infection ([Ann Diagn Pathol 2023;63:152103](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/36640642))
* 72 year old man with a primary sarcomatoid urothelial carcinoma of the ureter with heterologous chondrosarcoma and osteosarcoma elements and concurrent divergent squamous differentiation ([Urol Case Rep 2020;34:101484](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/33224730))

Treatment

* Surgery: transurethral resection of bladder tumor (TURBT), cystectomy, nephroureterectomy ([Ann Diagn Pathol 2007;11:395](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/18022122))
* Chemotherapy ([Clin Genitourin Cancer 2024;22:e53](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/37598012))
* Immunotherapy ([J Urol 2018;199:1129](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/29113841))
* Radiation therapy ([Eur Urol 2017;72:54](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/28040351))
* Targeted therapy ([Clin Genitourin Cancer 2023;21:e394](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/37316414))

Microscopic (histologic) description

* Most commonly associated with high grade urothelial carcinoma
* By definition, must have clear cut evidence of urothelial component (invasive urothelial carcinoma)
* Squamous components are similar to typical squamous cell carcinoma: nests of squamous epithelial cells, characterized by large nuclei with abundant eosinophilic cytoplasm and evidence of keratinization (intracellular keratin, intercellular bridges or keratin pearls)
* Squamous component may show basaloid or clear cell features
* Tumor associated eosinophilia in the bladder may present ([J Clin Pathol 1984;37:500](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/6725595))
* Presence of squamous component should be reported and providing estimation of the percentage of differentiation is strongly recommended

Cytology description

* High grade urothelial carcinoma component: high N:C ratio, nuclear pleomorphism, nuclear membrane irregularity and severe hyperchromasia
* Squamous component: keratinizing cells with high N:C ratio, large and hyperchromatic nuclei, irregular nuclear contour, polygonal cell shape, abundant eosinophilic cytoplasm or dense orangeophilic cytoplasm
* See [Paris system for urothelial neoplasia](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/bladdercytologyparissystemnew.html)

Negative stains

  + [Uroplakins](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsUroplakinii.html) (positive in urothelial component), [CK20](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsck20.html)

Molecular / cytogenetics description

* Basal squamous molecular type by gene expression signature subtyping ([Cell 2017;171:540](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/28988769))
  + Basal squamous subtype is enriched in *TP53* mutations
  + Tends to have elevated cell cycle activity and a significant inflammatory signature

Sample pathology report

* Bladder, tumor, transurethral resection:
  + Invasive urothelial carcinoma, high grade with squamous (30%) and glandular (10%) differentiation
  + Muscularis propria present and involved by carcinoma
  + Background urothelial mucosa with squamous metaplasia

Differential diagnosis

* [Squamous cell carcinoma](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/bladdersquamous.html):
  + Can be associated with *Schistosoma haematobium*
  + Must not have a urothelial carcinoma component
  + No criteria to distinguish squamous cell carcinoma with predominately well / moderately differentiated areas and focal poorly differentiated area from urothelial carcinoma with extensive squamous differentiation
* [Metastatic squamous cell carcinoma](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/bladdermets.html) secondarily involving bladder:
  + Squamous cell carcinoma arising from [cervical](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/cervixscc.html), [anal](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/anusscc.html) or [penile](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/penscrotumHPVassociatedSCC.html) origin or other locations should be excluded
  + Clinical presentation and imaging studies are crucial
  + Often HPV associated
* [Keratinizing squamous metaplasia](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/bladdersquamousmetaplasia.html) with **dysplasia** or **squamous cell carcinoma in situ**:
  + May be present in adjacent bladder mucosa in cases of bladder primary squamous cell carcinoma

Board review style question #1

[![](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/imgau/bladdersquamousdiffMicroYe2.jpg)](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/imgau/bladdersquamousdiffMicroYe2.jpg)

Invasive urothelial carcinoma with squamous differentiation is characterized by which of the following?

1. High risk HPV in situ hybridization positive
2. Intercellular bridges or keratinization
3. No clear cut evidence of urothelial component
4. p63 positive on immunostain

Board review style answer #1

**B.** Intercellular bridges or keratinization. The diagnosis is established based on squamous morphology characterized by intercellular bridges or keratinization on H&E stains. Answer A is incorrect because human papillomavirus (HPV) is not commonly associated with urothelial carcinoma with squamous differentiation. In contrast, high risk HPV positivity would support a secondary involvement by cervical, anal or penile squamous cell carcinoma. Answer C is incorrect because, by definition, clear cut evidence of urothelial component must be identified. Answer D is incorrect because p63 is also commonly positive in conventional urothelial carcinoma.

Board review style question #2

[![](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/imgau/bladdersquamousdiffMicroYe4.jpg)](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/imgau/bladdersquamousdiffMicroYe4.jpg)

A 50 year old woman presented with hematuria and was found to have a mass involving the bladder posterior wall shown above. Which of the following findings is most supportive of invasive urothelial carcinoma with squamous differentiation over secondary involvement by carcinoma from the other organs?

1. Component of uroplakin II positive conventional urothelial carcinoma
2. High risk HPV ISH positive
3. Mass lesion found at lower uterine segment
4. p16 positivity with a diffuse block-like staining pattern

Board review style answer #2

**A.** Component of uroplakin II positive conventional urothelial carcinoma. Uroplakin II is a highly specific immunohistochemical marker for urothelial differentiation. Answers B, C and D are incorrect since these findings would favor squamous cell carcinoma of cervical or uterine primary over bladder primary.
