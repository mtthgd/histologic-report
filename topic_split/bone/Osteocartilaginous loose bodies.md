Definition / general

* Osteocartilaginous loose bodies are detached fragments of bone or articular cartilage that are suspended in synovial fluid and entrapped within the joint space
* They can form in the setting of trauma, degenerative joint disease or neoplastic conditions, like synovial osteochondromatosis

Essential features

* Loose bodies are classified into chondral, osteochondral or osseous subtypes
* Histologic features include fragments of articular cartilage or bone
* Treatment in mild cases includes physical therapy and anti-inflammatory pain medications; definitive treatment requires arthroscopic removal

Terminology

* Intra-articular body

ICD coding

* ICD-10:  [M24.0](https://www.icd10data.com/ICD10CM/Codes/M00-M99/M20-M25/M24-/M24.0) - loose body in joint

Epidemiology

* M = F
* Predilection for adults; association with degenerative joint disease
* Trauma and fractures
* Osteochondritis dissecans
* Osteoarthritis
* Synovial osteochondromatosis
* Reference: [Acta Orthop Traumatol Turc 2018;52:404](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/29802031)

Sites

* Joints; most commonly in the shoulder or knee

Pathophysiology

* Trauma or arthritis can liberate bone and cartilage fragments into the joint space; may cause pain and reduced mobility
* Neoplastic conditions form excess tissue within the joint space that may break off and become entrapped in the joint space
* Chronic or large loose bodies may damage the articular cartilage of the joint and lead to osteoarthritis
* Reference: [Medicine (Baltimore) 2018;97:e12402](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/30290596)

Etiology

* Trauma, arthritis, inflammatory and neoplastic conditions

Clinical features

* May be asymptomatic
* Pain, swelling, sudden locking, crepitus or sudden decreased joint mobility
* Typically not palpable unless they are large and within small joints of the hands or feet
* Reference: [Acta Orthop Traumatol Turc 2018;52:404](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/29802031)

Diagnosis

* Fragments of bone or cartilage in the joint space on imaging
* Confirmed on histologic or gross only examination

Radiology description

* If an osseous component is present, loose bodies may be visible on Xray, computed tomography (CT) or ultrasound, although the best visualization of loose bodies is seen on magnetic resonance imaging (MRI)

Prognostic factors

* Few in number and small in size have better prognosis
* Benign; low local recurrence
* Following conservative treatment options or definitive arthroscopic removal, patients generally have a very good prognosis

Microscopic (histologic) description

* Once liberated into the joint space, loose bodies may become embedded in synovium, leading to resorption or serving as a nidus for new cartilage deposition
* Newly formed fibrous or hyaline cartilage may undergo endochondral ossification, forming concentric layers around the loose body
* As the loose body grows, the central portion may undergo necrosis and calcification as it becomes unable to be supported by the diffusion of synovial fluid
* Reference: [J Orthop Sci 2006;11:607](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/17139469)

  + Benign fragment of bone consistent with loose body

Differential diagnosis

* [Osteochondritis dissecans](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/jointsosteochondritis.html):
  + Benign, noninflammatory condition in young adults
  + Subchondral bone necrosis that may fragment and detach; cartilage fragments remain viable through synovial fluid nutrients
  + Flat, smooth nodules of avascular bone with or without overlying articular cartilage
* [Synovial chondromatosis](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/jointssynovialosteochondromatosis.html):

  [![Synovial chondromatosis](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/imgau/boneloosebodiesdehner08.jpg)](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/imgau/boneloosebodiesdehner08.jpg)

  [![Synovial chondromatosis](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/imgau/boneloosebodiesdehner09.jpg)](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/imgau/boneloosebodiesdehner09.jpg)

  [![Synovial chondromatosis](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/imgau/boneloosebodiesdehner10.jpg)](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/imgau/boneloosebodiesdehner10.jpg)

  + Benign, locally aggressive neoplasm
  + Forms multiple hyaline cartilaginous nodules within joint spaces
  + Potential for malignant transformation; pathogenic *FN1* or *ACVR1A* gene rearrangements, with most showing *FN1::ACVR2A* fusion ([Genes Chromosomes Cancer 2020;59:144](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/31589790))
* [Osteoarthritis](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/jointsDJD.html):
  + Imaging shows degenerative changes with narrowed joint space
  + Occurs in older age group
  + Multiple osteochondral loose bodies with concentric cartilage layers
* **Rice bodies**:
  + Smaller than osteocartilaginous loose bodies
  + Primarily composed of fibrinous material from inflamed synovium
  + Associated with inflammatory arthritis

Board review style question #1

[![BRQ image](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/imgau/boneloosebodiesdehner01.jpg)](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/imgau/boneloosebodiesdehner01.jpg)

A 35 year old man presented with sudden pain, swelling and locking of his right knee following a skateboarding injury. Imaging showed a small hyperintense focus within the joint space and arthroscopic removal showed a benign fragment of bone. Which of the following is the most likely diagnosis?

1. Osteoarthritis
2. Osteocartilaginous loose body
3. Osteochondritis dissecans
4. Synovial chondromatosis

Board review style answer #1

**B**. Osteocartilaginous loose body. Answers A, C and D are incorrect because the sudden onset of symptoms following a traumatic event argues against an underlying neoplastic or inflammatory cause.

Board review style question #2

Which of the following is true regarding osteocartilaginous loose bodies?

1. Patients typically have a very poor prognosis
2. Synovial chondromatosis is the most common cause
3. The curative treatment modality is nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs) and physical therapy
4. The most common location is within small joints of the hands and feet
5. They usually occur in a single joint, are most commonly the result of trauma and are unlikely to recur unless part of a neoplastic or inflammatory process

Board review style answer #2

**E**. They usually occur in a single joint, are most commonly the result of trauma and are unlikely to recur unless part of a neoplastic or inflammatory process. Answer C is incorrect because NSAIDs and physical therapy is the conservative treatment option for mild cases. Answer D is incorrect because the most common location for osteocartilaginous loose bodies is within the knee or shoulder. Answer B is incorrect because traumatic events are the most common cause of loose bodies. Answer A is incorrect because following conservative treatment options or definitive arthroscopic removal, patients generally have a very good prognosis.
