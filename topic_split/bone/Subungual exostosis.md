Definition / general

* Rare, benign osteocartilaginous lesion arising from the distal phalangeal bone below the nailbed
* First described by Dupuytren in 1847

Essential features

* Osteocartilaginous lesion involving the distal phalanx, lacking connection between the stalk of the lesion and medullary cavity of the native bone

Terminology

* Dupuytren exostosis (not recommended)

ICD coding

* ICD-O: **9213/0** - subungual exostosis (WHO Classification, page 345)
* ICD-11: [EE13.Y](https://icd.who.int/browse11/l-m/en#/http%3a%2f%2fid.who.int%2ficd%2fentity%2f1851131352) & [XH1XL9](https://icd.who.int/browse11/l-m/en#/http%3a%2f%2fid.who.int%2ficd%2fentity%2f1828005109) - certain disorders affecting the nails or perionychium & subungual exostosis

Epidemiology

* Adolescents and young adults
* More than 50% of cases are in patients younger than 18 years (average age: 25.7 years) ([Clin Orthop Relat Res 2014;472:1251](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/24146360))
* M:F = 1:1 ([Clin Orthop Relat Res 2014;472:1251](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/24146360))

Sites

* Toes most frequently involved (80% involve the first toe) ([Clin Orthop Relat Res 2014;472:1251](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/24146360))
* Fingers rarely involved ([Pediatr Investig 2020;4:292](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/33376957))

Etiology

* Unclear
* Preceding trauma and infection have been reported in 29% and 14% of cases, respectively ([Clin Orthop Relat Res 2014;472:1251](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/24146360))
* Presence of t(X;6)(q24-q26;q15-q25) rearrangement suggests this is a true neoplastic process ([Int J Cancer 2011;128:487](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/20340132))

Clinical features

* Most common clinical presentations include long standing pain (77%) followed by mass / swelling in the nail, nail changes such as erythema and deformity of the nailbed ([Clin Orthop Relat Res 2014;472:1251](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/24146360))
* Develops over a course of several months to years

Diagnosis

* Radiological and pathological correlation is recommended
* Limited biopsy samples from the periphery of the lesion may create an impression of a malignant cartilaginous neoplasm
* Reference: [Czerniak: Dorfman and Czerniak's Bone Tumors, 2nd Edition, 2015](https://www.amazon.com/exec/obidos/ASIN/0323023967/pathologyoutl-20)

Radiology description

* Pedunculated radiopaque mass on the dorsomedial aspect of the distal phalanx with nonaggressive growth
* Lack of continuity between the lesion and the medullary cavity of the distal phalanx
* Fibrocartilaginous cap appears hyperintense on T2 weighted images ([Eur J Radiol 2019;112:93](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/30777226))

Prognostic factors

* Subungual exostoses are benign tumors
* Local recurrence is seen in cases after incomplete excision in approximately 4% of cases ([Clin Orthop Relat Res 2014;472:1251](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/24146360))
* No malignant transformation or metastasis has been reported

Case reports

* 7 year old boy with subungual exostosis of the index finger ([Indian J Dermatol Venereol Leprol 2018;84:232](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/29350203))
* 8 year old boy and 36 and 37 year old women in 3 cases of subungual exostosis with different clinical presentations ([Skin Appendage Disord 2016;1:213](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/27386469))
* 65 year old woman with subungual exostosis of left long finger ([Iowa Orthop J 2013;33:228](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/24027490))

Treatment

* Complete marginal excision with minimal trauma to the nailbed is the optimal treatment ([Clin Orthop Relat Res 2014;472:1251](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/24146360), [J Pediatr Orthop B 2020;29:382](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/32195759))

Gross description

* Small bony and cartilaginous fragments

Microscopic (histologic) description

* Peripheral fibrocartilaginous tissue with underlying stalk composed of trabecular bone, which is attached to the subjacent bone
* Not continuous with the medullary cavity of the bone it arises from
* Amount of cartilaginous tissue is determined by the age of the lesion
* Early stage: cellular chondroid tissue with background proliferating fibrous tissue in the nailbed and may lack attachment to the underling phalangeal bone
* Cartilaginous cap may show hypercellularity and atypia of the chondrocytes, with occasional mitoses; the cartilage matrix undergoes endochondral ossification over time
* Late stage: eventually the lesion is composed of irregular trabecular bone with osteoblastic rimming with a thinned out or even absent cartilaginous cap ([Czerniak: Dorfman and Czerniak's Bone Tumors, 2nd Edition, 2015](https://www.amazon.com/exec/obidos/ASIN/0323023967/pathologyoutl-20))

Molecular / cytogenetics description

* Consistently demonstrates t(X;6)(q24-26;q15-q25), associated with increased expression of *IRS4* (insulin receptor substrate 4) gene
* Fusion partners not fully characterized; however, *COL12A1* is implicated in all cases described ([Int J Cancer 2011;128:487](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/20340132), [Int J Cancer 2006;118:1972](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/16284948))

What is a subungual exostosis?

Sample pathology report

* Hallux, excision:
  + Subungual exostosis (see comment)
  + Comment: Osteocartilaginous lesion with a thin cartilaginous cap. Per imaging the lesion involves the distal phalanx of the great toe with lack of continuity with the medullary cavity of the underlying bone. Overall the morphology and the imaging findings are consistent with subungual exostosis.

Differential diagnosis

* [Osteochondroma](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/boneosteochondroma.html):
  + Benign cartilaginous neoplasm with a cartilaginous cap
  + Medullary cavity of the stalk is continuous with that of the underlying bone
* **Florid reactive periostitis**:
  + Mixture of reactive woven bone and fibrous tissue without zonation typically arising from the periosteum of fingers (commonly in the proximal phalanx)
* [Bizarre parosteal osteochondromatous proliferation ([BPOP] Nora lesion)](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/bonebizarreparosteal.html):
  + Surface osteocartilaginous lesion commonly develops in the proximal and middle phalanges of hands and feet
  + Long tubular bones can also be affected
  + Characteristic basophilic stroma at the interface of the bone and cartilage (blue bone) is identified
  + Balanced translocation t(X;6) seen in subungual exostosis is not reported in BPOP
  + Recurrent cytogenetic abnormalities described in BPOP include t(1;17)(q32;q21), inv(7) and inv(6) ([Am J Surg Pathol 2004;28:1033](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/15252309), [Virchows Arch 2005;447:99](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/15926071), [Cancer Genet 2013;206:402](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/24412018))

Board review style question #1

Which bone is most commonly involved by subungual exostosis?

1. Distal phalanx of the fingers
2. Distal phalanx of the toes
3. Middle phalanx of the toes
4. Proximal phalanx of the fingers

Board review style answer #1

**B**. Distal phalanx of the toes
