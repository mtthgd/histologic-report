Definition / general

* Acute erythroid leukemia (AEL) or pure erythroid leukemia (PEL)
* Neoplastic proliferation of erythroid cells with features of maturation arrest (increased proerythroblasts) and a high prevalence of biallelic *TP53* alteration

Essential features

* Rare aggressive acute myeloid leukemia (AML) ([Am J Hematol 2017;92:292](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/28006859))
* Erythroid precursors ≥ 80% and proerythroblasts ≥ 30% of the bone marrow cells
* Myeloblasts are not increased in number but frequently show an aberrant immunophenotype

Terminology

* Acute myeloid leukemia, M6 type
* Acute erythroid leukemia
* Erythroleukemia
* Pure erythroid leukemia M6b

ICD coding

* ICD-10: [C94.00](https://www.icd10data.com/ICD10CM/Codes/C00-D49/C81-C96/C94-/C94.00) - acute erythroid leukemia, not having achieved remission

Epidemiology

* Extremely rare, Am J Hematol 2017;92:292)
* Any age, can be congenital and incidence peaks in 70 - 80 year old patients ([Int J Hematol 2017;106:711](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/28523571), [Cancers (Basel) 2023;15:3941](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/37568757))
* Both de novo and secondary (more often, either postexposure to cytotoxic therapy or progression of a prior myeloid neoplasm; cases of acute erythroid leukemia arising after cytotoxic therapy should be designated as myeloid neoplasm postcytotoxic therapy)

Sites

* Bone marrow or peripheral blood ([Am J Hematol 2017;92:292](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/28006859))
* Mass lesion (erythroblastic sarcoma) in tissue such as central nervous system, orbit, liver, ovary, etc. ([Haematologica 2020;105:e194](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/31949013))

Clinical features

* Anemia ([Am J Hematol 2017;92:292](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/28006859))
* Circulating erythroblasts
* Extramedullary mass ([Haematologica 2020;105:e194](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/31949013))

Diagnosis

* Erythroblasts ≥ 80% in bone marrow or peripheral blood with proerythroblasts ≥ 30%
* Myeloblasts are not increased ( For extramedullary mass lesion, sheets of erythroid precursor cells with proerythroblasts ≥ 30%
* Note 1: per WHO 2016, the category of acute erythroleukemia M6a no longer exists; cases previously classified as M6a are either myelodysplastic syndrome (MDS) or AML depending on the myeloblast percentage in bone marrow or peripheral blood
* Note 2: diagnosis of the majority of acute erythroid leukemia or PEL cases is different per 2022 WHO or International Consensus Classification (ICC); specifically, PEL with at least 2 *TP53* abnormalities (both mutations and aberrant or deleted chromosome 17p) is now classified as AML with mutated *TP53* by the ICC classification ([Virchows Arch 2023;482:27](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/36264379))

Prognostic factors

* Usually poor for adult patients with median overall survival of 3 - 6 months ([Mod Pathol 2018;31:705](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/29327715), [Mod Pathol 2011;24:375](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/21102413), [Cancers (Basel) 2023;15:3941](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/37568757))
* Prognosis is controversial for children and adolescent groups due to rarity; one study showed poor prognosis and the largest case studies showed relatively better prognosis with median overall survival of 69 months ([Blood Adv 2020;4:6000](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/33284945), [Cancers (Basel) 2023;15:3941](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/37568757))

Case reports

* 4 month old boy with fatigue, irritability, regurgitation, high fever, hepatosplenomegaly, anemia, leukocytosis, thrombocytopenia and pure erythroid leukemia ([PLoS One 2013;8:e63663](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/23667654))
* 2 year old girl presented with headache, vomiting and a de novo erythroid sarcoma in third ventricle ([Haematologica 2020;105:e194](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/31949013))
* 3 year old boy suddenly presented with epileptic seizures and postcritic left hemiplegia and erythroblastic sarcoma ([Acta Neuropathol Commun 2024;12:12](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/38243303))
* 44 year old woman with progressive fatigue, pancytopenia and pure erythroid leukemia ([Clin Case Rep 2019;7:1829](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/31534770))
* 48 year old man with shortness of breath, weight loss and pure erythroid leukemia ([Hematol Rep 2015;7:5674](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/25852849))

Treatment

* AML treatment protocol ([Am J Hematol 2017;92:292](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/28006859))
* Radiation therapy can be considered for localized mass lesion in orbit or CNS ([Haematologica 2020;105:e194](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/31949013))
* Bone marrow transplant

Microscopic (histologic) description

* Bone marrow biopsy: sheets or clusters of immature cells, tumor cells may show intrasinusoidal growth pattern ([Am J Hematol 2017;92:292](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/28006859))
* Bone marrow aspirate: erythroid precursors ≥ 80% and proerythroblasts ≥ 30% of bone marrow cells
  + Proerythroblasts have centrally located round nuclei, fine chromatin and 1 or more prominent nucleoli, deeply basophilic cytoplasm with vacuoles, often agranular
  + Ring sideroblasts are common
  + Myeloblasts are not increased
  + Dysmegakaryopoiesis is common and dysgranulopoiesis is infrequent
* Mass in tissue: poorly differentiated tumor cells ([Haematologica 2020;105:e194](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/31949013))

Cytology description

* Proerythroblasts are of medium to large size, with scant to moderate basophilic cytoplasm and dispersed chromatin; some have prominent nucleoli
* Erythroblasts are smaller with more condensed chromatin and polychromatic cytoplasm; some erythroblasts may be binucleated or multinucleated or with nuclear budding
* Cytoplasmic vacuoles, cytoplasmic blebbing or pseudopods are frequent

Positive stains

* Erythroid specific markers: [E-cadherin](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsecadherin.html) (positive in majority of cases), **hemoglobin A** and [glycophorin A](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/cdmarkerscd230.html#cd235) (usually positive but can be negative in poorly differentiated erythroblasts) ([Am J Hematol 2017;92:292](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/28006859))
* Non lineage specific markers: [CD36](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/cdmarkerscd30to39.html#cd36), [CD43](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/cdmarkerscd43.html), [CD71](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/cdmarkerscd71.html), [CD117](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/cdmarkerscd117.html), **GATA1**, **ferritin H**
  + These markers are unusually positive; however, they can be positive in other types of hematopoietic neoplasms
  + [CD36](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/cdmarkerscd30to39.html#cd36) is commonly positive in monocytic leukemia and acute megakaryocytic leukemia as well
  + [CD43](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/cdmarkerscd43.html) is positive in most AML, T cell acute lymphoblastic leukemia (T ALL) as well as some B cell ALL; it is a very useful marker for extramedullary poorly differentiated tumor to confirm hematopoietic lineage
  + [CD71](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/cdmarkerscd71.html) can be positive in other leukemic blasts although its expression levels in PEL should be very bright
  + [CD117](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/cdmarkerscd117.html) is positive in most AML and some T ALL
  + **GATA1** is a transcription factor for erythroid development and it is positive in both PEL and acute megakaryocytic leukemia ([Am J Clin Pathol 2017;147:420](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/28340113))
  + **Ferritin H** is expressed in erythroid precursors as well as macrophages
* Special stains: [PAS](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainspas.html) (usually in block-like pattern), [alpha naphthyl acetate esterase](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsnonspecificesterase.html), [acid phosphatase](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsacidphosphatase.html)

Negative stains

* [CD34](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/cdmarkerscd34.html) and [myeloperoxidase](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsmyeloperoxidase.html) ([Am J Hematol 2017;92:292](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/28006859))
* Megakaryocytic markers ([CD41](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/cdmarkerscd41.html) and [CD61](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/cdmarkerscd61.html)), myeloid markers ([CD13](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/cdmarkerscd13.html), [CD33](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/cdmarkerscd33.html)), [CD45](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/cdmarkerscd45.html) and [HLA-DR](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainshladr.html), usually negative but may be partially expressed in some cases ([Am J Hematol 2017;92:292](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/28006859))

Molecular / cytogenetics description

* Often complex karyotype, frequently -5/del(5q) and -7/del(7q) ([Am J Hematol 2017;92:292](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/28006859))
* Biallelic (or multihit) *TP53* loss of function mutation ([Nat Genet 2019;51:694](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/30926971))
* Pediatric PEL has distinctive molecular genetic changes that include relatively enriched *NUP98* fusion, t(1;16)(p31;q24) *NFIA::CBFA2T3* rearrangement, *NFIA::CBFA2T1* fusion and t(11;20)(p11;q11) / *ZMYND8::RELA* ([Haematologica 2020;105:e194](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/31949013), [Leukemia 2013;27:980](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/23032695), [PLoS One 2013;8:e63663](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/23667654), [Blood Adv 2020;4:6000](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/33284945), [Acta Neuropathol Commun 2024;12:12](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/38243303))

Sample pathology report

* Bone marrow, right, biopsy, clot section, aspirate smears, touch imprint and peripheral blood smear:
  + Pure erythroid leukemia, blasts 95% of total nucleated cells (see comment)
  + Comment:
    - Peripheral blood:
      * Red blood cells: normocytic normochromic anemia with slight anisopoikilocytosis
      * White blood cells: Leukopenia with few large proerythroblasts. The blasts have scant basophilic cytoplasm, round nuclei and open chromatin and few have prominent nucleoli. Cytoplasmic vacuoles are noted in some blasts.
      * Platelets: decreased with rare giant forms
    - Bone marrow biopsy:
      * Decalcification procedures performed to allow for histological assessment of submitted tissue
      * Gross measurement: 0.5 cm
      * Quality: adequate
      * Cellularity: 100%
      * Megakaryocytes: rare
      * Infiltrate: The marrow is nearly completely replaced by sheets of immature cells with a high N:C ratio, open chromatin and some with prominent nucleoli.
    - Bone marrow clot:
      * Quality: inadequate, blood only, no particles with many immature cells
    - Bone marrow smears:
      * Quality / cellularity: adequate
      * Granulocytes: markedly decreased
      * Erythrocytes: markedly increased erythroid precursors with increased proerythroblasts
      * Megakaryocytes: not seen
      * Lymphocytes: decreased, few small and mature cells
      * Bone marrow differential count: 500 total cells counted
        + Blasts percentage 95% (normal 0 - 5%)
        + Promyelocytes percentage 0 (normal 2 - 8%)
        + Myelocytes percentage 0 (normal 5 - 20%)
        + Metamyelocytes percentage 0 (normal 13 - 32%)
        + Granulocytes percentage 2% (normal 7 - 30%)
        + Monocytes percentage 0 (normal 0 - 5%)
        + Lymphocytes percentage 3% (normal 3 - 17%)
        + Plasma cells percentage 0 (normal 0 - 2%)
        + Eosinophils percentage 0 (normal 0 - 4%)
        + Basophils percentage 0 (normal 0 - 1%)
        + Pronormoblasts percentage 80% (normal 1 - 8%)
        + Normoblasts percentage 15% (normal 7 - 32%)
        + M:E ratio (2 - 4): N/A
    - Bone marrow touch imprint:
      * Quality: adequate, similar to smear with increased erythroid blasts
    - Flow cytometric analysis:
      * Aberrant erythroid blasts, 95.2% of total nucleated cells (F19 - 160)
    - Pending tests:
      * Cytogenetic karyotype and FISH

Differential diagnosis

* Other types of acute leukemia, especially [acute megakaryocytic leukemia](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/leukemiaM7.html):
  + Immunophenotype of the blasts will be compatible with myeloblasts, such as [MPO](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsmyeloperoxidase.html) positive, [CD41](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/cdmarkerscd40.html#cd41) / [CD61](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/cdmarkerscd61.html) expression for acute megakaryocytic leukemia, etc.
* **Other poorly differentiated malignancy** or **small blue round cell tumor**:
  + Immunophenotyping can aid in diagnosis of other poorly differentiated malignancies
    - Tumor cells positive for [cytokeratin](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsckgeneral.html) suggest carcinoma, while those expressing neuroendocrine markers may indicate a sarcoma or small round cell tumor
    - Additionally, tumor cells typically grow in sheets and exhibit adhesion to one another
* **Reactive erythroid hyperplasia**:
  + Percentage of proerythroblasts is low
  + No significant abnormalities of blasts immunophenotype
  + No genetic mutation detected
* [AML with myelodysplasia related changes (AML with MRC)](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/leukemiaAMLmulti.html):
  + Pure erythroid leukemia should not be diagnosed as AML with MRC even if there is a prior history of myeloid neoplasm, significant dysplasia of 2 lineages and a defining cytogenetic abnormality
  + AML with MRC requires minimal 20% myeloblasts while pure erythroid leukemia should not have a significant component of myeloblasts

Board review style question #1

Which of the following markers is most useful in diagnosing pure erythroid leukemia?

1. CD34
2. CD71
3. CD117
4. E-cadherin
5. Myeloperoxidase (MPO)

Board review style answer #1

**D**. E-cadherin is specific for pure erythroid leukemia (PEL) because it is only expressed in erythroid precursors in hematopoietic cells. Answer A is incorrect because CD34 is generally negative in PEL. Answer E is incorrect because MPO is negative in PEL. Answers B and C are incorrect because CD71 and CD117 are not lineage specific and can be expressed in all types of AML.

Board review style question #2

[![](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/imgau/leukemiaM6_liu05.jpg)](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/imgau/leukemiaM6_liu05.jpg)

A 7 year old boy presented with leukocytosis (55k/µL), anemia (Hb 7.5 g/dL) and thrombocytopenia (52k/µL). Peripheral blood smear (see image above) shows many mononucleated cells of variable sizes with high N:C ratio, round nuclei, open chromatin and deep blue cytoplasm. Some large cells have conspicuous nucleoli. By flow cytometric immunophenotyping, these cells are positive for CD36 and CD117 (heterogeneous) and negative for CD34, CD45, HLA-DR and all other lymphoid, myeloid and monocytic markers. Which of the following sentences is correct regarding this disease?

1. ≥ 20% of these cells in blood or bone marrow are required for diagnosis as pure erythroid leukemia
2. Cells are positive for CD61
3. Cells most likely have a normal karyotype (46, XY)
4. Cells show reactivity with alpha naphthyl acetate esterase, acid phosphatase and PAS
5. Prognosis for this patient is good

Board review style answer #2

**D**. Cells show reactivity with alpha naphthyl acetate esterase, acid phosphatase and PAS. These cells are consistent with proerythroblasts and the diagnosis is pure erythroid leukemia (PEL). Proerythroblasts are positive for alpha naphthyl acetate esterase, acid phosphatase and PAS. Answer B is incorrect because CD61 is a marker for megakaryocytic cells, although it may be positive on proerythroblastic leukemia cells occasionally. Answer C is incorrect because PEL normally has a complex karyotype. Answer A is incorrect because the diagnosis of PEL requires at least 30% proerythroblasts. Answer E is incorrect because the prognosis of PEL is generally poor.
