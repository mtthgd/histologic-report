Clinical features

* 3 - 9% of pediatric hematologic malignancies
* Estimated annual incidence in U.S. is 0.5 to 4 cases per million for children compared to 20 - 40 cases for adults
* Usually diagnosed at ages 6 - 8 years, although juvenile myelomonocytic leukemia is usually diagnosed at age 2 years
* Difficult to diagnose because:
  1. Less dysplasia at younger ages
  2. Dysplasia may be confused with treatment effects of G-CSF
  3. Bone marrow often hypocellular, resembling aplastic anemia
  4. Resembles HHV6 ([Pediatr Blood Cancer 2006;47:543](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/16333831)) or parvovirus infections ([Rinsho Ketsueki 2001;42:1096](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/11808077), [Pediatr Hematol Oncol 2000;17:475](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/10989468))
* **Modified WHO classification for childhood MDS**
  + Diagnosis requires:
    1. Sustained and unexplained cytopenia
    2. At least bilineage dysplasia
    3. A clonal cytogenetic abnormality
    4. At least 5% blasts ([Leukemia 2003;17:277](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/12592323)); may be most successful system for classification ([Arch Pathol Lab Med 2007;131:1110](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/17617000))
* 69% idiopathic, 24% associated with constitutional / inherited disorders (Down syndrome, neurofibromatosis, Bloom syndrome, Fanconi anemia, [Atlas of Genetics and Cytogenetics](http://atlasgeneticsoncology.org/Anomalies/childMDSID1101.html)), 7% therapy related ([Arch Pathol Lab Med 2007;131:1110](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/17617000))
* Similar prognosis as AML M6 and M7, with poor induction success rate ([Pediatr Blood Cancer 2007;49:17](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/16856158))
