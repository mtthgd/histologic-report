Definition / general

* Myelodysplastic neoplasms (MDS) comprise a heterogeneous group of diseases with variable outcomes depending on multiple parameters
* International Prognostic Scoring System for MDS is the most commonly used tool to predict long term outcomes

Essential features

* Outcomes of MDS are variable and depend on many different parameters including cytogenetics, blast percentage, hemoglobin levels, absolute neutrophil count and platelet count; the International Prognostic Scoring System for MDS is the most commonly used tool to predict long term outcomes
* Patients in the low risk group will often have more prolonged survival and their oncological goals include improving their cytopenias (neutropenia, anemia and thrombocytopenia) and reducing iron overload
* Patients in the high risk group with increased blasts and cytogenetic abnormalities often have shorter survival and are treated more aggressively with 5-azacitidine to delay the transformation to acute myeloid leukemia

Terminology

* Myelodysplastic syndrome / myelodysplastic neoplasm (MDS)
* International Prognostic Scoring System (IPSS)
* Revised International Prognostic Scoring System (IPSS-R)

ICD coding

* ICD-10: [D46.9](https://www.icd10data.com/ICD10CM/Codes/C00-D49/D37-D48/D46-/D46.9) - myelodysplastic syndrome, unspecified

Epidemiology

* Median age at the diagnosis of MDS: 77 years ([Br J Haematol 1992;82:358](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/1419819))
* Incidence of MDS below age 40 is extremely low (0.1 cases per 100,000) ([Blood Rev 2019;34:1](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/30314642))
* Early age of onset (Blood Rev 2019;34:1)
* Younger age of onset is more common amongst the Asian population ([Leuk Res 1998;22:453](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/9652732))
* Early onset can also be caused by exposure to chemicals (e.g., benzene) or radiation ([J Natl Cancer Inst 2012;104:1724](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/23111193))
* Men are more commonly affected compared to women, except in MDS with low blasts and 5q deletion (MDS 5q), where women are the predominate population affected ([Leuk Res 2011;35:1591](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/21708407))
* There is a higher rate of MDS among the White population ([Blood Rev 2019;34:1](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/30314642))

Sites

* Bone marrow

Pathophysiology

* Genetic defects play a major role in the pathogenesis of MDS, including cytogenetic abnormalities, genetic mutations and alterations, abnormal gene expression and epigenetic dysregulation ([Int J Clin Oncol 2019;24:885](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/31093808))
* Chromosomal abnormalities have been seen in at least 50% of MDS patients, including the abnormalities of chromosome 5, 7, 8 and 20; complex karyotypes are also identified ([Blood 2008;111:1534](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/17954704))
* Genetic abnormalities have been identified in over 50 genes in at least 80% of MDS patients, including *SF3B1*, *SRSF2*, *U2AF1*, *ZRSR2*, *LUC7L2*, *DDX41*, *ASXL1*, *EZH2*, *TET2*, *DNMT3A*, *IDH1* / *IDH2*, *CSNK1A1*, *RUNX1*, *BCOR*, *TP53* and *CUX1* ([Eur J Haematol 2015;95:3](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/25645650))

Etiology

* Old age is considered a significant risk factor and the risk increases with advancing age due to an increased propensity for genetic mutations ([Blood 2013;122:2943](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/23980065))
* Exposure to certain environmental toxins and certain chemicals (e.g., benzene), has been linked to an increased risk of developing MDS ([Leuk Lymphoma 1999;35:269](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/10706450))
* Previous exposure to chemotherapy or radiation therapy for the treatment of another malignancy is also known risk factor for developing secondary MDS ([Cancer Genet Cytogenet 2007;176:1](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/17574959), [Eur J Haematol 1991;47:17](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/1868912))

Clinical features

* MDS patients typically present with at least 1 cytopenia: neutropenia, anemia or thrombocytopenia ([Leukemia 2022;36:1720](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/35732829))
* Neutropenia can lead to increased susceptibility to infections ([Clin Hematol Int 2021;3:41](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/34595466))
* Anemia can contribute to persistent fatigue, weakness and shortness of breath ([Ann N Y Acad Sci 2019;1450:15](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/31008520))
* Thrombocytopenia can lead to easy bruising, petechiae and an increased risk of bleeding ([Cureus 2022;14:e27718](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/35949449))

Diagnosis

* See [diagnostic criteria table](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/myeloproliferativemdsprognosticscoring.html#MDSprogscoretables)
* See [features of dysplasia table](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/myeloproliferativemdsprognosticscoring.html#MDSprogscoretables) for the entities that require dysplasia as an essential diagnostic criteria; dysplasia must be seen in at least 10% of the total lineage on examination of the bone marrow aspirate smears, touch preparations, bone marrow clot section and bone marrow biopsy sections ([Leukemia 2022;36:1720](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/35732829))

Laboratory

* Cytopenia in at least 1 hematopoietic lineage is usually seen in most cases of MDS
  + Anemia: Hb Leukopenia: absolute neutrophil count 9/L
  + Thrombocytopenia: platelets 9/L
* Chromosome analysis or conventional karyotyping, fluorescence in situ hybridization (FISH) and next generation sequencing (NGS) for mutation analysis are required for some diagnoses
* Reference: [Am J Hematol 2020;95:1399](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/32744763)

Prognostic factors

* Revised IPSS (IPSS-R) utilizes cytogenetics, blast percentage in the bone marrow, hemoglobin levels, platelet levels and the absolute neutrophil count to create the prognostic risk scores and classify patients under the risk categories
* See [IPSS-R table](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/myeloproliferativemdsprognosticscoring.html#MDSprogscoretables) above ([Blood 2012;120:2454](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/22740453))
* Molecular IPSS (IPSS-M) utilizes molecular data for risk prognostication using a list of 31 focused genes: *TP53, MLL, FLT3, ASXL1, CBL, DNMT3A, ETV6, EZH2, IDH2, KRAS, NPM1, NRAS, RUNX1, SF3B1, SRSF2, U2AF1, BCOR, BCORL1, CEBPA, ETNK1, GATA2, GNB1, IDH1, NF1, PFH6, PPM1D, PRPF8, PTPN11, SETBP1, STAG2, WT1* ([NEJM Evid 2022;1:EVIDoa2200008](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/38319256))

Case reports

* 8 year old girl with childhood MDS and transformation to acute lymphoblastic leukemia ([Ann Lab Med 2013;33:130](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/23483089))
* 53 year old woman with fibromyalgia and MDS ([Case Rep Hematol 2011;2011:560106](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/22937307))
* 71 year old man with idiopathic cytopenia of undetermined significance (ICUS) progressing to MDS and acute myeloid leukemia (AML) ([Blood 2024;143:473](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/38300608))
* 72 year old woman with interstitial lung disease and MDS ([Front Med (Lausanne) 2021;8:673573](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/34434942))

Treatment

* Treatment approach can be divided based on the risk group; for those with low risk MDS, it depends on the transfusion needs
* Transfusion independent patients are followed with observation only
* Patients with severe anemia with increasing red blood cell transfusion needs can be managed with erythroid stimulating agents (ESA) ([Am J Hematol 2023;98:1307](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/37288607))
* Patients with severe neutropenia with increased infection risk can be managed with granulocyte colony stimulating factors (GCSF) ([Am J Hematol 2023;98:1307](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/37288607))
* Lenalidomide is the standard of care for low risk MDS with del(5q), anemia and increasing transfusion needs; it is contraindicated if there is thrombocytopenia
  + 5-azacitidine and decitabine are used for transfusion dependent, low risk MDS cases that have failed or are not candidates for growth factors or lenalidomide ([Am J Hematol 2023;98:1307](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/37288607))
* For low risk MDS that have failed multiple therapies, allogeneic stem cell transplant (allo-SCT) must be considered ([Am J Hematol 2023;98:1307](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/37288607))
* 5-azacitidine is the standard of care for high risk MDS; furthermore, allo-SCT should also be considered in high risk MDS ([Am J Hematol 2023;98:1307](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/37288607))

Microscopic (histologic) description

* MDS with defining genetic abnormalities
  + Myelodysplastic neoplasm with biallelic *TP53* inactivation (MDS bi*TP53*)
    - In general, MDS bi*TP53* has high risk morphologic findings, such as multilineage dysplasia, higher blast count and fibrosis in the bone marrow
    - Dysplastic changes overlap with those of other MDS types (see [features of dysplasia table](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/myeloproliferativemdsprognosticscoring.html#MDSprogscoretables))
    - Bone marrow is typically hypercellular but can be normocellular or hypocellular
    - Blast percentage is
    - Myelodysplastic neoplasm with low blasts and 5q deletion (MDS 5q)
      * Complete blood count and peripheral blood smear evaluation can show thrombocytosis in the absence of a cytopenia
      * Megakaryocytes are usually dysplastic with small and hypolobated forms (micromegakaryocytes)
      * Dysplasia in the erythroid and granulocytic lineages is less prominent; however, dysplastic changes can still overlap with those of other MDS types (see [features of dysplasia table](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/myeloproliferativemdsprognosticscoring.html#MDSprogscoretables))
      * Ring sideroblasts (or *SF3B1* mutation) can be present and do not exclude the diagnosis of MDS 5q, if the other criteria are met
      * Bone marrow is typically hypercellular or normocellular and can have megakaryocytic hyperplasia and erythroid hypoplasia
      * Blast percentage is
      * Myelodysplastic neoplasm with low blasts and *SF3B1* mutation (MDS *SF3B1*)
        + Complete blood count and peripheral blood smear evaluation can show normocytic to macrocytic anemia
        + Bone marrow is typically hypercellular with left shifted erythroid predominance and dyserythropoiesis
        + Ring sideroblasts are increased; ring sideroblasts are defined as having ≥ 5 iron granules or granules encircling at least one - third or more of the nucleus
        + Dysplastic changes overlap with those of other MDS types (see [features of dysplasia table](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/myeloproliferativemdsprognosticscoring.html#MDSprogscoretables))
        + Blast percentage is
      * MDS morphologically defined
        + MDS with increased blasts
          - Bone marrow is usually hypercellular
          - Dysplastic changes overlap with those of other MDS types (see [features of dysplasia table](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/myeloproliferativemdsprognosticscoring.html#MDSprogscoretables))
          - Subtypes
            * MDS with increased blasts 1 (MDS IB1): blast percentage is 5 - 9% in the bone marrow or 2 - 4% in the peripheral blood without significant fibrosis
            * MDS with increased blasts 2 (MDS IB2): blast percentage is 10 - 19% in the bone marrow or 5 - 19% in the peripheral blood or Auer rods without significant fibrosis
            * MDS with increased blasts and fibrosis (MDS-f): blast percentage is 5 - 19% in the bone marrow or 2 - 19% in the peripheral blood with fibrosis grade 2 to 3 out of 3
        + Myelodysplastic neoplasm, hypoplastic (MDS-h)
          - Bone marrow is hypocellular; the hypocellularity can be diffuse or patchy
          - Dysplastic changes overlap with those of other MDS types (see [features of dysplasia table](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/myeloproliferativemdsprognosticscoring.html#MDSprogscoretables))
          - Blast percentage is
          - Myelodysplastic neoplasm with low blasts (MDS LB)
            * Bone marrow is usually hypercellular
            * Dysplastic changes overlap with those of other MDS types (see [features of dysplasia table](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/myeloproliferativemdsprognosticscoring.html#MDSprogscoretables))
            * Blast percentage is

              + Bone marrow is usually hypercellular
              + Dysplastic changes overlap with those of other MDS types (see [features of dysplasia table](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/myeloproliferativemdsprognosticscoring.html#MDSprogscoretables))
              + Blast percentage is 5 - 19% in the bone marrow and 2 - 19% in the peripheral blood

Cytology description

* 500 cell count is recommended for morphologic evaluation in bone marrow aspirate smears and touch preparations
* Limitations occur when there is a dry tap or the aspirate smears are aspicular and suboptimal for evaluation
* Cytologic dysplastic changes are listed in the [features of dysplasia table](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/myeloproliferativemdsprognosticscoring.html#MDSprogscoretables), especially the top table (features of dysplasia seen in aspirate smears)
* Reference: [Haematologica 2012;97:723](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/22180437)

Peripheral smear description

* Cytopenia in at least 1 hematopoietic lineage is usually seen in most cases of MDS
  + Anemia: Hb Leukopenia: absolute neutrophil count 9/L
  + Thrombocytopenia: platelets 9/L
* Only granulocytic dysplasia and increased blasts are usually identified in the peripheral blood; erythroid dysplasia and megakaryocytic dysplasia are not seen
* Cytologic dysplastic changes are listed in the [features of dysplasia table](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/myeloproliferativemdsprognosticscoring.html#MDSprogscoretables), especially the top table (features of dysplasia seen in aspirate smears, under dysgranulopoiesis)
* Reference: [Blood 2023;142:2247](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/37774372)

Positive stains

* [CD34](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/cdmarkerscd34.html) and [CD117](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/cdmarkerscd117.html) can be used to enumerate blasts in the tissue sections
* [CD42b](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/cdmarkerscd40.html#cd42b), [CD61](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/cdmarkerscd61.html) and [factor VIII](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsfactorviii.html) can be used to evaluate for small megakaryocytes and atypical clustering in the tissue sections
* [Reticulin](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsreticulin.html) and [trichrome](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainstrichrome.html) are used to evaluate for fibrosis
* [PAS](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainspas.html) positivity in the erythroids is a feature of dysplasia
* [Prussian blue iron stain](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsiron.html) is used to visualize ring sideroblasts
* Reference: [J Clin Exp Hematop 2018;58:51](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/29998976)

Flow cytometry description

* There are no definitive diagnostic flow cytometry features that are seen in MDS
* Decreased side scatter in the granulocytes is suggestive of hypogranularity, which is a feature of granulocytic dysplasia ([Front Oncol 2016;6:161](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/27446807))
* [CD34](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/cdmarkerscd34.html) and [CD117](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/cdmarkerscd117.html) can be used to enumerate cases with increased blasts; the blasts can have aberrant expression of lymphoid antigens (e.g., [CD2](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/cdmarkerscd1.html#cd2), [CD56](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/cdmarkerscd56.html), etc.) ([Oman Med J 2021;36:e218](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/33585041))
* Granulocytes can have dyssynchronous expression patterns of immature and maturate antigens, particularly on neutrophils ([CD10](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/cdmarkerscd10.html), [CD11c](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/cdmarkerscd10to19.html#cd11c), [CD13](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/cdmarkerscd13.html), [CD15](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/cdmarkerscd15.html), [CD33](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/cdmarkerscd33.html)) and monocytes ([CD4](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/cdmarkerscd4.html), [CD11b](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/cdmarkerscd10to19.html#cd11b), [CD11c](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/cdmarkerscd10to19.html#cd11c), [CD14](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/cdmarkerscd10to19.html#cd14), [CD16](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/cdmarkerscd10to19.html#cd16), [CD64](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/cdmarkerscd60.html#cd64)) ([Front Oncol 2017;7:270](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/29188193))

Molecular / cytogenetics description

* Chromosomal abnormalities have been seen in at least 50% of MDS patients, including the abnormalities of chromosome 5, 7, 8 and 20; complex karyotypes are also identified ([Blood 2008;111:1534](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/17954704))
* Genetic abnormalities have been identified in over 50 genes in at least 80% of MDS patients, including *SF3B1*, *SRSF2*, *U2AF1*, *ZRSR2*, *LUC7L2*, *DDX41*, *ASXL1*, *EZH2*, *TET2*, *DNMT3A*, *IDH1* / *IDH2*, *CSNK1A1*, *NRAS*, *STAG2*, *RUNX1*, *BCOR*, *TP53* and *CUX1* ([Eur J Haematol 2015;95:3](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/25645650))
* Cases of childhood MDS can been associated with *SETBP1*, *GATA2*, *PTPN11*, *RAS* and *SAMD* mutations ([Nat Commun 2017;8:1557](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/29146900))

Updates in myelodysplastic syndromes

Sample pathology report

* Bone marrow, biopsy:
  + Hypercellular (80%) bone marrow with dyserythropoiesis and ~12% blasts (see comment)
  + Comment: Bone marrow aspirate smears show erythroid dysplasia and a mild increase in blasts, constituting ~10% of the total nucleated cells on the aspirate smear. The bone marrow core biopsy and clot section are hypercellular for age with increased scattered blasts highlighted by CD34 immunostain. Flow cytometric analysis shows increased abnormal myeloblasts that constitute about 11.3% of total events with a normal immunophenotype (CD13+, CD33+, CD34+, CD38+, CD117+, MPO+). These findings are compatible with a myelodysplastic neoplasm with increased blasts (MDS IB2); however, correlation with karyotype, FISH and NGS studies is required for complete evaluation.

Differential diagnosis

* [Vitamin B12](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/hematologyvitaminb12deficiency.html), [folate](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/hematofolatedefanemia.html) and [copper deficiency](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/bonemarrowcopperdeficiency.html):
  + Can lead to ineffective hematopoiesis and may mimic some features of MDS
  + Correlation with laboratory values, cytogenetics and molecular studies are required ([Proc (Bayl Univ Med Cent) 2019;32:589](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/31656431), [Case Rep Hematol 2021;2021:9661765](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/34881068))
* [Recent chemotherapy or radiation therapy exposure](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/bonemarrowneoplastictherapy.html):
  + Can cause bone marrow suppression, cytopenias and dysplastic changes, which may resemble MDS
  + Close examination of the patient's recent medication and radiation exposure is required ([Leukemia 2021;35:835](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/32595214))
* [Acute myeloid leukemias](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/bonemarrowneoplasticamlgeneral.html):
  + Can have accompanying dysplasia; however, they tend to have a higher blast count (> 20%) than MDS
  + In some cases of AML with defining genetic abnormalities (e.g., *PML::RARA*, *RUNX1::RUNX1T1*, *NPM1*, etc.), there may be Blood Res 2020;55:S1)
* Myeloproliferative neoplasms (e.g., [primary myelofibrosis](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/myeloproliferativemyelofibrosis.html)) and myelodysplastic / myeloproliferative neoplasms (e.g., [chronic myelomonocytic leukemia [CMML]](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/myeloproliferativecmml.html)):
  + Can have atypical or dysplastic findings, resembling MDS
  + These cases usually have a cytosis instead of a cytopenia ([Am J Hematol 2022;97:352](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/34985762))
* Autoimmune disorders, such as [systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE)](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/kidneysle.html) or [rheumatoid arthritis](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/jointsra.html):
  + Can cause autoimmune mediated bone marrow suppression, leading to cytopenias and dysplastic changes ([Ann Hematol 2018;97:2015](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/30091023))
* [Aplastic anemia](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/bonemarrowaplasticanemia.html), [paroxysmal nocturnal hemoglobinuria (PNH)](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/hematologyPNH.html) or [Fanconi anemia](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/bonemarrowfanconis.html):
  + Can mimic MDS-h
  + Flow cytometry can be used to rule out PNH
  + Molecular testing is required to differentiate between MDS-h, aplastic anemia and Fanconi anemia ([Cancers (Basel) 2021;13:3380](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/34298596))

Board review style question #1

[![](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/imgau/bonemarrowneoMDSprogscoremai29.jpg)](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/imgau/bonemarrowneoMDSprogscoremai29.jpg)

A 72 year old man presents with fatigue and easy bruising. Complete blood count reveals

* Hemoglobin: 8.5 g/dL
* Absolute neutrophil count: 3,500/μL
* Platelets: 60,000/μL

Peripheral blood smear shows dysplastic neutrophils and macrocytic red blood cells with occasional circulating nucleated RBCs; circulating blasts are not identified. The bone marrow aspirate smears show erythroid dysplasia with 4% blasts on differential. The marrow cellularity is 60% without evidence of fibrosis. A Prussian blue iron stain is shown above. Which of the following is the most likely myelodysplastic neoplasm (MDS) subtype given the provided information?

1. MDS-f
2. MDS-h
3. MDS IB2
4. MDS *SF3B1*

Board review style answer #1

**D**. MDS *SF3B1*. The Prussian blue iron stain shows ring sideroblasts. The presence of ring sideroblasts is most likely associated with *SF3B1* mutation. The diagnosis of MDS *SF3B1* requires at least 15% ring sideroblasts or 5% ring sideroblasts with the *SF3B1* mutation. Answer B is incorrect because the patient is 72 years old, so his normal marrow cellularity should be around 20 - 40%; the question stem states that the marrow cellularity is 60%, which would be hypercellular for age and precludes the diagnosis of MDS-h. Answer C is incorrect because blasts are not increased, which precludes the diagnosis of MDS IB2. Answer A is incorrect because the question stem states there is no fibrosis; MDS-f requires grade 2 - 3 of fibrosis.

Board review style question #2

A 65 year old woman with a history of chemotherapy for breast cancer presents with recurrent infections and persistent neutropenia. Blood work reveals

* White blood cells: 1,200/μL (neutrophils 500/μL)
* Hemoglobin: 11 g/dL
* Platelets: 150,000/μL

The peripheral blood smear shows 3% blasts. The bone marrow aspirate smears show dysplastic erythroid precursors and 8% blasts. Reticulin shows grade 1 fibrosis. Karyotype analysis shows a normal female. Which of the following is the most likely myelodysplastic neoplasm (MDS) subtype given the provided information?

1. MDS 5q
2. MDS bi*TP53*
3. MDS-f
4. MDS IB1

Board review style answer #2

**D**. MDS IB1. The blast percentage is within 5 - 9% in the bone marrow and 2 - 4% in the peripheral blood and there is no significant fibrosis. Answer A is incorrect because the patient has a normal karyotype. Answer B is incorrect because no next genome sequencing (NGS) or *TP53* mutation was mentioned. Answer C is incorrect because the question stem states there is grade 1 fibrosis; MDS-f requires grade 2 - 3 of fibrosis.
