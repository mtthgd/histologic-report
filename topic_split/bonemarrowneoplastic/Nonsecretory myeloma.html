Definition / general
  • Type of multiple myeloma in which there is no detectable evidence of monoclonal protein secretion by neoplastic plasma cells
Essential features
  • Nonsecretory myeloma is characterized by bone marrow plasma cell proliferation that is not associated with monoclonal protein secretion (Mediterr J Hematol Infect Dis 2017;9:e2017053)
  • Normal serum and urine protein electrophoresis and normal serum free light chain level are essential
  • Prognosis and response to treatment in nonsecretory myeloma are better than those of secretory myeloma (Eur J Haematol 2015;95:57)
  • Follow up of nonsecretory myeloma patients is challenging since there is no monoclonal protein production and CRAB features are usually mild or absent (Mediterr J Hematol Infect Dis 2017;9:e2017053)
Terminology
  • True nonsecretory multiple myeloma: neoplastic cells produce immunoglobulins but are unable to secrete them
  • False nonsecretory multiple myeloma: neoplastic cells produce immunoglobulins but they are not detectable in blood or urine
  • Oligosecretory multiple myeloma: neoplastic cells produce small amounts of immunoglobulins
  • Nonproducer multiple myeloma: neoplastic cells are unable to synthesize any immunoglobulins
ICD coding
  • ICD-O: 9732/3 - nonsecretory myeloma
  • ICD-11: 2A83.1 - plasma cell myeloma
Epidemiology
  • Mean age: 60 years (BMC Cancer 2023;23:930)
  • Affects men more than women
  • More common in African American patients
Sites
  • Bone marrow
Pathophysiology
  • Clonal proliferation of neoplastic plasma cells involving > 10% of bone marrow displacing precursor elements and leading to lytic bone lesions and cytopenias
  • IL6 overexpression with subsequent stimulation of osteoclastic activities manifested by lytic bone lesions (Immunol Res 2014;59:188)
  • Nonsecretory myeloma can be either a nonproducer type where the cytoplasm of plasma cells is devoid of immunoglobulins or a true nonsecretor type where the cytoplasm contains immunoglobulins but they are not extruded extracellularly (Cureus 2024;16:e54479)
Etiology
  • Genetic susceptibility
  • Chromosomal hyperdiploidy and translocations involving IGH locus 14q32.33 leading to overexpression of CCND1, CCND2, CCND3, NSD2, MAF, MAFA and MAFB are considered primary events

Diagnostic algorithm of nonsecretory myeloma

Clinical features
  • Asymptomatic in up to 40% of patients (Cureus 2024;16:e54479)
  • Bone pain due to lytic bone lesions
  • Anemia, hypercalcemia and renal impairment are milder and less frequent than those of secretory myeloma patients (Cureus 2024;16:e54479)
  • No monoclonal proteins detected by serum and urine protein electrophoresis
Diagnosis
  • Demonstration of bone marrow involvement by clonal plasma cell proliferation using flow cytometry or immunohistochemistry
  • Presence of CRAB features (hypercalcemia, renal impairment, anemia, bone lesions) in some cases
  • Normal serum and urine protein electrophoresis
  • Normal immunofixation
  • Normal serum free light chain level (considered oligosecretory if increased) (Mediterr J Hematol Infect Dis 2017;9:e2017053)
Laboratory
  • Calcium > 11 mg/dL
  • Creatinine > 2 mg/dL
  • Hemoglobin Normal serum protein electrophoresis (SPEP) and urine protein electrophoresis (UPEP)
  • Normal immunofixation (Mediterr J Hematol Infect Dis 2017;9:e2017053)
  • Normal serum free light chain level (Mediterr J Hematol Infect Dis 2017;9:e2017053)
Radiology description
  • Lytic bone lesions with or without pathological fracture demonstrated by computed tomography (CT), magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) or Xray (Cureus 2022;14:e29571)

Prognostic factors
  • Higher overall survival and complete response to treatment compared to secretory myeloma (Eur J Haematol 2015;95:57, BMC Cancer 2023;23:930)
Case reports
  • 60 year old man with gradually worsening left sided chest pain (J Hematol 2024;13:94)
  • 67 year old woman with malaise and fever (Clin Case Rep 2022;10:e05628)
  • 68 year old woman with shoulder pain (Cureus 2022;14:e29571)
  • 71 year old man with low back pain (Medicine (Baltimore) 2024;103:e36951)
Treatment
  • Quadruplet therapy: daratumumab (anti-CD38 monoclonal antibody), bortezomib (proteasome inhibitor), lenalidomide (immunomodulatory drug) and dexamethasone
  • CAR T cell therapy
  • Bone marrow transplant (Mediterr J Hematol Infect Dis 2017;9:e2017053)

Microscopic (histologic) description
  • Mature plasma cells with abundant basophilic cytoplasm, perinuclear hofs (prominent Golgi apparatus), eccentric nuclei and clock face chromatin without nucleoli
  • Immature plasma cells with dispersed nuclear chromatin, high N:C ratio and a prominent nucleolus (J Chin Med Assoc 2011;74:464)
  • In trephine biopsy, plasma cells may form interstitial clusters, focal nodules or diffuse sheets

Cytology description
  • Mature plasma cells with abundant basophilic cytoplasm, perinuclear hofs, eccentric nuclei and clock face chromatin without nucleoli
  • Immature plasma cells with dispersed nuclear chromatin, high N:C ratio and a prominent nucleoli (J Chin Med Assoc 2011;74:464)
  • Grape-like cytoplasmic inclusions due to immunoglobulin crystallization (Mott cells)
  • Hyaline intracytoplasmic inclusions (Russell bodies)
  • Vacuolated eosinophilic cytoplasm with granular texture (flame cells) due to IgA accumulation
  • Pale cytoplasm due to collections of immunoglobulin protein that overlie or invaginate into the nucleus of plasma cells (Dutcher body), especially in oligosecretory myeloma (Blood Res 2016;51:63)

Plasma cells in blood

Positive stains
  • CD38 and CD138 (J Chin Med Assoc 2011;74:464)
  • Variably positive for CD56, CD10, CD20, CD79a, BCL1 and MUM1
  • Light chain restriction by immunohistochemistry or in situ hybridization is not identified
Negative stains
  • CD45, CD19
  • Cytokeratin (J Chin Med Assoc 2011;74:464)
Flow cytometry description
  • Clonal plasma cell population which express CD38, CD138, with or without CD56, CD117 and partial CD45 (J Hematol 2024;13:94)
  • Cytoplasmic immunoglobulins (cIg) can be absent
  • CD19, CD20, CD79a and BCL1 are expressed in myeloma with t(11:14)

Molecular / cytogenetics description
  • Hyperdiploidy due to trisomy of odd numbered chromosomes, whole or partial chromosomal deletions and gains and chromosomal translocations, including IGH 14q32 translocations affecting various oncogenes such as CCND1, NSD2, FGFR3, MAF, CCND3, MAFB, MAFA and CCND2
  • Chromosome 13 monosomy or partial deletion
  • Activating mutations of KRAS, NRAS or BRAF
  • MYC rearrangement
  • Reference: Hematol Rep 2020;12:9054

Sample pathology report
  • Bone marrow, left Iliac bone, biopsy and aspirate:
    • Plasma cell myeloma (see comment)
    • Hypercellular marrow with 90% plasma cells
    • Trilineage hematopoiesis is markedly reduced.
    • There is moderate patchy increase in reticulin fibers.
    • Comment: The patient's clinical presentation with bone pain and the imaging finding of lytic bone lesions are noted. The bone marrow is hypercellular with sheets of atypical plasma cells involving 90% of the intertrabecular space. Concurrent flow cytometry demonstrated a CD38+, CD138+, CD19- plasma cell population. The negative SPEP and UPEP results, along with normal serum free light chain level, support the nonsecretory nature of the process.
Differential diagnosis
  • Secretory type multiple myeloma:
    • Monoclonal protein detection by SPEP, UPEP or monotypic serum free light chain elevation (oligosecretory type)
  • Mature B cell lymphoma with plasmacytic differentiation:
    • Clonal population that is CD19+, CD56-
    • MYD88 L265P mutation in lymphoplasmacytic lymphoma (Mod Pathol 2015;28:564)
  • Plasmablastic lymphoma:
    • Usually presents with oral mass
    • Lack of myeloma defining features
    • EBER is more frequently positive in plasmablastic lymphoma but not entirely specific (Front Immunol 2024;15:1354604)
  • Monoclonal gammopathy of undetermined significance:
    • Involvement of Lack of myeloma defining features
  • Solitary plasmacytoma:
    • Single bone lesion with lack of CRAB features
  • Metastatic carcinoma with plasmacytoid features:
    • Clinical history of invasive lobular carcinoma of breast or high grade urothelial carcinoma, although some cases can first present with metastasis
    • Positive epithelial markers (AE1 / AE3, CAM5.2, CK7, CK20, EMA)
    • Positive GATA3
    • CD138 should not be used as it can be positive in a high percentage of carcinomas (Dis Markers 2019;2019:4928315)

Board review style question #1


The image above depicts a bone marrow biopsy from a 60 year old patient with a compression fracture. Which of the following supports the diagnosis of nonsecretory myeloma over secretory type myeloma?

  1. High serum free light chain level
  2. Involvement of bone marrow by CD38+ atypical plasma cells
  3. Monoclonal protein detected by immunofixation
  4. Multiple lytic bone lesions
  5. Normal serum and urine protein electrophoresis (SPEP / UPEP) with normal serum free light chain level
Board review style answer #1
E. Normal serum and urine protein electrophoresis (SPEP / UPEP) with normal serum free light chain level in the presence of neoplastic plasma cells support the diagnosis of nonsecretory myeloma. Answer C is incorrect because monoclonal protein detected by immunofixation is a feature of secretory type myeloma. Answer D is incorrect because multiple lytic bone lesions can be observed in both nonsecretory myeloma and secretory type myeloma. Answer B is incorrect because the involvement of bone marrow by CD38+ atypical plasma cells is an essential diagnostic feature of both nonsecretory myeloma and secretory type myeloma. Answer A is incorrect because high serum free light chain level is seen in oligosecretory myeloma.
