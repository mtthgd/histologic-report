Definition / general

* Infection or inflammation of breast tissue, usually bacterial, often seen in association with lactation
* If untreated, may form abscess and fistulous tracts

Essential features

* Infection or inflammation of breast tissue, usually bacterial, often seen in association with lactation
* If untreated, may form abscess and fistulous tracts
* *Staphylococcus aureus* most common organism
* Microscopic examination reveals a mixed dense inflammatory infiltrate, predominantly neutrophils, in breast tissue

Terminology

* Puerperal mastitis

Epidemiology

* Incidence in 2 - 33% of lactating women ([Am J Epidemiol 2002;155:103](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/11790672), [Med Microbiol Immunol 2018;207:83](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/29350290))
* 3% with mastitis have an abscess ([BJOG 2004;111:1378](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/15663122))
* Risk factors: age (21 - 35 years old), mastitis with previous child, cracked or sore nipples, use of ointments, incorrect breastfeeding practices, peripartum antibiotic therapy ([Med Microbiol Immunol 2018;207:83](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/29350290))

Sites

* Subareolar (nonpuerperal)

Pathophysiology

* Infection via the mammary duct system; typically occurs within 2 - 3 weeks after the start of lactation ([Breastfeed Med 2014;9:239](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/24911394))
* *Staphylococcus aureus* is most common organism transmitted from skin or infant ([Breastfeed Med 2014;9:239](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/24911394))
* Accumulation of inadequately drained milk in ducts and lobules creates a microenvironment that fosters bacterial growth ([Hoda: Rosen’s Breast Pathology, 4th Edition, 2014](http://www.amazon.com/exec/obidos/ASIN/1451176538/pathologyoutl-20))
* Infrequent complication is abscess formation (localized collection of purulent material)
* Milk samples show loss of bacterial diversity ([J Hum Lact 2015;31:406](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/25948578))
* Subareolar or nonpuerperal abscesses most often a consequence of [squamous metaplasia of lactiferous ducts (SMOLD)](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/breastsmold.html) ([J Pathol Transl Med 2015;49:279](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/26095437))

Etiology

* Associated with lactation and cracks in nipple
* Common scenarios: infrequent feeding, oversupply of milk, rapid weaning, illness in mother or child, clogged duct ([J Hum Lact 1998;14:101](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/9775842))
* Also associated with eczema, nipple dermatoses and postreduction mammoplasty
* Bacteria often not isolated from culture
* Pregnancy related infections are usually due to *Staphylococcus*
  + Usually unilateral, often methicillin resistant *Staphylococcus aureus* (MRSA) ([Arch Surg 2007;142:881](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/17891866), [Asian J Surg 2009;32:55](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/19321404))
  + Also *Staphylococcus epidermidis* ([BMC Microbiol 2009;9:82](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/19422689), [J Hum Lact 2015;31:406](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/25948578))
* *Streptococcus* usually causes diffuse infections leading to scarring but is uncommon today ([J Hum Lact 2020;890334420965147](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/33201760))
* Tuberculosis may occur in endemic areas ([Med Princ Pract 2009;18:245](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/19349731), [Int J Infect Dis 2019;87:135](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/31442629))
* Nonpregnancy related infections may be polymicrobial or associated with diabetes ([Am J Med Sci 2009;338:123](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/19680016))

Clinical features

* Erythema, swelling, firmness, breast pain, fever, malaise, decreased milk outflow
* Abscess: well circumscribed fluctuant mass in addition to above features
* African Americans may have higher risk of abscess formation ([World J Surg 2009;33:2582](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/19669231))
* Obesity (BMI > 30) and smoking risk factors for abscess ([World J Surg 2009;33:2582](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/19669231))
* Zuska disease (periductal mastitis): clinical scenario of recurring subareolar abscesses and sinus formation ([Am J Surg 1951;81:312](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/14819475))
* Fungal mastitis: rare
  + Actinomycosis may develop in nipple and form a sinus tract
  + *Candida* infection may interfere with breast feeding but *Candida albicans* is not present in milk ducts in women with clinical symptoms of ductal candidiasis ([Breastfeed Med 2009;4:57](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/19500049))

Diagnosis

* Based on history and clinical findings
* Ultrasound preferred imagining modality ([Radiol Bras 2020;53:405](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/33304009))
* Biopsy may be warranted for suspected abscess, atypical presentation, recurrent infection or treatment failure; Gram stain and culture (aerobic and anaerobic) with sensitivities to guide antibiotic selection (rarely needed)
* FNA can be used to drain ([Am Fam Physician 2008;78:727](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/18819238))

Radiology description

* Ultrasound (preferred modality): abscess shows hypoechoic lesion of purulent material, well circumscribed, macrolobulated, irregular or ill defined with septa and thick echogenic rim ([Radiographics 2011;31:1683](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/21997989), [Radiographics 2007;27 Suppl 1:S101](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/18180221))
* Mammography of limited value due to nonspecific findings

Treatment

* Symptomatic treatment: effective milk removal via breastfeeding, pumping or hand expressing to fully empty breasts, analgesia, warm compress ([Am Fam Physician 2008;78:727](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/18819238))
* Kneading and dispersing manipulation for early stage disease ([Zhong Xi Yi Jie He Xue Bao 2009;7:1130](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/20015432))
* Antibiotics targeting *Staphylococcus* species are mainstay of treatment, although effectiveness is debated ([J Hum Lact 2008;24:311](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/18689718), [Cochrane Database Syst Rev 2013:CD005458](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/23450563))
* Aspiration or incision and drainage; now considered last resort for abscess management ([Radiographics 2011;31:1683](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/21997989), [Expert Rev Anti Infect Ther 2014;12:753](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/24791941))
* Urokinase irrigation may be useful for large abscesses ([J Ultrasound Med 2009;28:449](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/19321672))
* Ultrasound guided drainage ([Radiology 2004;232:904](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/15284435), [Br J Radiol 2005;78:186](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/15730981))
* Probiotics ([Benef Microbes 2014;5:169](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/24463206), [Appl Environ Microbiol 2008;74:4650](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/18539795), [Clin Infect Dis 2010;50:1551](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/20455694))

Lactational abscess

Gross description

* Excisions rare; specimens usually from incision and drainage procedures and typically received in fragments

Microscopic (histologic) description

* Mixed dense inflammatory infiltrate, predominantly neutrophils, in breast tissue
* May obscure underlying normal breast tissue
* Tissue necrosis may be present
* Gram stain for microorganisms may reveal bacterial forms (gram positive cocci) associated with neutrophilic infiltrate
* Granulation tissue and chronic inflammation with resolution
* Lymphocytes, giant cells and granulomas are not typical ([J Pathol Transl Med 2015;49:279](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/26095437))

Breast abscess and necrosis

Cytology description

* Background of acute inflammation and necrotic debris ([Cytopathology 1992;3:281](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/1288653))

Positive stains

* [Gram stain](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsgramstain.html) may highlight Gram positive bacterial forms

Sample pathology report

* Right breast, core needle biopsy:
  + Breast tissue with abundant acute inflammation and bacterial forms identified on Gram stain (see comment)
  + Comment: The histologic findings are compatible with acute mastitis and abscess formation in the appropriate clinical context. Clinical correlation and correlation with microbiology studies (if performed) is recommended.

Differential diagnosis

* [Squamous metaplasia of lactiferous ducts (SMOLD)](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/breastsmold.html):
  + Subareolar location
  + Lactiferous ducts with squamous metaplasia
  + Multinucleated giant cells associated with keratin debris
  + Strong association with smoking
* [Fat necrosis](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/breastfatnecrosis.html):
  + Infarcted adipocytes, lipid laden macrophages, foreign body giant cell reaction
  + Initially acute inflammatory infiltrate may be present, chronic inflammation predominates in older lesions
  + History of surgery, prior biopsy, radiation, trauma
* [Idiopathic granulomatous mastitis](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/breastgranulomatousmastitis.html):
  + Granulomatous inflammation (lymphocytes, plasma cells, histiocytes, multinucleated giant cells)
  + Lobulocentric pattern, may obliterate lobules
  + May have neutrophils forming microabscesses surrounding empty microcysts; features overlap with cystic neutrophilic granulomatous mastitis, which may have gram positive bacilli within microcysts (*Corynebacterium*)
* [Duct ectasia](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/breastductectasia.html):
  + Foamy histiocytes within lumen and within wall and epithelium of ducts, periductal fibrosis
  + Inflammatory cells typically chronic; acute inflammatory infiltrate rarely present
