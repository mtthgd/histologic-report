Definition / general

* A minimally invasive diagnostic procedure that obtains tissue samples from breast lesions using specialized needle devices, frequently under image guidance
* Image guided core biopsy has evolved to become the gold standard for tissue diagnosis, offering accuracy rates exceeding 95% when properly performed, enabling definitive tissue diagnosis prior to surgical intervention, thereby reducing unnecessary operations and improving treatment planning ([Diagnostics (Basel) 2023;13:980](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/36900124))

Essential features

* Percutaneous core biopsy, typically performed via 1 of 3 primary image guidance modalities (ultrasound, stereotactic, magnetic resonance imaging [MRI]) with specific indications
* Standardized risk stratification through breast imaging reporting and data system (BIRADS) classification
* Mandatory radiologic pathologic correlation ([Front Oncol 2023;13:1230083](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/37593094))

Terminology

* Current terminology according to 2024 standardized reporting guidelines ([Virchows Arch 2024;484:3](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/37770765))

  + Percutaneous core biopsy
  + Vacuum assisted core biopsy (VABB)
  + Ultrasound guided core biopsy (US CNB)
  + Stereotactic guided core biopsy (SCNB)

Epidemiology

* According to recent clinical data ([Eur J Radiol 2022;153:110361](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/35617870))
  + ~1.6 million breast biopsies are performed annually in the United States
  + Core biopsy utilization has increased by 12% annually since 2019
  + Most common in 40 - 70 year old women
  + Rising incidence in 40 - 49 year old women following new screening guidelines
  + Increased use in high risk screening programs
  + Growing application in male breast lesions
  + Higher utilization in dense breast populations requiring supplemental screening

Sites

* Anatomical considerations based on current technical guidelines ([Semin Intervent Radiol 2024;41:466](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/39664223))
  + Any quadrant of breast tissue
  + Axillary tail
  + Retroareolar region (requires special technique consideration)
  + Axillary lymph nodes
  + Specific considerations for
    - Superficial lesions (< 5 mm from skin)
    - Deep lesions (< 5 mm from chest wall)
    - Lesions near implants
    - Lesions in dense breast tissue
    - Multicentric lesions

Clinical features

* Breast core biopsy can be used for the initial evaluation of many types of breast lesions, sparing many patients with benign lesions from needing surgical intervention
  + Image guided core biopsy has become the gold standard in the diagnosis of breast lesions, with accuracy rates exceeding 95% when properly performed ([Diagnostics (Basel) 2023;13:980](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/36900124))
* Does not typically result in any cosmetic sequelae such as breast deformity or skin scarring
* Risk assessment categories
  + BIRADS classification and management according to 2024 American College of Radiology (ACR) guidelines ([StatPearls: Breast Imaging Reporting and Data System [Accessed 20 February 2025]](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/29083600))
    - BIRADS 0: incomplete
      * Requires additional imaging
      * Cannot be used as final assessment
      * Management: complete imaging evaluation
    - BIRADS 1: negative
      * No mammographic evidence of malignancy
      * Normal examination
      * Management: routine screening
    - BIRADS 2: benign
      * Definitively benign findings
      * Examples: calcified fibroadenomas, multiple secretory calcifications, fat containing lesions
      * Management: routine screening
    - BIRADS 3: probably benign
      * ≤ 2% risk of malignancy
      * Specific mammographic findings
      * Management: short interval follow up (6 months)
    - BIRADS 4: suspicious
      * Subdivided into 4A, 4B, 4C based on suspicion level
      * 4A: low suspicion (2 - 10% risk)
      * 4B: moderate suspicion (10 - 50% risk)
      * 4C: high suspicion (50 - 95% risk)
      * Management: tissue diagnosis is required, usually via core biopsy
    - BIRADS 5: highly suggestive
      * ≥ 95% risk of malignancy
      * Classic malignant findings
      * Management: tissue diagnosis is required, usually via core biopsy
    - BIRADS 6: known malignancy
      * Biopsy proven malignancy
      * May be used for monitoring neoadjuvant therapy response
      * Management: complete cancer staging and treatment planning
  + Pathologic risk category reporting system produced by the UK National Coordinating Committee for Breast Screening Pathology and endorsed by the European Commission working group on breast screening pathology ([J Clin Pathol 2004;57:897](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/15333647))
    - B1: normal tissue / inadequate sample (comment on microcalcifications and specimen adequacy)
    - B2: benign lesion (specify)
    - B3: uncertain malignant potential (includes [radial scar](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/breastradialscar.html), some papillary lesions, [atypical ductal hyperplasia](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/breastadh.html), lobular neoplasia)
    - B4: suspicious of malignancy (suggestive but not diagnostic due to scanty material or artifacts)
    - B5: malignant; specify if invasive or not, if possible; indicate grade of [ductal carcinoma in situ](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/breastmalignantDCIS.html)
* Radiologic pathologic correlation ([Ultrasonography 2018;37:107](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/29169231))
  + 4 breast imaging categories
    - Concordant malignancy: BIRADS 4 or 5 is diagnosed as malignancy on core biopsy
    - Discordant malignancy: BIRADS 2 or 3 is diagnosed as malignancy on core biopsy; this may be seen in well circumscribed malignant tumors including primary breast cancers such as some [triple negative](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/breastmalignanttripleneg.html) carcinomas, [encapsulated](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/breastmalignantencapsulatedpapillary.html) or [solid](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/breastmalignantsolidpapillarycarcinoma.html) papillary carcinomas or carcinoma with [medullary pattern](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/breastmalignantmedullary.html) as well as [metastases](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/breastmalignantmet.html) to the breast and involvement of the breast by [lymphoma](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/breastmalignantlymphoma.html)
    - Concordant benign: BIRADS 2, 3 or 4A with benign pathology on core biopsy
    - Discordant benign: BIRADS 4 or 5 but demonstrates benign pathology on core biopsy; this may occur secondary to a benign lesion mimicking cancer via imaging (e.g., [radial scars](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/breastradialscar.html), [complex sclerosing lesions](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/breastradialscar.html), [fat necrosis](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/breastfatnecrosis.html)), however, 4 - 30.9% show malignancy on subsequent surgical excision
  + Multidisciplinary discussion improves correlation, impacting patient management in up to 5.3% of cases ([Ann Diagn Pathol 2022;57:151864](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/34972038), [Can Assoc Radiol J 2016;67:122](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/26632099))

Diagnosis

* Breast core biopsy has several advantages as compared to fine needle aspiration biopsy
  + Can distinguish in situ from invasive carcinoma, helping to better plan subsequent management
  + Provides adequate formalin fixed paraffin embedded tissue with defined cold ischemic time, formalin fixation time and standard processing for optimal performance of breast cancer biomarker studies such as [ER](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainser.html), [PR](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsprog.html) and [HER2](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsbreasther2.html), which provide important prognostic and therapeutic information
* Breast core biopsy image guided modalities, 2024 technical guidelines ([Semin Intervent Radiol 2024;41:466](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/39664223))
  + Ultrasound guided
    - Advantages
      * Realtime visualization
      * No ionizing radiation
      * More comfortable for patients
      * Cost effective
      * Highest accuracy for mass lesions (98.7% accuracy)
      * May allow for simultaneous axillary lymph node sampling
    - Technical considerations
      * Optimal for masses > 5 mm
      * Multiple approach angles possible
      * Direct visualization of needle placement
      * Realtime confirmation of sampling
      * Color Doppler guidance option for vascular lesions
  + Stereotactic guided
    - Indications
      * Microcalcifications, 96.8% accuracy ([Eur Radiol 2023;33:6189](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/37042980))
      * Architectural distortions
      * Asymmetries not visible on ultrasound
    - Technical advances
      * Digital breast tomosynthesis guidance
      * Prone versus upright positioning options
      * 3D targeting capability
      * Realtime verification of calcification retrieval
      * Automated positioning systems
  + MRI guided
    - Indications
      * MRI only visible lesions
      * Occult lesions on conventional imaging
      * Monitoring of neoadjuvant therapy response
    - Technical requirements
      * Dedicated breast coils
      * MRI compatible equipment
      * Computer assisted targeting
      * Postbiopsy imaging verification
      * Marker placement confirmation
* Palpation guided core biopsy may be performed in clinical settings for large masses for which rapid diagnosis is desired
  + Challenges include the biopsy needle pushing away the mass rather than penetrating it and the lack of ability to confirm sampling of the area of interest via an imaging modality while performing the biopsy
* Needle selection ([Tomography 2023;9:1246](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/37489467))
  + Standard core biopsy
    - 14G: standard for mass lesions
    - Multiple passes required (minimum 4 - 5)
    - Limited tissue acquisition
    - Lower cost
    - Faster procedure time
  + Vacuum assisted core biopsy
    - 8 to 11G options available
    - Continuous vacuum assistance
    - Larger tissue samples
    - Single insertion multiple sampling
    - Option for complete lesion removal
    - Required for calcifications
    - Higher cost but better specimens
* Minimum sampling requirements ([Tomography 2023;9:1246](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/37489467))
  + Mass lesions: 4 - 5 cores with 14G
  + Calcifications: 12 cores minimum
  + Architectural distortions: 6 - 8 cores
  + Consider additional samples for
    - Heterogeneous lesions
    - Larger lesions (> 2 cm)
    - Planned neoadjuvant therapy
    - Molecular testing requirements

Laboratory

* Routine laboratory tests are not required for most patients prior to breast core biopsy ([Breast Cancer Res Treat 2011;132:917](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/21698409), [Br J Radiol 2017;90:20160594](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/27648482))
* Specific laboratory testing considerations ([Breast Cancer Res Treat 2011;132:917](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/21698409), [Br J Radiol 2017;90:20160594](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/27648482))
  + Coagulation profile if on anticoagulation
  + Pregnancy test when indicated

Treatment

* Postprocedure care
  + Compression for 10 - 15 minutes
  + Ice pack application
  + Activity restrictions for 24 hours
  + Return precautions
* Follow up recommendations are based on pathology results
* Potential therapeutic applications for complete lesion removal in selected cases ([Front Oncol 2023;13:1230083](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/37593094))
* Complications are usually mild / minimal and only rarely (< 1%) are severe ([Tomography 2023;9:1246](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/37489467))
  + Immediate complications
    - Bleeding / hematoma
      * Incidence: 1 - 7%
      * Risk factors
        + Anticoagulation
        + Larger needle gauge
        + Multiple passes
        + Deeply located lesions
        + Vascular lesions
      * Management
        + Manual compression
        + Ice application
        + Monitoring
        + Rarely requires intervention
    - Vasovagal reactions
      * Incidence: 3 - 5%
      * Risk factors
        + Anxiety
        + Prone positioning
        + Prolonged procedure
        + Pain
      * Prevention
        + Adequate local anesthesia
        + Patient positioning
        + Monitoring
        + Patient education
    - Pneumothorax
      * Incidence: < 0.1%
      * Risk factors
        + Deep lesions
        + Thin patients
        + Upper outer quadrant location
      * Prevention
        + Careful approach planning
        + Realtime imaging guidance
        + Patient positioning
  + Delayed complications
    - Infection
      * Incidence: < 0.2%
      * Risk factors
        + Diabetes
        + Immunosuppression
        + Hematoma formation
      * Prevention
        + Sterile technique
        + Prophylactic antibiotics in high risk cases
      * Epithelial displacement
        + Incidence: 2 - 4%
        + More common with
          - Vacuum assistance
          - Multiple passes
          - Papillary lesions
    - Marker migration
      * Incidence: 1 - 8%
      * Risk factors
        + Hematoma formation
        + Breast movement
        + Time delay to imaging
      * Prevention
        + Immediate postprocedure imaging
        + Proper marker selection

Microscopic (histologic) description

* Number and size of cores per block / slide can vary significantly depending on the size of the biopsy needle, number of cores obtained and laboratory protocols ([Breast Cancer Res Treat 2011;132:917](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/21698409), [Br J Radiol 2017;90:20160594](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/27648482))
* Cores may be separated into those containing calcifications and those without calcifications if this is the main indication for biopsy ([Breast Cancer Res Treat 2011;132:917](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/21698409), [Br J Radiol 2017;90:20160594](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/27648482))
* Blocks may be later radiographed to assess for calcifications if not found on initial slides
* Multiple levels per block, with intervening unstained slides, are often performed to ensure adequate microscopic assessment of the tissue, diagnostic stains or biomarker studies
* Grossly, H&E stained breast core biopsy slides tend to have a pink appearance for benign and blue appearance for malignant lesions; however, there are many exceptions to this general rule of thumb:
  + Exception: pink appearing slide from a malignancy with low tumor volume or low cellularity tumor on microscopic evaluation (e.g., scant low grade [invasive lobular carcinoma](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/breastmalignantlobularclassic.html))
  + Exception: blue appearing slide of a benign lesion with abundant proliferation on microscopic evaluation (e.g., benign [intraductal papilloma](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/breastpapilloma.html) with florid [usual ductal hyperplasia](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/breastepithelialductalhyperplasia.html))
* Core biopsy site can range from a small area of fibrous tissue with mild myofibroblast and vascular proliferation or may have significant associated [fat necrosis](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/breastfatnecrosis.html), inflammation, hemosiderin or hematoidin blood breakdown products, blood and hematoma, depending on the timing of the biopsy and subsequent excision, degree of tissue damage from the biopsy and patient wound healing
* Core biopsy site may have a defect in the tissue from the removal of the clip
* Core biopsy site often displays foreign material or other features specific to the [biopsy clip or marking device](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/breastbiopsymarkingdevices.html) deployed

Board review style question #1

A breast screening detected a lesion. Which of the following breast imaging reporting and data system (BIRADS) classifications should undergo core biopsy for tissue diagnosis?

1. BIRADS 0
2. BIRADS 1
3. BIRADS 2
4. BIRADS 3
5. BIRADS 4

Board review style answer #1

**E**. BIRADS 4. BIRADS category 4 is suspicious and should undergo core biopsy for tissue diagnosis. Answer A is incorrect because BIRADS 0 is incomplete and requires further imaging evaluation. Answers B, C and D are incorrect because BIRADS categories 1, 2 and 3 are benign and do not require core biopsy for tissue diagnosis. Also note, BIRADS category 5 is highly suggestive of malignancy, which is not listed as an answer choice, but also requires tissue diagnosis.

Board review style question #2

[![](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/imgau/breastcoreJorns05.jpg)](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/imgau/breastcoreJorns05.jpg)

Image guided breast core biopsy offers high diagnostic accuracy (> 95%) with only uncommon and rarely (< 1%) severe complications. What is a risk factor for bleeding / hematoma following breast core biopsy?

1. Anxiety
2. Diabetes
3. Multiple passes
4. Prone position
5. Thin patient

Board review style answer #2

**C**. Multiple passes. Bleeding / hematoma after breast core biopsy occurs in 1 - 7% of patients and is usually not serious enough to require significant interventions, typically being managed by manual compression and application of ice only. Risk factors for developing bleeding / hematoma after breast core biopsy include anticoagulation, use of a larger needle, multiple passes and targeting of deeply located and vascular lesions. Answers A, B, D and E are incorrect because these are not risk factors for bleeding / hematoma. Rather, they are risk factors for other uncommon complications following breast core biopsy including vasovagal reaction (risk factors: anxiety and prone position, infection (risk factor: diabetes) and pneumothorax (risk factor: thin patient, particularly when a deep lesion is targeted).
