Definition / general

* Sexually transmitted infection caused by gram negative rod *Klebsiella granulomatis*, formerly
  called *Calymmatobacterium granulomatis*, which has characteristic bipolar staining
* Sexually transmitted disease which affects genital skin and mucosa and causes inguinal lymphadenopathy; rarely becomes disseminated
* May occur in children of infected mothers via birth canal ([Am J Clin Pathol 1997;108:510](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/9353089))
* May mimic carcinoma ([Genitourin Med 1990;66:380](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/2245986))

Terminology

* Also called granuloma venereum, donovanosis

Epidemiology

* Endemic in tropical and developing areas, including India, Guyana, New Guinea, central Australia, southern Africa
* Rare in USA and Europe

Sites

* Anogenital skin, rarely oral mucosa or pharynx
* In females, vulva and perianal area are frequent sites; only rarely affects uterus, fallopian tubes, ovaries

Etiology

* Due to  *Klebsiella granulomatis*, an intracellular gram negative coccobacillus, previously termed *Calymmatobacterium granulomatis* and *Donovania granulomatis*

Clinical features

* Begins as a raised papular lesion, which eventually ulcerates with a beefy red, friable granulation tissue at the base
* Abundant granulation tissue accumulates and forms a protuberant, soft, painless mass that may mimic carcinoma ([Genitourin Med 1990;66:380](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/2245986))
* Regional lymph nodes are spared or show only nonspecific reactive changes, in contrast to [chancroid](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/penscrotumchancroid.html) and [lymphogranuloma venereum](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/penscrotumLGV.html)
* Untreated cases may develop extensive scarring, often associated with lymphatic obstruction and lymphedema (elephantiasis) of the external genitalia ([Robbins & Cotran Pathologic Basis of Disease, 9th Edition, Chapter 8](https://www.amazon.com/exec/obidos/ASIN/1455726133/pathologyoutl-20))

Diagnosis

* Microscopic examination of smears from ulcer base or histologic sections of ulcer is preferred
* Culture is difficult to perform and not routinely available
  + Does not grow on artificial solid media but has been cultured in chicken embryonic yolk sacs, on human monocytes and on human epithelial (Hep - 2) cells
* A serologic test, based on indirect immunofluorescence, is more useful in confirming the diagnosis in long - standing lesions, less useful in early disease
* A diagnostic PCR test has been developed
* Electron microscopic examination may be helpful

Case reports

* 18 year old woman with primary endometrial and endocervical granuloma inguinale ([Br J Vener Dis 1983;59:198](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/6850268))
* 27 year old woman with donovanosis as incidental finding on Pap test ([J Cytol 2013;30:217](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/24130421))
* 27 and 36 year old women with concomitant malacoplakia and granuloma inguinale of cervix ([Int J Gynecol Pathol 2008;27:282](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/18317211))
* Donovanosis affecting cervix, uterus and adnexae ([Am J Trop Med Hyg 1984;33:632](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/6476208))
* Granuloma inguinale of cervix ([Genitourin Med 1990;66:380](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/2245986))

Treatment

* Doxycycline 100 mg orally twice a day for at least 3 weeks and until all lesions have completely healed

Microscopic (histologic) description

* Inflammatory cells, mainly neutrophils and some macrophages
* Plump histiocytes with thin walled vacuoles containing multiple bacteria
* Bacteria appear as straight or curved dumbbell shaped rods with prominent bipolar granules (Donovan bodies), resembling a "safety pin"
* This classic "safety pin" appearance is more evident in Giemsa stain and not so apparent in alcohol fixed smears
* Epithelioid histiocytes may be seen, but giant cells are not seen ([Pantanowitz: Cytopathology of Infectious Diseases, page 106](https://www.amazon.com/exec/obidos/ASIN/1461402417/pathologyoutl-20))
* Relative paucity of epithelial cells
* Intact capillaries may be seen in scrapings and conventional pap smears ([Diagn Cytopathol 1986;2:138](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/3720486))

Positive stains

* [Giemsa](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsgiemsa.html)
  and [Warthin-Starry silver stains](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainswarthin.html) highlight Donovan bodies

Negative stains

* [Ziehl-Neelsen stain](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsacidfast.html) - to rule out mycobacteria
* [Gomori's methenamine silver stain](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsgms.html) - to rule out histoplasm

Electron microscopy description

* Prominent electron dense granules are present in periphery of cytoplasm
* Typical gram negative cell wall structure and surrounding electron dense capsule layer ([J Med Microbiol 1997;46:579](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/9236742),  [J Med Microbiol 1998;47:1069](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/9856642))

Differential diagnosis

* [Follicular cervicitis](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/cervixchroniccervicitis.html): accompanying inflammatory infiltrate is lymphoid predominant and not neutrophilic
* **Malakoplakia**: Michaelis-Gutmann bodies are more dense and round with concentric lamination, compared with donovan bodies ([Cytopathology 1991;2:271](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/1782364))

Additional references
