Definition / general
  • Primary tumors that develop outside the CNS and spread to the brain and spinal cord parenchyma through blood stream or by direct extension from adjacent anatomical structures (though less commonly), resulting in brain and spinal cord metastases
  • Leptomeningeal carcinomatosis refers to metastatic involvement of the pia arachnoid or subarachnoid space (Surg Neurol Int 2013;4:S209)
Essential features
  • Essential: detection of malignant nonprimary cells within the brain or spinal cord parenchyma
  • Desirable: the criteria for the diagnosis of primary tumor type is fulfilled
Terminology
N/A
ICD coding
  • ICD-11: 2D50 - malignant neoplasm metastasis in brain (with extension code for primary tumor type)
Epidemiology
  • Metastatic tumors are the most common CNS neoplasms
  • Exact incidence is mostly unknown due to variable sources of data ranging from preimaging era autopsy studies to single institution surgical series
  • Incidence rates in population based studies range from 8.3 to 14.3 per 100,000 people; most patients with CNS metastases have synchronous extracerebral metastases (Curr Oncol Rep 2012;14:48)
  • The most common primary sites noted in the Metropolitan Detroit Cancer Surveillance System for CNS metastases are lung, breast, skin, kidney and gastrointestinal tract with incidence proportions of 20%, 5%, 6.5%, 6.8% and 1.8%, respectively (J Clin Oncol 2004;22:2865)
  • Lung cancer is the most frequent metastasis to the CNS irrespective of patient sex and is the most common brain metastasis occurring in men; in women, breast cancer is the most commonly occurring CNS metastasis (Nat Rev Dis Primers 2019;5:5)
  • Brain metastases from primary lung are reported at the age of 40 - 49 years, with primary melanoma, renal or colorectal cancer at the age of 50 - 59 years and with breast cancer at the age of 20 - 39 years (Anticancer Res 2012;32:4655)
  • In pediatric patients, metastatic neoplasms to the CNS are rare, accounting for an estimated 1 - 10% of all brain tumors and there are relatively few reports in the pathology literature concerning solid (that is, nonhematopoietic) (J Neurosurg Pediatr 2014;14:129)
  • Germ cell tumors, sarcomas and neuroblastoma are common offenders (Curr Oncol Rep 2012;14:48)
  • Overall, there has been an increase in the incidence of brain metastases over time, possibly because of increased survival times for cancer patients overall, more sensitive diagnostic methods and a relative increase in cancer incidence as the population ages (Br J Cancer 2009;101:1919)
Sites
  • Most metastatic tumors reach the CNS through hematogenous dissemination or retrograde spread through cranial nerves; hematogenous pattern of dissemination is commonly seen in head and neck squamous cell carcinomas of the head and neck region and malignant salivary gland tumors (Cancer 1996;78:1781)
  • Apart from metastases to brain and spinal cord parenchyma, metastases to leptomeninges / subarachnoid space, dura and skull are also relatively common
      Breast and prostate carcinomas, known for bone metastases, can directly extend from adjacent skull into the brain parenchyma (Surg Neurol Int 2013;4:S245)
  • Most brain metastases occur at the corticomedullary and gray-white junction, where narrow and sharp branching of blood vessels provide the most direct route; frequently, the terminal watershed areas of arterial circulation are also affected, areas between the middle and posterior cerebral arteries being frequently involved (Surg Neurol Int 2013;4:S245)
  • Other sites include choroid plexus, pineal gland, pituitary and optic nerve (Surg Neurol Int 2013;4:S245)
  • Leptomeningeal spread is seen in small cell lung carcinoma (SCLC) and melanoma; nonetheless, it is most frequently associated with breast carcinomas due to its overall high incidence (Curr Opin Neurol 2009;22:665)
  • Spinal epidural metastases are associated with prostate, breast, lung and kidney carcinomas along with non-Hodgkin lymphoma and multiple myeloma (J Neurooncol 2005;75:43)
  • Tumor to tumor metastasis arises when a rapidly growing, systemic malignant neoplasm, such as melanoma and lung or breast carcinoma spreads to a slower growing primary CNS tumor, such as pituitary adenoma or meningioma (Neuropathology 2009;29:303)
Pathophysiology
  • Blood - brain barrier (BBB) and blood - cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) barrier protect the CNS; however, their permeability can be altered, facilitating entry of metastatic tumor cells
    • Blood - brain barrier (BBB)
      • Made up of endothelial cells with low rate of transcellular transport and numerous efflux pumps connected by continuous tight junctions
      • 2 basement membranes made up of pericytes and astrocytic terminal processes also play a supportive role in the BBB's functions; hence, only small, uncharged molecules are permitted to pass through without the need for a specific transporter
      • Research has shown that monocytes can cross the BBB in response to homeostatic changes in the brain and a similar mechanism is utilized by tumor cells (Nat Rev Immunol 2011;11:762)
    • Blood - cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) barrier
      • Blood - CSF barrier is formed by choroid plexus epithelial cells, connected by tight junctions; intervening capillaries demonstrate fenestrations and intercellular gaps, allowing free passage of molecules between both compartments (Methods Mol Biol 2011;686:101)
      • Primary cancer cells express complement protein (C3), which can disrupt the blood - CSF barrier, allowing mitogens to enter the CSF (Cell 2017;168:1101)
  • Seeding
    • In order for metastastatic cells to survive, after they enter the circulation via bloodstream or through the lymphatic system, they must undergo adhesive (circulatory) arrest in the vasculature and then exit, either as single isolated cells or emboli
    • Circulatory arrest occurs at branch points in capillaries, where tumor cells get more exposure to surrounding environment due to decreased flow rate; after being arrested, there is upregulation of genes encoding for mitogenic enzymes and growth factors, such as COX2 and HBEGF, associated with cell migration across the BBB
    • Vascular growth, endothelial permeability and extracellular breakdown is promoted by VEGF and metalloproteinases produced by tumor and immune cells (Ann Biomed Eng 2011;39:2223, Arzneimittelforschung 1976;26:1686, Nat Rev Clin Oncol 2011;8:344, Int J Cancer 2012;131:1071)
  • Colonization
    • Growth of tumor, colonization and chemoresistance is promoted by surrounding astrocytes, microglia and immune cells; they release inflammatory chemokines such as interferon alpha (IFNα) and TNF, thereby activating transcription and cell survival pathways (Nature 2016;533:493)
    • Additionally, tumor cells develop several adaptative mechanisms that facilitate brain colonization, including glucose oxidation, pentose phosphate pathway activation and the glutathione system activity to reduce production of reactive oxygen species (Cancer Res 2007;67:1472, Cancer Res 2015;75:554)
  • Leptomeningeal metastases (LM)
    • Refers to metastases that grows in the lining of the brain or spine or within the CSF
    • Melanoma, lung and breast cancers have the highest rates of leptomeningeal metastases, which can occur either with or without brain parenchymal involvement
    • COX2 and TGFβ signalling pathways play key roles in facilitating the escape tumor cells into the CSF; prognosis is very poor (Cancer Res 2014;74:2385, Cancer Res 2009;69:828)
Etiology
  • Genetic analyses have associated unique driver mutations with certain primary tumors and brain metastases
    • Lung cancer brain metastases
      • Mutations in EGFR and ALK gene rearrangement are significant; targeted therapy, including tyrosine kinase inhibitors (TKIs) show strong penetration and effectiveness in the CNS (N Engl J Med 2017;377:829, N Engl J Med 2017;376:629)
      • Mutations in the tumor suppressor LKB1 and KRAS97 genes, hyperactivity within the WNT signalling pathway and upregulation of ANGPT4 and PDGFRB genes have also been linked to the development of brain and bone metastases in lung adenocarcinoma (Nat Rev Dis Primers 2019;5:5)
    • Breast cancer brain metastases
      • HER2 positive and triple negative tumors are most likely to develop brain metastases
      • Important driver mutations associated with cell growth and proliferation in breast cancer include ERBB2, CCND1, CDKN2A and CDKN2B as well as tumor suppressors (PTEN and TP53); breast carcinomas, which are HER2 positive and triple negative, are prone to develop brain metastases (Clin Cancer Res 2017;23:2617)
    • Melanoma brain metastases
      • Driver mutations involved in the development of melanoma metastases include CDKN2A, BRAF, NRAS and KIT (Cell 2012;150:251)
    • Renal cell carcinoma brain metastases
      • The most frequent mutation in both familial and sporadic clear cell renal cell carcinoma (RCC) is the inactivation of VHL, a tumor suppressor gene (Proc Natl Acad Sci U S A 2001;98:9754)
      • RCC brain metastases and primary tumors, when compared, show mutations in the genes PTEN, PIK3CA and CDKN2A, which are unique to brain metastases (Front Oncol 2017;7:230)
    • Colorectal carcinoma brain metastases
      • 4 consensus molecular subtypes (CMS) have been studied
        • CMS1: microsatellite instability and immune activation
        • CMS2: WNT and MYC upregulation
        • CMS3: metabolic dysregulation
        • CMS4: TGFβ activation, cellular invasion and angiogenesis (Nat Med 2015;21:1350)
      • Mutations in PIK3CA, KRAS and NRAS are also associated with colorectal carcinoma brain metastases

Clinical features
  • Those with multiple or posterior fossa metastases commonly present with headaches (up to 50% of patients)
  • 15 - 25% of patients with headaches also have concurrent papilloedema
  • ~40% of patients with brain metastases acquire neurological deficits (weakness or numbness) and 15 - 20% experience seizures; acute, stroke-like symptoms due to intralesional hemorrhage may be seen in 5 - 10% of patients, especially those with tumors that have a tendency of bleeding, such as melanoma and RCC (Nat Rev Dis Primers 2019;5:5)
  • Multiple metastases may result in elevated intracranial pressure secondary to mass effect, vasogenic oedema or altered CSF flow; this leads to altered mentation and impaired cognition
Diagnosis
  • Rapid brain imaging is crucial when acute neurological symptoms arise
  • Brain imaging techniques have significantly improved over the years and laboratory investigations also play a key role in the diagnostic process
Laboratory
  • Laboratory investigations essentially include tests such as complete blood count (CBC), electrolyte panel, coagulation screen and liver function tests
  • Serum CEA level is implicated with brain metastasis in advanced non-small cell lung cancer (NSCLC) (Tumour Biol 2012;33:1065)
  • Novel, innovative method for diagnosing leptomeningeal metastases from epithelial tumors is by detection of circulating tumor cells in cerebrospinal fluid; it works similar to the FDA approved technique for counting circulating tumor cells (CTCs) in the blood of patients with epithelial tumors (Neuro Oncol 2017;19:1248)
Radiology description
  • Computed tomography (CT) and magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) are the primary imaging modalities
  • Advanced imaging techniques, such as proton magnetic resonance spectroscopy (MRS), contrast enhanced magnetic resonance perfusion (MRP), diffusion weighted imaging (DWI) and diffusion tensor imaging (DTI) also help in making specific histological diagnoses, detecting hemorrhage and distinguishing metastasis from high grade glioma and other solitary enhancing brain lesions (Surg Neurol Int 2013;4:S209)
    • Computed tomography (CT)
      • Solitary lesion or multiple mass lesions associated with surrounding vasogenic edema (variable amount), in the absence of hemorrhage, can appear hypodense, isodense or hyperdense compared to the brain (Radiology 1980;136:657)
      • Melanoma metastases tend to be hyperdense to brain on CT even in the absence of hemorrhage; iodinated contrast enhancement is vital and brain metastases may demonstrate ring, nodular or solid enhancement (Surg Neurol Int 2013;4:S209)
    • Magnetic resonance imaging (MRI)
      • Metastases appear isointense or hypointense on T1, hyperintense on T2
      • Melanoma metastases are T1 hyperintense due to melanin production
      • Metastatic lesions with intralesional hemorrhage may show T1 signal hyperintensity, depending on the age of hemorrhage (Surg Neurol Int 2013;4:S209)
      • Small metastases can be detected by Gadolinium contrast enhancement (Radiology 1987;165:619)
    • Magnetic resonance spectroscopy (MRS)
      • Both brain metastases and high grade gliomas show enhanced choline / creatine peak ratios compared to normal brain (Radiographics 2006;26:S173)
      • Choline / creatine ratios between high grade gliomas and metastases can be variable and often overlap, making this ratio alone unreliable for distinguishing these entities (Acta Radiol 2010;51:316)
      • Brain metastases exhibit elevated lipid and lactate peaks (Radiographics 2006;26:S173)
    • Magnetic resonance perfusion (MRP)
      • Highly vascular metastatic lesions show elevated relative cerebral blood volume (rCBV) compared to normal white matter, similar to most glioblastomas
      • CNS lymphoma typically demonstrates lower rCBV compared to metastases and high grade glioma (J Magn Reson Imaging 2006;24:817)
    • Diffusion weighted imaging (DWI) / diffusion tensor imaging (DTI)
      • Facilitated diffusion in the form of elevated values of apparent diffusion coefficient (ADC) is demonstarated by metastases, likewise primary glial neoplasms, in comparison to white matter (Radiographics 2006;26:S173)
      • Crucial technique: examine ADC values in the nonenhancing and T2 hyperintense regions surrounding a tumor in order to differentiate high grade gliomas from metastases; primary neoplasms have lower ADC values in areas of infiltration when compared to metastases
      • Metastases can also be distinguished from pyogenic abscesses, which show markedly restricted diffusion in their central nonenhancing portions (Magn Reson Imaging 2003;21:645)
    • Fluorodeoxyglucose positron emission tomography (FDG-PET)
      • Metastases often present as areas of hypometabolism, similar to nonneoplastic conditions like infarction, whereas the cerebral cortex is highly FDG avid in comparison (Radiology 2003;226:181)
  • Nonparenchymal central nervous system metastases
    • Pachymeningeal metastases are located in the dura mater; they appear as focal nodular or diffuse enhancing dural masses and often spare the subarachnoid space but can invade adjacent brain parenchyma
    • Leptomeningeal carcinomatosis can occur in a sheet-like or nodular pattern (Prog Neurol Surg 2012;25:55)
    • When leptomeningeal involvement is diagnostically challenging on CT, cytological analysis of cerebrospinal fluid becomes an important diagnostic tool (AJR Am J Roentgenol 1981;136:145)

Prognostic factors
  • Recent research indicates that survival rates for patients with brain metastases have improved but these rates can vary significantly depending on histopathologic and molecular features as well as diagnosis specific prognostic factors
  • Graded Prognostic Assessment (GPA), introduced in 2008, is a tool used to estimate survival, tailor treatments and stratify patients in clinical trials (J Clin Oncol 2020;38:3773)
Case reports
  • 47 year old woman with a known case of nasopharyngeal carcinoma with T5 / T11 metastases after 16 months (Medicine (Baltimore) 2017;96:e9175)
  • 50 year old woman, previously diagnosed with malignant spindle cell melanoma, presented with left sided hemiparesis and slurred speech after a year (Oncol Lett 2017;14:1275)
  • 50 year old woman with a history of bowel resection secondary to a gastrointestinal neuroendocrine tumor presented 18 months later with a 4 month history of left sided weakness and tremors (Cureus 2024;16:e70615)
  • 64 year old man with leptomeningeal melanomatosis secondary to shoulder melanoma (Hum Pathol 2003;34:625)
  • 68 year old man with altered mental status and gait disturbance; MRI and CT revealed advanced pancreatic cancer with brain and para-aortic lymph node metastases (Asian J Neurosurg 2015;10:35)
  • 77 year old woman with obstructive hydrocephalus due to gallbladder carcinoma (Arch Pathol Lab Med 2001;125:1120)
  • 79 year old man with a known case of prostatic adenocarcinoma, status postradiotherapy, presented with raising PSA after 9 years along with a frontal lesion (Curr Oncol 2015;22:e493)
  • Middle aged woman, in a known case of lung carcinoma, presented with a single cerebellar mass (Case #425)
Treatment
  • Existing treatment approach for brain metastases includes a combination of surgery, radiotherapy and systemic medical regimens; the primary goal is to maximize both quality of life and overall survival
    • Medical treatment
      • Systemic chemotherapies, molecular therapy and immunotherapy target the tumor, with aim to shrink size, slow growth and prevent symptoms related to mass effect
      • In EGFR mutant or ALK positive NSCLC, EGFR and ALK tyrosine kinase inhibitors have shown good CNS penetration (CNS response rates are ~40 - 80%)
      • Targeted therapy trials for breast cancer metastases have focused on HER2 positive lesions; trastuzumab (anti-HER2 monoclonal antibody) is commonly used but has shown limited BBB penetration
      • Use of BRAF inhibitors (vemurafenib and dabrafenib) as single agents has shown response rates ranging from 20% to 38% in brain metastases from BRAF mutant melanoma
      • Development of immunomodulators, specifically immune checkpoint inhibitors that target surface proteins like CTLA4 (ipilimumab), PD1 (pembrolizumab and nivolumab) and antiPDL1 (atezolizumab, MEDI4736), has been useful in cases of brain metastases from patients with lung cancer (NSCLC and SCLC) and melanoma (Nat Rev Dis Primers 2019;5:5)
    • Neurosurgery
      • Surgical resection is essential to obtain tissue diagnosis, relieve cereberal pressure and reduce mass effect and vasogenic oedema
      • For patients with a KPS score > 70, stable systemic disease and a solitary, accessible lesion, surgical resection is beneficial
      • Stereotactic laser ablation or laser interstitial thermal therapy (LITT) is used for deep seated lesions and tumors that have recurred after stereotactic radiosurgery (SRS) (Nat Rev Dis Primers 2019;5:5)
    • Radiotherapy
      • Recent advancements have broadened the use of SRS for patients with a larger number of brain metastases
      • Hypofractionated SRS, comprising 3 - 5 treatments, is increasingly used for brain metastases located near radiation sensitive structures, such as the optic nerves and chiasm as well as the brainstem (Nat Rev Dis Primers 2019;5:5)
      • SRS has significantly reduced the neurocognitive side effects associated with whole brain radiotherapy

Gross description
  • Sharply demarcated, spherical masses that rarely infiltrate the surrounding brain parenchyma
    • Central necrosis and hemorrhage are commonly seen in larger tumors and in metastases from melanoma, choriocarcinoma and clear cell RCC
    • Metastatic lung and breast carcinomas may appear cystic
    • Metastatic lesions might exhibit characteristic features of primary tumors, such as the glistening mucoid appearance of mucinous adenocarcinoma or the gray to black pigmentation seen in melanoma
  • Leptomeningeal carcinomatosis can be observed as multiple small nodules or with opacification of the leptomeninges at the cranial and spinal nerve roots and base of the brain

Microscopic (histologic) description
  • There is usually sharp demarcation from adjacent brain parenchyma and the histological features are similar to the primary tumor
  • Adjacent glial tissue may show reactive astrocytosis, microglial proliferation, necrosis and vascular proliferation; these features may sometimes lead to misinterpretation as glioblastoma, especially in small biopsy samples (Surg Neurol Int 2013;4:S245)
    • Meningeal carcinomatosis
      • Neoplastic cells are observed within the subarachnoid space, along the perivascular spaces
    • Malignant melanoma (Surg Neurol Int 2013;4:S245)
      • Epithelioid cells contain abundant pink cytoplasm, large nuclei and prominent cherry red nucleoli; frequent mitotic figures and scattered melanophages are noted
      • Tumor may display spindle cells, mimicking sarcoma
    • Sarcoma
      • Variable histological features, essentially associated with primary tumor's origin; these can range from bundles of spindle cells with cigar shaped, elongated nuclei in leiomyosarcoma to nests of large polygonal cells with granular eosinophilic cytoplasm and uniform round nuclei and prominent nucleoli in alveolar soft part sarcoma (Surg Neurol Int 2013;4:S245)
    • Carcinoma
      • Carcinomas form the majority of CNS metastases
      • Neoplastic cells are typically cohesive, polygonal, round, cuboidal or columnar and are arranged in sheets, tubules, trabeculae, nests, papillae or acinar structures with moderate to abundant cytoplasm
      • Squamous tumors are composed of flattened cells with dense pink cytoplasm, exhibiting intercellular bridges and keratin pearls, diagnostic features of squamous differentiation
      • Small cell carcinoma exhibits primitive epithelial and neuroendocrine differentiation, composed of small cells with scant eosinophilic cytoplasm, oval to spindle shaped nuclei with dark, smudgy nuclear chromatin and inconspicuous nucleoli; mitotic figures and necrosis are common
      • Choriocarcinoma comprises mononucleated epithelioid cells that resemble cytotrophoblast, surrounded by larger multinucleated syncytiotrophoblasts (Surg Neurol Int 2013;4:S245)

Cytology description
  • Neoplastic cells metastasized to subarachnoid space and other CSF pathways can be examined by cytology
  • Common methods of CSF dissemination include hematogenous spread, direct tumor extension or by migration along perineural and perivascular spaces (Fluids Barriers CNS 2011;8:14)
    • Modified Romanovsky stains, such as Diff-Quik, are used on cytospin preparations
    • Some tumors may show discohesive pattern of spread, resulting in isolated cell clusters or individual cells
    • Immunocytochemistry or flow cytometry can also be used for identification (Surg Neurol Int 2013;4:S245)
  • Best practice techniques for optimal results and better detection include obtaining at least 10.5 mL of CSF, performing a second collection if the first is negative, collecting CSF as close to the tumor as possible (if guided by neuroimaging) and ensuring quick processing (Fluids Barriers CNS 2011;8:14)

Molecular / cytogenetics description
  • Epidermal growth factor receptor (EGFR) activating mutations are seen in up to 25% of non-small cell lung carcinomas, largely in adenocarcinomas; other mutations include ALK / ROS1 / RET translocations and MET amplification (Brain Tumor Pathol 2017;34:8)
  • FGFR1 amplifications are seen in squamous non-small cell lung carcinomas
  • BRAF / NRAS / KIT mutations can be detected in metastatic melanomas
  • HER2 amplification in breast cancers
  • KRAS / NRAS mutations in colorectal cancers (Brain Tumor Pathol 2017;34:8)

Sample pathology report
  • Frontal space occupying lesion, biopsy:
    • Metastatic adenocarcinoma, consistent with colorectal primary (see comment)
    • Comment: Histological sections reveal a moderately differentiated adenocarcinoma. Immunohistochemical stains show that these tumor cells are positive for CK AE1 / AE3, CK20, CDX2 and negative for CK7, SALL4. Reactive gliosis, necrosis and thrombosed vessels are also seen. Selected slides of previous colon biopsy have been reviewed and show similar histomorphological features; thus, the findings in this case are best in keeping with a metastatic adenocarcinoma from patient's known history of colorectal primary.
Differential diagnosis
  • Glioblastoma with epithelioid or rhabdoid differentiation:
    • Resembles metastatic carcinomas or melanomas
    • While negative stains for melanoma and carcinoma could suggest a possibility of glioma, positive expression of GFAP, Olig2 and SOX2 is helpful
  • Hemangioblastoma:
    • Most common differential diagnosis of metastatic RCC
    • Hemangioblastoma exhibits relatively foamy cytoplasm and dark nuclei with degenerative atypia
    • Metastatic RCC, clear cell type typically has clear / pale cytoplasm and vesicular nuclei with prominent nucleoli and may display high grade features, including mitoses and necrosis
    • RCCs are generally positive for EMA, CD10 and RCC, whereas hemangioblastomas are positive for inhibin A, D2-40, neuron specific enolase (NSE) and S100
  • Choroid plexus carcinoma:
    • Occurs mostly in infants; metastatic carcinoma is not usually a differential diagnosis in this age group
    • Rare examples of choroid plexus carcinoma have been reported in adults
    • Immunostains such as KIR7.1 and stanniocalcin 1 may be of aid in addition to GFAP, S100 and transthyretin
  • Anaplastic meningioma:
    • Dural and intraventricular metastases can sometimes be mistaken for anaplastic meningioma
    • Most cases of meningioma are positive for EMA but this immunostain is also expressed in almost all carcinomas; moreover, high grade meningioma may show positivity for various cytokeratins, which can also complicate the diagnostic process
    • PR positivity can aid in the diagnosis
  • Germ cell neoplasm:
    • Primary or metastatic neoplasms are commonly seen in children and young adults
    • Most primary CNS germ cell tumors are located in the midline, unlike metastatic germ cell tumors, which are predominantly seen in cerebral hemispheres
    • Knowledge of a prior or synchronous systemic neoplasm is essential

Board review style question #1


A 62 year old woman presented with complaints of headache and right temporal mass for 4 months. A brain biopsy, as seen in the image shown above, was reported as metastatic adenocarcinoma. Immunohistochemical profile showed CK7 and TTF1 positivity in tumor cells, while CK20, CDX2, GATA3 and PAX8 were negative. What is the most likely primary site of origin of the metastasis in this case?

  1. Breast
  2. Colon
  3. Lung
  4. Ovary
  5. Pancreas
Board review style answer #1
C. Lung adenocarcinoma is positive for CK7 and TTF1 immunostains and negative for CK20, CDX2, GATA3 and PAX8. Answer A is incorrect because breast carcinoma is positive for CK7 and GATA3 immunostains and negative for CK20, TTF1, CDX2 and PAX8.
