Definition / general
  • Papillary tumor of the pineal region (PTPR) is a neuroepithelial tumor composed of epithelial-like cells arranged in papillary and solid patterns, with immunopositivity for cytokeratins
  • CNS WHO grade 2 or 3 (definite grading criteria remain to be defined)
Essential features
  • Rare neuroepithelial tumor of the pineal region composed of epithelial-like cells arranged in papillary and solid patterns, with immunopositivity for cytokeratins
  • Corresponds to WHO grade 2 or 3 with no definite grading criteria (J Clin Neurosci 2011;18:1007)
  • Originally described by Jouvet et al. in 2003 (Am J Surg Pathol 2003;27:505)
  • Affects mainly adults in the third and fourth decades; rare in children (Brain Pathol 2016;26:199, Clin Neurol Neurosurg 2021;201:106404)
  • Variable biological behavior with common recurrences
ICD coding
  • ICD-O: 9395/3 - papillary tumor of the pineal region
  • ICD-11: 2A00.20 & XH3904 - tumors of the pineal gland or pineal region & papillary tumor of the pineal region
Epidemiology
  • Rare, ~200 cases have been reported (Curr Oncol 2022;29:7558)
  • Age: mainly adults, rare in children (range from 1 to 71 years, with a median of 35 years) (Neurochirurgie 2015;61:138, J Neuropathol Exp Neurol 2006;65:1004, J Neuropathol Exp Neurol 2014;73:747, Neuropathol Appl Neurobiol 2006;32:278, Am J Surg Pathol 2014;38:106, Clin Neurol Neurosurg 2021;201:106404)
  • Gender: no sex predilection, although some reports suggested a female preponderance (Curr Oncol 2022;29:7558, J Clin Neurosci 2011;18:1007)
Sites
  • Pineal region, which is a composite anatomical space comprising the pineal gland and surrounding structures (Cancers (Basel) 2022;14:3646)
Pathophysiology
  • Proposed to originate from specialized ependymocytes of the subcommissural organ (SCO), which is a part of the circumventricular organs (Neuropathol Appl Neurobiol 2006;32:278, J Neuropathol Exp Neurol 2006;65:1004, Am J Surg Pathol 2003;27:505)
  • This hypothesis is supported by the unique immunohistochemical, ultrastructural and molecular characteristics of PTPR, which could all be related to the predecessor cells (i.e., the specialized ependymocytes of the SCO) (J Neuropathol Exp Neurol 2006;65:675, Brain Pathol 2016;26:199, Am J Surg Pathol 2003;27:505)
Etiology
  • Unknown
Clinical features
  • Clinical picture is similar to other pineal parenchymal tumors
  • Signs and symptoms related to increased intracranial pressure due to aqueductal obstruction; the most common are headaches (79%) and visual disturbances (61%) (J Clin Neurosci 2011;18:1007, Ann Clin Lab Sci 2011;41:174)
  • Neuroophthalmological dysfunction due to compression of the dorsal midbrain (e.g., impairment of upward gaze [Parinaud syndrome]) (Curr Oncol 2022;29:7558)
  • Brainstem or cerebellar dysfunction
Diagnosis
  • Neuroimaging (AJNR Am J Neuroradiol 2008;29:187)
  • Biopsy
  • WHO essential diagnostic criteria
    • Papillary growth pattern with epithelial-like cells
    • Characteristic immunohistochemical staining pattern (e.g., positivity for cytokeratins, SPDEF, CD56)
    • Pineal region location
    • For unresolved cases: confirmatory DNA methylation profiling
Radiology description
  • Well circumscribed masses (J Comput Assist Tomogr 2008;32:279, Ann Clin Lab Sci 2011;41:174)
  • Mixed solid and cystic components (J Clin Neurosci 2011;18:1007)
  • Frequent aqueductal obstruction with hydrocephalus
  • Intrinsic T1 hyperintensity in some cases (AJNR Am J Neuroradiol 2008;29:187, J Comput Assist Tomogr 2008;32:279)
  • T2 hyperintense (Cancers (Basel) 2022;14:3646)
  • Heterogeneous postcontrast enhancement (J Clin Neurosci 2011;18:1007)
  • Proton MR spectroscopy demonstrated an extremely high myoinositol, which is drastically different from other pineal tumors; thus it could be used as a specific noninvasive diagnostic signature (Brain Sci 2022;12:802)
  • Contrast enhanced MRI of the entire craniospinal axis is recommended because local tumor recurrence and leptomeningeal tumor spread may occur (Cancers (Basel) 2022;14:3646)

Prognostic factors
  • Frequent local recurrences (50 - 64.5%) (J Neurooncol 2013;112:223, J Neuropathol Exp Neurol 2006;65:1004, J Clin Neurosci 2011;18:1007)
  • Spinal dissemination may rarely occur (J Clin Neurosci 2011;18:1007, J Neuropathol Exp Neurol 2006;65:1004)
  • Prognostic factors associated with a longer overall survival include gross total resection and younger patient age (J Neurooncol 2013;112:223)
  • Some reports suggested that increased mitotic and proliferative (as measured by Ki67 expression) activities are associated with worse prognosis; however, the relation remains uncertain and cut off points for prognosis are not determined (Am J Surg Pathol 2014;38:106, Ann Clin Lab Sci 2011;41:174)
Case reports
  • 15 month old boy with papillary tumor of pineal region (J Neurosurg Pediatr 2011;7:534)
  • 9 year old girl with a papillary tumor of pineal region presented with excessive appetite and weight gain (Surg Neurol Int 2022;13:488)
  • 29 year old man with a recurrent papillary tumor of pineal region misdiagnosed as pineocytoma (Asian J Neurosurg 2021;16:398)
  • 61 year old woman with papillary tumor of pineal region (Cureus 2023;15:e34725)
  • 62 year old man with late ventricular and spinal dissemination of papillary tumor of pineal region (Folia Neuropathol 2016;54:72)
Treatment
  • Gross total resection is the mainstay of treatment (Curr Oncol 2022;29:7558)
  • Adjuvant radiotherapy plays an important role in controlling subsequent tumor recurrence (Curr Oncol 2022;29:7558)
  • Role of chemotherapy remains unclear and different regimens have been reported (Neuro Oncol 2015;17:v37)
  • Targeted therapy with mTOR kinase inhibitors is reported to be effective in controlling recurrent tumors with chromosome 10 deletions, specifically with inactivating mutations in the PTEN tumor suppressor gene on chromosome 10, which is involved in the PI3K / AKT / mTOR signaling pathway (Neuro Oncol 2019;21:137, Curr Oncol 2022;29:7558)

Gross description
  • Well circumscribed, grossly indistinguishable from pineocytomas
Microscopic (histologic) description
  • Well demarcated epithelial-like tumor with variable proportions of loose papillary structures and more densely cellular solid areas (J Neuropathol Exp Neurol 2006;65:1004)
  • In papillary areas, the vascularized papillary cores are covered by layers of large eosinophilic cells, forming plump perivascular pseudorosettes resembling astroblastic rosettes rather than pseudorosettes of ependymoma
    • Tumor cells are cuboidal to columnar with well defined borders
    • They frequently detach from the vascular wall with the formation of an apparent clear perivascular space (Neuropathol Appl Neurobiol 2006;32:278, Am J Surg Pathol 2003;27:505, J Neuropathol Exp Neurol 2006;65:1004, Am J Surg Pathol 2012;36:916)
  • In solid areas, cells with clear or vacuolated cytoplasm may be seen
    • Ependymal-like differentiation (true rosettes, tubules or canals) is often observed (J Clin Neurosci 2011;18:1007, J Neuropathol Exp Neurol 2006;65:1004, Ann Clin Lab Sci 2011;41:174, Neuropathol Appl Neurobiol 2006;32:278)
  • Nuclei are round to oval with occasional nuclear membrane indentation
    • They are often basally located
    • Small nucleolus is frequently discernible and the chromatin pattern may be fine, stippled or clumped (J Clin Neurosci 2011;18:1007)
  • Occasional tumor cells with anisonucleosis, nuclear hyperchromasia and multinucleation may be detected (J Clin Neurosci 2011;18:1007, Am J Surg Pathol 2012;36:916)
  • Periodic acid-Schiff (PAS) positive, diastase sensitive glycogen granules and PAS positive, diastase resistant eosinophilic cytoplasmic inclusions are sometimes present (J Clin Neurosci 2011;18:1007)
  • Signet ring cells may be identified (J Clin Neurosci 2011;18:1007)
  • Vascular changes: hyalinization and unusual vessels with multiple lumina are frequently seen
    • Slight endothelial hyperplasia may be encountered but no vascular endothelial proliferation (Am J Surg Pathol 2012;36:916, J Clin Neurosci 2011;18:1007)
  • Necrosis is often present (Neuropathol Appl Neurobiol 2006;32:278, J Clin Neurosci 2011;18:1007)
  • Mitotic activity is moderate in most cases (ranges from absent to frequent) (J Clin Neurosci 2011;18:1007)
  • Biological behavior of PTPRs is variable
    • Although the majority correspond to CNS WHO grade 2, more aggressive cases may correspond to grade 3 but definite histological grading criteria remain to be defined

Cytology description
  • Hypercellular smears with papillary tissue fragments, small clusters of cells and single cells (Diagn Cytopathol 2016;44:1098, Pathology 2010;42:474)
  • Papillary fragments have inner vascular cores lined by several layers of cells with moderate amounts of pale, filmy cytoplasm with short and thick processes (Diagn Cytopathol 2016;44:1098, Pathology 2010;42:474, Cureus 2024;16:e61308, Lacruz: Central Nervous System Tumors - Diagnostic Pathology, 2023)
  • Nuclei are round or oval with occasional nuclear membrane irregularity
    • They are often eccentrically located within the cells, lending a plasmacytoid appearance
    • Inconspicuous nucleoli and finely dispersed chromatin are seen (Diagn Cytopathol 2016;44:1098, Pathology 2010;42:474)
  • Nuclear pleomorphism ranges from minimal to marked (Pathology 2010;42:474)
  • Single clear cytoplasmic vacuoles are frequent (Diagn Cytopathol 2016;44:1098)
  • No mitotic figures (Pathology 2010;42:474)
  • Background looks clear or striped (tigroid) but not fibrillary (Lacruz: Central Nervous System Tumors - Diagnostic Pathology, 2023, Diagn Cytopathol 2016;44:1098)

Positive stains
  • Cytokeratins (100%), including CAM5.2 (100%), AE1 / AE3 (67%), CK18 (95%) and KL1 (100%): cytokeratin positivity is the most distinctive immunohistochemical feature, especially CK18, which typically predominates in perivascular areas in papillary structures (Neuropathol Appl Neurobiol 2006;32:278, J Neuropathol Exp Neurol 2006;65:1004, Ann Clin Lab Sci 2011;41:174, J Clin Neurosci 2011;18:1007)
  • CD56 (100%) (J Clin Neurosci 2011;18:1007)
  • SPDEF (79%): has been proposed as a diagnostic marker (Brain Pathol 2016;26:199)
  • Vimentin (97%): particularly in paranuclear cytoplasm adjacent to vessel walls (J Neuropathol Exp Neurol 2006;65:1004, J Clin Neurosci 2011;18:1007)
  • S100 (78.6 - 95%) (Neuropathol Appl Neurobiol 2006;32:278, J Neuropathol Exp Neurol 2006;65:1004, J Clin Neurosci 2011;18:1007)
  • NSE (97 - 100%) (J Neuropathol Exp Neurol 2006;65:1004, J Clin Neurosci 2011;18:1007)
  • MAP2 (86.7 - 92%) (Neuropathol Appl Neurobiol 2006;32:278, J Neuropathol Exp Neurol 2006;65:1004)
  • Nestin (100%) (J Clin Neurosci 2011;18:1007)
  • EMA (6.7 - 80.6%): variable staining intensity in a small dot or ring-like distribution, similar to ependymomas; also in a membranous or cell surface pattern (Neuropathol Appl Neurobiol 2006;32:278, J Neuropathol Exp Neurol 2006;65:1004, J Clin Neurosci 2011;18:1007)
  • Transthyretin (58%) (J Neuropathol Exp Neurol 2006;65:1004, J Clin Neurosci 2011;18:1007)
  • FOXJ1 (100%) (Am J Surg Pathol 2017;41:1410)
  • Ki67 is variable (range from 0 to > 10%); high proliferative activity has been linked to younger patient age (J Neuropathol Exp Neurol 2006;65:1004, J Clin Neurosci 2011;18:1007)
Negative stains
  • GFAP: often negative (focal or low immunoreactivity in 12 - 17%) (Neuropathol Appl Neurobiol 2006;32:278, J Neuropathol Exp Neurol 2006;65:1004, Ann Clin Lab Sci 2011;41:174, J Clin Neurosci 2011;18:1007)
  • Synaptophysin: some tumors (45%) may show focal or low immunoreactivity (J Clin Neurosci 2011;18:1007, J Neuropathol Exp Neurol 2006;65:1004, Neuropathol Appl Neurobiol 2006;32:278)
  • Chromogranin A: some tumors (43%) may show focal or low immunoreactivity (J Clin Neurosci 2011;18:1007, J Neuropathol Exp Neurol 2006;65:1004, Neuropathol Appl Neurobiol 2006;32:278)
  • NeuN: some tumors (25%) may be positive (J Clin Neurosci 2011;18:1007)
  • NFP: usually negative, rarely positive (weak focal in 4%) (J Clin Neurosci 2011;18:1007, J Neuropathol Exp Neurol 2006;65:1004)
  • E-cadherin (J Clin Neurosci 2011;18:1007, J Neuropathol Exp Neurol 2006;65:1004)
  • Claudin2 (Am J Surg Pathol 2012;36:916)
  • Kir7.1: some tumors (20%) may show focal or weak immunoreactivity (Neuropathol Appl Neurobiol 2006;32:278, J Neuropathol Exp Neurol 2006;65:1004, J Clin Neurosci 2011;18:1007)
  • Stanniocalcin 1: some tumors (27%) may show focal or weak immunoreactivity (J Clin Neurosci 2011;18:1007)
  • CK7: some tumors (33%) may be positive (J Clin Neurosci 2011;18:1007)
  • CK20: some tumors (14%) may be positive (J Neuropathol Exp Neurol 2006;65:1004, J Clin Neurosci 2011;18:1007)
  • CK5/6: some tumors (14%) may be positive (J Neuropathol Exp Neurol 2006;65:1004, J Clin Neurosci 2011;18:1007)
  • BRAF V600E: these midline papillary tumors do not harbor the BRAF V600E mutation (Neurosurgery 2015;77:621)
Electron microscopy description
  • Ultrastructurally, it possesses neuroendocrine, secretory and ependymal organelles that likely originate from the subcommissural organ (SCO) (Ann Clin Lab Sci 2011;41:174, J Clin Neurosci 2011;18:1007)
  • Cohesive cells with epithelial features (Pathology 2010;42:474)
  • Tight junctions and zonulae adherentes form junctional complexes
    • Many interdigitated ependymal-like processes at the basal pole are surrounded by basement membrane
    • Microvilli are readily identified at the apical pole but cilia are rare (J Clin Neurosci 2011;18:1007, Pathology 2010;42:474, J Clin Neurosci 2011;18:1007, Indian J Med Paediatr Oncol 2020;41:412, Pathology 2010;42:474)
  • Slightly irregular and peripherally located nuclei, small nucleoli and delicate heterochromatin (Pathology 2010;42:474, Egypt J Neurol Psychiatry Neurosurg 2020;56:55)
  • Cytoplasm contains abundant rough endoplasmic reticulum with distended cisternae filled with secretory product and perinuclear intermediate filaments as well as varying proportions of small Golgi complexes and mitochondria
    • Occasional lysosomes and scant dense core vesicles may be detected (Am J Surg Pathol 2003;27:505, Pathology 2010;42:474, Egypt J Neurol Psychiatry Neurosurg 2020;56:55)

Molecular / cytogenetics description
  • PTPR shows typical chromosomal alterations including losses of chromosome 10, 3 and 22q as well as gains of chromosomes 4, 5, 8p, 9 and 12 (Brain Pathol 2016;26:199, Neuropathol Appl Neurobiol 2006;32:278)
  • Chromosome 10 loss in PTPR is linked to PTEN mutations and activation of the phosphoinositide 3 kinase / Akt / mTOR signaling pathway (J Neuropathol Exp Neurol 2014;73:747, Curr Oncol 2022;29:7558)
  • DNA methylation profiling is distinct and clearly distinguishes PTPR from ependymomas and pineal parenchymal tumors
    • 2 methylation subgroups are defined: PTPR A and PTPR B
      • Further methylation subclasses of PTPR B have been proposed (Acta Neuropathol Commun 2024;12:117)
      • More aggressive clinical course is suggested for PTPR group B; however, the prognostic significance of these molecular subgroups needs further evaluation (Curr Oncol 2022;29:7558, Brain Pathol 2016;26:199)
  • PTPR shows high expression of genes such as zinc finger homeodomain transcription factor (ZFH4), RFX3, TTR mRNA, CGRP and SPDEF, which encode proteins reported to be expressed in SCO; this further supports the putative origin of PTPR from SCO (Brain Pathol 2016;26:199, J Clin Neurosci 2011;18:1007)

Case 6: papillary tumor of the pineal region

Sample pathology report
  • Pineal region mass lesion, gross total resection:
    • Papillary tumor of the pineal region
    • CNS WHO grade 2 or 3 (definite grading criteria are not well defined)
    • Molecular genetics: DNA methylation profiling
Differential diagnosis
  • Normal pineal:
    • Lobular architecture, less epithelial morphology, no mitoses
    • Calcifications (brain sand)
    • Synaptophysin diffusely+
    • CAM5.2-
    • Very low Ki67 index
  • Metastatic adenocarcinoma:
    • Generally older patients
    • More anaplastic
    • Clinical history and imaging workup of the patient (J Clin Neurosci 2011;18:1007)
    • Organ specific immunohistochemical markers (e.g., TTF1 and GATA3)
    • Often express CK7 and / or CK20, which are absent or very low in PTPR (J Neuropathol Exp Neurol 2006;65:1004, J Clin Neurosci 2011;18:1007)
    • EMA+
    • Often vimentin-, S100-, nestin- and NSE- (J Clin Neurosci 2011;18:1007, J Neuropathol Exp Neurol 2006;65:1004)
  • Choroid plexus tumors:
    • Overt epithelial morphology and conspicuous basement membrane
    • E-cadherin+ (J Clin Neurosci 2011;18:1007, J Neuropathol Exp Neurol 2006;65:1004)
    • CD56- or only weakly positive (J Clin Neurosci 2011;18:1007, J Neuropathol Exp Neurol 2006;65:1004)
    • MAP2 rarely positive (18.9%) (Neuropathol Appl Neurobiol 2006;32:278, J Neuropathol Exp Neurol 2006;65:1004, Neuropathol Appl Neurobiol 2006;32:278)
    • Kir7.1+ (Neuropathol Appl Neurobiol 2006;32:278, J Neuropathol Exp Neurol 2006;65:1004, Am J Surg Pathol 2006;30:66)
    • Stanniocalcin 1+
    • Synaptophysin+ in most cases
  • Ependymomas:
    • GFAP+ especially in fibrillary perivascular pseudorosettes (Neuropathol Appl Neurobiol 2006;32:278)
    • CK18- and CAM5.2- (Neuropathol Appl Neurobiol 2006;32:278, J Clin Neurosci 2011;18:1007)
    • CK AE1 / AE3 may be positive due to cross reactivity with GFAP (J Clin Neurosci 2011;18:1007)
    • MAP2- (Acta Neuropathol 2003;106:385, Acta Neuropathol 2004;108:89)
  • Astroblastoma:
    • Often, strong and diffuse GFAP immunoreactivity
    • MN1 alteration
    • Specific DNA methylation profile
  • Pineal parenchymal tumors:
    • No epithelial differentiation
    • Synaptophysin diffusely and strongly+ (Am J Surg Pathol 2017;41:1410, J Clin Neurosci 2011;18:1007)
    • Diffuse nuclear positivity for CRX (Am J Surg Pathol 2017;41:1410)
    • FOXJ1- (Am J Surg Pathol 2017;41:1410)
  • Papillary meningiomas:
    • Limited CK expression (J Clin Neurosci 2011;18:1007)
    • Alterations of PBRM1
  • Germ cell tumors:
    • Distinctive morphology of different subtypes
    • Immunoreactivity for germ cell markers (e.g., SALL4) (J Clin Neurosci 2011;18:1007)
Additional references
  • WHO Classification of Tumours Editorial Board: Central Nervous System Tumours, 5th Edition, 2022, Kleinschmidt-DeMasters: Diagnostic Pathology - Neuropathology, 3rd Edition, 2022
Board review style question #1


A 34 year old woman presented with headache and visual disturbances. MRI showed a pineal region heterogeneously enhancing mass lesion with solid and cystic components. Gross total resection of the mass was done and the histopathological features of the lesion are shown in the figure above. Immunohistochemistry showed positive reaction for CK18, SPDEF, vimentin and CD56 and negative reaction for EMA, synaptophysin and E-cadherin. Which of the following is the most likely diagnosis?

  1. Choroid plexus papilloma
  2. Metastatic papillary adenocarcinoma
  3. Papillary tumor of the pineal region
  4. Pineocytoma
Board review style answer #1
C. Papillary tumor of the pineal region. Papillary tumor of the pineal region is characterized by pineal region location, papillary growth pattern with epithelial-like cells and typical immunohistochemical staining for cytokeratins, SPDEF and CD56. Answer A is incorrect because choroid plexus papilloma is negative for CD56 and positive for E-cadherin. Answer B is incorrect because metastatic adenocarcinoma is positive for EMA. Answer D is incorrect because pineocytoma is diffusely positive for synaptophysin.

Board review style question #2
Which of the following is typical of papillary tumor of the pineal region (PTPR)?

  1. Frequent local recurrences
  2. Immunonegative for cytokeratins
  3. Pineal gland is the proposed histogenetic origin
  4. Predominates in children
Board review style answer #2
A. Frequent local recurrences. PTPR is characterized by frequent local recurrences. Answer B is incorrect because PTPR is typically immunoreactive for cytokeratins. Answer C is incorrect because specialized ependymocytes of the subcommissural organ are the proposed origin of PTPR. Answer D is incorrect because PTPR affects mainly adults and rarely occurs in children.
