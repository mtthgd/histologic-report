Definition / general

* Hemostasis is a highly complex process of clot formation in response to blood vessel injury ([Indian J Anaesth 2014;58:515](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/25535411))
* Disorders in the hemostatic pathway can lead to either hypercoagulability (in which excessive clot formation occurs) or bleeding disorders (in which clot formation does not adequately stop bleeding)

Essential features

* Hemostasis can be categorized into 3 stages ([Curr Med Chem 2004;11:2245](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/15379710)):
  + Primary hemostasis is the formation of a weak platelet plug at the site of vascular injury
  + Secondary hemostasis is the formation of a fibrin plug by coagulation factors; coagulation factor activation occurs via the intrinsic and extrinsic pathways, which merge to form the common pathway
  + Tertiary hemostasis describes the anticoagulation and fibrinolytic balance to promote healing and prevent excessive clotting
* The 3 major constituents of the hemostatic system are platelets, coagulation proteins and endothelium ([Cardiovasc Diagn Ther 2018;8:568](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/30498682))

Primary hemostasis

* During injury to vascular endothelium, the first response to prevent blood loss is vasoconstriction which activates endothelial cells ([Ann Med 2012;44:405](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/21585250))
* Formation of the platelet plug in primary hemostasis is primarily orchestrated by interactions between the endothelium, von Willebrand factor (vWF) and platelets ([Arterioscler Thromb Vasc Biol 2020;40:1441](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/32375545))
* Activated endothelial cells secrete vWF, which recruits platelets to the site of injury and results in a sequence of platelet adhesion, activation and aggregation ([Int J Biochem Cell Biol 2021;131:105900](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/33301925), [Hematol Oncol Clin North Am 2021;35:1069](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/34391603))
* Platelet adhesion occurs when vWF, bound to exposed subendothelial collagen, interacts with glycoprotein Ib / IX / V on platelets ([Arterioscler Thromb Vasc Biol 2008;28:403](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/18174460))
* vWF is a plasma glycoprotein, stored in alpha granules secreted by platelets and Weibel-Palade bodies in the endothelium ([Blood 2015;125:2019](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/25712991))
* Platelet activation results in changes in platelet and membrane morphology and secretion of microparticles from intracellular granules
* Upon activation, platelet cytoplasm and cytoskeleton rearrange to increase surface area and enable adherence to endothelium ([Thromb Haemost 2002;88:186](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/12195687))
* Platelet aggregation is mediated by glycoprotein IIb / IIIa (GpIIb / IIIa) on the platelet surface
* During platelet activation, GpIIb / IIIa undergoes a conformational change into the active form; activated GpIIb / IIIa has high affinity for ligand binding and promotes platelet aggregation into the initial platelet plug ([Blood 2007;109:5087](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/17311994))

Secondary hemostasis

* In secondary hemostasis, the fibrin plug is formed by the activated coagulation factors ([Ann Med 2012;44:405](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/21585250))
  + Fibrinogen is cleaved into fibrin by factor II (thrombin) at the end of the common pathway ([Arterioscler Thromb Vasc Biol 2011;31:494](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/21325671))
  + Insoluble fibrin is crosslinked by factor XIII, which strengthens the fibrin plug
* Coagulation factors are synthesized in the liver, except factor VIII and vWF ([Blood Coagul Fibrinolysis 2000;11:S69](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/10850568))
* Coagulation proteins circulate as inactive zymogens; factors must undergo enzymatic cleavage to function ([Hemodial Int 2006;10:S2](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/17022746))
* Factors II, VII, IX and X and protein C and S are synthesized in a vitamin K dependent manner; vitamin K epoxide reductase is a key enzyme in the vitamin K cycle ([Vitam Horm 2008;78:23](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/18374188))
  + Warfarin is an inhibitor of vitamin K epoxide reductase and suppresses the synthesis of active factors II, VII, IX and X and protein C and S
  + Protein C and S are anticoagulant proteins that degrade factor Va and VIIIa ([Adv Clin Exp Med 2013;22:459](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/23986205))
* The model of secondary hemostasis is comprised of 2 separate pathways, the intrinsic and extrinsic pathway, that merge to form a common pathway; in vivo, it appears that these pathways may not be so distinctly separated

Intrinsic pathway

* Intrinsic pathway is comprised of factors VIII, IX, XI, XII, prekallikrein and high molecular weight kininogen ([Indian J Anaesth 2014;58:515](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/25535411))
* The pathway is initiated when factor XII (Hageman factor) is activated by exposure to negatively charged molecules such as inorganic polyphosphate released by platelets ([Cell 2009;139:1143](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/20005807))
* Factor XIIa initiates a chain of activation involving factor XI, IX, VIII and ultimately factor X, initiating the common pathway with the activation of thrombin (factor II) ([Arterioscler Thromb Vasc Biol 2019;39:331](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/30700128))
  + Factor VIIIa increases the ability of factor IXa to activate factor X ([Thromb Res 2007;119:1](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/16487577))
  + Hemophilia A, B and C are inherited deficiencies of factor VIII, IX and XI, respectively; [hemophilia A](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/coagulationfactorVIIIdef.html) and [B](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/coagulationfactorIXdef.html) are X linked recessive disorders ([Acta Neuropathol Commun 2021;9:111](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/34158126))
* Factor XIIa additionally converts prekallikrein to kallikrein; the functions of kallikrein include converting prorenin to renin, kininogen to bradykinin and further activating factor XII ([J Clin Invest 2002;109:1007](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/11956236))
* The function of the intrinsic and common pathways of coagulation is measured with activated partial thromboplastin time (aPTT); aPTT measures the time it takes plasma to form a clot when exposed to factor XII activator ([Methods Mol Biol 2013;992:111](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/23546708))
  + Prolonged aPTT can be indicative of a factor deficiency, such as hemophilia or an inhibitor to the intrinsic or common pathway; an aPTT mixing study is used to differentiate between these causes ([Eur J Haematol 2020;104:519](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/32049377))

Extrinsic pathway

* Extrinsic pathway is comprised of tissue factor and factor VII ([Indian J Anaesth 2014;58:515](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/25535411))
* Tissue factor (TF) is a glycosylated protein expressed on subendothelial tissues; TF is activated in response to vascular wall damage ([Curr Pharm Des 2015;21:1152](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/25312727))
* Activated TF binds to and activates factor VII
* Factor VIIa converts factor X into factor Xa, initiating the common pathway
* Clinically, the extrinsic and common coagulation pathways are assessed by prothrombin time (PT); PT is a measurement of the time it takes for clot formation when plasma is exposed to tissue factor ([StatPearls: Prothrombin Time [Accessed 15 June 2022]](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/31334989))
  + Prolonged PT can reveal a clotting factor deficiency or inhibitor to the extrinsic or common pathway; a PT mixing study can differentiate between these causes ([Mayo Clin Proc 2007;82:864](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/17605969))
  + International normalized ratio (INR) is the ratio between a patient's PT and a standardized, control PT value ([StatPearls: Prothrombin Time [Accessed 15 June 2022]](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/31334989))

Common pathway

* Both the intrinsic and extrinsic pathways of coagulation lead to the activation of factor X
* Common pathway involves fibrinogen (factor I), factors II, V and X
* Prothrombinase complex is formed by factor Xa, factor V and calcium; this complex converts prothrombin (factor II) to thrombin (factor IIa) ([Indian J Anaesth 2014;58:515](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/25535411))
* Thrombin converts fibrinogen to fibrin, further activates factors V and VIII and cleaves factor XIII ([Blood 2005;106:2605](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/15994286))
* Factor XIII further stabilizes the clot by covalently crosslinking fibrin ([Blood 2005;106:2605](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/15994286))
* Antithrombin is a protease inhibitor that inhibits activated coagulation factors, primarily thrombin and Xa ([J Thromb Haemost 2020;18:3142](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/32780936))
  + Heparin inhibits thrombin generation by increasing antithrombin activity ([Prog Mol Biol Transl Sci 2019;163:1](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/31030744))

Tertiary hemostasis

* Tertiary hemostasis is the process of fibrinolysis (clot dissolution), in which fibrin is cleaved to prevent excessive clotting
* Tissue type plasminogen activator (tPA) is produced by endothelial cells to mediate clot breakdown; urokinase type plasminogen activator (uPA) also functions in fibrinolysis ([Front Immunol 2019;10:1348](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/31258531))
* In the presence of fibrin, tPA and uPA cleave plasminogen to plasmin ([J Thromb Haemost 2007;5:804](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/17408411))
* Plasmin is a serine protease that degrades fibrin clot to soluble fibrin and fibrinogen degradation products ([Int J Mol Sci 2021;22:2758](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/33803235))
  + D dimer is a soluble fibrin degradation product that is used as an important clinical marker for coagulation and fibrinolysis ([Am J Hematol 2019;94:833](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/30945756))
* Fibrinolysis inhibitors, such as antiplasmin (which binds and inactivates fibrin) and plasminogen activator inhibitor 1 (PAI1; inhibitor of uPA / tPA), prevent excessive fibrinolysis ([FEBS J 2005;272:4852](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/16176259), [Transfus Apher Sci 2019;58:572](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/31427261))
