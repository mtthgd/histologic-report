Definition / general
  • Chronic granulomatous disease (CGD) is a rare primary immunodeficiency of childhood that affects phagocyte function due to defective genes that encode the subunits of nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide phosphate (NADPH) oxidase complex
  • It generally presents with severe recurrent bacterial and fungal infections as well as granuloma formation
Essential features
  • Rare inherited disorder characterized by defective phagocyte function due to mutations in genes encoding components of the NADPH oxidase enzyme complex
  • Clinical presentation includes recurrent bacterial and fungal infections along with granuloma formation within various organs
  • Colonoscopy may show brownish dots over a yellowish edematous mucosa, so called leopard sign
  • Microscopic features of CGD colitis include microgranulomas, pigmented macrophages, tissue eosinophilia and active / chronic colitis
  • Definitive treatment could be achieved by stem cell transplantation
Terminology
  • Fatal granulomatous disease of childhood was initially used to describe the disease in the 1950s (AMA J Dis Child 1959;97:387)
ICD coding
  • ICD-10: D71 - functional disorders of polymorphonuclear neutrophils
  • ICD-11: 4A00.0Y - other specified functional neutrophil defects
Epidemiology
  • ~1/200,000 of live births in the United States (J Pediatric Infect Dis Soc 2018;7:S2)
  • Higher incidence in Arab populations, including Arab citizens of Israel (1.49 per 100,000 live births) (Am J Hematol 2017;92:28)
  • Usually diagnosed within the first 1 - 3 years of life
  • M > F
Sites
  • Any organ could be affected by infections and granulomatous inflammation
    • Colon (most common), lungs, lymph nodes, liver, skin and bones
  • Any part of the gastrointestinal tract from mouth to anus
    • Inflammatory complications (stricture, fissure, fistula, obstruction)
    • Perirectal disease is especially common
  • Reference: Clin Gastroenterol Hepatol 2016;14:395
Pathophysiology
  • NADPH oxidase enzyme complex is composed of membranous and cytoplasmic subunits; its function is to generate reactive oxygen species (ROS) in activated phagocytes
  • ROS dysfunction leads to inefficient autophagy, proinflammatory phenotype with increased secretion of proinflammatory cytokines (TNFα, IL1β, IL6) and autoimmunity, which contributes to the hyperinflammation associated with CGD
  • Reference: Clin Rev Allergy Immunol 2021;61:101
Etiology
  • X linked recessive inheritance (65%): mutation on CYBB gene (Xp21.1)
  • Autosomal recessive inheritance (35%): mutations on CYBB (16q24), NCF1 (7q11.23), NCF2 (1q25), NCF4 (22q13.1), CYBC1 (17q25.3) (Clin Rev Allergy Immunol 2021;61:101)
Clinical features
  • Typically presents early in life with life threatening infections, including catalase positive microorganisms Staphylococcus aureus, Burkholderia cepecia complex, Serratia marcescens, Nocardia species, Aspergillus species) and Mycobacterium tuberculosis (Br Med Bull 2016;118:50)
  • Most common cause of mortality is invasive fungal infections (especially Aspergillus)
  • Granulomatous inflammation is seen in multiple organs, including the lungs, brain, gastrointestinal and genitourinary tract
  • Half of patients present with gastrointestinal symptoms consistent with inflammatory bowel disease (abdominal pain, diarrhea, bloody stool, tenesmus, abdominal distention), which could be mixed with Crohn's disease; gastric outlet obstruction and esophageal obstruction could be seen
  • Liver involvement includes liver abscesses, nodular regenerative hyperplasia, noncirrhotic portal hypertension and hepatosplenomegaly (J Allergy Clin Immunol Pract 2023;11:1401)
  • Skin manifestations include granulomas, infections and a lupus-like syndrome
  • In X linked recessive patients, deletion could involve adjacent genes causing contiguous X chromosome gene deletion syndromes while also presenting with McLeod syndrome, Duchenne muscular dystrophy or X linked retinitis pigmentosa (Front Genet 2023;13:970204)
Diagnosis
  • In a newborn, various symptoms (such as deep seated infections with bacteria or fungi, recurrent pneumonia, chronic inflammatory manifestations [colitis, liver abscess, fistula formation, failure to thrive, etc.]) trigger the workup for CGD (J Allergy Clin Immunol Pract 2019;7:1763)
  • Historically, the nitro blue tetrazolium test (NBT) has been used to measure superoxide generation in phagocytes indirectly
  • More recently, flow cytometry with fluorescent probes (2'7'-dichlorofluorescein diacetate [DCF], dihydrorhodamine-123 [DHR-123]) are used to measure oxidase activation
  • Abnormal results are suggested to be confirmed with genetic testing
Laboratory
  • Due to prolonged and occult infections, erythrocyte sedimentation rate and C reactive protein are usually increased
  • Anemia, thrombocytosis, hypergammaglobulinemia and hypoalbuminemia could be seen due to the chronic inflammation
  • Fecal calprotectin is increased (representing gastrointestinal inflammation) and has a high negative predictive value
  • Reference: J Clin Immunol 2017;37:741
Radiology description
  • Various radiologic imaging methods (Xray, computed tomography [CT], magnetic resonance imaging [MRI], positron emission tomography [PET] scan) could be used to detect and follow up on infections
  • MRI has been shown to accurately detect colitis activity, when compared with endoscopic imaging (J Clin Immunol 2019;39:494)

Prognostic factors
  • Overall survival has increased up to 90% with modern treatment options, with the majority of patients reaching adulthood
  • X linked recessive patients present a more severe disease course at an earlier onset compared to autosomal recessive ones
  • Female carriers of X linked disease usually have a better course without significant infections
  • Residual ROS production by neutrophils is found to be more predictive of survival rather than the specific NADPH oxidase mutation
  • References: Pediatr Allergy Immunol 2016;27:242, N Engl J Med 2010;363:2600
Case reports
  • Newborn boy with extensive skin lesions and lymphadenopathy (Front Immunol 2021;12:663883)
  • 7 year old boy with recurrent episodes of inflammatory colitis (Front Pediatr 2023;10:964025)
  • 15 year old girl with long term history of vulvar lichen planus and suspected Crohn's disease (GE Port J Gastroenterol 2020;27:119)
Treatment
  • Antibacterial / antifungal agents (trimethoprim / sulfamethoxazole and itraconazole) and immunomodulatory agents (IFNγ) are used in a usually prolonged course in acute infections and as lifelong prophylaxis
  • For the granulomatous inflammation in different organs and its complications, corticosteroids are found useful
  • Use of immunomodulatory agents (such as TNFα antagonist infliximab, IL1β receptor antagonist anakinra and mTORC1 inhibitor rapamycin) are controversial in CGD patients since they could cause mortal infections
  • PPAR gamma agonist, originally approved as an antidiabetic agent, is shown to restore the microbicidal capacity of phagocytes with clinical benefit in CGD patients
  • Treatment of liver abscesses is challenging and often requires surgical intervention
  • Cure could be achieved by allogeneic hematopoietic stem cell transplantation, although high risk patients are more prone to complications related to the transplant
  • Gene therapy is a promising ongoing effort with various challenges (mutagenic risk, need for more targeted gene editing, high cost) to overcome
  • Reference: Pediatr Allergy Immunol 2016;27:242

Microscopic (histologic) description
  • Acute colitis
    • Cryptitis, crypt abscesses, ulceration
  • Chronic colitis
    • Lymphoplasmacytic infiltrate, basal cell plasmacytosis, Paneth cell metaplasia and crypt distortion (Am J Surg Pathol 2013;37:1365)
  • Increased number of eosinophils or eosinophilic microabscesses
  • Granulomas are frequent (50%)
    • Mostly as microgranulomas composed of 5 - 15 epithelioid histiocytes
    • Poorly or well formed
    • With or without Langhans or foreign body type giant cells
    • No necrosis
    • Seen in both superficial and deep mucosa as well as submucosa
  • Enlarged, pigmented macrophages (Am J Surg Pathol 2013;37:1365)
    • 50 - 100 micrometers
    • Seen in the majority of colon and small biopsies
    • Not described in the upper gastrointestinal tract
    • Seen in superficial and deep mucosa, submucosa, muscularis propria and around Auerbach plexus
    • Cytoplasmic, golden brown, granular or eosinophilic crystalline and refractile under the light microscope
  • Small bowel biopsies could show similar acute and chronic inflammation features, including villous shortening, although less common compared to the colon
  • Biopsies from the stomach could harbor acute and chronic gastritis (focal or diffuse), increased number of eosinophils and granuloma formations
  • Esophagus is the least commonly involved part of the gastrointestinal (GI) tract; acute esophagitis or increased intraepithelial lymphocytes have been described
  • No dysplasia has been described in gastrointestinal biopsies of CGD patients
  • Liver biopsies show clusters of portal pigmented macrophages as well as portal plasma cells and lymphocytes, parenchymal granulomas and mild to moderate fibrosis
  • Discoid or subacute lupus erythematosus-like lesions are described in skin biopsies of female X linked carriers

Molecular / cytogenetics description
  • Described mutations in CGD patients include small intragenic deletions or insertions, missense, nonsense and splice mutations
  • Array comparative genomic hybridization and multiplex ligation dependent probe amplification methods are suggested to detect copy number variations
  • Genetic counseling is warranted for mothers and sisters of affected male patients
  • Reference: Pediatr Allergy Immunol 2016;27:242

Sample pathology report
  • Ascending colon, biopsy:
    • Active colitis, nonnecrotizing microgranuloma formation and pigmented histiocytes (see comment)
    • Special stains for mycobacterial and fungal microorganisms are negative
    • Comment: Observed histopathologic changes are consistent with chronic granulomatous disease associated colitis in the setting of the patient's known history of chronic granulomatous disease.

  • Ascending colon, biopsy:

    • Comment: Observed histopathologic changes could be consistent with colitis associated with chronic granulomatous disease in the appropriate clinical setting and supporting laboratory / molecular features. Differential diagnosis includes infections, Crohn's disease and sarcoidosis.
Differential diagnosis
  • Crohn's disease:
    • Clinical presentation and histology could overlap
    • Detection of defective neutrophil function and specific mutations in CGD would help
  • Sarcoidosis:
    • Gastrointestinal symptoms and histology could overlap
    • Pigmented macrophages are more specific for CGD
    • Sarcoidosis granulomas are typically larger and well formed and may coalesce
    • Detection of defective neutrophil function and specific mutations in CGD would help
  • Melanosis coli:
    • Pigmented macrophages are in the superficial layers of the GI tract, without accompanying acute / chronic inflammation and granulomas
  • Granulomatous infections:
    • Special stains would help detect possible microorganisms
    • Microbiology correlation would be confirmatory
Board review style question #1


A 6 year old boy presented with recurrent episodes of fever, diarrhea and abdominal pain. The nitroblue tetrazolium test (NBT) did not show increased NBT reduction. Genetic analysis revealed a hemizygous pathogenic variant on CYBB. The colonic biopsy showed granuloma formation in a background of active colitis and lymphoplasmacytic infiltrate. Which of the following best describes the pathophysiology of this disorder?

  1. Defective phagocyte oxidative burst
  2. Dysfunctional B lymphocyte maturation
  3. Impaired production of complement proteins
  4. Reduced T lymphocyte proliferation
Board review style answer #1
A. Defective phagocyte oxidative burst. The case represents the typical clinical presentation of chronic granulomatous disease (CGD), which is characterized by a defect in the nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide phosphate (NADPH) oxidase enzyme complex within phagocytes (neutrophils, macrophages). This defect leads to an impaired oxidative burst, which is crucial for the phagocytes' ability to generate reactive oxygen species (ROS) and effectively kill engulfed pathogens. Answers B, C and D are incorrect because they do not accurately describe the primary defect seen in CGD.
