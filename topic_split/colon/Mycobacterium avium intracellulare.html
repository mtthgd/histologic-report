Definition / general
  • Infectious disease of the colon or small intestine caused by the nontuberculous mycobacteria (NTM) Mycobacterium avium and Mycobacterium intracellulare (StatPearls: Mycobacterium avium Complex [Accessed 20 December 2024])
  • Gastrointestinal infection by these mycobacteria is rare and usually observed opportunistically in patients with conditions such as human immunodeficiency virus (HIV) infection or organ transplant recipients (Endoscopy 1988;20:279, Transplant Proc 2017;49:2362, StatPearls: Mycobacterium avium Complex [Accessed 20 December 2024], Semin Respir Crit Care Med 2018;39:325, Am J Transplant 2013;13:77)
Essential features
  • Infection of the gastrointestinal tract caused by Mycobacterium avium and Mycobacterium intracellulare
  • Mostly affects the immunocompromised population; those with HIV infection with low CD4 counts ( Diagnosis can be challenging due to atypical microscopic patterns; acid fast bacilli (AFB) special stain should be considered on samples from HIV / AIDS patients with low CD4 counts and biopsies showing clusters of macrophages
  • Control of HIV infection using highly active antiretroviral therapy (HAART) is the most important factor in treating disseminated disease in HIV / AIDS patients
Terminology
  • Mycobacterium avium and Mycobacterium intracellulare are the 2 original members of the Mycobacterium avium complex (MAC) or Mycobacterium avium intracellulare complex (MAIC) of nontuberculous mycobacterial species that require genetic testing to be distinguished (StatPearls: Mycobacterium avium Complex [Accessed 20 December 2024])
ICD coding
  • ICD-10: A31.8 - other mycobacterial infections
Epidemiology
  • Few studies describe the epidemiology of extrapulmonary nontuberculous mycobacteria in the United States (Semin Respir Crit Care Med 2018;39:325)
  • Estimates of extrapulmonary nontuberculous mycobacteria incidence vary between 1.5 and 1.9 cases per 100,000 population, with a median age of 51 years, with over 50 - 67% of cases being caused by MAC (Emerg Infect Dis 2017;23:1627, Semin Respir Crit Care Med 2018;39:325)
  • Individuals with comorbidities that compromise the immune system, such as HIV infection and other underlying immune deficiency disorders, are susceptible to extrapulmonary nontuberculous mycobacteria infections, especially disseminated disease (Semin Respir Crit Care Med 2018;39:325, Am J Transplant 2013;13:77, Endoscopy 1988;20:279, Transplant Proc 2017;49:2362, StatPearls: Mycobacterium avium Complex [Accessed 20 December 2024])
  • Involvement of the gastrointestinal tract by MAC is usually part of disseminated disease in AIDS patients with a low CD4 count (Ann Diagn Pathol 2020;49:151638)
  • MAC infection causes diarrhea in up to 10% of patients with a CD4 count of Medicine 2009;37:357)
  • M. avium is the most common disease causing species, constituting > 95% of cases of disseminated MAC (DMAC) (Microbiol Spectr 2017;5:1)
Sites
  • Duodenum is most often involved, followed by the small bowel, colon and rectum (Am J Gastroenterol 1989;84:1521, J Infect Dis 1999;179:S461, Am J Gastroenterol 2022;117:e2151)
Pathophysiology
  • MAC is ubiquitous in soil and water sources and acquired by inhalation or ingestion
  • Infection is usually acquired through the gastrointestinal route as M. avium strains are resistant to the stomach's acidic environment (pH 2.2)
  • It enters the mucosal epithelial cells of the intestine from the apical membrane by using the mucosal cell's fibronectin to bind to the integrin receptor
  • Multiple studies have shown that in vitro, intestine-like environments with low oxygen tension and hyperosmolarity enhance the ability of these bacteria to invade enterocytes
  • Once it enters the enterocyte, it expresses an invasive phenotype and invades surrounding enterocytes and submucosal macrophages
  • It enters the macrophages using various receptors such as the complement receptors CR1, CR3 and CR4, the mannose receptor and scavenger receptors
  • Organisms may replicate unchecked in the macrophages, especially in the immunocompromised who have decreased host defenses (such as production of cytokines like IL8 and IL12, cell mediated killing by macrophages and granuloma formation) until the host cell ruptures (Arch Immunol Ther Exp (Warsz) 2000;48:521, Microbes Infect 2001;3:37, Infect Immun 2001;69:1515, Proc Natl Acad Sci U S A 2007;104:11038, J Infect Dis 1999;179:S461)
  • In addition, the growth of MAC can be stimulated by both the high serum levels of triglycerides and the iron overload seen in patients with AIDS (J Infect Dis 1999;179:S461)
  • Organism disseminates through the lymphatics to lymph nodes and the rest of the body, further replicating in the macrophages
  • Hematogenous dissemination occurs concurrently, spreading through the blood; most bacteria are seen within infected mononuclear cells (J Infect Dis 1999;179:S461)
Etiology
  • Infection by nontuberculous mycobacteria Mycobacterium avium intracellulare complex
Clinical features
  • Infection with Mycobacterium avium complex may be asymptomatic or present with nonspecific symptoms in immunocompetent patients
  • Isolated GI tract infection is very rare; the most common manifestations of GI involvement are diarrhea and weight loss
  • Patients with disseminated MAC infections in the setting of AIDS often have persistent, high grade fevers, profound night sweats, weight loss, anorexia, anemia, fatigue and diarrhea, usually associated with cramping abdominal pain
  • References: Gastroenterology 2023;165:541, Microbiol Spectr 2017;5:1, StatPearls: Mycobacterium avium Complex [Accessed 20 December 2024], J Infect Dis 1999;179:S461, Gastrointest Endosc 2005;61:775, Clin J Gastroenterol 2022;15:722
Diagnosis
  • Esophagogastroduodenoscopy (EGD) or colonoscopy with biopsies for histopathological evaluation, acid fast bacilli (AFB) staining and nucleic acid amplification tests
  • Microbiologic isolation of the organisms from the site of infection is the gold standard for diagnosis (StatPearls: Mycobacterium avium Complex [Accessed 20 December 2024])
  • Nasogastric aspirate sampling can be a useful diagnostic maneuver in isolating the organism (Transplant Proc 2017;49:2362)
  • AFB smears and cultures of blood and ascitic fluid may demonstrate the organism
  • Blood cultures have a predicted sensitivity of 90% with a single blood culture for disseminated disease (Microbiol Spectr 2017;5:1, Cureus 2022;14:e28007)
Laboratory
  • Most cases of intestinal MAC occur in immunocompromised hosts with CD4 cell count of Ann Diagn Pathol 2020;49:151638, StatPearls: Mycobacterium avium Complex [Accessed 20 December 2024], Microbiol Spectr 2017;5:1)
  • Disseminated disease shows severe anemia out of proportion to neutropenia or thrombocytopenia as well as elevated transaminases and alkaline phosphatase (Microbiol Spectr 2017;5:1, J Infect Dis 1999;179:S461)
  • Blood cultures have a predicted sensitivity of 90% with a single blood culture for disseminated disease (Microbiol Spectr 2017;5:1)
Radiology description
  • Computed tomography (CT) scan in cases of disseminated MAC may show diffuse chest or abdominal adenopathy and concurrent pulmonary MAC may be seen as a fibrocavitary disease (nonspecific) (Microbiol Spectr 2017;5:1)
  • Gut wall thickening due to extensive intestinal disease can be observed radiographically (J Infect Dis 1999;179:S461)
Prognostic factors
  • Exposure to MAC does not usually cause local intestinal or disseminated disease in immunocompetent individuals, though isolated case reports have been described
  • In patients with HIV, survival rates depend on compliance with highly active antiretroviral therapy (HAART) and treatment can lead to recovery even in extensive disease but relapse is common (StatPearls: Mycobacterium avium Complex [Accessed 20 December 2024])
  • Untreated intestinal MAC infection can progress to involve the entire gut (J Infect Dis 1999;179:S461)
  • Risk of MAC bacteremia is ~60% within 1 year for HIV patients with MAC colonization in the gastrointestinal tract with a relative hazard of 6.0 (95% confidence interval = 2.5 - 14.6) as compared to those without GI involvement (J Infect Dis 1994;169:289)
  • Some HIV infected patients deteriorate with immune reconstitution (after ART) termed immune reconstitution syndrome (IRIS), which may take a long time to resolve (median: 19.5 months; maximum: > 5 years) in patients who respond to therapy (Microbiol Spectr 2017;5:1)
Case reports

  • 41 year old man with MAC enterocolitis (Am J Gastroenterol 2022;117:e2151)
  • 51 year old woman with intestinal MAC infection in an immunocompetent host (Am J Gastroenterol 2021;116:S780)
  • 55 year old man with HIV / AIDS (Clin J Gastroenterol 2022;15:722)
  • 57 year old woman with intestinal MAC presenting with small bowel atrophy (Endoscopy 2024;56:E755)
  • 66 year old man with MAC infection in an immunocompetent host presenting with colonic ulceration and rectal bleeding (Am J Gastroenterol 2010;105:S323)
  • 68 year old woman with a history of a kidney transplant (Cureus 2022;14:e28007)
Treatment
  • If there is no disseminated disease: 6 - 18 months of multidrug therapy depending on site, state of immunosuppression and treatment response
    • Standard 3 drug macrolide containing regimen
    • Addition of a parenteral aminoglycoside for extensive disease or therapeutic failure
  • For disseminated disease in persons without HIV: very rare occurrence so no definitive protocols are established
    • IFNγ has been useful in some congenital and acquired immunodeficiencies
    • For disseminated MAC following bone marrow or solid organ transplantation, consider a reduction in immunosuppression
  • For disseminated disease in HIV / AIDS cases: control of HIV using highly active antiretroviral therapy (HAART) is the most important factor in treatment
    • Initial therapy with a macrolide (clarithromycin or azithromycin) plus ethambutol
    • Sustained increase in CD4 count to > 100/μL for at least 6 months, with 12 complete months of antimycobacterial therapy, is an indication to stop therapy for MAC if the patient is asymptomatic with documentation of a negative mycobacterial blood culture prior to discontinuation of therapy
    • Closely monitor for relapse, especially if CD4 count falls below 100 cells/μL
  • Treatment of immune reconstitution syndrome consists of NSAIDs and a short course of systemic corticosteroids with concurrent MAC therapy
  • Reference: Microbiol Spectr 2017;5:1
Microscopic (histologic) description
  • Histological patterns of colonic MAC are similar to duodenal / small intestine MAC infection; the findings can be classified as typical or atypical patterns (Ann Diagn Pathol 2020;49:151638)
  • Typical pattern
    • Diffuse lamina propria expansion by foamy histiocytes
  • Atypical patterns
    • Scattered single histiocytes
    • Loosely organized epithelioid granuloma-like collections
    • Deep submucosal involvement with mucosal sparing (Case Rep Gastrointest Med 2019;2019:8129597, Diseases 2018;6:36)
  • Histiocytes may range from large round, polygonal cells with distended eosinophilic granular / foamy cytoplasm (typical) to oval spindly cells with pale vacuolated cytoplasm
  • Intracytoplasmic bacilli are usually numerous, densely packed and better highlighted on AFB or PAS special stains

Positive stains
  • AFB staining methods such as Ziehl-Neelsen / kinyoun / fite / auramine-rhodamine stain the mycobacteria
    • Mycobacteria stain bright red on AFB stains and organisms tend to align themselves in stacks
  • PAS and PASD stains highlight MAC histiocytes and stain the bacilli (Ann Diagn Pathol 2020;49:151638)
  • Aberrant staining of bacilli with GMS special stain (J Am Soc Cytopathol 2017;6:223)
Sample pathology report
  • Colon, random, biopsy:
    • Colonic mucosa and submucosa with numerous foamy macrophages showing acid fast organisms, consistent with Mycobacterium avium intracellulare complex infection (see comment)
    • Comment: The biopsy shows colonic mucosa with lamina propria expanded by foamy macrophages. AFB and fite stains highlight numerous intracytoplasmic delicate acid fast bacilli. These bacilli are also highlighted on the PASD special stain. These findings are consistent with Mycobacterium avium intracellulare complex infection.
Differential diagnosis
  • Whipple disease:
    • Rarely can involve colon
    • Stains for AFB are negative
  • Tuberculosis:
    • Well formed epithelioid granulomas with central necrosis
    • Usually rare to few bacilli on AFB stains
    • MAC bacilli are usually longer than tubercle bacilli
  • Sarcoidosis:
    • Well formed, naked, solid epithelioid granulomas
    • No necrosis
    • Frequent multinucleated giant cells
    • Stains for AFB are negative
  • Langerhans cell histiocytosis:
    • Diffuse infiltrate of large polygonal histiocytes with abundant pale pink cytoplasm
    • Elongated oval irregular nuclei with nuclear grooves and folds
    • Discrete granuloma-like clusters, eosinophils, multinucleated giant cells
    • Stains for AFB are negative
    • S100 protein, CD1a and langerin stains are positive
  • Histoplasmosis:
    • Diffuse mucosal infiltrates of foamy macrophages with distended eosinophilic vacuolated cytoplasm with faint blue dots of intracytoplasmic inclusions and a clear halo
    • PAS, GMS and Giemsa stains show positive budding yeasts
    • Stains for AFB are negative
Board review style question #1


A 45 year old HIV positive man has presented with diarrhea, weight loss and abdominal pain. CD4 cell count is
  1. Histoplasmosis
  2. Mycobacterium avium intracellulare infection
  3. Sarcoidosis
  4. Tuberculosis
Board review style answer #1
B. Mycobacterium avium intracellulare infection. The H&E image highlights lamina propria expansion by foamy histocytes. The AFB stain highlights the densely packed bacilli in the histiocytes. Answer D is incorrect because well formed, caseating granulomas are not identified. Answer C is incorrect because well formed, noncaseating granulomas are seen with sarcoidosis and AFB stains are negative. Answer A is incorrect because intracytoplasmic Histoplasma will stain by PASD and GMS special stains and not on AFB stain.
