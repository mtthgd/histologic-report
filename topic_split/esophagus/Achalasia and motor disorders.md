Definition / general

* Esophageal motor disorder characterized by lack of progressive peristalsis and partial / incomplete relaxation of lower esophageal sphincter (LES), preventing passage of food into stomach
* Preferentially involves circular layer of muscularis propria, which is hypertrophied
* Patients with achalasia may also have GERD ([Eur J Gastroenterol Hepatol 2006;18:369](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/16538107))
* 5% risk (33x normal) of esophageal squamous cell carcinoma, at mean 21 - 28 years after diagnosis of achalasia ([Anticancer Res 2000;20:3717](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/11268444))
* Also increased risk of aspiration, Barrett esophagus, *Candida* infection, gastroesophageal reflux, lower esophageal diverticula, peptic ulceration, stricture ([Ann Surg 2006;243:196](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/16432352))

Terminology

* Also called cardiospasm, megaesophagus

Pathophysiology

* Due to T cell mediated destruction or complete absence of myenteric ganglion cells in lower third of esophagus ([Am J Gastroenterol 2005;100:1404](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/15929777))

Etiology

* **Secondary causes:** Allgrove syndrome ([World J Gastroenterol 2006;12:4764](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/16937455)), amyloidosis, Chagas disease (*Trypanosoma cruzi*, common in South America, destroys myenteric plexus of esophagus, duodenum, colon, ureter), diabetic autonomic neuropathy, polio, sarcoidosis, surgical ablation of dorsal motor nuclei, thyroid disease ([World J Gastroenterol 2007;13:594](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/17278227)), tumor

Clinical features

* Most cases are primary, i.e. idiopathic, usually young adults with progressive dysphagia, nocturnal regurgitation and aspiration of undigested food
* Can occur in children

Treatment

* Esophagomyotomy ([World J Gastroenterol 2006;12:5921](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/17009388), [GI Motility Online: Surgical Treatment for Achalasia [Accessed 13 February 2019]](https://www.google.com/url?&rct=j&url=http://www.nature.com/gimo/contents/pt1/full/gimo53.html)), dilation ([World J Gastroenterol 2006;12:5763](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/17007039)), botulinum toxin to inhibit LES cholinergic neurons

Gross description

* Progressive dilation of esophagus above LES, variable wall thickness

Microscopic (histologic) description

* **Early:** Auerbach / myenteric plexus has lymphocytic inflammation (cytotoxic T cells, eosinophils) with germinal centers and submucosal glandular atrophy
* **Late:** marked depletion / absence of ganglion cells in myenteric plexus (middle of esophagus, may be normal at LES) and replacement of nerves by collagen with muscular hypertrophy; squamous mucosa markedly hyperplastic with papillomatosis and basal cell hyperplasia resembling GERD ([J Gastroenterol Hepatol 2006;21:727](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/16677160))

Positive stains

* [p53](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsp53.html), T > B cells ([Am J Surg Pathol 2001;25:1413](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/11684958))

Electron microscopy description

* Smooth muscle cells have nuclear and cytoplasmic inclusions, marked loss of small nerve fibers, paucity of granules in nerve fibers; also nonspecific filament disarray, mottling of myocyte fiber density, thick and long cytoplasmic dense bodies, long dense plaques ([Am J Clin Pathol 1983;79:319](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/6829502))

Differential diagnosis

* **Normal aging**
* **Pseudoachalasia**
* **Visceral neuropathies**
