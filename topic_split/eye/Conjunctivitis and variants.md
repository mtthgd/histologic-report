Allergic conjunctivitis

Epidemiology

* Prevalence of 15 - 40% ([Curr Opin Allergy Clin Immunol 2011;11:471](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/21785348))

Sites

* Unilateral or bilateral; affects palpebral and bulbar conjunctiva

Pathophysiology

* Ocular surface inflammation (usually mast cell driven) is associated with type 1 hypersensitivity reactions ([eMedicine: Allergic Conjunctivitis [Accessed 24 April 2018]](http://emedicine.medscape.com/article/1191467-overview))
* May be part of systemic atopic reaction to a systemic allergen
* Most prevalent types (95%) are perennial and seasonal (hay fever eyes) ([Curr Opin Allergy Clin Immunol 2013;13:543](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/23974684))
* Most frequent allergens are tree and grass pollens
* More severe forms
  + Chronic vernal keratoconjunctivitis: more common in spring (vernal); conjunctival scarring, eyelid thickening, ptosis, corneal neovascularization, ulceration, thinning, infection, keratoconus, and vision loss
  + Atopic keratoconjunctivitis: eyelid tightening, loss of eyelashes, cataracts
  + [Giant papillary conjunctivitis](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/eyechronicconj.html#giantpapillaryconjunctivitis): eyelid inflammation and ptosis

Clinical features

* History of allergy, asthma, or eczema
* Itching (pruritus), chemosis, tearing and redness with or without sneezing and nasal discharge
* 2 - 3 times increased risk for herpetic infection ([Curr Eye Res 2006;31:721](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/16966144))

Diagnosis

* Cobblestoning (vernal) or papillary hypertrophy (giant papillary) of upper tarsal conjunctiva in chronic forms

Treatment

* Allergy type medications; topical cyclosporine if severe ([Cornea 2007;26:1035](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/17893529))
* Patient education, lifestyle modification and topical medications (antihistamines, mast cell stabilizers, corticosteroids, immunomodulators)

Vernal
keratoconjunctivitis

Microscopic (histologic) description

* May have eosinophils in conjunctival scraping ([Acta Ophthalmol (Copenh) 1988;66:381](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/3195319))

Cytology description

* Consistent with clinical diagnosis ([Indian J Pathol Microbiol 1992;35:118](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/1483711))

Differential diagnosis

* Atopic keratoconjunctivitis
* [Giant papillary keratoconjunctivitis](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/eyechronicconj.html#giantpapillaryconjunctivitis):
  + Lymphocyte and eosinophil predominant, versus predominance of mast cells and eosinophils in allergic cases
* Vernal keratoconjunctivitis

Chronic conjunctivitis

Pathophysiology

* Conjunctivitis that persists for 4+ weeks

Etiology

* Unilateral:
  + Due to keratitis, nasolacrimal duct obstruction, occult foreign body, neoplasm, tuberculosis, phthiriasis palpebrarum (due to lice, [Int Ophthalmol 2012;32:467](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/22707338))
* Bilateral:
  + Due to bacteria, tuberculosis ([Int Ophthalmol 2014;34:655](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/23928944)), virus, [trachoma](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/eyetrachoma.html), other microorganisms
  + Also allergic or chemical causes, inflammation of meibomian glands of eyelid
  + Tarsoconjunctival crypts containing bacteria ([Am J Ophthalmol 2012;154:527](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/22609049))
  + Giant fornix syndrome ([Ophthal Plast Reconstr Surg 2012;28:4](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/21862948))
  + Vitamin A deficiency

Clinical features

* Insidious onset; slow progression of ocular discomfort, conjunctival injection, discharge

Diagnosis

* Conjunctival scrapings and culture are useful for diagnosis ([Eur J Ophthalmol 1997;7:19](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/9101190))

Case reports

* 50 year old woman with relapsing polychondritis and obliterative microangiopathy ([Cornea 2006;25:621](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/16783155))
* Boy with X linked hypogammaglobulinemia and bilateral inflammation ([J Paediatr Child Health 1996;32:463](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/8933411))

Treatment

* Antibiotics, artificial tears, topical medications

Microscopic (histologic) description

* 2 basic patterns:
  + Chronic follicular conjunctivitis (associated with cosmetics, infectious mononucleosis, molluscum contagiosum, moraxella, phthiriasis palpebrarum, psittacosis, topical medications, trachoma)
  + Chronic papillary conjunctivitis (tarsal conjunctiva)
* Numerous goblet cells and papillary folds
* Chronic inflammatory cells (lymphoid follicles and plasma cells)
* Isolated islands of epithelium may form retention cysts that calcify
* Late changes are epithelial atrophy, keratinization and stromal scarring
* Note that lymphocytes are common within normal conjunctiva

Cytology description

* Scrapings may yield more specific findings than impression cytology in chronic conjunctivitis ([Eur J Ophthalmol 1997;7:19](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/9101190))

Molecular / cytogenetics description

* PCR is gold standard for chlamydia related conjunctivitis ([Semin Ophthalmol 2013;28:216](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/23627371))

Differential diagnosis

* [Lymphoma](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/eyeconjlymphoma.html) ([Ophthalmology 1999;106:757](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/10201599), [Cornea 2001;20:427](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/11333335))

Additional references

* [Eagle: Eye Pathology: An Atlas and Text, 2nd Edition, 2011](http://www.amazon.com/exec/obidos/ASIN/1608317889/pathologyoutl-20)

Giant papillary conjunctivitis

Definition / general

* Noninfectious inflammatory disorder due to mechanical and immunologic processes (type I and type IV hypersensitivity)

Terminology

* Also known as contact lens induced papillary conjunctivitis

Epidemiology

* Young adults with history of atopy
* 1 - 5% of soft contact lens wearers
* Average onset after 10 - 20 months of contact lens use

Etiology

* Associated with soft, hydrogel contact lenses ([Trans Am Ophthalmol Soc 1999;97:205](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/10703125))
* Also exposed sutures, prostheses, rigid lenses (incidence only 10% of soft lenses) ([Clin Experiment Ophthalmol 2007;35:535](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/17760635), [Cont Lens Anterior Eye 2008;31:41](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/17826299))
* Also ocular surgery, foreign body, cyanoacrylate glue

Clinical features

* Itching, burning, foreign body sensation, redness
* Mucus accumulating in inner canthus noticed in morning
* Blurred vision, contact lens coating
* Usually bilateral (10% unilateral)

Diagnosis

* Upper tarsal conjunctiva has inflammation and papules (usually > 0.3 mm)

Treatment

* Change contact lenses more frequently ([Eye Contact Lens 2003;29:S37](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/12772728))
* Mast cell stabilizers
* Olopatadine and fluorometholone ([Eye Contact Lens 2010;36:210](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/20531202))

Giant papillary conjunctivitis

Microscopic (histologic) description

* Thickened and irregular epithelium
* Epithelial cells show polymorphism with greater number of conjunctival goblet cells
* Upper tarsal conjunctival biopsies show mast cells, eosinophils and basophils in epithelium and substantia propria

Cytology description

* Impression cytology: honeycomb pattern consistent with giant papillae, increased inflammation and mucous strands ([Ophthal Plast Reconstr Surg 2005;21:39](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/15677951))

Differential diagnosis

* [Allergic conjunctivitis](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/eyechronicconj.html#allergicconjunctivitis)
* Toxic conjunctivitis

Additional references

* [Immunol Allergy Clin North Am 2008;28:83](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/18282547), [Curr Opin Allergy Clin Immunol 2008;8:445](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/18769199)

Infectious conjunctivitis

Definition / general

* See also [Granulomatous conjunctivitis](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/eyeconjgranulomatous.html), [Trachoma](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/eyetrachoma.html), [Infectious keratitis](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/eyecorneainfectiouskeratitis.html)
* Often due to inadequate personnel hygiene

Epidemiology

* In children, bacterial conjunctivitis is more common than viral
* Viral conjunctivitis is highly contagious

Sites

* Bulbar and palpebral conjunctiva

Etiology

* Most bacterial conjunctivitis is acute; chronic bacterial conjunctivitis is often caused by Chlamydia, rarely by Moraxella
* Adenovirus conjunctivitis:
  + Most common cause of viral conjunctivitis ([Arch Soc Esp Oftalmol 2013;88:108](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/23473088))
  + 60% of ER conjunctivitis patients in Florida
  + Detected by PCR ([Optometry 2007;78:236](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/17478342))
* Other viruses:
  + Herpes simplex virus
  + Varicella zoster virus
  + Enterovirus 70
  + Coxsackie A24
  + Molluscum contagiosum
  + Vaccinia
  + Human immunodeficiency virus
* Alcaligenes xylosoxidans conjunctivitis:
  + Rare; can cause chronic conjunctivitis ([Cornea 2007;26:868](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/17667625))
* Conjunctivitis within first 4 weeks of life
  + Usually *S. aureus, S. epidermidis, S. pneumoniae*, *M. catarrhalis*
  + Also *C. trachomatis, N. gonorrhoeae*, herpes simplex virus (HSV)
  + Mothers with untreated chlamydial infections have a 30 - 40% chance of transmission to newborns
  + Unilateral or bilateral ([Pediatr Rev 2010;31:196](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/20435711))
* Bacterial conjunctivitis in children:
  + Should exclude infected children from school until asymptomatic ([J Pediatr Ophthalmol Strabismus 2007;44:101](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/17410961))
  + Local antibiotics may not be necessary ([Prescrire Int 2007;16:120](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/17585426))
  + Often due to *H. influenzae* and *Streptococcus pneumoniae* ([Ophthalmology 2008;115:51](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/17572497))
  + *S. aureus* is uncommon in children ([Acad Emerg Med 2007;14:1](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/17119185))
* Bacterial conjunctivitis in adults:
  + Often due to *Staphylococcus aureus*, may be methicillin resistant
  + *Streptococcus pneumoniae* is associated with outbreaks ([Pediatr Infect Dis J 2006;25:906](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/17006286))
  + Delayed use of antibiotics may be appropriate ([BMJ 2006;333:321](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/16847013))
* Hyperacute conjunctivitis:
  + Rapid onset
  + Due to *Neisseria gonorrhoeae* or *N. meningitides* ([Pediatr Rev 2010;31:196](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/20435711))
  + Other bacteria are Pneumococcus species, Pseudomonas ([Infection 2012;40:579](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/22350960))
* Gonococcal conjunctivitis:
  + Due to autoinoculation
  + Rare in developed countries ([J Fr Ophtalmol 2007;30:e18](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/17878816))
  + May cause corneal perforation ([Med J Malaysia 2006;61:366](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/17240593))
* Influenza A virus conjunctivitis:
  + Outbreak from poultry ([Proc Natl Acad Sci U S A 2004;101:1356](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/14745020))

Clinical features

* Bacterial: itching, tearing, redness, purulent discharge, matting of eyelids
* Viral: irritation, photophobia, watery discharge

Case reports

* Child with acute conjunctivitis caused by *Ewingella americana* ([J Pediatr Ophthalmol Strabismus 2012;49](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/22998132))
* 23 year old woman with conjunctivitis due to *Achromobacter xylosoxidans* ([Arq Bras Oftalmol 2009;72:261](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/19466343))
