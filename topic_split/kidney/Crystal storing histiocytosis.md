Definition / general

* Crystal storing histiocytosis (CSH) is a rare condition with crystalline material, usually kappa light chain, that accumulates in cytoplasm of histiocytes ([Kidney Int 2016;89:507](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/26806837))
* Strongly associated with underlying plasma cell neoplasm or lymphoma with plasma cell differentiation ([Diagnostics (Basel) 2023;13:271](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/36673081), [Ren Fail 2023;45:2145970](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/36632756))

Essential features

* Accumulation of crystalline deposits within histiocytes (immune cells) in the kidney ([Kidney Int 2016;89:507](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/26806837), [Diagnostics (Basel) 2023;13:271](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/36673081))
* Crystals identified in histiocytes within kidney but not in glomerular cells (mesangial, endothelial, podocytes), tubular epithelial cells or endothelial cells ([Ren Fail 2023;45:2145970](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/36632756))
* These deposits are typically composed of monoclonal immunoglobulin light chains (usually kappa) and are commonly associated with underlying hematologic malignancies, such as multiple myeloma or other lymphoproliferative disorders ([Kidney Int 2016;89:507](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/26806837), [Diagnostics (Basel) 2023;13:271](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/36673081))

Terminology

* Crystal storing histiocytosis (CSH)
* Synonym: crystal bearing histiocytosis

ICD coding

* ICD-10: [D76.3](https://www.icd10data.com/ICD10CM/Codes/D50-D89/D70-D77/D76-/D76.3) - other histiocytosis syndromes

Epidemiology

* Renal CSH represents Adults are affected with a wide age range (mean 60 years, range 40 - 75 years); slight male predominance ([Diagnostics (Basel) 2023;13:271](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/36673081))

Sites

* Most common site of involvement is the bone marrow, followed by the head and neck, with the kidneys being the third most frequently affected ([Ren Fail 2023;45:2145970](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/36632756))

Pathophysiology

* Multifactorial, exact mechanism not fully understood ([Diagnostics (Basel) 2023;13:271](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/36673081))
  + Increased production - specifically seen in patients with plasma cell dyscrasias
  + Abnormal, impaired or decreased excretion of immunoglobulin, as evidenced by the frequent association with Fanconi syndrome due to concurrent crystal driven proximal renal tubular dysfunction
  + Some postulate more sequence abnormalities at specific sites occur in immunoglobulin heavy / light chains that promote crystallization or adversely affect intralysosomal degradation more than simple overproduction
  + More frequently caused by the kappa light chain rather than a specific heavy chain due to hydrophobic residues in the variable (V) domain of kappa light chain
  + It has been proposed that unusual amino acid substitutions in the variable region of the stored kappa light chain (including Leu59) lead to configurational changes and resistance to lysosomal degradation with subsequent accumulation
  + Other contributing factors may be inherited or acquired histiocyte processing defects resulting in immunoglobulin crystal formation
  + Renal CSH might be associated with filtered load of paraprotein with 60% of cases associated with visceral epithelial cell crystals that can be secondary to leakage of filtered kappa paraproteins through damaged tubules
  + Factors such as dehydration and metabolic acidosis might contribute

Etiology

* Increased production of immunoglobulin in plasma cell disorder ([Diagnostics (Basel) 2023;13:271](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/36673081))
* Linked to lymphomas, including lymphoplasmacytic lymphoma, low grade B cell lymphoproliferative disorders, diffuse large B cell lymphoma
* Monoclonal gammopathy of uncertain significance (MGUS), sometimes associated with type I cryoglobulinemia
* Decreased degradation or defective phagocytosis ([Diagnostics (Basel) 2023;13:271](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/36673081))

Clinical features

* Median age of 60 years (range 40 - 75 years), with slight male predominance ([Diagnostics (Basel) 2023;13:271](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/36673081))
* Most frequent clinical manifestation during renal involvement by CSH is acute or acute-on-chronic renal failure and proteinuria ([Diagnostics (Basel) 2023;13:271](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/36673081))
* Nonspecific constitutional symptoms such as fever and easy fatigability may be present ([Histopathology 2016;68:482](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/26118455))
* Other organ specific clinical features can be seen in cases with concurrent bone marrow, lymph nodes, liver, spleen and gastrointestinal tract involvement ([Histopathology 2016;68:482](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/26118455))
* Rarely CSH is the first clue to underlying lymphoma or plasma cell disorder lymphocytosis

Diagnosis

* Laboratory tests, including serum protein electrophoresis and immunofixation, are indicated to unveil underlying monoclonal spike in the setting of MGUS, multiple myeloma or lymphoma ([Histopathology 2016;68:482](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/26118455))
* Detection of serum free light chains (sFLC), and other laboratory workup such as complete blood count, serum creatinine, electrolytes (including calcium), lactate dehydrogenase and β2 microglobulin are indicated in this setting ([Histopathology 2016;68:482](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/26118455))
* Often associated with elevated kappa light chains in serum or urine; when involved, IgG is the most common heavy chain followed by IgM, IgA and IgD ([Histopathology 2016;68:482](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/26118455))
* Gold standard for the diagnosis of renal CSH is histopathology examination via renal biopsy, utilizing immunohistochemistry / fluorescence and electron microscopy to confirm the diagnosis or rule out other histiocytic lesions / tumors ([Diagnostics (Basel) 2023;13:271](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/36673081))

Prognostic factors

* Prognosis is highly variable and dependent on age and extent of underlying associated disease
* Factors that can help in the stratification of the prognosis are: serum LDH levels, unifocal versus systemic organ involvement and concurrent systemic symptoms of Waldenström macroglobulinemia ([Diagnostics (Basel) 2023;13:271](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/36673081))

Case reports

* 58 year old man diagnosed with a low grade B cell lymphoproliferative disorder and with crystal storing histiocytosis affecting the kidney ([Am J Kidney Dis 2002;39:183](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/11774118))
* 66 year old woman presenting with albumin predominant moderate proteinuria and light chain proximal tubulopathy and crystal storing histiocytosis both in the kidney and in the bone marrow ([Pathologica 2021;113:285](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/34463673))
* 67 year old White man with a rise in creatinine to 1.8 mg/dl from a baseline of 1.3 mg/dl, proteinuria, hematuria and serum IgG kappa monoclonal gammopathy and a membranoproliferative glomerulonephritis with a crystal storing histiocytosis IgG kappa on renal biopsy ([Kidney Int 2016;89:507](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/26806837))

Treatment

* Chemotherapy or autologous stem cell transplant to treat the underlying cause

Microscopic (histologic) description

* Interstitium
  + Histiocytes contain abundant needle shaped eosinophilic intracytoplasmic inclusions
  + Crystals typically are
    - Fuchsinophilic with Masson trichrome stain
    - Negative or weakly positive for PAS stain
    - Silver negative (eosinophilic with Jones methenamine silver)
  + Toluidine blue stain on semithin sections for electron microscopy highlights intracytoplasmic globules in histiocytes
* Glomerulus
  + Glomerular capillary lumina contain marginated histiocytes with intracytoplasmic monoclonal crystalline proteins
  + Membranoproliferative pattern of injury
    - Capillary loops distended with histiocytes containing monoclonal crystals
    - Global or segmental capillary wall remodeling
  + Mesangial expansion by infiltrating histiocytes
  + Glomerular involvement less common than interstitial involvement ([Ann Diagn Pathol 2019;43:151403](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/31494491))
* Coexisting monoclonal protein associated pathology
  + > 70% show concurrent disease due to monoclonal gammopathy
  + Crystalline glomerulopathy
    - Includes crystals within podocytes, mesangium or endothelial cells
  + Amyloidosis
  + Light chain proximal tubulopathy
  + Light chain cast nephropathy

Immunofluorescence description

* Monotypic restriction of the crystal deposits can be revealed with immunofluorescence (IF), frequently for kappa light chain
* Paraffin based IF methods after specific retrieval techniques (e.g., pronase / proteinase k) can help in unveiling monotypic restriction when frozen IF is negative and histology is suggestive ([Ren Fail 2023;45:2145970](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/36632756))

Electron microscopy description

* Crystals are electron dense and variably shaped, including rhomboid, needle and rectangular
  + Rarely electron lucent
  + May reveal lattice substructure
* Crystals are free in the cytoplasm of histiocytes or occasionally membrane bound
* Immunogold EM can be used to further document monotypical nature of the deposits
* Granular and needle shaped crystals could also be seen in the podocytes, mesangial cells and histiocytes ([Ren Fail 2023;45:2145970](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/36632756))

Sample pathology report

* Left kidney, needle core biopsy:
  + Renal crystal storing histiocytosis involving glomeruli (see comment)
  + Comment
    - Microscopic description: Global and diffuse lobular appearance of the glomerular tuft for the engulfment of the capillary lumens by numerous foamy macrophages (CD68 PGM1+), with cytoplasm occupied by globular hyaline aggregates weakly positive to PAS stain, fuchsinophilic on Masson trichrome and eosinophilic on Jones with formation of double contours of the basement membranes. Congo Red is negative. Interstitial fibrosis and tubular atrophy extended to approximately 10% of the cortex. Interstitial infiltrate composed of lymphocytes and scarce polytypic plasma cells in fibrotic and nonfibrotic areas associated with widespread acute tubular damage with exfoliation and vacuolization of the tubular cells, rare intratubular hyaline casts. No significant arterial abnormalities.
    - Immunofluorescence: Diffuse and global coarsely granular positivity for IgG (3+), monotypic for kappa immunoglobulin light chain (2/3+) within the intracytoplasmic globular aggregates of glomerular intracapillary macrophages described in light microscopy. Trace positive staining for lambda and negative for IgA, IgM, C3, C1q and fibrinogen.
    - Electron microscopy: Global endocapillary hypercellularity characterized by intracapillary macrophages with cytoplasm engulfed by electron dense globular deposits, sometimes polygonal / crystal shaped, focally extended to subendothelial and paramesangial areas. Podocyte foot process effacement extending over 70% of the surface of the capillary loops. The glomerular basement membrane thickness is within the average range for sex and age, measured at approximately 240 nm in areas distant from regions of hypercellularity. Acute damage to the tubules with cytoplasmic vacuolization and focal loss of microvilli.

Differential diagnosis

* **Mycobacterial and fungal infections**:
  + Frequently associated with granulomatous (often necrotizing) inflammation in mycobacterial infection, immunocompromised history and angioinvasion frequent with fungal infection
  + Special histochemical stains like [Ziehl-Neelsen](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsacidfast.html) for mycobacteria and [PAS](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainspas.html) / [GMS](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsgms.html) for fungi can help
* [Whipple disease](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/smallbowelwhipple.html):
  + [PAS](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainspas.html) positive, PCR for *Tropheryma whipplei*
* Storage diseases (e.g., [Fabry nephropathy](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/kidneyfabry.html) and [Gaucher disease](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/livergauchers.html)):
  + Enzyme studies and distinct clinical picture can help differentiation
* [Langerhans cell histiocytosis](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/lymphnodeslch.html):
  + [S100](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsS100.html), [CD1a](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/cdmarkerscd1a.html), [langerin](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/cdmarkerscd200.html) positive, *BRAF* V600E mutated ([Diagnostics (Basel) 2023;13:271](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/36673081))
* [Rosai Dorfman disease](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/lymphnodessinushistiocytosiswithmassivelymph.html):
  + [S100](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsS100.html) positive ([Diagnostics (Basel) 2023;13:271](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/36673081))
* [Malakoplakia](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/kidneymalakoplakia.html):
  + Michaelis-Gutmann basophilic inclusions containing calcium and iron positive for [von Kossa](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainscalcium.html) stain and [Perls stain](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsiron.html) ([Diagnostics (Basel) 2023;13:271](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/36673081))
* [Hereditary cystinosis](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/bonemarrowuncommonstorage.html):
  + Clinical picture variable, due to defective amino acid cysteine resulting in histiocytic accumulation

Board review style question #1

[![](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/imgau/kidneycrystalstoringhistioMicroLimperio5.jpg)](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/imgau/kidneycrystalstoringhistioMicroLimperio5.jpg)

Which of the following immunostains is represented in the image shown above and would be positive in the foam cells that characterize crystal storing histiocytosis?

1. CD1a
2. CD68
3. CD138
4. S100

Board review style answer #1

**B.** CD68 is a marker of macrophage lineage and highlights the foam cells containing the crystals of renal crystal storing histiocytosis (CSH). Answer D is incorrect because S100 can be positive in histiocytes associated with various tumor and tumor-like conditions (e.g., Langerhans cell histiocytosis [LCH] and Rosai Dorfman disease), aiding in the differentiation of other entities. Answer A is incorrect because CD1a is positive in Langerhans cells, aiding in the differential diagnosis of LCH. Answer C is incorrect because CD138 is a marker of plasma cells, which can contain globular hyaline material in their cytoplasm (Russell bodies and Mott cells) due to immunoglobulin storage. This distinction helps differentiate plasma cells from the foam histiocytes seen in CSH.

Board review style question #2

Which of the following is the renal compartment most frequently involved in crystal storing histiocytosis?

1. Glomeruli
2. Interstitium
3. Tubules
4. Vessels

Board review style answer #2

**B.** Interstitium is the most frequent site involved by the foam histiocytes of crystal storing histiocytosis. Answer A is incorrect because glomeruli are involved in a minority of the cases showing a membranoproliferative pattern, to be distinguished from crystalline glomerulopathy in which crystals are stored within the cytoplasm of glomerular cells (endothelial, mesangial cells and podocytes). Answer C is incorrect because tubules can accumulate crystals composed of monoclonal immunoglobulins or light chains; however this condition is classified as light chain proximal tubulopathy. Answer D is incorrect because vessels are rarely affected by crystal storage disease.
