Definition / general
  • Also known as amebic liver abscess (ALA)
  • Caused by infection with the anaerobic protozoan Entamoeba histolytica
Essential features
  • Caused by protozoan Entamoeba histolytica through fecal - oral transmission
  • Diagnosis is made by correlating laboratory, imaging and histologic features including necrotic debris with amebic organisms and mononuclear inflammation
  • Prognosis is generally good with antibiotics
Terminology
  • Other names: amebiasis, amebic liver abscess (when present in liver)
ICD coding
  • ICD-10: A06.4 - amebic liver abscess
Epidemiology
  • Common in tropics (e.g., Asia, Africa, Central and South America)
    • Second leading cause of parasite disease related deaths in those regions (World J Hepatol 2024;16:316)
  • Male predilection with peak incidence at ~40 years of age
  • Alcohol consumption is strongly associated with ALA and can be seen in up to 85% of patients (World J Hepatol 2024;16:316)
Sites
  • Intestine, liver
  • Less commonly lung or brain
Pathophysiology
  • Life cycle comprises 2 stages: amebic cyst and trophozoite (Virulence 2023;14:2158656)
  • Major cause of infection due to contaminated water or food with Entamoeba histolytica cysts or person to person contact
  • Trophozoites reside in the intestinal lumen and feed on bacterial organisms
  • Invasion of the intestinal mucosa or blood vessels can lead to the involvement of distant sites
Etiology
  • Fecal - oral route

Clinical features
  • 90% of patients infected with Entamoeba histolytica are asymptomatic (Can J Gastroenterol Hepatol 2018;2018:4601420)
  • ALA presents within 2 - 4 weeks of infection with symptoms consisting of diarrhea, fever and aching right upper quadrant pain
  • 3 radiographic patterns of abscess have been reported (types I - III; see Radiology description) (Abdom Radiol (NY) 2021;46:4148)
    • Type I: typically more severe and presents acutely
    • Type II / III: delayed presentations with mild to moderate disease
  • Complications of ALA include fistula formation involving surrounding organs, amebic empyema / lung abscess, septic emboli and secondary bacterial infection (World J Hepatol 2024;16:316, Virulence 2023;14:2158656)
Diagnosis
  • Serologic testing (e.g., enzyme linked immunosorbent assay [ELISA])
    • Antiamebic antibodies can be found in 95% of cases and may persist up to 6 - 12 months after infection eradication (World J Hepatol 2024;16:316)
  • Imaging studies (e.g., ultrasonography, computed tomography [CT] scan)
  • Stool examination
    • 10 - 40% of patients have concurrent intestinal amebiasis (World J Hepatol 2024;16:316)
    • PCR studies can be a sensitive detection method
Laboratory
  • May show leukocytosis, elevated transaminases and elevated alkaline phosphatase
Radiology description
  • Type I (66%): absent or incomplete abscess wall with irregular edges and enhancement (Abdom Radiol (NY) 2021;46:4148)
  • Type II (28%): complete abscess wall with rim enhancement
  • Type III (6%): nonenhancing abscess wall

Prognostic factors
  • Generally favorable prognosis with recovery in most patients
  • Disease outcome is associated with single nucleotide polymorphisms within the protein 2 (kerp2) locus (World J Clin Cases 2022;10:13157)
  • Recurrence is seen in ~9% of cases posttreatment and is associated with abscesses > 10 cm in size, the presence of bacterium Prevotella, carriers of resistance genes (nim), Entamoeba histolytica genotypes and elevated matrix metalloproteinase levels (World J Hepatol 2024;16:316)
Case reports
  • 34 year old man from Venezuela with amebic liver abscess (Cureus 2024;16:e53902)
  • 54 year old man with COVID and concurrent amebic liver abscess (BMC Infect Dis 2021;21:1134)
  • 66 year old man with recurrent with amebic liver abscess (Clin Case Rep 2023;11:e8138)
Treatment
  • Metronidazole and paromomycin (luminal agent)
  • Percutaneous drainage can be considered in patients who fail medical therapy or have signs of impending rupture (World J Hepatol 2024;16:316)
  • Surgical intervention in cases of ALA rupture with diffuse peritonitis
Gross description
  • Cystic lesion often in the right liver lobe with fibrous capsule containing thick, brown fluid often resembling anchovy paste
Microscopic (histologic) description
  • Abundant necrotic debris with amebic organisms and mononuclear inflammation most frequently found at the edge of the lesion
  • Trophozoites (40 - 60 micrometers) have round, eccentric nuclei and abundant eosinophilic to amphophilic foamy cytoplasm, which may harbor ingested red blood cells

Differential diagnosis
  • Pyogenic liver abscess:
    • Inflammation with neutrophilic predominance
    • Associated with diabetes, older age and previous liver surgery
    • Negative serology for Entamoeba histolytica
  • Echinococcal cyst:
    • Eosinophilia on labs (favors echinococcal cyst) and imaging can be used to differentiate
  • Macrophages:
    • Can mimic trophozoites
    • CD68 positive
Board review style question #1


Which of the following features is typical of the lesion shown in the image above, if found within the liver?

  1. Anchovy paste-like cystic contents
  2. Abundant accompanying neutrophilic exudate
  3. CD68 positive cells
  4. Solid, well circumscribed mass on gross examination
Board review style answer #1
A. Anchovy paste-like cystic contents. The organism in the image is Entamoeba histolytica and this sample represents an amebic liver abscess (ALA), which typically presents as a cystic mass with contents similar to anchovy paste. Answer D is incorrect because ALA does not present as a solid but rather a cystic mass. Answer B is incorrect because ALA is not associated with abundant neutrophilic exudate as can be seen with pyogenic liver abscess. Answer C is incorrect because Entamoeba histolytica trophozoites are PAS positive and do not stain with CD68 like macrophages, which can mimic trophozoites.

Board review style question #2
Which of the following is a major risk factor for amebic liver abscess?

  1. Excessive alcohol consumption
  2. Female sex
  3. Old age
  4. Recreational drug use
Board review style answer #2
A. Excessive alcohol consumption. Excessive alcohol consumption is seen in up to 85% of patients and is considered to be a risk factor for amebic liver abscess. Answers B and C are incorrect because this disease is usually seen in younger to middle aged men. Answer D is incorrect because recreational drug use is not a major risk factor for amebic liver abscess.
