Definition / general

* Extramedullary hematopoiesis (EMH) of the liver is the formation of blood cellular components in the liver as opposed to the bone marrow
* Occurs physiologically in fetal development and pathologically, usually when bone marrow function is compromised or cannot maintain normal red blood cell levels

Essential features

* Commonly occurs when diseases of the blood or bone marrow cause anemia
* May present with hepatomegaly or rarely, a tumor-like mass in the liver
* Frequently asymptomatic; symptoms usually arise from local mass effects or associated underlying diseases

Terminology

* Hematopoiesis occurring outside the medulla of the bone

ICD coding

* ICD-10: [D75.89](https://www.icd10data.com/ICD10CM/Codes/D50-D89/D70-D77/D75-/D75.89) - other specified diseases of blood and blood forming organs

Epidemiology

* Reported median age is 50 years with near equal gender distribution ([Am J Clin Pathol 2022;158:277](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/35511690))
* Reported median age of patients with idiopathic EMH is 71 years, with equal gender distribution ([Blood Cancer J 2018;8:119](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/30455416))

Sites

* Pathologic EMH commonly involves the lymph nodes, spleen and liver ([Blood Cancer J 2018;8:119](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/30455416), [J Med Imaging Radiat Oncol 2012;56:538](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/23043573))
  + Other uncommon locations, such as the central nervous system, ovaries, lungs and pleural space, pericardium, paravertebral regions and nasopharynx and paranasal sinuses, have been reported
  + Liver is the second most common organ, constituting 25% of all EMHs in one series
* EMH can also be seen in hepatocellular adenoma, hepatoblastoma, hepatocellular carcinoma and vascular tumors, including hemangioma, infantile hepatic hemangioendothelioma and angiosarcoma ([Int J Surg Pathol 2022;30:339](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/34665056))

Pathophysiology

* Physiologic extramedullary hematopoiesis
  + Occurs during fetal development in multiple locations, including the yolk sac, para-aortic region, liver and placenta, before moving almost completely to the marrow shortly before birth; it may persist in the presence of neonatal hepatitis or anemia, particularly in thalassemic patients ([Stem Cell Res Ther 2021;12:139](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/33597015))
* Pathologic extramedullary hematopoiesis
  + Not fully understood
  + Mobilization of hematopoietic stem cells from the bone marrow to extramedullary organs via peripheral blood due to changes in the bone marrow microenvironment and body cytokine milieu; occurs in malignancy, anemia, infection and metabolic stress states ([Cell Mol Life Sci 2020;77:2723](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/31974657), [Front Hematol 2024;3:1371823](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/39668982))
  + Specific cytokines, such as KIT ligands, CXCL12, G-CSF, IL1 family members, LIF, TNFα and CXCR2, may contribute to the migration of circulating hematopoietic stem cells to the liver ([Cytokine 2015;76:214](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/26093947), [Stem Cells Dev 2012;21:2142](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/22121892), [Exp Mol Med 2024;56:549](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/38443597), [Mol Med Rep 2016;13:587](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/26648325))

Etiology

* Physiologic in utero ([J Blood Med 2010;1:13](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/22282679))
* Pathologic causes include ([Am J Clin Pathol 2022;158:277](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/35511690), [Blood Cancer J 2018;8:119](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/30455416), [J Blood Med 2010;1:13](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/22282679))
  + Hematologic neoplasms: myeloproliferative neoplasms, myelodysplastic syndromes, acute myeloid leukemia, lymphomas and plasma cell neoplasms
  + Anemia: hemolytic anemias, thalassemia, aplastic anemia
  + Bone marrow replacement conditions, such as metastatic malignancies
  + Infection
  + EMH has been reported in solid tumors like breast cancer, lung cancer, renal cell carcinoma, colon cancer and Kaposi sarcoma ([Cancer Manag Res 2018;10:1461](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/29922090), [Exp Mol Med 2024;56:549](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/38443597))

Clinical features

* Hepatomegaly: usually diffuse but can rarely be focal and mimic a tumor ([Br J Radiol 1999;72:906](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/10645201), [Diagn Cytopathol 1997;16:51](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/9034738), [Skeletal Radiol 2012;41:911](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/22101909))
* Often asymptomatic, with symptoms usually relating to mass effects rather than liver dysfunction; nonspecific abdominal complaints are the most common symptoms ([Skeletal Radiol 2012;41:911](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/22101909))
* When splenectomy is used to treat extramedullary hematopoiesis, a subsequent increase in liver extramedullary hematopoiesis often occurs ([Acta Haematol 1991;85:184](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/1677228), [Am J Med 1991;90:111](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/1986577))

Diagnosis

* Clinical diagnosis can be made based on history, clinical examination and imaging ([Clin Radiol 2020;75:565](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/31973940))
* Definitive diagnosis can be made by tissue or fine needle aspiration biopsy

Radiology description

* Usually diffuse hepatomegaly but occasionally focal
* Computed tomography (CT): mild or absent contrast enhancement ([Clin Radiol 2020;75:565](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/31973940))
* Magnetic resonance imaging (MRI)
  + Newer / more active lesions: T2 hyperintense with mild to moderate contrast enhancement
  + Older / less active lesions: T2 hypointense with absent to mild contrast enhancement ([Cancer Imaging 2006;6:182](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/17208673))
* Radionuclide scintigraphy: usually diffuse but rarely focal increased liver uptake ([Eur J Nucl Med Mol Imaging 2011;38:166](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/20625724), [Semin Nucl Med 1985;15:239](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/2994233))

Prognostic factors

* EMH does not affect the prognosis of patients and the follow up outcomes of EMH are equally acceptable in the context of hematological diseases
* None of the patients with idiopathic EMH, an incidental finding during the evaluation of unrelated symptoms, harbored occult malignancies or subsequently developed myeloproliferative neoplasms or other myeloid malignancies
  + Extensive investigation targeting myeloproliferative neoplasms or other malignancies in idiopathic EMH is not suggested and simple monitoring might be adequate ([Blood Cancer J 2018;8:119](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/30455416))

Case reports

* 30 year old woman, 50 year old woman and 52 year old man with extramedullary hematopoiesis and hepatic masses ([World J Clin Cases 2022;10:6626](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/35979290))
* 33 year old woman with idiopathic myelofibrosis and intrahepatic extramedullary hematopoiesis mimicking a hypervascular hepatic neoplasm ([Korean J Radiol 2008;9:S34](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/18607123))
* 35 year old woman with idiopathic myelofibrosis and liver mass mimicking intrahepatic neoplasm ([Dig Liver Dis 2020;52:1205](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/32402745))
* 52 year old woman with 2.5 cm, 5 cm and 7 cm liver masses and normal serum alpha fetoprotein (AFP) ([Arch Pathol Lab Med 2003;127:631](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/12708916))
* 57 year old woman with liver allograft mass ([Radiol Case Rep 2023;18:2325](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/37179804))
* 59 year old man with mismatch repair deficient colon cancer on adjuvant chemotherapy ([Cureus 2021;13:e12899](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/33654584))

Treatment

* Management depends on the severity of symptoms and clinical condition of the patient
* Individualized nonstandard approach, lacking high quality clinical evidence
* If asymptomatic and the anatomic location is not at risk, wait and watch strategy may be adopted
* If symptomatic, hydroxyurea, radiation and transfusion are sometimes used ([Exp Ther Med 2016;12:3859](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/28101170))

Gross description

* Liver may present with hepatomegaly or a localized soft mass lesion with a reddish appearance if a resection or autopsy case is received

Microscopic (histologic) description

* Clusters of megakaryocytes, myeloid precursors and erythroid precursors in any combination ([Am J Clin Pathol 2022;158:277](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/35511690))
* Morphology of precursor hematopoietic cells is similar to physiologic bone marrow hematopoietic cells
* Physiologic fetal liver extramedullary hematopoiesis classically occurs with erythropoiesis in sinusoids and myelopoiesis around portal tracts ([Scand J Immunol 1989;30:399](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/2683033))
* Pathologic extramedullary hematopoiesis occurs in the sinusoids and the space of Disse
  + Portal tract involvement develops as a late feature or in extensive cases ([Am J Clin Pathol 2022;158:277](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/35511690))
  + May also appear as a focal mass lesion mimicking a neoplastic process
* Grading of EMH in the liver can aid in identifying patients who may have an underlying hematologic condition ([Am J Clin Pathol 2022;158:277](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/35511690))

Cytology description

* Megakaryocytes with large, multilobated hyperchromatic nuclei and abundant cytoplasm are present, admixed with orthochromic and polychromatophilic normoblasts as well as myeloid precursors at various stages of maturation ([Diagn Cytopathol 2006;34:807](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/17115434))

Sample pathology report

* Liver, biopsy:
  + Liver with extensive extramedullary hematopoiesis (see comment)
  + Comment: This liver biopsy shows marked extramedullary hematopoiesis in the sinusoids of the lobular parenchyma. The portal architecture is preserved and there is minimal chronic inflammatory infiltrate in portal areas. Acidophil bodies, steatosis, ballooning degeneration, Mallory-Denk bodies, ground glass cytoplasmic changes or cholestasis are not seen. Immunohistochemical stains for CD61, CD71 and myeloperoxidase (MPO) highlight the megakaryocytes and the erythroid and myeloid precursor cells. These findings are often found in conditions such as hematologic disorders (e.g., myeloproliferative neoplasms, hemoglobinopathies and hemolytic anemia), infections and other bone marrow replacement conditions (e.g., bone metastases with malignancies). Correlation with clinical findings is suggested.

Differential diagnosis

* [Abscess / infections](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/liveramebicabscess.html):
  + Single or multiple cavities; may have fibrous capsule
  + Necrotic material with numerous neutrophils with or without lymphocytes and macrophages but not megakaryocytes or erythroid precursors
* [Lymphoproliferative disorders](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/lymphomapolymorphicimmunedeficiency.html):
  + Do not affect all three lineages; typically involve the lymphoid or myeloid lineages and occasionally megakaryocytes or erythroid precursors
* [Carcinoma](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/livertumorhcc.html):
  + Cancer cells with irregular nuclear contours and hyperchromatic nuclei usually occur in large clusters or sheets rather than singly or in small groups like megakaryocytes
  + Erythroid and lymphoid precursors do not accompany carcinoma cells
  + Negative megakaryocytic marker (such as [CD61](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/cdmarkerscd61.html)) and positive epithelial markers (such as [AE1 / AE3](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsae1ae3.html) and [CAM5.2](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainscam52.html))

Board review style question #1

[![](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/imgau/livertumorEMHZhangBRQ1.jpeg)](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/imgau/livertumorEMHZhangBRQ1.jpeg)

A 35 year old man with a medical history of idiopathic warm hemolytic anemia treated with long term steroids presents to the emergency department with right lower quadrant abdominal pain and fever. He has a new leukocytosis of 19,000/mm3 and longstanding anemia with a hemoglobin of 7.5 mg/dL. CT scan of the abdomen shows signs suggestive of appendicitis, a 2 cm liver mass and a 13 cm diffusely enlarged spleen. He undergoes appendectomy and recovers well. A subsequent core biopsy of his liver mass shows the following histologic morphologies (see image above). What is the diagnosis of this liver mass?

1. Ectopic spleen
2. Extramedullary hematopoiesis
3. Liver abscess
4. Liver lymphoproliferative neoplasm

Board review style answer #1

**B**. Extramedullary hematopoiesis. Megakaryocytes, myeloid and erythroid precursors are visible within the liver parenchyma. The small liver mass is due to extramedullary hematopoiesis, likely secondary to the patient's hemolytic anemia. This condition usually presents with diffuse liver enlargement and the tumor-like mass is an uncommon manifestation. Liver extramedullary hematopoiesis usually causes symptoms due to mass effects rather than organ dysfunction. This liver mass was identified incidentally. Answer C is incorrect because a liver abscess would contain necrotic debris and neutrophils instead of megakaryocytes and erythroid and myeloid precursors. Answer D is incorrect because lymphoproliferative neoplasms typically involve only lymphoid or myeloid lineages. Rarely, these neoplasms can originate from megakaryocytes or erythroid precursors. These disorders do not affect all 3 lineages. Answer A is incorrect because the splenic architecture, which includes red pulp, white pulp and the marginal zone, is absent.

A 65 year old woman with a medical history of resected hepatocellular carcinoma, myelofibrosis and hypertension presents with 6 months of reduced appetite and nausea after eating. Abdominal ultrasound shows a diffusely enlarged liver (transverse length: 28 cm) and spleen (craniocaudal length: 14 cm). The patient's blood tests are remarkable for a white blood cell count of 3,000/mm3, hemoglobin of 6.5 g/dL, positive HBsAb, negative HBsAg and negative HBcAg. A core liver biopsy is performed showing sinusoids with trilineage of hematopoietic megakaryocytes and erythroid and myeloid precursors (first image). Immunohistochemical stains for CD61, CD71 and myeloperoxidase (fourth image) highlight the megakaryocytes (second image) as well as the erythroid (third image) and myeloid (fourth image) precursor cells. What is the most likely cause of her hepatomegaly?

1. Chronic hepatitis B infection
2. Extramedullary hematopoiesis
3. Hepatocellular carcinoma
4. Lymphoma

Board review style answer #2

**B**. Extramedullary hematopoiesis. Liver sinusoids contain megakaryocytes and erythroid and myeloid precursors. The clinical history of anemia and myelofibrosis is the likely cause. Positive CD61, CD71 and myeloperoxidase (MPO) immunohistochemical stains further confirm the diagnosis. Answer A is incorrect because chronic hepatitis B infection typically shows portal chronic inflammation with or without interface hepatitis, fibrosis and ground glass appearance of hepatocytes rather than intrasinusoidal hematopoiesis. Answer C is incorrect because hepatocellular carcinoma typically shows a loss of portal architecture and frequently displays a thickened trabecular architecture. The hepatic architecture is preserved in this case. Answer D is incorrect because lymphomas generally involve only lymphoid or myeloid lineages. Rarely, lymphomas can arise from megakaryocytes or erythroid precursors. They do not affect all 3 hematopoietic lineages.
