Definition / general

* Poorly differentiated neuroendocrine carcinomas are high grade, primary malignant epithelial neoplasms of liver or intrahepatic bile ducts with neuroendocrine differentiation
* Divided into small cell carcinoma (SCNET) and large cell carcinoma (LCNEC)

Essential features

* Exceedingly rare, virtually a diagnosis of exclusion after ruling out the possibility of metastasis
* Can be a component of mixed neuroendocrine - nonneuroendocrine neoplasm (MiNEN) with hepatocellular carcinoma or cholangiocarcinoma
* Ki67 proliferation index > 20% (typically > 50%) with abundant apoptosis and necrosis

Terminology

* Poorly differentiated neuroendocrine carcinoma, including small cell carcinoma (SCNEC) and large cell carcinoma (LCNEC)
* Mixed neuroendocrine - nonneuroendocrine neoplasm (MiNEN)
* Not recommended: carcinoid tumor of the liver, atypical carcinoid tumor of the liver

ICD coding

* ICD-O: [8246/3](https://iris.who.int/bitstream/handle/10665/96612/9789241548496_eng.pdf?sequence=1#page=68) - neuroendocrine carcinoma, NOS
* ICD-11
  + [2C12.0Y](https://icd.who.int/browse/2024-01/mms/en#1605020868%2Fother) - other specified malignant neoplasm of liver
  + [2C12.1Y](https://icd.who.int/browse/2024-01/mms/en#1938186540%2Fother) - other specified malignant neoplasms of intrahepatic bile ducts
  + [XH9SY0](https://icd.who.int/browse/2024-01/mms/en#1294602000) - small cell neuroendocrine carcinoma
  + [XH0NL5](https://icd.who.int/browse/2024-01/mms/en#1278312485) - large cell neuroendocrine carcinoma

Epidemiology

* Exceedingly rare (0.3% of neuroendocrine neoplasms, Endocrine 2017;58:368, [Endocr Relat Cancer 2010;17:909](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/20702725), [Case Rep Oncol 2023;16:681](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/37933308))
* Usually not associated with chronic liver disease / cirrhosis

Sites

* Liver and intrahepatic bile ducts

Pathophysiology

* Not known; 2 hypotheses ([Case Rep Oncol 2023;16:681](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/37933308))
  + Originated from neuroendocrine cells in the intrahepatic bile duct epithelium
  + Dedifferentiated from other malignant cells (malignant hepatocytes)

Etiology

* Not known

Clinical features

* Not well known due to rarity
* It is extremely important to rule out metastatic disease from other organs (e.g., gastrointestinal [GI] tract, pancreas and lung) by clinical workup (e.g., endoscopy, colonoscopy) and imaging study (e.g., gallium 68 dotatate scan and positron emission tomography / computed tomography [PET / CT])

Diagnosis

* Diagnosis of exclusion after ruling out metastasis (fluorodeoxyglucose [FDG] PET scan can be helpful)
* Due to its rarity and lack of characteristic imaging appearance, diagnosis is made by cytology / fine needle biopsy or on surgical specimens

Radiology description

* Large solitary mass with necrosis
* Hyperenhancement in the arterial phase and washout in the portal venous phase due to rich blood supply (similar to hepatocellular carcinoma [HCC]) ([Case Rep Oncol 2023;16:681](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/37933308))
* Gallium 68 dotatate PET / computed tomography (CT) scan may show uptake in the lesion and can be used to rule out metastasis

Prognostic factors

* Not known; generally considered poor prognosis

Case reports

* 22 year old woman with primary hepatic NEC ([Case Rep Oncol 2023;16:681](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/37933308))
* 39 year old man with mixed NEC and HCC ([Front Surg 2021;8:678853](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/34336917))
* 76 year old man with poorly differentiated HCC and NEC ([Am Surg 2016;82:1121](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/28206942))
* 84 year old woman with mixed NEC and HCC ([World J Surg Oncol 2021;19:78](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/33726764))

Treatment

* Surgical resection
* Chemotherapy: platinum based regimen (not established)

Gross description

* Typically large solitary mass with areas of hemorrhage and necrosis; may be cystic

Frozen section description

* High grade malignant neoplasm with necrosis, apoptosis and mitotic activity

Microscopic (histologic) description

* Similar histomorphology of poorly differentiated neuroendocrine carcinoma of anywhere in the body
* Solid sheet-like proliferation of the tumor cells with geographic area of necrosis, high mitotic figures and abundant apoptosis ([Front Oncol 2013;3:2](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/23346552))
  + Small cell NEC: cellular pleomorphism with high N:C ratio, nuclear atypia, hyperchromasia, nuclear molding and frequent mitotic figures
  + Large cell NEC: more rounded, marked nuclear pleomorphism with prominent nucleoli
* May have signet ring cell morphology with intracytoplasmic vacuoles negative for mucin but positive for neuroendocrine markers ([Semin Liver Dis 2010;30:422](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/20960381))
* If mixed neuroendocrine - nonneuroendocrine neoplasm (MiNEN), 2 types have been reported ([J Chin Med Assoc 2009;72:430](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/19686999))
  + Collision type tumor: 2 simultaneous but histologically distinct tumors without histologic admixture
  + Combined type tumor: both components intermingle with each other and cannot be separated in the transitional area

Positive stains

* [Cytokeratins](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsckgeneral.html) ([CK AE1 / AE3](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsae1ae3.html), [CAM5.2](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainscam52.html), [CK8](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsck8.html) / [CK18](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsck18.html)): may show perinuclear dot-like staining pattern
* [Synaptophysin](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainssynaptophysin.html): usually positive; rarely weak or focal
* [Chromogranin](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainschromogranin.html): usually positive; can be weak and patchy or can be almost absent
* [INSM1](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsinsm1.html): new marker of neuroendocrine differentiation; may have better sensitivity in neuroendocrine carcinoma ([Am J Pathol 2015;185:3164](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/26482608))
* [p53](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsp53.html): aberrant expression, either overexpression or null type expression ([PLoS One 2017;12:e0187667](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/29112960))
* No site specific IHC stain for hepatic NEC: panel still can be used to exclude metastasis ([Torbenson: Tumors of the Liver (AFIP Atlas of Tumor Pathology), 4th Edition, 2018](https://www.amazon.com/exec/obidos/ASIN/1933477415/pathologyoutl-20))
* Caveat
  + Occasional HCCs can show aberrant staining for [synaptophysin](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainssynaptophysin.html) or [CD56](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/cdmarkerscd56.html), which does not qualify as the combined HCC - NEC ([Torbenson: Biopsy Interpretation of the Liver, 4th Edition, 2021](https://www.amazon.com/exec/obidos/ASIN/197515729X/pathologyoutl-20))
  + Neuroendocrine tumor can show focal [arginase](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsarginase1.html), [HepPar1](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsheppar1.html), [glypican 3](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsglypican3.html) and [AFP](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsafp.html) staining ([Torbenson: Biopsy Interpretation of the Liver, 4th Edition, 2021](https://www.amazon.com/exec/obidos/ASIN/197515729X/pathologyoutl-20), [Torbenson: Tumors of the Liver (AFIP Atlas of Tumor Pathology), 4th Edition, 2018](https://www.amazon.com/exec/obidos/ASIN/1933477415/pathologyoutl-20))
  + [TTF1](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsttf1.html) can be positive in NEC regardless of the site of origin ([Mod Pathol 2000;13:238](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/10757334))

Negative stains

* [RB1](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsrb.html): loss of [Rb](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsrb.html) stain ([Cancer Discov 2022;12:692](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/34880079))
* Hepatocellular markers: [HepPar](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsheppar1.html), [arginase1](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsarginase1.html), [glypican 3](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsglypican3.html), unless mixed tumor (can show patchy positivity) ([Torbenson: Biopsy Interpretation of the Liver, 4th Edition, 2021](https://www.amazon.com/exec/obidos/ASIN/197515729X/pathologyoutl-20), [Torbenson: Tumors of the Liver (AFIP Atlas of Tumor Pathology), 4th Edition, 2018](https://www.amazon.com/exec/obidos/ASIN/1933477415/pathologyoutl-20))
* [Beta catenin](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsbetacatenin.html) ([J Hepatocell Carcinoma 2018;5:61](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/29984212))

Sample pathology report

* Liver, mass biopsy:
  + Involved by poorly differentiated neuroendocrine carcinoma, consistent with small cell carcinoma (see comment)
  + Comment: Primary neuroendocrine tumor of the liver is exceedingly rare. Radiologic and clinical correlation is recommended to exclude the possibility of metastatic disease.

Differential diagnosis

* [Metastatic neuroendocrine carcinoma](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/livertumormet.html) from other sites:
  + GI tract: [CDX2](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainscdx2.html) with or without [SATB2](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainssatb2.html)
  + Pancreas: [PAX8](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainspax8.html)
  + Lung: [TTF1](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsttf1.html)
  + Prostate: [NKX3.1](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsnkx3.html)
* [Well differentiated neuroendocrine tumor (WDNET)](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/livertumorcarcinoid.html), grade 3:
  + Organoid architecture
  + Presence of low grade areas (tumor heterogeneity) favors WDNET
* Poorly differentiated [hepatocellular carcinoma](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/livertumorHCC.html):
  + Positive for hepatocellular markers
* [Hepatoblastoma](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/livertumorhepatoblastoma.html):
  + Positive for [beta catenin](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsbetacatenin.html) and hepatocellular markers
* **Lymphoma**:
  + Negative for [cytokeratin](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsckgeneral.html) and neuroendocrine markers
* [Melanoma](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/skintumormelanocyticmelanoma.html):
  + Positive for [SOX10](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainssox10.html), [HMB45](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainshmb45.html), [MelanA](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsmart1.html), [PRAME](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsPRAME.html)
  + Negative for [cytokeratin](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsckgeneral.html) and neuroendocrine markers
* Metastatic small round blue cell tumors: [Ewing sarcoma](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/boneewing.html), [desmoplastic small round blue cell tumor](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/softtissueDSRCT.html), [alveolar rhabdomyosarcoma](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/softtissuealvrhabdo.html), etc.:
  + Negative for cytokeratin and neuroendocrine markers

Board review style question #1

[![](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/imgau/livertumorNEcarcinomaLee02.jpg)](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/imgau/livertumorNEcarcinomaLee02.jpg)

A large hepatic mass, shown in the image above, is biopsied. The tumor cells are positive for cytokeratin, synaptophysin and chromogranin. The Ki67 proliferative index is increased to more than 80%. Which of the following is characteristic of neuroendocrine carcinoma of the liver?

1. Hepatic primary is more common than metastasis
2. It can be seen as a component of hepatocellular carcinoma or cholangiocarcinoma
3. It is a well differentiated neuroendocrine tumor
4. It usually arises in the background of a cirrhotic liver
5. Tumor cells are typically positive for arginase, HepPar1 or glypican 3

Board review style answer #1

**B.** It can be seen as a component of hepatocellular carcinoma or cholangiocarcinoma. This tumor is a high grade neuroendocrine carcinoma. Primary hepatic neuroendocrine carcinoma is exceedingly rare and the diagnosis should be made after carefully excluding possible metastatic disease. It can be a component of mixed neuroendocrine - nonneuroendocrine neoplasm (MiNEN) with hepatocellular carcinoma or cholangiocarcinoma.
Answer A is incorrect because metastatic lesions in the liver are more common than hepatic primary. Answer C is incorrect because it is a poorly differentiated neuroendocrine carcinoma (PDNEC). Answer D is incorrect because neither primary hepatic PDNEC nor metastatic neuroendocrine neoplasms are usually found in the noncirrhotic background. Answer E is incorrect because hepatic PDNEC does not typically express hepatocellular markers unless it is mixed HCC - PDNEC.
