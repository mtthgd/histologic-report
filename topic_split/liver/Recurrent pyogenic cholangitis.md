Definition / general

* Recurrent attacks of obstructive cholangitis due to *Escherichia coli* infection in the setting of liver fluke infection or hepatolithiasis

Essential features

* Recurrent bile duct injury due to flukes, hepatoliths or bacterial infection
* Treatment often requires surgery
* Patients are at increased risk for cholangiocarcinoma

Terminology

* Sometimes termed “Oriental cholangiohepatitis” or “primary hepatolithiasis” ([Adv Anat Pathol 2011;18:318](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/21654363))

ICD coding

* ICD-10: [K83.09](https://www.icd10data.com/ICD10CM/Codes/K00-K95/K80-K87/K83-/K83.09) - other cholangitis

Epidemiology

* Generally occurs in Southeast Asia or in patients from that region but may occur elsewhere ([Neth J Med 2016;74:401](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/27905307), [Hawaii Med J 2007;66:9](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/17381035), [World J Surg 2005;29:858](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/15951933))

Etiology

* Patients often have liver flukes (e.g. *Clonorchis sinensis*) or hepatolithiasis as the initial inciting factor ([J Emerg Med 2012;43:e107](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/19959317))
* *E. coli*, found in the bile of most patients during acute attacks, appears to be the second inciting factor

Clinical features

* Usually occurs in young adults of either sex; more common in rural areas ([Abdom Radiol (NY) 2017;42:46](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/27770158))
* Symptoms: acute or recurrent bouts of suppurative cholangitis accompanied by abdominal pain, fever and jaundice ([Semin Liver Dis 2011;31:33](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/21344349))
* Patients are at increased risk for developing cholangiocarcinoma

Radiology description

* Dilation of extrahepatic bile ducts with relatively mild / no dilation of intrahepatic ducts, localized dilatation of the lobar or segmental bile ducts, increased periportal echogenicity, segmental hepatic atrophy, gallstones ([Clin Res Hepatol Gastroenterol 2012;36:515](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/22921337), [Abdom Radiol (NY) 2017;42:46](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/27770158))
* Peripheral ducts may taper abruptly ([Abdom Radiol (NY) 2017;42:2964](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/28660331))
* Localized intrahepatic segmental ductal stenosis may be present, especially in the lateral segment of the left lobe or posterior segment of the right hepatic lobe

Prognostic factors

* Patients with bilateral disease or who do not undergo operative treatment fare worse ([ISRN Surg 2013;2013:536081](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/23762626))

Case reports

* 39 year old Korean woman with acute cholecystitis ([Case Rep Gastrointest Med 2017;2017:8575674](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/28932607))
* 48 year old Vietnamese woman with abdominal pain ([Dig Dis Sci 2016;61:3147](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/26602913))
* 74 year old South Asian man with symptoms concerning for cholangiocarcinoma ([Int J Surg Case Rep 2012;3:235](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/22503913))

Treatment

* Some cases require surgery ([Int J Surg. 2014;12:789](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/24918695), [HPB (Oxford) 2016;18:821](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/27506993))
* Other cases may be managed endoscopically (stone extraction, sphincterotomy, stent placement) ([J Community Hosp Intern Med Perspect 2015;5:27858](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/26333855), [Dig Dis Sci 1997;42:865](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/9125665))

Microscopic (histologic) description

* Dilation and fibrosis of bile ducts with acute or chronic inflammation ([Adv Anat Pathol 2011;18:318](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/21654363))
* Other findings include hepatic / intraductal abscesses and secondary sclerosing cholangitis
* Liver flukes or bile duct stones may be seen

Sample pathology report

* Liver, right lobe, partial hepatectomy:
  + Bile ducts with acute and chronic inflammation, fibrosis and hepatolithiasis (see comment)
  + Background liver parenchyma with reactive change
  + Comment: The findings are compatible with the patient’s reported history of recurrent pyogenic cholangitis. The examined sections are negative for dysplasia or malignancy.

Differential diagnosis

* [Caroli syndrome](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/livercarolisdisease.html):
  + Congenital and therefore usually presents earlier in life
* [Primary sclerosing cholangitis](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/liverPSC.html):
  + Lesser degree of inflammation
  + Hepatoliths generally not present
* **Segmental cholangiectasia**:
  + High clinical and pathologic overlap, except this disease occurs in non-Asian patients ([Hum Pathol 2015;46:426](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/25600951))

A young Asian patient presents with recurrent abdominal pain. Workup indicates hepatolithiasis with obstructive cholangitis. Which bacteria is most likely contributing to his symptoms?

1. *Escherichia coli*
2. *Mycobacterium tuberculosis*
3. *Pseudomonas aeruginosa*

Board review style question #2

A 50 year old Asian woman is found to have a liver mass. Biopsy is consistent with cholangiocarcinoma. She endorses a history of recurrent abdominal pain and fevers. What is the most likely underlying diagnoses?

1. Benign recurrent intrahepatic cholestasis
2. Crohn’s disease
3. Longstanding drug induced liver injury
4. Primary biliary cholangitis
5. Recurrent pyogenic cholangitis

Board review style answer #2

**E**. Recurrent pyogenic cholangitis
