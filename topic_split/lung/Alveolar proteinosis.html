Definition / general
  • Pulmonary alveolar proteinosis (PAP) is a rare disease characterized by abnormal accumulation of acellular alveolar surfactant due to impaired surfactant clearance or abnormal surfactant production
  • Considered a tissue response to alveolar injury, not a specific entity, since primary, secondary and congenital forms exist
Essential features
  • PAP is the tissue pattern observed when there is impaired surfactant homeostasis, either due to decreased clearance by alveolar macrophages or abnormal production
  • PAP can be primary (usually due to autoantibodies directed against granulocyte-macrophage colony-stimulating factor [GM-CSF]), secondary (due to environmental exposure such as silica dust, hematolymphoid malignancy, adverse drug reaction, immunosuppression or infection) or congenital (due to mutations detrimental to surfactant or macrophage function)
  • Chest computed tomography (CT) shows crazy paving pattern (not specific to this entity)
  • Histology will show acellular and coarsely granular eosinophilic material in the alveoli with absent to mild associated fibrosis and minimal inflammation
Terminology
  • Alveolar lipoproteinosis, alveolar phospholipidosis, alveolar phospholipoproteinosis
ICD coding
  • ICD-10: J84 - other interstitial pulmonary diseases
Epidemiology
  • Slight male predominance, with a ratio of M:F = 2:1 in primary autoimmune PAP and M:F = 1.2:1 in secondary PAP (Int J Mol Sci 2024;25:7092)
  • Median age for diagnosis is 51 years but PAP occurs over a wide age range, including both infants and the elderly
  • Autoimmune PAP accounts for ~90% of all patients, due to GM-CSF autoantibodies (Nat Rev Dis Primers 2019;5:16)
Sites
  • Lungs
Pathophysiology
  • Pulmonary surfactant, secreted by type II cells, is composed of phospholipids, cholesterol and surfactant proteins A - D
  • It prevents alveolar collapse by reducing surface tension and provides protection from infection and inflammation
  • The balance between surfactant production and clearance maintains homeostasis, but this balance is disrupted in PAP
  • GM-CSF is a cytokine that provides signaling for the differentiation of alveolar macrophages
    • Disruption of GM-CSF signaling axis reduces alveolar macrophages, which are crucial in the maintenance of surfactant homeostasis; this imbalance results in the accumulation of lipoproteinaceous material in alveoli in primary autoimmune PAP
  • Other causes of PAP disrupt surfactant homeostasis through different mechanisms, such as toxic damage to alveolar macrophages from drugs or environmental exposure and genetic mutations affecting surfactant production or macrophage function (Int J Mol Sci 2024;25:7092)
Etiology
  • Can be congenital (2% of cases), primary (most commonly autoimmune) or secondary
  • Congenital cases are usually due to a mutation in a surfactant protein or GM-CSF receptor genes (e.g., CSF2RA or CSF2RB) (Respirology 2020;25:816)
  • Secondary PAP is associated with environmental exposures (silica dust, aluminum, fiberglass, chemicals, immunosuppressive or cytotoxic drugs), immunosuppression, malignancy (especially hematolymphoid neoplasms) and infection (Nocardia, mycobacteria, aspergillosis, human immunodeficiency virus [HIV])
  • Smoking increases the risk of developing PAP (Nat Rev Dis Primers 2019;5:16)

Clinical features
  • Patients with autoimmune PAP present as adults in their third to fifth decade, with or without respiratory symptoms (dyspnea, cough, sputum) and systemic symptoms (fatigue, weight loss)
  • Some cases of primary autoimmune PAP may be asymptomatic and discovered incidentally on imaging
  • Patients with hereditary PAP present in late infancy or childhood, with similar symptoms as above
  • Patients with secondary PAP present in the context of the underlying environmental exposure or other underlying clinical condition (Nat Rev Dis Primers 2019;5:16)
Diagnosis
  • Diagnosis is often made based on the combination of clinical presentation, imaging, bronchoalveolar lavage (BAL) and laboratory findings
    • Bronchoalveolar lavage: milky and opaque with cloudy sedimentation and translucent supernatant
    • Laboratory evidence of GM-CSF autoantibodies
  • Biopsy may be used for diagnosis in difficult cases (usually transbronchial biopsy, occasionally wedge biopsy)
  • References: Respir Care 2011;56:1016, N Engl J Med 1958;258:1123
Laboratory
  • Serum GM-CSF antibodies identified on ELISA or latex agglutination test are elevated in autoimmune PAP
  • Lactate dehydrogenase (LDH) is elevated in up to 80% of patients
  • C reactive protein (CRP) and erythrocyte sedimentation rate (ESR) are also elevated (Int J Mol Sci 2024;25:7092)
Radiology description
  • Chest Xray: diffuse symmetrical bilateral alveolar opacities in perihilar and basilar distribution; confluent infiltrates in all 5 lobes referred to as batwing or butterfly wings
  • High resolution CT: diffuse ground glass opacities with intralobular thickening, referred to as crazing paving pattern (Int J Mol Sci 2024;25:7092)

Prognostic factors
  • Usually does not progress to chronic fibrosis
  • Secondary PAP has a worse prognosis than primary PAP (Nat Rev Dis Primers 2019;5:16)
Case reports
  • 15 year old girl with a 4 month history of cough and dyspnea was diagnosed with alveolar proteinosis (Cureus 2023;15:e39254)
  • 31 year old pregnant woman with known alveolar proteinosis developed preeclampsia in the third trimester but overall had a favorable outcome (Medicina (Kaunas) 2022;58:984)
  • 51 year old woman who was diagnosed with alveolar proteinosis in the clinical setting of severe SARS-CoV-2 pneumonia (Case Rep Pulmonol 2022;2022:1842566)
Treatment
  • Asymptomatic patients with primary autoimmune PAP may be monitored without specific therapy
  • Whole lung lavage (also diagnostic) is standard of care in symptomatic primary and some secondary PAP
  • Emerging therapies include GM-CSF therapy and therapy targeting GM-CSF autoantibodies (plasmapheresis, rituximab) (Int J Mol Sci 2024;25:7092)

Microscopic (histologic) description
  • Alveoli are expanded by amorphous eosinophilic and coarsely granular material consisting of surfactant material and cellular debris from type II pneumocytes and necrotic alveolar macrophages
  • Cholesterol clefts and foamy macrophages may be present and associated with the exudate
  • Associated type 2 pneumocyte hyperplasia may be present
  • Absent to mild associated interstitial fibrosis with minimal associated chronic inflammation (Lancet Respir Med 2018;6:554)

Cytology description
  • Bronchoalveolar lavage commonly shows a paucicellular specimen with abundant extracellular granular to globular material, which is basophilic on Diff-Quik and eosinophilic on Pap stain (Diagn Cytopathol 2001;24:389)
  • Bronchoalveolar lavage specimens may also show foamy, enlarged macrophages with eosinophilic granules (Int J Mol Sci 2024;25:7092)

Positive stains
  • PAS is positive in granular exudate but is not specific and not required for diagnosis
  • Oil red O (nonspecific, positive in all causes of foamy macrophages in bronchoalveolar lavage)
Negative stains
  • Fungal stains such as GMS should always be done to exclude infectious mimics such as Pneumocystis and diffuse alveolar involvement by Histoplasma
Electron microscopy description
  • Exudate contains lamellar bodies, lipid particles and cellular debris

Sample pathology report
  • Lung, right upper lobe, biopsy:
    • Pulmonary alveolar proteinosis (see comment)
    • Comment: Sections of the lung show multifocal alveolar accumulation of coarsely granular eosinophilic material. The interstitium appears thin without evidence of significant interstitial fibrosis or inflammation. GMS fungal stain is negative. These findings support the above diagnosis.
Differential diagnosis
  • Pneumocystis pneumonia and rare alveolar filling pattern of histoplasmosis:
    • GMS shows presence of organisms

Board review style question #1
Which of the following is the characteristic bronchoalveolar lavage (BAL) fluid appearance in patients with alveolar proteinosis?

  1. BAL specimen is bloody
  2. BAL specimen is clear
  3. BAL specimen is milky
  4. BAL fluid is puruluent
Board review style answer #1
C. BAL fluid is milky in alveolar proteinosis due to the abnormal accumulation of surfactant phospholipids and proteins. Answer A is incorrect because bloody BAL is not typical of pulmonary alveolar proteinosis (PAP); instead, it is commonly seen in diffuse alveolar hemorrhage. Answer B is incorrect because a clear specimen is not commonly seen in PAP unless the patient is undergoing whole lung lavage, where saline is instilled until BAL appearance becomes clear. Answer D is incorrect because purulent fluid would be indicative of infection.

Board review style question #2


A 55 year old man presents with dyspnea and cough for 5 months. The imaging studies show nonspecific bilateral infiltrates. Histology is pictured above. GMS stain is negative. What is the diagnosis?

  1. Alveolar proteinosis
  2. Amyloidosis
  3. Pneumocystis infection
  4. Pulmonary edema
Board review style answer #2
A. Alveolar proteinosis demonstrates the classical histologic features of alveolar accumulation of coarsely granular eosinophilic material. Answer D is incorrect because the secretions in pulmonary edema are not granular. Answer C is incorrect because Pneumocystis is visible on GMS. Answer B is incorrect because amyloidosis does not cause alveolar deposits; amyloid deposits are found in the interstitium.
