Definition / general
  • Pulmonary Langerhans cell histiocytosis (PLCH) is a disease that involves abnormal clusters of monocytes, macrophages and dendritic cells and includes granulomatous lesions (Asian J Surg 2022;45:1441, Nat Med 2021;27:851)
  • Fibrosis in PLCH is bronchiolocentric (Zander: Pulmonary Pathology, 3rd Edition, 2024)
  • References: Asian J Surg 2022;45:1441, Diagn Cytopathol 2016;44:1102, Nat Med 2021;27:851
Essential features
  • PLCH is a rare disease that involves abnormal aggregates of monocytes, macrophages and dendritic cells (Asian J Surg 2022;45:1441)
  • PLCH is characterized by growth of Langerhans cells, which are a type of dendritic cell that expresses the surface receptor CD1a (Asian J Surg 2022;45:1441, Diagn Cytopathol 2016;44:1102)
  • In adults, PLCH is mostly seen in patients with a history of smoking and can be treated with smoking cessation (N Engl J Med 2000;342:1969, Adv Respir Med 2017;85:277)
  • Cause of PLCH lesions is a somatic activating mutation in the mitogen activated protein kinase (MAPK) pathway, which leads to increased growth of CD207+ / CD1a+ cells found in LCH lesions (Nat Med 2021;27:851)
Terminology
  • Langerhans cell histiocytosis was previously known as eosinophilic granuloma, Hand-Schüller-Christian disease, Letterer-Siwe disease and initially termed histiocytosis X (Br J Haematol 2022;199:728)
  • Now referred to as Langerhans cell histiocytosis (LCH) (Br J Haematol 2022;199:728)
  • LCH in the lung is called pulmonary Langerhans cell histiocytosis (PLCH)
ICD coding
  • ICD-10: C96.0 - multifocal and multisystemic (disseminated) Langerhans cell histiocytosis
    • Code used to indicate a diagnosis of Langerhans cell histiocytosis
    • Short description is multifocal and multisystemic Langerhans cell histiocytosis
    • This code is applicable to histiocytosis X, multisystemic and Letterer-Siwe disease
  • The following codes above C96.0 contain annotation back references that may be applicable to C96.0
    • ICD-10
      • C00 - D49 - neoplasms
      • C81 - C96 - malignant neoplasms of lymphoid, hematopoietic and related tissue
      • C96 - other and unspecified malignant neoplasms of lymphoid, hematopoietic and related tissue
Epidemiology
  • M > F (Br J Haematol 2022;199:728)
  • 79% White ethnic groups (Br J Haematol 2022;199:728)
  • Annual incidence is < 5 cases per million population (Mayo Clin Proc 2019;94:2054)
  • PLCH is mostly seen in adults (N Engl J Med 2000;342:1969)
  • Peak age of diagnosis: 20 - 40 years (UpToDate: Pulmonary Langerhans cell histiocytosis [Accessed 14 February 2025])
  • PLCH is mostly seen in young smokers (Adv Respir Med 2017;85:277)
  • > 90% of patients with PLCH are cigarette smokers (UpToDate: Pulmonary Langerhans cell histiocytosis [Accessed 14 February 2025])
  • References: Br J Haematol 2022;199:728, Mayo Clin Proc 2019;94:2054, Adv Respir Med 2017;85:277, N Engl J Med 2000;342:1969
Sites
  • Lung parenchyma
Pathophysiology
  • Activating somatic mutations occur in the MAPK pathway genes in 85% of cases of Langerhans cell histiocytosis, specifically in CD207+ cells within LCH lesions (Nat Med 2021;27:851)
  • Most notable mutation is BRAF V600E (Nat Med 2021;27:851)
  • Activation of the MAPK pathway leads to increased growth of CD207+ / CD1a+ cells that accumulate in LCH lesions (Nat Med 2021;27:851)
  • References: N Engl J Med 2018;379:856, Nat Med 2021;27:851
Etiology
  • Cause of Langerhans cell histiocytosis is genetic mutation (Br J Haematol 2022;199:728)
  • Presence of the B‐Raf proto‐oncogene, serine / threonine kinase BRAF V600E mutation in ~50% of cases and other somatic mutations activating the MAPK signaling pathway (Br J Haematol 2022;199:728)

Clinical features
  • Adults with PLCH often present with dyspnea and chest pain (Int J Clin Exp Pathol 2015;8:3354)
  • Cough with sputum (World J Clin Cases 2019;7:1892)
  • Loss of appetite and fatigue (World J Clin Cases 2019;7:1892)
Diagnosis
  • Contrast enhanced computed tomography (CT) scan of chest (Diagn Cytopathol 2016;44:1102)
  • Biopsy is commonly used to diagnose Langerhans cell histiocytosis (Diagn Cytopathol 2016;44:1102)
  • Bronchoalveolar lavage (BAL) cytology to assess the morphology of cells (Diagn Cytopathol 2016;44:1102)
  • Immunohistochemistry (Diagn Cytopathol 2016;44:1102)
Radiology description
  • Small nodules within lobules of the lung (Adv Respir Med 2017;85:277)
  • PLCH typically forms tree in bud lesions (Adv Respir Med 2017;85:277)
  • Can present with indented nodules and cystic lesions with thin and thick walls (Adv Respir Med 2017;85:277)

Prognostic factors
  • Overall prognosis for pulmonary LCH is good (Adv Respir Med 2017;85:277)
  • > 85% patients with PLCH survive 10 years (Adv Respir Med 2017;85:277)
Case reports
  • 29 year old woman with PLCH presented with lung, skin and oral lesions (Respirol Case Rep 2020;8:e00603)
  • 40 year old man with PLCH with cough and left sided pleuritic chest pain (N Engl J Med 2022;387:2449)
  • 52 year old man with PLCH with cough (World J Clin Cases 2019;7:1892)
Treatment
  • Smoking cessation (Adv Respir Med 2017;85:277)
  • Steroids (Respirol Case Rep 2020;8:e00603)
  • Cladribine or cytarabine therapy in progressive PLCH (Adv Respir Med 2017;85:277)
Gross description
  • Excisional biopsy in a patient with LCH shows small nodules and cysts (Int J Clin Exp Pathol 2015;8:3354)

Microscopic (histologic) description
  • Macrophages, eosinophils, fibrosis and granulomas are found on H&E
  • Number of eosinophils varies
  • Langerhans cells are large and have a pale, somewhat eosinophilic cytoplasm and a convoluted nucleus that has a longitudinal crease and looks like a coffee bean (Front Med (Lausanne) 2021;7:582581)
  • Older lesions become fibrotic
  • Can present as a stellate shaped scar

Positive stains
  • S100 in atypical histiocytes nuclear and cytoplasmic staining (Diagn Cytopathol 2016;44:1102)
  • CD1a has cytoplasmic and membranous positivity (Diagn Cytopathol 2016;44:1102)
  • Langerin (CD207) membranous staining (Pediatr Dev Pathol 2004;7:607)
Negative stains
  • CD68: Langerhans cells will be negative (not to be used as a diagnostic criterion because CD68 has also been found to be positive in some PLCH cases) (Pathol Res Pract 2008;204:315)
Electron microscopy description
  • On electron microscopy, Birbeck granules are seen within Langerhans cells (Front Med (Lausanne) 2021;7:582581)

Molecular / cytogenetics description
  • Most common mutation in PLCH patients is BRAF V600E (Nat Med 2021;27:851)
  • Other mutations in PLCH are NRAS, KRAS and MAP2K1 genes (Front Med (Lausanne) 2021;7:582581)
  • These mutations cause activation of the MAPK / extracellular signal regulated kinase (ERK) signaling pathway (Front Med (Lausanne) 2021;7:582581)

What is Langerhans cell histiocytosis?

Sample pathology report
  • Right lung, upper lobe; wedge biopsy:
    • Pulmonary Langerhans cell histiocytosis (PLCH) (see comment)
    • Comment: Lung biopsy specimen shows macrophages and eosinophils on H&E stain. Langerin, CD1a and S100 immunostains are positive, confirming the diagnosis of PLCH.
Differential diagnosis
  • Neoplasms, sarcoidosis, hypersensitivity pneumonitis, infections (Front Med (Lausanne) 2021;7:582581)
    • Neoplasms: would not have a cystic appearance
    • Sarcoidosis: nonnecrotizing epithelioid granulomas with exclusion of other diseases (Semin Diagn Pathol 2007;24:150)
    • Hypersensitivity pneumonitis: caused by environmental exposure such as having birds as pets, bacteria or being exposed to chemicals (NIH: Interstitial Lung Diseases - Hypersensitivity Pneumonitis [Accessed 27 February 2025])
    • Infections: would have an associated infective cause such as bacteria, viruses or fungi
  • Usual interstitial pneumonia (UIP):
    • Patchy fibrosis with fibroblastic foci and often honeycombing
  • Desquamative interstitial pneumonitis:
    • May coexist with PLCH
  • Chronic eosinophilic pneumonia:
    • Can have eosinophils and macrophages but no Langerhans cells
  • Granulomatosis with polyangiitis:
    • Necrotizing noncaseating granulomatous vasculitis
  • Lymphangioleiomyomatosis (LAM):
    • Low grade destructive metastasizing perivascular epithelioid cell that results from the growth of LAM cells in the lung, kidney and axial lymphatics
  • Centrilobular emphysema:
    • Cyst walls are not visible on imaging
  • Pneumocystis jiroveci pneumonia:
    • Atypical, opportunistic fungal pulmonary infection; characterized by pneumatoceles (gas filled cysts), subpleural blebs; perihilar distribution
  • Idiopathic pulmonary fibrosis:
    • Basal and subpleural distribution, reduced lung volumes
  • Protein deposition diseases, such as Sjögren syndrome and light chain deposition disease:
    • Smooth walled simple cysts
    • Associated with autoimmune disease
  • Reference: (Radiopaedia: Pulmonary Langerhans Cell Histiocytosis [Accessed 14 February 2025])
Board review style question #1


A 45 year old man with a history of smoking presents to the emergency room with dyspnea and chest pain. Contrast enhanced chest CT shows a lesion in the right lung with small nodules and small thin walled cysts. A lung biopsy of the lesion is performed, which is followed by H&E and immunohistochemistry (IHC) staining of the biopsy specimen. Eosinophils and macrophages are seen on H&E (see above). No microorganisms are seen on GMS or AFB stain of the biopsy sample. Blood cultures are also negative for bacteria and fungi. Which of the following is the most likely diagnosis?

  1. Empyema
  2. Pulmonary Langerhans cell histiocytosis
  3. Pulmonary tuberculosis
  4. Squamous cell carcinoma of the lung
Board review style answer #1
B. Pulmonary Langerhans cell histiocytosis (PLCH). The chest CT scan findings of nodules and thin walled cysts along with characteristic H&E showing eosin are classic of PLCH. Answer C is incorrect because these are sheets of cells rather than granulomas (rounded). In tuberculosis, necrotizing granulomas are seen. Answer D is incorrect because the radiologic finding of multiple nodules and cysts on chest CT is not found in squamous cell carcinoma of the lung. Answer A is incorrect because there were no neutrophils or necrotic material found. There is also no history of pneumonia in this patient.

Board review style question #2


A 53 year old woman with a history of heavy cigarette smoking for 25 years presents to the clinic with cough and shortness of breath. Chest CT (see above) shows nodules with thick walled cysts. A bronchoalveolar lavage (BAL) showed cells with indented nuclei. Which of the following is the best treatment for this patient?

  1. Antibiotics
  2. Nodule resection
  3. Smoking cessation
  4. Steroids
Board review style answer #2
C. Smoking cessation is the best treatment for a pulmonary Langerhans cell histiocytosis (PLCH) patient who has a history of smoking. Answer D is incorrect because steroids are not the first line of treatment for PLCH. Answer A is incorrect because antibiotics are not helpful in pulmonary Langerhans cell histiocytosis because it is not caused by a bacterial infection. Answer B is incorrect because the nodules in PLCH do not need to be surgically removed for treatment.
