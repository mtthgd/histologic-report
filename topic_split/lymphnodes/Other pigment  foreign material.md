Asbestos

Terminology

* Also called Ferruginous bodies

Epidemiology

* Usually due to industrial / occupational exposure

Sites

* Most common in thoracic / hilar lymph nodes
* Concentration of asbestos fibers in lymph nodes is 2 - 3x higher than in lung

Clinical features

* Inhaled asbestos fibers have iron protein mucopolysaccharide coating
* Enlarged lymph nodes are common
* Associated symptoms / signs of pulmonary asbestosis

Diagnosis

* Biopsy of affected lymph node
* Bleach digestion for confirmation

Radiology description

* Mediastinal / hilar lymphadenopathy

Prognostic factors

* Pulmonary asbestosis is a risk factor for lung carcinoma and mesothelioma

Case reports

* 26 year old man with mesothelioma due to asbestos exposure ([Case Rep Med 2011;2011:951732](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/21776278))

Gold

Definition / general

* Colloidal gold is used for anti-tumor therapy, to treat autoimmune diseases and for drug delivery ([Wikipedia: Colloidal gold [Accessed 22 June 2018]](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Colloidal_gold))
* Lymphadenopathy and lymph node infarction are uncommon complications of gold injections; occur via accumulation of gold particles in macrophages

Sites

* Cervical, axillary, mesenteric lymph nodes, depending on route of administration

Clinical features

* Tender enlarged lymph nodes
* Benign process with resolution of symptoms on withdrawal of gold treatment

Diagnosis

* Biopsy
* Darkfield microscopy and autometallography methods demonstrate gold nanoparticles 15 to 50 nm

Laboratory

* Polymorphonuclear neutrophilia, leukopenia, thrombocytopenia
* Hepatic toxicity

Radiology description

* Gold deposits can mimic intranodal axillary calcific deposits on mammography in patients with rheumatoid arthritis ([Proc (Bayl Univ Med Cent) 2013;26:28](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/23382606))

Case reports

* 34 year old woman with lymphadenopathy and lymph node infarction ([J Clin Pathol 2001;54:562](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/11429432))
* Intramammary lymph node gold deposits simulating microcalcifications on mammogram ([Hum Pathol 1988;19:992](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/3402989))
* Lymphadenopathy and lymph node infarction ([Am J Med 1986;80:537](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/3082202))

Gross description

* Extensive necrosis of lymph node

Microscopic (histologic) description

* Subtotal or complete infarction with peripheral rim of organizing granulation tissue in region of subcapsular sinus
* Small focus of residual viable lymphoid tissue has features of follicular hyperplasia
* Center of node has ghost outlines of necrotic cells

Iron

Definition / general

* Iron deposition in lymph nodes is rare
* Also called hemosiderosis
* May be associated with [hemochromatosis](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/liverhemochromatosis.html)

Sites

* Portal, splenic, mesenteric or axillary, depending on the primary cause

Pathophysiology

* Parenteral iron administration
* Multiple transfusions: red blood cells are lysed and iron is deposited in macrophages in the liver and spleen followed by drainage to adjacent lymph nodes
* Hereditary hemosiderosis: abnormalities in iron transport - most common is *HFE1* gene mutation; also mutations in *TfR2, HJV, HAMP*, ferroportin-V162del ([Hepatology 2005;42:466](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/15986403))
* Anemia of inflammation: associated with rheumatoid arthritis, gout ([Ann Rheum Dis 1970;29:81](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/5416103))

Clinical features

* Depends on the primary cause
* Enlarged lymph nodes

Diagnosis

* Biopsy with Perls Prussian blue stain

Radiology description

* CT: enlarged and hyperdense lymph nodes ([AJR Am J Roentgenol 1981;136:1191](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/6786034))

Prognostic factors

* Depends on the primary cause, severity and extent of the disease

Case reports

* Women, 19 to 49 years, with lymphadenopathy after single infusion of iron dextran ([J Clin Pathol 1968;21:492](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/5697350))

Treatment

* Reduce iron overload, treat the primary cause

Gross description

* Enlarged lymph nodes

Lipogranuloma

Definition / general

* Also called lipophagic granuloma
* Secondary to various inflammatory and neoplastic conditions or primary lesion of lymph node
* In West, commonly due to mineral oil ingestion or total parenteral nutrition

Case reports

* 27 year old woman with prior giant cell tumor ([Acta Cytol 2002;46:772](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/12146051))

Microscopic (histologic) description

* Giant cells (mono or multinuclear) with foamy and vacuolated cytoplasm

Melanosis

Definition / general

* A rare manifestation of a completely regressed melanoma

Terminology

* Tumoral melanosis
* Nodular melanosis
* Nodal melanosis

Epidemiology

* Very rare in lymph nodes

Sites

* Sentinel lymph nodes of regressed melanoma
* Mesenteric lymph nodes

Pathophysiology

* Possibilities include:
  + A regressed focus of metastatic melanoma
  + Melanophages migrating to the lymph node from the primary lesion

Clinical features

* Enlarged lymph nodes
* Yellow brown spindle bodies in mesenteric nodes may be due to melanosis coli ([Histopathology 1978;2:47](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/208951))
* The paucity of cases precludes adequate evaluation of long term implications and treatment outcomes

Case reports

* 36 year old man with lymph node melanosis and metastatic melanoma of unknown primary ([Arch Pathol Lab Med 2009;133:1332](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/19653733))
* 83 year old man with melanosis coli involving pericolonic lymph nodes associated with the herbal laxative Swiss Kriss ([Arch Pathol Lab Med 2004;128:565](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/15086301))
* Tumoral melanosis involving the sentinel lymph nodes ([J Cutan Pathol 2007;34:284](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/17302615))
* Lymph node melanosis from a primary cutaneous lesion combining a nodular (tumoral) melanosis and a congenital dermal melanocytic nevus ([Am J Dermatopathol 2012;34:653](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/22456514))

Gross description

* Enlarged lymph nodes with or without brown pigment deposition

Microscopic (histologic) description

* Subcapsular and sinusoidal accumulation of pigmented cells with vesicular chromatin and inconspicuous nucleoli
* Cells may be spindle shaped or epithelioid

Prosthesis related

Definition / general

* See also [silicone](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/lymphnodessiliconeimplant.html)
* May be an incidental finding at radical prostatectomy ([J Clin Pathol 2002;55:623](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/12147660))

Case reports

* 76 year old woman with histiocytic lymphadenitis post shoulder joint replacement ([Am J Clin Pathol 1993;99:314](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/8447293))
* 78 year old woman with right pelvic cystic mass due to chromium in hip prosthesis ([Acta Cytol 2003;47:270](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/12685200))
* Post hip replacement with coexisting metastatic prostate adenocarcinoma to same lymph node ([Hum Pathol 1998;29:95](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/9445141))

Microscopic (histologic) description

* Sinuses distended by large macrophages with abundant granular PAS+ eosinophilic cytoplasm representing polyethylene ([Am J Surg Pathol 1989;13:1050](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/2596617))
* May contain black specks which resemble "dirt"
* Pelvic lymph nodes in patients with cobalt chromium or titanium hip implants may have heavy metal microparticles or polyethylene particles with associated florid sinus histiocytosis ([Am J Surg Pathol 1994;18:83](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/8279630))
