Definition / general

* Lymph node enlargement due to proliferation of some or all compartments or cellular components of lymph nodes, reflecting antigenic stimulation
* Can be acute or chronic depending on the pathogenic agents

Essential features

* Lymph node enlargement
* Acute: painful or tender; chronic: nontender
* 4 architectural patterns: follicular hyperplasia, sinus histiocytosis, interfollicular / mixed and diffuse
* Clonal B or T cell proliferations are generally not detected but reactive clonal populations may occur
* Lacks significant cytologic or architectural atypia

Terminology

* Reactive lymphoid hyperplasia
* Reactive follicular hyperplasia
* Diffuse paracortical hyperplasia
* Sinus histiocytosis
* Acute nonspecific lymphadenitis
* Chronic lymphadenitis

ICD coding

* ICD-10: [R59.9](https://www.icd10data.com/ICD10CM/Codes/R00-R99/R50-R69/R59-/R59.9) - enlarged lymph nodes, unspecified

Epidemiology

* Represents the reaction of lymphoid tissue to intrinsic or environmental antigens
* Most lymph node enlargements are reactive
* Can occur in any age group and gender
* In children, most lymphadenopathies are benign; in adults, chance of malignancy increases with age ([Mayo Clin Proc 2000;75:723](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/10907389))

Sites

* Any lymph node group can be affected depending on the etiology and area of drainage ([Clin Pediatr (Phila) 2011;50:683](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/21224249))

Pathophysiology

* Lymph nodes filter lymph drained from tributary regions
* Antigenic substances reaching the lymph nodes evoke an immune reaction ([Semin Diagn Pathol 2018;35:4](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/28619469))
* Bacteria and fungi cause predominantly inflammatory reactions; viruses and drugs cause predominantly immune reactions
* Acute nonspecific lymphadenitis: acute inflammation of the lymph node due to an infectious or inflammatory etiology
* Chronic nonspecific lymphadenitis: chronic inflammation of the lymph node in response to a pathogen / antigen, known or unknown

Etiology

* Infections: virus, bacteria, fungi and protozoa
* Chemicals: environmental pollution
* Drugs: phenytoin, allopurinol, gold, penicillin, quinidine
* Altered tissue components: inflammation, tissue necrosis
* Inflammatory / autoimmune conditions, such as rheumatoid arthritis
* Other antigens or allergens, including vaccines ([Mayo Clin Proc 2000;75:723](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/10907389), [Iran J Med Sci 2014;39:158](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/24753638), [Pediatr Clin North Am 2002;49:1009](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/12430623))

Clinical features

* Clinical features reflect the underlying cause
* Enlarged lymph nodes ([Am Fam Physician 2016;94:896](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/27929264))
* In acute lymphadenitis, can be painful or tender and nodes may be soft or fluctuant, with or without associated fever, weight loss, malaise, loss of appetite and redness of overlying skin
* In chronic lymphadenitis, enlarged lymph nodes are commonly painless ([Mayo Clin Proc 2000;75:723](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/10907389))

Diagnosis

* Clinical examination
* Histopathology: biopsy is rarely performed if reactive nature is clinically obvious
* In chronic lymphadenitis, biopsy and histopathology evaluation are necessary
* Exclusion of specific causes: clinical, laboratory and imaging findings
* If suspicious, exclude lymphoid neoplasia by immunohistochemistry, flow cytometry and required genetic studies

Laboratory

* Laboratory findings depend on the etiology and nature of the process (acute versus chronic)
* Complete blood count (CBC) may show leukocytosis with neutrophilia or lymphocytosis and erythrocyte sedimentation rate (ESR) may be elevated
* Confirmatory tests for specific etiologic agents (infectious agents) may be positive

Radiology description

* Enlarged lymph node(s)
* Advanced imaging techniques may help to distinguish reactive lymph node enlargement from tumor metastases or other specific lymph node enlargements ([Mol Imaging Biol 2013;15:40](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/22588595), [Indian J Gastroenterol 2016;35:55](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/26923374))

Prognostic factors

* Benign, usually self limiting process with good prognosis
* Prognosis depends on the etiology and appropriate and timely management of the cause

Case reports

* 40 year old man who received dasatinib for chronic myeloid leukemia presented with a large axillary mass ([Medicine (Baltimore) 2020;99:e22791](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/33157925))
* 41 year old woman with a history of nasopharyngeal carcinoma and enlarged cervical lymph nodes ([Clin Nucl Med 2022;47:367](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/34653051))
* 57 year old woman with rheumatoid arthritis and generalized lymphadenopathy ([Case Rep Rheumatol 2014;2014:386328](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/24839573))
* 68 year old man with right cheek melanoma and multiple FDG avid lymph nodes in the left axilla ([Clin Nucl Med 2021;46:433](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/33782318))

Treatment

* Treat the underlying cause

Gross description

* Enlarged, soft lymph nodes with tan, homogenous cut surfaces
* Acute lymphadenitis: suppuration may lead to necrosis and abscess formation
* Chronic lymphadenitis: thickened capsule and fibrosis may be obvious ([Walker: Clinical Methods - The History, Physical, and Laboratory Examinations, 3rd Edition, 1990](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/21250045))

Microscopic (histologic) description

* 4 architectural patterns of reactive hyperplasia are described, depending on the etiology: follicular / nodular, sinus histiocytosis, interfollicular / mixed and diffuse
  + Follicular hyperplasia (most common reactive pattern)
    - Enlarged follicles, varying in size and shape, may coalesce and display different configurations
    - Prominent germinal center and mantle zone
    - Germinal centers show mixed small and large lymphocytes, centrocytes and centroblasts
    - Centroblasts polarize to the medial pole / medullary side forming the darker zone and centrocytes accumulate at the peripheral pole / capsular side forming the lighter zone ([Immunity 2016;45:471](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/27653600))
    - Centroblasts are 3 - 4 times larger than the inactivated lymphocytes and show a narrow rim of basophilic cytoplasm and large, round to oval vesicular nuclei with 1 - 3 prominent peripheral nucleoli
    - Mitotic figures are frequent
    - Centrocytes are smaller lymphocytes with scant cytoplasm, cleaved nuclei, clumped chromatin and small or absent nucleoli
    - Numerous tingible body macrophages are a characteristic feature of follicular hyperplasia ([Semin Diagn Pathol 2018;35:4](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/28619469), [Am J Surg Pathol 1987;11:866](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/3499826))
    - Other cells: plasma cells, T cells and follicular dendritic cells
    - PD-1+ T cells (T follicular helper cells) are seen predominantly at the periphery of the germinal center ([Ann Diagn Pathol 2020;44:151421](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/31751845))
    - Mantle zone: around the light zone, small lymphocytes
    - Interfollicular areas may show rare transformed cells (immunoblasts), small lymphocytes, plasma cells and high endothelial venules
    - Monocytoid B cell proliferation may be seen in follicular hyperplasia around cortical sinuses ([Pathol Res Pract 1998;194:559](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/9779490))
  + Sinus histiocytosis
    - Expansion of sinuses by histiocytes
    - Common pattern in lymph nodes draining tumor ([Br J Surg 1983;70:317](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/6860905))
  + Interfollicular / mixed patterns
    - Paracortical hyperplasia: seen usually in viral infections, nodes draining tumors and autoimmune disorders ([Pathol Res Pract 2001;197:237](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/11358009), [Hum Pathol 1975;6:363](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/1079509), [APMIS 2001;109:419](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/11506473))
    - Mixed small lymphocytes and immunoblasts, with increased vascularity and interdigitating dendritic cells
    - Granulomatous lymphadenitis: necrotizing, nonnecrotizing or suppurative granulomas depending on the etiology ([Rev Infect Dis 1986;8:322](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/3726392))
  + Diffuse
    - Architecture may be effaced
    - Diffuse proliferation of mixed small lymphocytes, immunoblasts and plasma cells ([APMIS 2001;109:419](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/11506473))
    - Immunoblasts may rarely mimic Hodgkin / Reed-Sternberg cells
* Acute lymphadenitis
  + Sinus dilatation followed by accumulation of neutrophils, vascular dilatation and edema of the capsule
  + Suppurative or necrotizing inflammation may be seen depending on the etiology ([Semin Pediatr Surg 2006;15:99](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/16616313))
* Chronic lymphadenitis
  + Follicular hyperplasia, prominence of postcapillary venules, increased number of immunoblasts, fibrosis, plasma cells and histiocytes
  + Capsule may be inflamed or fibrotic ([Semin Pediatr Surg 2006;15:99](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/16616313))

Cytology description

* Cellular smears with mixed small and large lymphocytes, tingible body macrophages and other cell types including histiocytes and follicular dendritic cells
* In acute lymphadenitis, a prominence of neutrophils may be seen

Peripheral smear description

* In reactive lymphadenopathy, peripheral smears may show leukocytosis with either lymphocytosis or neutrophilia, depending on the underlying cause

Positive stains

* Follicles: positive for pan-B cell markers, such as [CD19](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/cdmarkerscd19.html), [CD20](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/cdmarkerscd20.html), [CD22](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/cdmarkerscd20to29.html#cd22), [CD79a](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/cdmarkerscd79a.html), [PAX5](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainspax5.html)
* Germinal centers: positive for germinal center markers, such as [CD10](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/cdmarkerscd10.html) and [BCL6](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsbcl6.html)
* Follicular dendritic cells: positive for [CD21](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/cdmarkerscd21.html), [CD23](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/cdmarkerscd23.html)
* T cells: [CD3](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/cdmarkerscd3.html), [CD4](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/cdmarkerscd4.html), [PD-1](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainspd1.html)
* Plasma cells: [CD138](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/cdmarkerscd138.html)
* Paracortex (T cells): positive for pan-T cell markers, such as [CD3](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/cdmarkerscd3.html), [CD2](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/cdmarkerscd1.html#cd2), [CD5](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/cdmarkerscd5.html) and [CD7](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/cdmarkerscd1.html#cd7), mixed [CD4](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/cdmarkerscd4.html) and [CD8](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/cdmarkerscd8.html) positive T cells
* Immunoblasts: positive for [CD30](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/cdmarkerscd30.html) ([StatPearls: Histology, Lymph Nodes [Accessed 4 December 2024]](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/32644479))
* [Ki67](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainski67.html): high proliferation rate in the germinal centers with polarization (dark zone with higher rate of proliferation and light zone with lower rate)

Negative stains

* Germinal center B cells: negative for [BCL2](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsbcl2.html)

Flow cytometry description

* Mixed B and T cell populations
* T cells typically show mixed [CD4](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/cdmarkerscd4.html)+ and [CD8](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/cdmarkerscd8.html)+ subsets ([Cytopathology 2022;33:505](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/35023246), [J BUON 2013;18:739](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/24065493))
* Usually no immunophenotypic aberrancies but rarely clonal [CD10](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/cdmarkerscd10.html)+ B cell proliferations are detected by flow cytometry ([Am J Clin Pathol 2004;121:464](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/15080297))
  + B cells polytypic based on [light chain](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainskappa.html) pattern (except rare cases as above)

Molecular / cytogenetics description

* Polyclonal pattern of B and T cell gene rearrangements
* Negative for cytogenetic or molecular abnormalities

Sample pathology report

* Lymph node, left cervical, needle core biopsy:
  + Reactive lymphoid hyperplasia and no morphologic or immunophenotypic evidence of a lymphoproliferative disorder

Differential diagnosis

* Follicular hyperplasia
  + [Classic follicular lymphoma](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/lymphomafollicularnodalusual.html):
    - Effaced architecture, back to back follicles, invasion of capsule or adjacent structures, no polarization of the follicle, no tingible body macrophages, no well defined mantle zones
    - Positive for [CD10](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/cdmarkerscd10.html), [BCL6](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsbcl6.html) and [BCL2](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsbcl2.html), monotypic [light chain](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainskappa.html) expression, monoclonal B cell gene rearrangement and presence of t(14;18)
  + [In situ follicular neoplasia](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/lymphomafollicularneoplasiainsitu.html):
    - No polarization with bright expression of [CD10](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/cdmarkerscd10.html) and [BCL2](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsbcl2.html) in involved germinal centers, monotypic [light chain](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainskappa.html) expression, monoclonal B cell gene rearrangement and presence of t(14;18)
* Sinus histiocytosis
  + Expansion of the sinuses with tumor cells
    - [Melanoma](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/skintumormelanocyticmelanoma.html):
      * Positive for [S100](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainss100.html) and melanocytic markers
    - [Mesothelioma](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/pleuramesothelioma.html):
      * Positive for [WT1](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainswt1.html) and other mesothelial markers
    - [Anaplastic large cell lymphoma](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/lymphomanonbanaplastic.html):
      * Positive for T cell markers, [CD30](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/cdmarkerscd30.html) and may be positive for [ALK1](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsalk.html)
    - [Rosai-Dorfman disease](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/lymphnodessinushistiocytosiswithmassivelymph.html):
      * Sinuses with accumulation of histiocytes with enlarged, round to oval hypochromatic nuclei and abundant eosinophilic cytoplasm, often containing engulfed intact inflammatory cells known as emperipolesis ([Am J Surg Pathol 2021;45:35](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/33177341))
* [Classic Hodgkin lymphoma (CHL)](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/lymphomanonBclassic.html):
  + Architectural effacement with an inflammatory infiltrate containing large atypical cells with complex nuclear features (Hodgkin / Reed-Sternberg cells) in a mixed background of lymphocytes, histiocytes, plasma cells and eosinophils
  + Generally morphologically distinct from reactive conditions but cases with partial nodal involvement, primarily interfollicular involvement or more mononuclear Hodgkin / Reed-Sternberg (HRS) forms can create diagnostic challenges
  + Prolonged or atypical EBV associated lymphadenitis may develop and be observed on biopsy evaluation ([Mod Pathol 2012;25:1149](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/22627742))
  + [EBV](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainseber1.html)+ CHL can be distinguished based on cytologic atypia; [EBER](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainseber1.html) expression should be exclusive to the HRS cells
    - Positive: [CD30](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/cdmarkerscd30.html), [PAX5](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainspax5.html), [CD15](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/cdmarkerscd15.html), [EBER](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainseber1.html), [EBV LMP1](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainseber1.html), variable dim [CD20](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/cdmarkerscd20.html)
    - Negative: [EBV EBNA2](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsEBNA2.html), other B cell markers
* [Immunosuppression related](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/lymphomapolymorphicimmunedeficiency.html) and [EBV associated lymphoproliferations](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/lymphnodesebv.html):
  + May show greater variation in morphology and marker expression
  + Morphology may range from hyperplasias to polymorphic lymphoproliferative disorders to morphologic lymphoma
  + Caution is advised in this setting, including correlation with clinical and radiographic status, type of immune suppression and whether the proliferation is viral associated ([Leukemia 2022;36:1720](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/35732829))
* Specific causes of lymphadenitis need to be ruled out
  + Acute:
    - [Lymphogranuloma venereum](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/lymphnodesotherinfections.html#lymphogranulomavenereum), [cat scratch disease](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/lymphnodescatscratch.html), [bubonic plague](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/lymphnodesotherinfections.html#yersiniapestis), [tularemia](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/lymphnodesotherinfections.html#tularemia), [anthrax](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/skinnontumoranthrax.html), [typhoid fever](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/colonsalmonella.html), **melioidosis**
  + Chronic:
    - [Tuberculosis](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/lungnontumortb.html), [leprosy](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/skinnontumorleprosy.html), [atypical mycobacteria](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/lymphnodesatypicalmycobacteria.html), [syphilis](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/skinnontumorsyphilis.html), [fungal](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/lungnontumorotherfungi.html) or [parasitic infections](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/lungnontumorotherparasites.html):
      * Special stains for the causative microorganisms will be positive

Board review style question #1

If a biopsy were performed, which morphologic feature would best differentiate an EBV+ classic Hodgkin lymphoma from an acute EBV lymphadenitis?

1. Cytologic atypia in CD30+ cells
2. Lack of detection of EBV protein EBNA2
3. Paracortical expansion
4. Positivity by CISH for EBV encoded RNA (EBER)

Board review style answer #1

**A**. Cytologic atypia in CD30+ cells. Malignant processes involving the lymph node can best be recognized by architectural distortion and cytologic atypia. Hodgkin / Reed-Sternberg cells in classic Hodgkin lymphoma will show complex nuclear features and express CD30, CD15 (often) and dim PAX5. When EBV associated, they will express EBER and EBV LMP1.
Answer B is incorrect because Hodgkin / Reed-Sternberg cells may express EBER and LMP1 but are always negative for EBNA2. EBNA2 may be expressed in EBV associated lymphadenitis but is not always present.
Answer D is incorrect because EBER can be expressed (although with different patterns) in EBV+ lymphadenitis as well as EBV+ classic Hodgkin lymphoma.
Answer C is incorrect because paracortical expansion can be seen in both reactive conditions and lymphoma. EBV+ lymphadenitis generally shows a paracortical expansion.

Board review style question #2

[![](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/imgau/lymphnodesreactivegeneralBalakrishna34.jpg)](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/imgau/lymphnodesreactivegeneralBalakrishna34.jpg)

A 10 year old girl presents with bilateral cervical lymph node enlargement. Her parents state that she has had a fever and throat pain for the past few days. On examination, she is found to have multiple enlarged bilateral cervical lymph nodes. The above image shows a low power view of the cervical lymph node biopsy (H&E, 10x). What is the most likely diagnosis based on the H&E image?

1. Burkitt lymphoma
2. Follicular hyperplasia
3. Follicular lymphoma
4. Paracortical expansion

Board review style answer #2

**B**. Follicular hyperplasia. The presence of hyperplastic secondary follicles with prominent germinal centers, which show polarization and tingible body macrophages and mantle zone, is favorable for reactive follicular hyperplasia. Answer C is incorrect because follicular lymphoma shows effaced architecture with back to back follicles, absence of polarization and tingible body macrophages; it also lacks well defined mantle zones. Answer A is incorrect because Burkitt lymphoma is a diffuse proliferation of medium sized atypical lymphoid cells with blastoid morphology and conspicuous 1 - 3 nucleoli. Answer D is incorrect because paracortical hyperplasia is the expansion of interfollicular areas with mixed small lymphocytes, immunoblasts, vessels and interdigitating dendritic cells.
