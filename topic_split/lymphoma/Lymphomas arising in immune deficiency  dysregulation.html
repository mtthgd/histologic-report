Definition / general
  • Hematolymphoid proliferations associated with either primary or secondary immune deficiency
    • Heterogeneous group, both clinicopathologically and etiologically
    • Increased risk of developing lymphomas and leukemias (lymphoma > leukemia)
    • Also increased risk for nonhematopoietic tumors
    • Traditionally classified according to 4 major immunodeficiency backgrounds in which they arise
      • Primary immunodeficiencies
      • Human immunodeficiency virus (HIV) infection
      • Posttransplantation status
      • Other iatrogenic immunodeficiencies: patients treated with immunosuppressive drugs for autoimmune disease or in conditions other than posttransplantation
Essential features
  • Epstein Barr virus (EBV) / human herpesvirus 4 and Kaposi sarcoma herpesvirus / human herpesvirus 8 (KSHV / HHV8) play an important pathogenetic role in many of the lesions arising in the different clinical settings
  • 5th edition of the WHO Classification recommends the use of 3 diagnostic components for reporting immune deficiency / dysregulation associated lymphoproliferative diseases (IDD LPDs)
    • Histopathologic diagnosis: hyperplasia, polymorphic LPD, lymphoma
    • Presence or absence of causally associated virus: EBV, KSHV / HHV8
    • Immune deficiency / dysregulation setting (major immunodeficiency background)
Terminology
  • Lymphoid proliferations and lymphomas associated with immune deficiency and dysregulation
  • Hyperplasia arising in immune deficiency / dysregulation
    • Nondestructive posttransplant lymphoproliferative disorder (PTLD) (not recommended by WHO)
  • Polymorphic lymphoproliferative disorders arising in immune deficiency / dysregulation
    • Polymorphic lymphoma; polymorphic posttransplant lymphoproliferative disorder (P-PTLD) (not recommended by WHO)
    • P-PTLD-like lymphoproliferative disorder (not recommended by WHO)
  • EBV+ mucocutaneous ulcer
  • Lymphomas arising in immune deficiency / dysregulation
    • Monomorphic posttransplant lymphoproliferative disorder (PTLD) (not recommended by WHO)
    • Monomorphic PTLD-like lymphoproliferative disorder (not recommended by WHO)
    • Classic Hodgkin lymphoma, PTLD (not recommended by WHO)
  • Inborn error of immunity associated lymphoid proliferations and lymphomas
    • Primary immunodeficiency (not recommended by WHO)
    • Fatal infectious mononucleosis (not recommended by WHO)
    • Monomorphic lymphoproliferative disorder (not recommended by WHO)

Epidemiology
Primary immunodeficiencies (PIDs)
  • Most common disorders: ataxia telangiectasia, Wiskott-Aldrich syndrome, Nijmegen breakage syndrome, X linked lymphoproliferative syndrome, autoimmune lymphoproliferative syndrome, X linked hyper IgM syndrome, common variable immunodeficiency and severe combined immunodeficiency (Pathology 2020;52:40)
  • ~60% of all neoplasms are LPDs; non-Hodgkin lymphoma (NHL) is most common
    • Not all PIDs have been proven to be associated with increased risk of LPDs
  • Exact prevalence unknown; estimated ranges: 1:1,000 - 5,000
    • ~400 new cases/year in the U.S.
  • Lymphomas in patients with PIDs show similar distribution in male and female patients: 29% and 34%, respectively (Pathology 2020;52:40)
  • Variably increased risk for LPD, according to specific PID
  • Majority present in childhood
    • Exception: common variable immunodeficiency (CVID) may present in young or mid adulthood (Leuk Res 2015;39:389)
    • Recent increase in LPDs at older ages due to improved survival of PID patients

Posttransplantation setting
  • PTLD accounts for 15 - 20% of tumors among adult transplant recipients (~51% in children) (Blood Rev 2024;64:101167)
    • Suggests that immunologic reaction in allograft is a pathogenic factor
    • Frequency is 20 - 120% higher than the general population
  • Significant variability ranging from 20% and related to type of transplant (World J Transplant 2020;10:29, N Engl J Med 2018;378:549)
    • Multiple organ transplants: ~20% of transplant patients
    • Small intestine: ~20% of transplant patients
    • Kidney: 0.8 - 2.5% of transplant patients
    • Pancreas: 0.5 - 5% of transplant patients
    • Liver: 2 - 8% of transplant patients
    • Lungs: 3 - 10% of transplant patients
    • Hematopoietic stem cell transplant:

    HIV associated (Virchows Arch 2023;482:227)
    • More common in men (84%)
    • Most common type: diffuse large B cell lymphoma (DLBCL) (48%), followed by Burkitt lymphoma (18%)
      • Often involving the central nervous system
      • Others: primary effusion lymphoma and plasmablastic lymphoma
      • Classic Hodgkin lymphoma also occurs at a higher rate in the setting of HIV infection than in the general population
        • However, it has been proposed that the association with immune deficiency is much weaker than with non-Hodgkin lymphoma and Kaposi sarcoma
    • Lymph nodes are the most common primary site affected at diagnosis (47%), whilst the gastrointestinal (GI) tract was the most common extranodal site of involvement (19%)
    • Usually EBV positive (EBER in situ hybridization)
    • No correlation between EBV viral load and the occurrence of lymphoma in HIV patients (J Immunol 2004;172:6931)

Other iatrogenic immunodeficiencies
  • Incidence not well characterized (Blood Rev 2024;64:101167)
    • Overall risk of lymphoma increased 2 - 12x in RA patients
    • Severe disease activity associated with higher risk
    • Nondestructive LPD accounts for 10 - 15% of methotrexate (MTX) LPD in several reports
  • Median age at diagnosis for DLBCL: 62 years
  • Median age at diagnosis for hepatosplenic T cell lymphoma (HSTCL) in Crohn's disease: 22 years
  • Sex: M:F ratio related to underlying disease in most instances
  • EBV is detected in about 80% of classic Hodgkin lymphoma and in 25 - 60% of DLBCL or other B cell lymphomas (Blood Rev 2024;64:101167)

Etiology
Primary immunodeficiencies (PIDs)
  • Gene mutations cause functional defects that compromise the immune system
    • Ataxia telangiectasia (AT): ATM
    • Nijmegen breakage syndrome (NBS): NBN (nibrin)
    • X linked hyper IgM syndrome: CD40 or CD40 ligand (CD40LG)
    • Wiskott-Aldrich syndrome: WAS
    • Cartilage hair hypoplasia syndrome: RMRP
    • X linked lymphoproliferative syndrome (XLP): SH2D1A or BIRC3 / XIAP
    • Autoimmune lymphoproliferative syndrome (ALPS): FAS (TNFRSF6)
    • Hyper IgE syndrome: STAT3 dominant negative mutation
  • Basis for increased risk of hematologic neoplasms in PID is multifactorial
    • Impaired host immunosurveillance
    • Chronic antigen stimulation
    • EBV infection drives a subset of LPDs
    • Defective DNA mismatch repair involved in ataxia telangiectasia and Nijmegen breakage syndrome
    • Possible unknown oncogenic viruses may be involved

Posttransplantation setting
  • Risk for PTLD increases with increasing immunosuppression
    • Type of organ: multivisceral > lung > liver > heart > kidney (Pathology 2020;52:40)
    • Age younger than 10 years or older than 60 years (Pathology 2020;52:40, Surg Pathol Clin 2016;9:55)
  • EBV (primarily) (Pathology 2020;52:40)
    • EBV seronegativity is the most important risk factor at transplantation; infection can be acquired primarily through the allograft
      • Significantly higher risk in children due to frequent EBV naive pretransplant status (Blood Rev 2024;64:101167)
      • Lower risk in adults due to decreased host immunosurveillance and EBV reactivation rather than primary infection
    • 80% of PTLDs are EBV+ (Arch Pathol Lab Med 2007;131:1209)
    • Primary EBV infection increases risk for PTLD by 10 - 75x (N Engl J Med 2018;378:549)
  • HHV8 / KSHV is much less common
  • For allogeneic hematopoietic stem cell transplant (HSCT), human leukocyte antigen (HLA) mismatch and age above 50 at the time of transplant are additional risk factors (Surg Pathol Clin 2016;9:55)

Iatrogenic immunodeficiencies
  • Type of immunosuppressive agent
    • Methotrexate, TNFα antagonists, etc.
      • Methotrexate activated lytic EBV infection in host cells
    • Kidney transplant patients taking mycophenolate mofetil are at a higher risk of developing LPDs in the central nervous system, while calcineurin inhibitors appear to be protective (Blood Rev 2024;64:101167)
  • Dose and duration of drug therapy
  • Impaired cell mediated immunity
    • Possible role of physiologic immunosenescence
  • Underlying disease type and disease activity
    • Chronic antigenic stimulation of the immune system
  • Infection by EBV
  • Possible patient genetic predisposition
  • Chronic antigenic stimulation
    • B cell immune dysregulation in rheumatoid arthritis (RA) could drive B cell expansion
      • Autoimmune activity increased due to rheumatoid factor, anticyclic citrullinated peptide antibodies and free light chains
    • T cell immune dysregulation in RA can lead to loss of tolerance
    • Leads to permissive conditions for EBV+ B cell proliferation
  • In the context of Crohn's disease, the risk is increased further by therapy with azathioprine and 6-mercaptopurine (6-MP) associated with the development of EBV negative hepatosplenic T cell lymphoma (Blood Rev 2024;64:101167)

HIV setting
  • Immunosuppression, dysregulation of cytokine secretion and induction of chronic B cell activation
  • Loss of immunoregulatory control of oncogenic herpesviruses like EBV and KSHV / HHV8
  • Genetic abnormalities similar to HIV negative cases (e.g., IGH rearrangements, MYC, BCL6)
  • Decreased incidence since the introduction of antiretroviral therapy (ART)
    • Fewer cases of primary central nervous system lymphoma and relatively more cases of Burkitt lymphoma and classic Hodgkin lymphoma (Blood Rev 2024;64:101167)
Clinical features
  • Similar to the presentation of lymphomas in immunocompetent patients

Treatment
  • Regular therapeutic strategies associated with additional treatments, depending on the IDD setting (Blood Rev 2021;49:100807, Pathol Int 2019;69:21, Med Oncol 2009;26:1)
    • HIV related: treatment if complemented with combination antiretroviral therapy (cART)
    • Iatrogenic related: management may involve withdrawal or adjustment of immunosuppressive therapy or immune modulators

Microscopic (histologic) description
Hyperplasia
  • Retention of architecture of the tissue
  • Composed of polyclonal B and T cells
    • Small clones may be seen by molecular studies in some cases, assumed to be reactive clonal outgrowths to an antigen
  • Patterns (more than 1 can be seen in the same specimen) (Blood Rev 2024;64:101167)
    • Follicular
    • Interfollicular / paracortical
    • T cell and histiocytic: may be admixed with immunoblasts and increased number of vessels (Blood Rev 2024;64:101167)
    • Follicular involution or regression: similar to Castleman disease (Blood Rev 2024;64:101167)
    • Nonnecrotizing granulomas, small numbers of plasma cells and poorly defined germinal centers: may be seen in patients with common variable immunodeficiency (Blood Rev 2024;64:101167)

Lymphoproliferative disorders of varied malignant potential (polymorphic LPD)
  • Heterogenous in morphologic composition and clinical behavior
  • Partial effacement / destruction of underlying architecture due to polymorphic infiltrate in interfollicular distribution
    • Small lymphocytes, plasmacytoid lymphocytes
    • Immunoblasts, Reed-Sternberg and Hodgkin-like cells, histiocytes
  • Often associated with necrosis, ranging from single cell necrosis to large areas of coagulative necrosis and significant areas of ulceration
  • Pathogenesis not completely understood; the level of immune deficiency or dysregulation likely plays a role, as these lesions tend to regress following reduction of immunosuppression
  • EBV is almost universally present; probably plays a central role for development
  • EBV+ mucocutaneous ulcer closely related to polymorphic LPD
    • Superficial ulcer in mouth, skin, GI tract
    • Polymorphous infiltrate, with or without Reed-Sternberg and Hodgkin cells and usually EBV+
  • Hodgkin-like LPDs
    • These lesions resemble but do not fulfill the criteria for classic Hodgkin lymphoma
    • Contain Reed-Sternberg and Hodgkin-like cells that are CD20+, CD45 / LCA+

Lymphomas
  • Polymorphous cytologic features are common
  • Non-Hodgkin lymphomas
    • B cell lymphomas are more common than T cell lymphomas
      • Frequently EBV+
    • DLBCL is the most common
      • Sheets of centroblasts or immunoblasts (mitotic figures common)
      • Many cases are polymorphous with plasmacytoid differentiation
      • May show geographic necrosis
      • Immunophenotype similar to DLBCLs in immunocompetent patients
    • Burkitt lymphoma is more common in X linked lymphoproliferative syndrome
    • Acute lymphoblastic leukemia / lymphoma (ALL / LBL)
  • Hodgkin lymphomas
    • Second most common LPD; ~ 10% of all lymphomas in PID patients
    • Classic Hodgkin lymphoma is most common in PID patients
      • Lymphocyte depleted and mixed cellularity types common
        • Attributable to feeble immune response
      • Reed-Sternberg and Hodgkin cells: CD15 (variable), CD30+, PAX5+ (dim), CD45 / LCA-
      • Inflammatory background
      • Extranodal location in some cases
    • Nodular lymphocyte predominant Hodgkin lymphoma is uncommon except in patients with autoimmune lymphoproliferative syndrome
  • Low grade B cell lymphomas
    • Uncommon number of tumors described
      • Extranodal marginal zone lymphoma
      • Follicular lymphoma, lymphoplasmacytic lymphoma
      • Chronic lymphocytic leukemia / small lymphocytic lymphoma
  • T cell lymphomas
    • ~ 5% of cases of immunodeficiency associated LPDs; tumors described include
      • Peripheral T cell lymphoma, not otherwise specified
      • Extranodal NK / T cell lymphoma, nasal type
    • Hepatosplenic T cell lymphoma
      • Effacement of splenic red pulp
      • Infiltration of liver and bone marrow sinusoids
        • Sinusoids expanded by lymphoma cells
      • Intermediate size cells with
        • Irregular nuclear contours, indistinct nucleoli and pale cytoplasm
        • Brisk mitotic rate and karyorrhectic debris

IHC stains
  • DLBCL (Pathology 2020;52:40)
    • CD20+, CD79a+, PAX5+
    • BCL6+ in ~ 70%, IRF4 / MUM1+ in ~ 50%
    • CD10+ in ~ 30%, BCL2+ in ~ 20%
    • Can be divided into germinal center (GC) and non-GC phenotypes
      • GC: ~ 40%; CD10+, BCL6+, IRF4 / MUM1-
      • Non-GC: ~ 60%; more likely to be EBV+
      • EBV+ shows latency type II pattern: LMP1+ and EBNA2-
    • Ki67 index high; T cell antigens (-)
  • Classic Hodgkin lymphoma (Pathology 2020;52:40)
    • Reed-Sternberg and Hodgkin cells
      • CD30+, CD15 (variable), PAX5+ (dim)
      • EBV (variable), CD45 / LCA-, T cell antigens (-)
  • Hepatosplenic T cell lymphoma (Blood 2020;136:2018)
    • CD3+, CD4-, CD8 (variable), CD5-, CD56 (variable)
    • TIA+, granzyme M+, granzyme B (variable)
    • TCRγ / TCRδ+ in ~ 75%; TCRα / TCRβ in ~ 25%
    • Perforin-, EBV-

Sample pathology report
  • Excisional lymph node biopsy, right neck:
    • Paracortical hyperplasia, EBV and HHV8 negative, associated with autoimmune lymphoproliferative syndrome (ALPS) (see comment)
    • Comment: The patient is an 11 year old boy with a history of hepatosplenomegaly since he was 4 years old and lymphadenopathy since he was 6 years old. During this period, a lymph node biopsy showed reactive lymphoid hyperplasia. Laboratory exams showed an increase of IgG and mild lymphocytosis. All viral serologies were negative. Another biopsy from the neck lymph node was performed and showed a polymorphic proliferation in the interfollicular area composed of small lymphocytes, immunoblasts and plasma cells associated with atretic follicles and Castleman-like changes. Immunohistochemistry showed the interfollicular area positive for CD3, CD5 and CD7 and partial loss of CD4 / CD8. B cell markers (CD20, PAX5 and CD79a) stained normal follicles, CD30 stained reactive immunoblasts and EBER and HHV8 were negative. Flow cytometry immunophenotype of peripheral blood observed a T cell population negative for CD4 and CD8 (15%) and preserved CD7. This population was also positive for TCRα / TCRβ and negative for TCRγ / TCRδ. B cell population was polyclonal. Genetic testing was performed and showed the mutation FAS, c.865T>G p.L208X, exon 8. The clinicopathologic findings are consistent with ALPS.
Differential diagnosis
  • Reactive lymphadenopathy:
    • Self limited lymph node enlargement, of recent (acute lymphadenitis) or chronic onset
    • Follicular or interfollicular lymphoid hyperplasia
    • Negative for clonality of T cells, B cells or plasma cells, by flow cytometry or gene rearrangements
  • EBV+ mucocutaneous ulcer:
    • Localized extranodal cutaneous or mucosal ulcer, mainly oral region
    • Underlying immunosuppression, including elderly, HIV infection and status post organ transplant
    • Shallow ulcer, polymorphic infiltrate and large B cells positive for EBV
    • Healing after removal of immunosuppression
  • Lymphomas arising in immune deficiency / dysregulation
    • Acute lymphoblastic leukemia / lymphoma:
      • B ALL more frequent in children
      • T ALL / LBL more frequent in mediastinum
      • Diffuse infiltrate of B or T cell lymphoblasts
      • B ALL positive for CD34 (60 - 80%), TdT (~95%) and CD10 (80 - 90%)
      • T ALL positive for CD34 (30 - 50%), TdT (~90%) and CD10 (10 - 30%)
    • Burkitt lymphoma:
      • Endemic form in Sub-Saharan Africa; common jaw and orbital
      • Sporadic form in Western hemisphere; mainly in children
        • Extranodal and abdominal
      • Diffuse infiltrate with a starry sky pattern
    • Diffuse large B cell lymphoma:
      • Most common adult non-Hodgkin lymphoma
      • Affects across all ages, predominantly in adults
      • Nodal or extranodal, diffuse large cell infiltrate, highly heterogeneous
      • Subset arises from low grade lymphoma
    • Plasmablastic lymphoma:
      • Patients with HIV infection or immunosuppression are susceptible
      • Predominantly extranodal
      • Monomorphic large cell lymphoma with plasma cell phenotype
      • EBV+ in 70% of cases
    • Classical Hodgkin lymphoma:
      • Predominantly nodal disease; 4 subtypes
      • Polymorphic infiltrate with Hodgkin and Reed-Sternberg cells
      • EBV+ in 20% (nodular sclerosis) to 60% (mixed cellularity)
    • Nodular lymphocyte predominant Hodgkin lymphoma:
      • B cell neoplasm with a prominent background of inflammatory cells
      • Predominantly nodular
      • Large cells positive for CD20, OCT2 and PAX5; variable reactivity with CD30
      • Rare EBV+ cases
    • Peripheral T cell lymphoma, NOS:
      • Affects adults, usually not history of immunodeficiency
      • Diffuse effacement of nodal architecture
      • Intermediate to large atypical lymphocytes
      • EBV is usually negative
    • Hepatosplenic T cell lymphoma:
      • ~20% arise in patients with a history of immunodysregulation
      • Massive splenomegaly with small or absent peripheral lymphadenopathy
      • Sinusoidal involvement of bone marrow, liver and spleen
      • Trisomy 8 and isochromosome 7 in ~60% of cases
    • Extranodal T / NK cell lymphoma:
      • Aggressive T / NK cell lymphoma, predominantly in extranodal sites
      • Diffuse effacement of affected tissues architecture, with angiocentricity and angiodestruction
      • 100% EBV associated

Board review style question #1
Which of the following lists the correct association between disease and mutation?

  1. Ataxia telangiectasia (AT): CD40 or CD40 ligand (CD40LG)
  2. Autoimmune lymphoproliferative syndrome (ALPS): FAS (TNFRSF6)
  3. Wiskott-Aldrich Syndrome: ATM
  4. X linked lymphoproliferative syndrome (XLP): NBN (nibrin)
Board review style answer #1
B. Autoimmune lymphoproliferative syndrome (ALPS): FAS (TNFRSF6). Answer A is incorrect because ataxia telangiectasia (AT) is associated with ATM mutation. Answer C is incorrect because Wiskott-Aldrich syndrome is associated with WAS mutation. Answer D is incorrect because X linked lymphoproliferative syndrome (XLP) is associated with mutations in SH2D1A or BIRC3 / XIAP.

Board review style question #2


What statement is correct about lymphoid proliferations and lymphomas associated with immune deficiency and dysregulation?

  1. According to the 5th WHO classification, the diagnosis should incorporate histology, viral status and associated immune condition
  2. EBV+ mucocutaneous ulcer (MCU) usually presents as multiple small ulcers associated with lymphadenopathy
  3. Epstein Barr virus / human herpesvirus 4 and Kaposi sarcoma herpesvirus / human herpesvirus 8 (KSHV / HHV8) are rarely related to lymphoid proliferations and lymphomas associated with immune deficiency / dysregulation
  4. Histologic presentation of lymphomas associated with immune deficiency and dysregulation is different from those arising in immunocompetent patients
Board review style answer #2
A. According to the 5th WHO classification, the diagnosis of disorders associated with immune deficiency or dysregulation should incorporate histology, viral status and associated immune condition. Answer B is incorrect because EBV+ mucocutaneous ulcer usually presents as a well circumscribed ulcer in mucosae or skin without systemic symptoms, such as lymphadenopathy. Answer D is incorrect because the histologic presentation of lymphomas associated with immune deficiency and dysregulation is similar to those arising in immunocompetent patients and classified according to standard nomenclature. Answer C is incorrect because EBV / human herpesvirus 4 and KSHV / HHV8 are usually related to lymphoid proliferations and lymphomas associated with immune deficiency and dysregulation.
