Definition / general

* Aggressive large B cell lymphoma with plasmablast / immunoblast morphologic features with plasma cell differentiation, usually in association with immunodeficiency

Essential features

* Aggressive lymphoma with unfavorable prognosis ([Am J Surg Pathol 2004;28:736](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/15166665))
* Distinct entity, according to the 2017 WHO classification
* Predominantly occurs in extranodal sites (most common in oral cavity)
* Commonly occurs in young adults with HIV infection or iatrogenic immunosuppression ([Am J Surg Pathol 2004;28:736](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/15166665), [Histopathology 2007;51:774](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/17944927))
* Pathogenesis is not well understood
* Requires histologic, immunophenotypic and clinical correlation for definitive diagnosis
* Monomorphic sheets of intermediate to large cells with plasmablastic / immunoblastic features, prominent nucleoli and positivity for plasmacytic markers ([Am J Surg Pathol 2004;28:736](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/15166665))

Terminology

* Plasmablastic lymphoma (PBL); subtype of diffuse large B cell lymphoma with sufficient distinctive features that is now considered a distinct entity

ICD coding

* ICD-O: [9735/3](https://apps.who.int/iris/bitstream/handle/10665/96612/9789241548496_eng.pdf?sequence=1#page=96) - plasmablastic lymphoma

Epidemiology

* Mostly occurs in adults with HIV infection or with iatrogenic immunosuppression
* In a 10 year study, most patients were men with a median age at presentation of 50 years ([Am J Hematol 2019;94:E127](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/30734351))

Sites

* First described in the oral cavity ([Blood 1997;89:1413](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/9028965))
* Presents frequently in extranodal sites, especially of the head and neck region
* Other common sites include skin, gastrointestinal tract and bone
* Nodal involvement is generally infrequent but is seen in 30% of posttransplant cases ([Blood 2015;125:2323](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/25636338))

Pathophysiology

* Derived from post germinal center B cells (plasmablast)

Etiology

* Not well understood
* Epstein-Barr virus (EBV) positivity is observed in 75% of cases ([Am J Surg Pathol 2014;38:875](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/24832164))
* Iatrogenic immunosuppression (e.g., posttransplant) and HIV positive patients
* Few cases are reported in immunocompetent individuals ([J Med Case Rep 2011;5:168](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/21539737))

Clinical features

* Mass forming lesion in extranodal sites, typically in the context of HIV infection, iatrogenic immunosuppression and posttransplant setting
* Disease is commonly disseminated and higher stage at the time of presentation (stage 3 - 4) with bone marrow involvement (75% of HIV positive patients and 50% of posttransplant patients) ([Blood 2015;125:2323](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/25636338))
* Elevated serum paraproteins may be detected

Diagnosis

* Requires correlation with clinical features to exclude plasmablastic plasma cell myeloma, including the presence of serum monoclonal proteins, SPEP / IFX studies and surveillance for lytic lesions
* Diagnosis can be complicated by overlapping morphologic and phenotypic features with plasmablastic plasma cell myeloma with extramedullary involvement
* *MYC* translocation is observed in approximately 50% of PBL cases ([Am J Surg Pathol 2010;34:1686](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/20962620))
* EBV positivity is present in approximately 75% of cases but may be rarely detected in myeloma ([Am J Surg Pathol 2014;38:875](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/24832164))
* Diffuse C-MYC expression by immunohistochemistry and high Ki67 proliferative index (70 - 90%) favors PBL over myeloma ([Histopathology 2007;51:774](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/17944927), [Blood 1997;89:1413](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/9028965))

Laboratory

* Serology for HIV
* Paraprotein levels may be elevated
* Monoclonal spike should not be present ([Mod Pathol 2005;18:806](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/15578069))

Radiology description

* Typically presents as an extranodal mass
* Diffuse bone marrow involvement is seen in 30% of patients
* Findings can range from an inflammatory process to a destructive lesion of bone and soft tissue ([Case Rep Ophthalmol Med 2018;2018:4746050](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/30420931))
* Imaging studies have been suggested for staging of the disease ([Radiographics 2018;38:2051](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/30339518), [Acta Radiol 2011;52:970](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/21911839))

Prognostic factors

* Prognosis is unfavorable
* Median survival is 6 - 12 months ([Am J Hematol 2019;94:E127](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/30734351), [Am J Surg Pathol 2014;38:875](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/24832164))
* *MYC* translocation is associated with worse prognosis ([Blood 2015;125:2323](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/25636338), [Am J Surg Pathol 2014;38:875](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/24832164))

Case reports

* 29 year old HIV positive man with a 2 week history of a gingival mass ([J Clin Exp Dent 2020;12:e429](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/32382394))
* 39 year old man with 1 month history of swelling of the oral cavity ([Medicine (Baltimore) 2020;99:e22335](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/32991445))
* 55 year old man with plasmablastic lymphoma of the small intestine ([World J Gastroenterol 2012;18:6677](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/23236245))
* 59 year old man with painless rectal bleeding ([J Med Case Rep 2011;5:168](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/21539737))
* 64 year old man with radionecrosis of the jaw ([Int J Surg Case Rep 2016;29:94](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/27837702))

Treatment

* Newer treatment modalities using V-EPOCH (bortezomib with etoposide, prednisone, vincristine, cyclophosphamide and doxorubicin) have shown increased survival ([Br J Haematol 2019;184:679](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/29527667))
* Despite newer treatments, prognosis remains unfavorable

Gross description

* No distinct or specific features on gross examination
* Soft tissue mass can be well circumscribed or irregular
* Cut surface is usually gray-tan to yellowish in color, with a fleshy appearance

Frozen section description

* Definitive diagnosis of plasmablastic lymphoma should be avoided at the time of frozen section, as definitive diagnosis requires phenotyping and clinical correlation
* Descriptive diagnosis, such as lymphoma with plasmablastic features or lymphoma with features suggestive of plasmablastic lymphoma, allows for adequate workup and definitive diagnosis
* Findings are similar to those seen on permanent sections; they consist of monomorphic sheets of intermediate to large sized cells with round eccentric nuclei

Microscopic (histologic) description

* 2 morphologic variants recognized: monomorphic PBL and PBL with plasmacytic differentiation
  + Monomorphic PBL:
    - Consist mostly of immunoblastic cells
  + PBL with plasmacytic differentiation:
    - Cells with plasmacytic differentiation / plasmablasts that are intermediate to large in size with round eccentric nuclei, prominent nucleoli and bluish cytoplasm ([Crane: Survival Guide to Lymph Node Pathology, 2021](https://www.innovativesciencepress.com/product-page/survival-guide-to-lymph-node-pathology))
      * Seen more frequently in the setting of HIV infection
      * Oral, nasal and paranasal sinuses are commonly affected
      * Anaplastic / plasmablastic plasma cell myeloma is an important differential that needs to be ruled out
        + Lytic bone lesions and monoclonal serum immunoglobulins can be seen in both
        + Bone marrow is not frequently involved in plasmablastic lymphoma
        + History of immune deficiency or EBER positivity by in situ hybridization is useful in establishing a diagnosis of plasmablastic lymphoma
        + *MYC* translocation will favor plasmablastic lymphoma
* Mitotic figures are increased
* Apoptotic cells and tingible body macrophages may be identified

Cytology description

* Sheets of monomorphic cells of intermediate to large sized lymphoid cells with plasmacytic differentiation and immunoblastic or plasmablastic morphology
* Large cells with abundant basophilic cytoplasm; large nucleus with prominent nucleolus

Positive stains

* [CD138](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/cdmarkerscd138.html), [CD38](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/cdmarkerscd38.html), [MUM1](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsmum1.html), [CD79a](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/cdmarkerscd79a.html) (40%) ([Blood 2015;125:2323](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/25636338), [Haematologica 2010;95:1342](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/20418245))
* [CD30](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/cdmarkerscd30.html) and [EMA](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsema.html) are frequently expressed
* [CD56](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/cdmarkerscd56.html) is detected in 25% of cases
* [IRF4 / MUM1](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsmum1.html), **XBP1** and **PRDM1** are positive
* [Ki67](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainski67.html) proliferation index is high (> 90%)
* Cytoplasmic [light chains](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainskappa.html) are usually expressed
* [EBER](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainseber1.html) by in situ hybridization present in approximately 70% of cases ([Am J Surg Pathol 2014;38:875](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/24832164))

Negative stains

* [CD20](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/cdmarkerscd20.html), [CD45](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/cdmarkerscd45.html) and [PAX5](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainspax5.html) (typically negative but may show weak expression in a subset of cells)
* [HHV8](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainshhv8.html)
* T cell antigens are usually negative but may show aberrant expression ([Mod Pathol 2018;31:718](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/29327711))

Flow cytometry description

* [CD38](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/cdmarkerscd38.html), [CD138](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/cdmarkerscd138.html) or [MUM1](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsmum1.html) positive population
* Negative or dim expression [CD45](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/cdmarkerscd45.html), [CD20](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/cdmarkerscd20.html) and [PAX5](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainspax5.html)
* [CD56](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/cdmarkerscd56.html) may be positive in a subset of cells
* [Light chain](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainskappa.html) restriction may be seen

Electron microscopy description

* Electron microscopy is not routinely undertaken for diagnosis
* Case studies that have incorporated electron microscopy have shown ([Exp Mol Pathol 2011;90:85](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/20969860))
  + Euchromatic nuclei with 1 or more nucleoli
  + Numerous ribosomes
  + Scant strands of rough endoplasmic reticulum

Molecular / cytogenetics description

* Genetic alterations in *MYC* gene are found in approximately 50% of cases ([Mod Pathol 2017;30:85](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/27687004))
  + *MYC* translocation, gain and overexpression at mRNA level
  + Seen more often in EBV positive tumors
* *PRDM1α* gene mutations (missense mutations) are associated with PRDM1 / Blimp1 expression ([Mod Pathol 2017;30:85](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/27687004))
* Clonal rearrangements in immunoglobulin genes
  + Clonal *IGH* rearrangement is present
* More recent studies that used whole genome sequencing (WES) have found multiple mutations ([Nat Commun 2021;12:5183](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/34465776))
  + *NRAS*: 31% of cases, mostly missense mutations
  + NOTCH signaling pathway: 26%
  + *STAT3*: 25% of cases, clustered in the SH2 domain
    - 47% of HIV positive patients
    - 10% of HIV negative patients

Sample pathology report

* Anterior hard palate, biopsy:
  + Plasmablastic lymphoma (see comment)
  + Comment: Histologic sections consist of squamous lined mucosa diffusely involved by lymphoma. The lymphoma is seen in sheets and composed of large atypical plasmablasts. Apoptotic figures and brisk mitotic activity are seen.
  + Immunohistochemical studies show that lymphoma cells are positive for MUM1, CD138 (variable), CD38 (variable), CD30 (variable) and C-MYC (~10%), while negative for CD20, PAX5, CD3 and HHV8. Lymphoma cells are lambda light chain restricted as detected by in situ hybridization (ISH). Kappa light chains are decreased (by ISH). Kappa light chains are markedly decreased (by ISH). EBER (by ISH) is diffusely positive. Ki67 proliferation index is approximately 100%.

Differential diagnosis

* [Diffuse large B cell lymphoma with plasmablastic features](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/lymphomadiffuse.html):
  + Typically nodal
  + Not readily associated with immunodeficiency
  + [CD20](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/cdmarkerscd20.html) and [PAX5](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainspax5.html) are strongly positive
  + [EBV](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainseber1.html) infection (identified by positive serology or tissue positivity for [EBER](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainseber1.html) by in situ hybridization) and *MYC* translocation are seen in < 10% of cases
* [Plasmablastic / anaplastic plasma cell myeloma](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/lymphomamyeloma.html):
  + More common in older individuals
  + Serum paraproteins are elevated with monoclonal gammopathy
  + Myeloma defining signs (CRAB: calcium elevation, renal insufficiency, anemia and bone abnormalities) usually present
  + Bone marrow involvement common
  + [EBV](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainseber1.html) infection and *MYC* translocation are less common
* [Primary effusion lymphoma (PEL)](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/lymphomaeffusion.html):
  + Typically presents in cavitary spaces but may be mass forming (solid PEL)
  + [HHV8 (LANA)](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainshhv8.html) immunostaining will be positive
  + Commonly coinfected with [EBV](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainseber1.html)
* [ALK positive large B cell lymphoma](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/lymphomaALKpositive.html):
  + Typically lacks association with immunosuppression
  + By definition, should be [ALK](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsalk.html) positive by immunohistochemistry

Board review style question #1

[![](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/imgau/lymphomaplasmablasticpoveda06.jpg)](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/imgau/lymphomaplasmablasticpoveda06.jpg)

What is the diagnosis of this hard palate lesion in a 54 year old man with HIV?

1. Diffuse large B cell lymphoma
2. Plasmablastic lymphoma
3. Angioimmunoblastic T cell lymphoma
4. Monomorphic epitheliotropic T cell lymphoma
5. Mantle cell lymphoma

Board review style answer #1

**B.** Plasmablastic lymphoma. The patient is a 54 year old man with history of HIV who underwent biopsy of a hard palate mass. The lymphoma is present as sheets of large atypical cells with prominent nucleoli. Immunohistochemical studies showed that lymphoma cells were positive for MUM1, CD138, CD38, CD30 and C-MYC (~10%) and negative for CD20 (therefore, choices A and E are eliminated), PAX5, CD3 (choices C and D are eliminated), ALK (ALK positive large B cell lymphoma would be ruled out) and HHV8 (primary effusion lymphoma is ruled out). Lymphoma cells are lambda light chain restricted as detected by in situ hybridization (ISH). Kappa light chains are decreased (by ISH). EBER (by ISH) is diffusely positive. The history, site, morphologic appearance and immunohistochemical findings support a diagnosis of plasmablastic lymphoma.

Which of the following stains is usually positive in plasmablastic lymphoma?

1. ALK
2. CD3
3. CD20

Board review style answer #2

**D.** EBER by in situ hybridization can be seen in other entities such as large B cell lymphoma and primary effusion lymphoma; however, the other stains are negative in plasmablastic lymphoma.
