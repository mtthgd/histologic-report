Definition / general

* An area of sclerotic bone related to chronic osteomyelitis that is present at root of tooth ([J Mass Dent Soc 2003;52:52](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/12723214))
* Tooth often demonstrates pulpitis or pulpal necrosis or previous restoration ([J Mass Dent Soc 2003;52:52](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/12723214), [J Endod 2013;39:977](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/23880262))

Essential features

* Dense sclerotic bone, seen radiographically, in area of a tooth with pulpitis or previous restoration
* Lesion resolves with treatment of affected tooth

Terminology

* Focal sclerosing osteomyelitis ([J Mass Dent Soc 2003;52:52](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/12723214))

ICD coding

* ICD-10: [M27.2](https://www.icd10data.com/ICD10CM/Codes/M00-M99/M26-M27/M27-/M27.2) - inflammatory conditions of jaws

Epidemiology

* 4 - 7% of population ([J Endod 2013;39:977](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/23880262))
* Early adolescence / young adults but can be any age ([J Mass Dent Soc 2003;52:52](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/12723214))
* Mandible more frequently than maxilla ([J Mass Dent Soc 2003;52:52](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/12723214))

Sites

* Oral cavity, mandible or maxilla, root of tooth ([J Endod 2013;39:977](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/23880262))

Pathophysiology

* Related to chronic inflammation of pulp of tooth causing sclerotic bone formation ([J Mass Dent Soc 2003;52:52](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/12723214))

Etiology

* Longstanding or low intensity infection with chronic inflammation and trabecular bone formation ([J Mass Dent Soc 2003;52:52](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/12723214), [J Endod 2013;39:977](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/23880262))

Clinical features

* Only clinical finding may be degenerative pulp disease ([J Endod 2013;39:977](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/23880262))

Diagnosis

* Diagnosis made by imaging of jaw

Radiology description

* Diffuse radiopaque borders concentrically arranged around root apex ([J Endod 2013;39:977](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/23880262))

Prognostic factors

* Most regress after treatment of affected tooth

Case reports

* 23 year old woman, 27 year old man and 32 year old woman with radiopaque apical lesions ([Bratisl Lek Listy 2009;110:713](http://bmj.fmed.uniba.sk/2009/11011-10.pdf))
* 28 year old man with radiopaque lesion on routine imaging ([Tex Dent J 2003;120:178](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/12674967))
* 57 year old woman with pain in right maxillary anterior region after extraction of upper left incisor ([J Mass Dent Soc 2003;52:52](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/12723214))

Treatment

* Treatment of infection in affected tooth with hopeful regression after root canal therapy or extraction ([J Mass Dent Soc 2003;52:52](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/12723214))

Gross description

* Bone, often fragmented

Frozen section description

* Frozen section not performed

Microscopic (histologic) description

* Replacement of cancellous bone with compact bone ([J Endod 2013;39:977](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/23880262))
* Scant fibrous tissue ([J Mass Dent Soc 2003;52:52](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/12723214))
* Often no obvious inflammation ([J Endod 2013;39:977](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/23880262), [J Mass Dent Soc 2003;52:52](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/12723214))

Sample pathology report

* Mandible, partial mandibulectomy:
  + Sclerotic bone consistent with condensing osteitis

Differential diagnosis

* **Idiopathic sclerosis**:
  + Not inflammatory or neoplastic
  + Intraosseous radiopacity of noninflammatory trabecular bone
* [Cemento-osseous dysplasia](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/mandiblemaxillafloridcemento.html):
  + May demonstrate multiple foci
  + Cellular fibrovascular connective tissue with mixture of bone and cementum-like particles
  + Radiographically characterized by radiolucency or radiopacity surrounded by radiolucency
* [Osteoma](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/mandiblemaxillaosteoma.html):
  + May be associated with [Gardner syndrome](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/colontumorgardner.html), particularly when multiple
  + Demonstrates expansion / growth over time with possible displacement of teeth
  + No association with inflammation

Board review style question #1

[![BRQ image](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/imgau/mandiblemaxillacondensingosteitiskalmar_01.jpg)](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/imgau/mandiblemaxillacondensingosteitiskalmar_01.jpg)

A panoramic Xray is obtained of a patient's mandible. To confirm the diagnosis of condensing osteitis, which radiographic feature should be present?

1. Mixed radiopaque / radiolucent lesion
2. No lesion identified
3. Radiolucent lesion
4. Radiopaque lesion

Board review style answer #1

**D.** Radiopaque lesion. Condensing osteitis is most often seen on panoramic Xray as a radiopaque lesion. There is always a lesion identified as it is how the diagnosis is made. Radiolucent lesions include other cysts such as odontogenic keratocyst, dentigerous cyst or periapical cyst.

Board review style question #2

What is the most common histologic finding in condensing osteitis?

1. Acute osteomyelitis
2. Compact / sclerotic bone
3. Marked chronic inflammatory infiltrate
4. Replacement of bone by fibrous tissue

Board review style answer #2

**B.** Compact / sclerotic bone. Condensing osteitis is related to chronic inflammation causing sclerotic bone formation. Acute osteomyelitis will not be seen as this is a chronic process. There also will not be a chronic inflammatory infiltrate given the chronicity of the process. There is scant fibrous tissue in this process; it is a reactive boney process.
