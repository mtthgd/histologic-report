Definition / general

* Metastases to the jaw and oral cavity are uncommon, ~ 1% of oral malignancies
* However, up to 25% of oral cavity metastases represent the first manifestation of disease
* Metastases to the jaw often indicate end stage disease, and are associated with poor survival

Epidemiology

* Uncommon, ~ 1% of all oral malignancies
* Most common in 5th to 7th decades; mean age ~ 54 years
* Common primary sites of origin:
  + Males: lung, kidney, liver, prostate
  + Females: breast, gynecologic, kidney, colorectum

Sites

* Jaw bones are more frequently affected than oral soft tissue
  + 2 : 1 ratio
  + Mandible more common than maxilla
* The gingiva is the most common site of oral soft tissue metastases

Etiology

* Pathogenesis of oral metastases is not fully understood but current hypotheses include:
  + A multistage process in which cells detach themselves from the primary tumor and are transported by lymphatic or blood vessels
  + In the oral soft tissues, chronically inflamed mucosa, especially gingiva, has a rich capillary network, which can trap the malignant cells and cause metastases
  + Possible role of Batson’s plexus, a valveless vertebral venous network that permits the retrograde movement of tumor cells from lungs towards the face

Clinical features

* Variable; symptoms include jaw pain, swelling, paresthesias, toothache, tooth abscess, non-healing tooth infection, tooth exfoliation
* May present with mental nerve neuropathy, also known as “numb chin syndrome”
  + Characterized by facial numbness along distribution of mental branch of trigeminal nerve
  + Most cases that are not dental in origin are associated with malignant tumors or diffuse metastatic disease

Diagnosis

* Dependent on clinical, radiologic and pathologic correlation

Radiology description

* Varies from well circumscribed to poorly circumscribed radiolucencies
* Most metastases to jaw tend to be expansile, lytic lesions with ill defined margins and cortical destruction

Prognostic factors

* Metastases to the jaw often indicate end stage disease, and are associated with poor survival
  + Mean time to death from diagnosis of jaw metastasis was 12 months versus 8 months for oral soft tissue metastasis ([J Craniomaxillofac Surg 2016;44:1047](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/27270028))

Case reports

* 45 year old woman with pain and swelling in jaw for 3 months ([J Clin Diagn Res 2015;9:ZD25](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/26393225))
* 58 year old man with three month history of unilateral anesthesia of chin and lip ([Br Dent J 2010;208:283](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/20379242))
* 60 year old man with lung metastasis to mandible ([J Oral Maxillofac Pathol 2015;19:385](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/26980970))
* 62 year old man with lung metastasis to gingiva ([Iran J Med Sci 2015;40:287](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/25999632))
* 69 year old man with progressive hypoesthesia of corner of mouth and chin ([Anticancer Res 2010;30:1819](http://ar.iiarjournals.org/content/30/5/1819.long))
* 75 year old man with 3 week history of numbness over lower lip ([BMJ Case Rep 2011 Apr 15;2011](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/22701024))

Treatment

* Depending on type of metastasis, treatment includes surgical resection, sometimes combined with radiation therapy or chemotherapy

Positive stains

* Depending on primary site, the workup for metastatic lesions usually includes [CK7](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsck7.html) and [CK20](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsck20.html), plus:
  + Breast: [ER](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainser.html) / [PR](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsprog.html), [GATA3](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsgata3.html), [mammaglobin](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsmammaglobin.html)
  + Lung: [TTF1](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsttf1.html), [napsin A](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsnapsina.html)
  + Prostate: [PSA](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainspsa.html), [PSAP](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainspap.html), [prostein](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsprostein.html), **NKX3.1**
  + Gynecologic: [PAX8](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainspax8.html), [WT1](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainswt1.html)
  + Thyroid: [TTF1](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsttf1.html), [PAX8](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainspax8.html), [thyroglobulin](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsthyroglobulin.html), [calcitonin](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainscalcitonin.html)
  + Kidney: [EMA](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsema.html), [PAX8](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainspax8.html), [PAX2](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainspax2.html), [CAIX](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainscarbonicanhydraseix.html), [RCC](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsrcc.html), [CD10](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/cdmarkerscd10.html)
  + Colon: [CDX2](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainscdx2.html)

Differential diagnosis

* The differential is broad, mainly based on radiologic findings, and includes:
  + [Odontogenic cysts](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/mandiblemaxillaodontogeniccyst.html)
  + Benign and malignant [odontogenic tumors](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/mandiblemaxillaadenomatoid.html)
  + Reactive / inflammatory processes related to odontogenic infection or inflammation
  + Salivary gland neoplasms
  + Other metastases such as [lymphoma](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/mandiblemaxillalymphoma.html), melanoma, etc.
