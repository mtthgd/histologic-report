Definition / general
  • Osteosarcoma constitutes a primary malignant bone neoplasm in which tumor cells produce immature (osteoid) bone
  • Osteosarcoma of maxillofacial region is called gnathic osteosarcoma
Essential features
  • Jaw is the fourth most common site (gnathic osteosarcoma) and primarily occurs in the third and fourth decade of life (Clin Pract 2024;14:965)
  • Distinct clinical and biological behavior as compared to osteosarcoma of the extremities
  • Neoplastic spindle cells with osteoid deposition and variable cellular (osteoblastic, chondroblastic and fibroblastic) differentiation (J Maxillofac Oral Surg 2018;17:38)
Terminology
  • Osteosarcoma (WHO recommended)
ICD coding
  • ICD-O:
    • 9180/3 - osteosarcoma, NOS
    • 99186/3 - conventional osteosarcoma
  • ICD-11: 2B51.0 - osteosarcoma of bone and articular cartilage of jaw
Epidemiology
  • Jaw bones account for 6% of osteosarcoma cases with mandible being the most common site
  • More common in third and fourth decades of life, as compared to axial osteosarcoma
  • No sex predilection (J Int Med Res 2020;48:300060520971488)
Sites
  • Mandible (alveolar ridge) is the most common location, followed by maxilla (antrum) (Front Oncol 2023;13:1006622)
Pathogenesis
  • Chromosomal instability:
    • Hallmark of osteosarcoma, chromosomal segregation errors during cell division (chromoanagenesis; chromothripsis / chromoplexy) leads to supernumerary chromosome that results in chromosomal instability
  • Target genes involved in the development of osteosarcoma:
    • TP53 and RB1 genes commonly mutated (high grade osteosarcoma)
    • MDM2 and CDK4 gene amplifications (low grade and dedifferentiated osteosarcomas) (Dis Markers 2021;2021:8818363, Crit Rev Oncol Hematol 2020;156:103126)
Etiology
  • Most cases are sporadic in nature
  • Ionizing radiation, pre-existing Paget disease of the bone, fibrous dysplasia and chronic osteomyelitis are important risk factors (Clin Pract 2024;14:965)
Diagrams / tables
  • See Molecular / cytogenetics images section
Clinical features
  • Localized jaw swelling with pain and paresthesia (most common presentation) and tooth displacement or loss and misalignment (J Maxillofac Oral Surg 2018;17:38)
  • Nasal obstruction and epistaxis (commonly in maxillary bone tumors) (J Neurol Surg B Skull Base 2021;82:e138)
  • Facial disfigurement, hard tender swelling of maxilla with bone destruction (Int J Surg Case Rep 2021;87:106425)
Diagnosis
  • Imaging:
    • CT scan is particularly useful for assessing biopsy and staging
    • MRI is preferred for intraosseous tumor extension and soft tissue involvement (Dentomaxillofac Radiol 2020;49:20190202)
  • Open biopsy is the gold standard for diagnosis
Laboratory
  • Not clinically relevant
Radiology description
  • Imaging findings depend on amount of mineralized matrix produced by the tumor
  • Most cases are mixed radiolucent and radio dense, with osteodestructive growth pattern and soft tissue extension (suggesting high grade lesions), classic periosteal reaction (Codman triangle and sunburst appearance) (Dentomaxillofac Radiol 2020;49:20190202)
  • Widening of the periodontal ligament space of the involved teeth due to tumour infiltration

Prognostic factors
  • Size, cranial / brain extension and distant metastases at the time of presentation
  • Residual disease (post operative) (Am J Rhinol Allergy 2021;35:132)
Case reports
  • 34 year old woman, gingival swelling with loosening of the lower right canine (Clin Case Rep 2023;11:e7198)
  • 45 year old man presented with painless oral swelling for 5 months (Int J Surg Case Rep 2022;93:106909)
  • 47 year old woman presented with painless quickly increasing swelling near anterior mandible for three months (Cureus 2024;16:e53713)
  • 62 year old woman presented with large painful swelling on right mandibular area for 2 months (J Egypt Natl Canc Inst 2023;35:30)
  • 65 year old man referred to the otorhinolaryngology outpatient clinic with difficulty of swallowing and pain on right of face for 3 months (Medicina 2022;58:1141)
Treatment
  • Treatment of choice; surgical resection with clear margins (particular mandible and maxilla)
  • Slightly improved survival with (neo)adjuvant chemotherapy setting in cases of positive margins, recurrence and high grade tumors (Medicine 2019;98:e13875)

Microscopic (histologic) description
  • Tumor comprises highly atypical cells and associated immature and lace-like neoplastic osteoid deposition (conventional osteosarcoma)
  • Extent of matrix deposition range from focal immature osteoid to heavily mineralized sclerotic bone formation
  • Entrapped pre-existing trabeculae infiltrated by tumor cells and neoplastic bone (osteoid) → osteodestructive and permeation of marrow spaces
  • Pseudo capsule (rim of periosteum around the invasive edge after cortical destruction)
  • Predominant matrix determine the conventional osteosarcoma subtypes:
    • Chondroblastic osteosarcoma: conventional osteosarcoma with predominant cartilage differentiation (loose basophilic intercellular substance with cells lying in lacunar spaces), common in jaw bones (J Cancer Res Ther 2018;14:471)
    • Osteoblastic osteosarcoma: abundant tumor osteoid production → from early dense fibrillar eosinophilic substance to lace-like pattern and variable mineralization and ossification (tumor bone formation)
    • Fibroblastic osteosarcoma: confluent proliferation of atypical spindle cells (mimicking fibrosarcoma) with high grade cytologic atypia and irregular mineralized matric deposition
  • Low grade central osteosarcoma and parosteal osteosarcoma (Low grade osteosarcoma): abundant fibroblastic stromal component with embedded irregular and interlacing bone trabeculae
  • Low cellularity and minimal cytologic atypia

Sample pathology report
  • Posterior mandibular mass, Biopsy:
    • Conventional Osteosarcoma, osteoblastic type, high grade (see comments).
    • Comment: The biopsy shows a malignant neoplasm that comprises cellular areas of oval to spindle cells with marked cytologic atypia, nuclear pleomorphism and brisk mitotic activity. Areas of fibrillar eosinophilic material deposition (osteoid) by tumor cells seen. The findings are consistent with osteosarcoma with osteoblastic differentiation. Radiological correlation is advised.
Differential diagnosis
  • Limited biopsy may not contain osteoid deposition areas and poses a diagnostic challenge
  • Sarcomatoid squamous cell carcinoma (SCC):
    • Bleeding ulcer on alveolar ridge, limited jaw opening and pain, chronic sinus discharge (oral cavity), nasal obstruction and epistaxis (maxillary involvement) and scalp skin ulcer with underlying bone destruction (temporal region)
    • Mandibular ramus (most common site), followed by buccal region, paranasal sinuses (particularly maxilla) and scalp skin
    • Male predilection
    • Peak in forth to sixth decades of life
    • Microscopy: sheets of spindle cells arranged in fascicular pattern, hyperchromatic nuclei and conspicuous nucleoli; absent keratinization and epithelial nests (J Oral Maxillofac Pathol 2019;23:163)
    • Positive p40 and CK5/6 immunostains are diagnostic for SCC (J Oral Maxillofac Pathol 2019;23:163)
  • Fibrous dysplasia (FD):
    • Involves facial bones (mandible and maxilla) and skull (particularly frontal bone)
    • Occurs in children and young adults (second tothird decade)
    • Monostotic forms (involves one bone) and polyostotic (multiple bones)
    • Presentation includes painless jaw swelling, nasal obstruction (maxilla involvement) and scalp swelling (cranial bone involvement)
    • Characterized by GNAS mutation
    • Microscopy: mixture of fibrous tissue (mostly hypocellular and contains bland spindle cells) and irregular curvilinear trabeculae of woven bone without osteoblastic rimming (J Clin Med 2023;12:4100)
    • Fibrous dysplasia of jaw can show osteoblastic rimming and lamellar bone differentiation as it matures
    • Mimic low grade osteosarcomas (Parosteal and low grade central) due to lack of cytologic atypia, fibrous tissue and lamellar bone
    • MDM2 gene amplification by FISH or positive staining of MDM2 and CDK4 immunostains might aid in differentiating these lesions (Mod Pathol 2010;23:1279)
  • Central giant cell granuloma (CGCG):
    • Exclusively involves the jaw (particularly anterior mandible)
    • Young age (less than 30 years) with female predilection
    • Imaging: solid and cystic tumor with prominent calcification and contrast enhancing cyst wall (> 90% of the cases)
    • Microscopy: tumor with lobulated architecture, lobules separated by thin fibrous septae that may contains osteoid or woven bone; lobules contain spindle to polygonal shaped mononuclear stromal cells and admixed multinucleated osteoclast-like giant cells; the background contains hemorrhage and hemosiderin pigment (J Oral Maxillofac Pathol 2020;24:413)
  • Conventional chondrosarcoma:
    • Mostly involves maxilla and nasal septum than mandible
    • Slight predilection to middle aged man
    • Imaging: osteolytic (high T2 weighted signal intensity) lesion, range from well defined to aggressive destructive with stippled to irregular masses opacities (best seen on CT scan)
    • Microscopy: tumor exhibits lobular architecture with permeative growth pattern; oval to polygonal cells with small uniform nuclei, evenly dispersed chromatin and inconspicuous nucleoli within lacunar spaces surrounded by basophilic matrix (well differentiated tumor; resembles hyaline cartilage); high cellularity with irregular distribution, multinucleation and nuclear pleomorphism and brisk mitotic activity correspond to higher grade (mimic chondroblastic type osteosarcoma) (J Med Chem 1986;29:2149)
  • Osteoblastoma:
    • Often involves body of mandible (intraosseous or periosteal) among craniofacial bones
    • Rare tumor, occurs in second to third decades of life with slight female predominance
    • Presents with localized swelling and pain
    • Imaging: circumscribed round (radiolucent to radiopaque) lesion up to 20 mm diameter without sclerotic rim or periosteal reaction
    • Microscopy: anastomosing trabeculae of bone and osteoid, rimmed by osteoblasts with a background of rich fibrovascular stroma; lack of cortex infiltration, nuclear pleomorphism, atypical or brisk mitoses distinguish it from osteoblastic type conventional osteosarcoma (Virchows Arch 2020;476:455)
  • Cementoblastoma:
    • Develops on apical third of third root (permanent first molar in posterior mandible), mandibular premolar and maxillary molar are second most common sites of involvement
    • Characteristically causes slow painful expansion of jaw
    • Occurs in second and third decades of life, no sex predilection
    • Imaging: a well defined mixed density lesion within the root of the associated tooth; characteristic radiolucent rim in continuity of the periodontal ligament usually present
    • Microscopy: dense cellular cementum mass fused with resorbed surface of the root (resembles bone) with prominent reversal lines; peripheral radiating new matrix deposition along with plump cementoblasts and cementoclasts within immature vascularized fibrous tissue (mimic primary bone tumor) (Oncol Lett 2016;11:3761, Radiol Case Rep 2024;19:2085)
  • Mucosal melanoma:
    • In head and neck, nasal cavity / septum and maxillary sinus are common sites
    • Nasal endoscopy: pigmented polypoidal mass
    • Microscopy: polymorphic tumor cell population: spindled, epithelioid, rhabdoid, clear and undifferentiated cell morphology
    • HMB45+ (Head Neck Pathol 2016;10:298)
  • Odontogenic sarcoma:
    • Mixed odontogenic neoplasm with malignant ectomesenchymal component
    • Involves mandible (particularly posterior region)
    • Locally aggressive expansile lesions with or without pain or tooth displacement
    • Imaging: rounded (benign appearing) radiolucent lesion with cortical expansion and destruction
    • Microscopy: epithelial (benign) component resembles dental lamina line strands or large islands of odontogenic epithelium
    • Peripheral cells with nuclear palisading and reverse polarity without stellate reticulum
    • Ectomesenchymal (malignant) component: hypercellular with varying cytologic atypia and mitoses (fibrosarcoma-like areas)
    • Dysplastic dentine with or without enamel matrix can be present (ameloblastic fibrodentinosarcoma or ameloblastic fibroodontosarcoma) (J Oral Maxillofac Pathol 2020;24:157)

Board review style question #1


A 39 years old male presented with a swelling on the right side of the face; examination shows bleeding gum at third molar. An open biopsy performed which shows a hypocellular spindle cell lesion with abundant fibroblastic stroma, minimal cytologic atypia, few mitosis and well formed bone trabeculae. Which of the following cytogenetic abnormality will be present in this tumor?

  1. EWSR1::FLI1 fusion translocation
  2. GNAS mutation
  3. MDM2 and CDK4 genes amplification
  4. TP53 germline gene mutation
Board review style answer #1
C. The histologic findings described above are characteristic of parosteal osteosarcoma that is cytogenetically characterized by a supernumerary ring chromosome (one or more). The target genes in the amplicons include MDM2 and CDK4 (amplified in > 85% of the cases). Answers B is incorrect as parosteal osteosarcoma closely resembles with fibrous dysplasia and lacks GNAS mutation (usually seen in fibrous dysplasia). Answer D is also incorrect as TP53 germline gene mutation is seen in Li-Fraumeni syndrome (predisposition for childhood soft tissue and bone sarcomas). Answer A is incorrect because EWSR1::FLI1 fusion is a hallmark of Ewing Sarcoma.

Board review style question #2
 


A 40 year old male presented with history of an ulcerated growth in the ramus of the mandible. The biopsy shows atypical round cell within the lacunar spaces and intervening loose basophilic background with focal lacy osteoid deposition. A diagnosis of conventional osteosarcoma with chondroblastic differentiation was made. Which of the following conditions can predispose to secondary osteosarcoma?

  1. Enchondromatosis
  2. Epstein Bar Virus (EBV) infection
  3. McCune-Albright Syndrome
  4. Prior head and neck ionizing radiation
  5. Smoking
Board review style answer #2
D. Ionizing radiation for head and neck cancer is a well accepted treatment modality. It can predispose to new malignancies particularly sarcoma. Ionizing radiations causes point mutation in p53 gene and RB gene alterations (as seen in osteosarcoma). Answer B is incorrect and EBV is most causes carcinoma (Nasopharyngeal carcinoma) in head and neck region and lymphomas (NK/T cell lymphoma which presents as lethal midline granuloma of nose and Burkitt’s Lymphoma). Answer C is also incorrect as McCune Albright syndrome is characterized by fibrous dysplasia of bone, café au lait skin lesions and hyperfunctioning endocrinopathies. Answer A is incorrect because enchondromatosis, as name implies, comprises multiple cartilaginous neoplasms of bone and myxomas. Answer E is incorrect because smoking is risk factor for squamous cell carcinoma.
