Definition / general

* Highly aggressive malignancy with rhabdomyoblastic differentiation
* Most commonly arises in the craniofacial bones of young adults
* Characterized by the presence of *TFCP2* rearrangement, with either *TFCP2::FUS* or *TFCP2::EWSR1*

Essential features

* High grade malignancy, usually showing a combination of spindle cell and epithelioid morphologies; however, may also show round cell morphology
* Evidence of rhabdomyoblastic differentiation, most reliably demonstrated by immunostaining for desmin, MyoD1 or myogenin
* May show concomitant expression of cytokeratins (notably AE1 / AE3) and ALK by IHC
* Confirmation of *TFCP2* rearrangement by molecular modalities (e.g., FISH [dual color break apart probe] or RNA next generation sequencing [NGS])

Terminology

* *FET::TFCP2* fusion rhabdomyosarcoma (RMS) ([Head Neck Pathol 2023;17:546](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/36374445))

ICD coding

* ICD-O: [8912/3](https://seer.cancer.gov/tools/solidtumor/current/STM_Other_Sites.pdf#page=472) - intraosseous spindle cell rhabdomyosarcoma with *TFCP2 / NCOA2* rearrangements
* ICD-11: [2B66.Y & XH0GA1](https://icd.who.int/browse/2024-01/mms/en#76069495%2Fother%20%26%201795620341) - other specified malignant neoplasms of other and unspecified parts of mouth & rhabdomyosarcoma, NOS

Epidemiology

* Mean age: 34 years (median: 29.5 years; range: 7 - 86 years) ([Histopathology 2024;84:776](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/38114270))
  + 56.8% of cases arise in patients Transl Pediatr 2024;13:178)
* Slight female predominance; F:M = ~1.5:1 in latest literature reviews ([Histopathology 2024;84:776](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/38114270))
* No known causal connections or risk factors

Sites

* Majority (66.6%) of cases present in craniofacial bones ([Histopathology 2024;84:776](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/38114270))
  + Mandible (34.1%), maxilla (31.8%), other craniofacial (34.1%)
* Other skeletal locations ([Histopathology 2024;84:776](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/38114270))
  + Pelvic bones, vertebrae, femur
* Nonskeletal locations ([Histopathology 2024;84:776](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/38114270))
  + Peritoneum and abdominal wall, chest wall and shoulder, bladder
* Unknown primary site (3% of overall cases)

Pathophysiology

* Transcription factor cellular promoter 2 (*TFCP2*) is involved in DNA synthesis and cell survival
  + Rearrangement of *TFCP2* with a breakpoint at the 5' end of exon 2 preserves the DNA binding domain of the protein
  + *TFCP2* encodes the late SV40 factor (LSF) oncoprotein, which is a transcription factor that functions as a coactivator for *YAP*, a key transcription factor downstream of the Hippo and Wnt signaling pathways ([Histopathology 2021;79:347](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/33382123))
  + LSF overexpression has been implicated in a variety of cancers, including breast cancer (where it upregulates expression of epidermal growth factor receptor [EGFR]); it is overexpressed in most cases of hepatocellular carcinoma, pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma and some cases of oral squamous cell carcinoma ([Histopathology 2021;79:347](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/33382123))
* *TFCP2* is fused with either *FUS* or *EWSR1*
  + *FUS* and *EWS* are members of the *FET* RNA binding protein family (*FUS, EWS, TAF15*); these tumors have occasionally been referred to as *FET::TFCP2* rhabdomyosarcomas ([Head Neck Pathol 2021;15:374](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/32504289), [EMBO Rep 2019;20:e45766](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/30962207))
  + *FUS* and *EWSR1* have similar structures and are involved in a variety of cellular processes such as transcriptional regulation, RNA splicing and DNA damage repair; they are often identified as the 5' part of chimeric fusions driving various leukemias and sarcomas ([Nat Commun 2024;15:51](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/38168093))
* Despite being a fusion driven sarcoma, *TFCP2* rearranged rhabdomyosarcomas show complex genomic profiles; genomic instability may be due to homologous recombination deficiency ([Nat Commun 2024;15:51](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/38168093))
* RNA sequencing shows extremely high ALK production in these tumors; this is a result of intragenic deletions and aberrant splicing, with preservation of the kinase domain, which causes the formation of short transcripts
  + Upstream, alternate promoters have been hypothesized to result in ALK activation and may be a direct result of the chimeric genes *FUS::TFCP2* and *EWSR1::TFCP2* ([Nat Commun 2024;15:51](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/38168093), [Mod Pathol 2020;33:404](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/31383960))
  + As ALK expression is by mechanisms other than rearrangement, these tumors are likely not susceptible to ALK inhibitors such as crizotinib ([Nat Commun 2024;15:51](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/38168093))
* Concomitant homozygous deletion of *CDKN2A* is present in virtually all cases, with codeletion of adjacent *MTAP* gene in most of these cases ([Nat Commun 2024;15:51](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/38168093), [Mod Pathol 2020;33:404](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/31383960))
* Genomic evolution has been seen in a small subset of *FUS::TFCP2* tumors, in which the local recurrence showed gain of intragenic *ALK* deletions and homozygous *CDKN2A* deletions compared to the original tumor; the genomes in these cases became increasingly complex, with evidence of homologous recombination deficiency ([Nat Commun 2024;15:51](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/38168093))
* Compared to other RMS subtypes, differentially expressed genes in rhabdomyosarcoma with *TFCP2* rearrangement include *ALK, TERT, IGFBP5* and *PAPPA2*
  + These genes are associated with biological processes relating to muscle (downregulation of troponins, myosin heavy chains) and keratinization (upregulation of multiple keratins) ([Nat Commun 2024;15:51](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/38168093))

Etiology

* Unknown

Clinical features

* Most cases present with rapidly progressive swelling at the involved site; specific symptoms are caused by localized mass effect of the tumor
  + Such symptoms include but are not limited to headache, nasal congestion, exophthalmos, toothache ([Head Neck Pathol 2023;17:546](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/36374445), [Mod Pathol 2020;33:404](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/31383960))
  + However, a significant proportion of cases masquerade as a benign process (e.g., gingival hyperplasia) ([Histopathology 2024;84:776](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/38114270))
* Average tumor size at presentation is 59 mm ([Histopathology 2024;84:776](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/38114270))

Diagnosis

* Due to the rapid growth and aggressive nature of these tumors, which often leads to disfigurement in the head and neck region and site specific symptoms from mass effect, they can be suspected on clinical examination
* Diagnosis can be suspected on imaging (such as computed tomography or magnetic resonance imaging) if a destructive mass is seen arising from the bone; however, histological and molecular diagnosis is essential
* WHO blue book histological criteria for diagnosis
  + Essential: a high grade epithelioid, spindle cell or mixed tumor; MyoD1 or myogenin expression
  + Desirable: location in bone; *TFCP2* rearrangement or typical immunophenotype with coexpression of AE1 / AE3 and ALK
* Diagnosis relies on biopsy and subsequent immunohistochemistry / molecular testing

Radiology description

* Computed tomography (CT): hypodense, lytic intraosseous lesion; often locally destructive at time of presentation, with extension into soft tissues in majority of cases ([Transl Pediatr 2024;13:178](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/38323177))
* Magnetic resonance imaging (MRI)
  + T1 weighted: hypointense and isointense signal in relation to soft tissues
  + T2 weighted: solid tumor component with hyperintense signal ([Head Neck Pathol 2023;17:546](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/36374445))

Prognostic factors

* Overall prognosis is poor, compounded by the fact that most tumors present at a locally advanced stage, often with bone and soft tissue destruction
  + Overall mean survival time estimates vary from 8 to 18.8 months, though follow up data is limited ([Transl Pediatr 2024;13:178](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/38323177), [Histopathology 2024;84:776](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/38114270))
  + 0.5 year, 1 year and 3 year overall survival rates of 72%, 57% and 28%, respectively ([Histopathology 2024;84:776](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/38114270))
  + Significant proportion of patients do not achieve R0 resections and hence have no disease free interval ([Histopathology 2024;84:776](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/38114270))
* Distant metastases involving bone, lung or lymph nodes are present at diagnosis or follow up in 65% of cases ([Mod Pathol 2020;33:404](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/31383960))
* Unfavorable prognostic factors
  + Age
    - When stratified by age, patients ages 18 - 24 show the worst prognosis (1 year overall survival of 0%) ([Histopathology 2024;84:776](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/38114270))
  + Use of standard chemotherapy (as opposed to escalated, intensive, multimodal regimens)
  + Presence of *CDKN2A* gene alteration
    - Most commonly, homozygous deletion of *CDKN2A* gene; however, amplification has also been reported ([Histopathology 2024;84:776](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/38114270))
    - 0.5 year overall survival: 59% (positive for *CDKN2A* gene alteration) versus 77% (negative for *CDKN2A* gene alteration) ([Histopathology 2024;84:776](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/38114270))
  + Presence of *ALK* gene alteration
    - Most commonly, hemizygous or homozygous deletion of *ALK* gene ([Mod Pathol 2020;33:404](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/31383960))
    - Presence of *ALK* gene alteration: hazard ratio = 2.796 ([Histopathology 2024;84:776](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/38114270))
* Favorable
  + Age
    - Patients > 50 years of age have 1 year and 3 year overall survival rates of 82% and 45%, respectively ([Histopathology 2024;84:776](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/38114270))
  + Location
    - Overall prognosis appears more favorable for tumors arising in the mandible compared to other craniofacial bones
    - Disease free survival appears higher for tumors arising in the mandible; however, the paucity of cases precludes the establishment of statistical significance ([Virchows Arch 2020;477:725](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/32556562))
    - Risk of progressive disease appears lower for tumors arising in the mandible; Chrisinger et al. identified 16 cases with progressive disease, of which only 1 case had a primary tumor arising in the mandible ([Virchows Arch 2020;477:725](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/32556562))

Treatment

* Primary treatment is heterogeneous, ranging from surgery alone to a combination of neo / adjuvant multimodal therapies, including chemotherapy, radiation therapy and targeted therapies; overall, different combinations of treatment modalities were of no statistical significance ([Histopathology 2024;84:776](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/38114270))
* Surgery
  + Mainstay of treatment; however, tumor deemed too advanced for surgery in a significant proportion of cases ([Histopathology 2024;84:776](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/38114270))
* Chemotherapy and radiation therapy
  + No established protocol
  + Case reports include use as neoadjuvant, adjuvant and monotherapy
  + Neoadjuvant chemotherapy may result in longer progression free survival in some cases ([Nat Commun 2024;15:51](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/38168093))
* Targeted therapies
  + ALK inhibitors
    - Anecdotal cases of disease stabilization have been reported following combined radiotherapy and ALK inhibitors ([Histopathology 2023;82:478](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/36350070))
    - Overall, tumors with advanced stage and extensive pretreatment show limited sensitivity to ALK inhibitors ([Nat Commun 2024;15:51](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/38168093))
    - In cases of metastatic disease, some cases may show partial response to ALK inhibitors ([Nat Commun 2024;15:51](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/38168093))
  + Vascular endothelial growth factor (VEGF) inhibitors
    - Have been used either alone or in conjunction with ALK inhibitors
    - Most cases showed progressive disease despite therapy ([Histopathology 2023;82:478](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/36350070))

Microscopic (histologic) description

* Malignant tumor with varied morphology
* Most commonly, the tumor is biphasic (spindle cell and epithelioid) but may comprise either spindle cell, epithelioid, round cells or any combination of the 3 ([Histopathology 2024;84:776](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/38114270))
  + Combined spindle cell and epithelioid = 58%
  + Pure spindle cell = 18%
  + Pure epithelioid = 9%
  + Pure round cell = 1.5%
  + Combined spindle and round cell = 1.5%
  + Combined spindle cell, epithelioid and round cell = 1.5%
* Tumor grows in sheets or fascicles with extensive infiltration at the advancing front
* Tumor cells are typically large with prominent nucleoli; although some areas may appear monotonous (typical for fusion sarcomas), pleomorphism can be marked (due to aneuploidy)
* Mitotic activity ranges from 3 - 66 mitotic figures/10 high power fields ([Mod Pathol 2020;33:404](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/31383960))
* Necrosis is often present ([Nat Commun 2024;15:51](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/38168093), [Histopathology 2024;84:776](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/38114270))
* Rhabdomyoblasts are rare
  + Focally present in 11% of cases and always associated with dominant spindle cell and epithelioid subtypes ([Histopathology 2024;84:776](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/38114270))
  + Rhabdomyoblastic lineage confirmed through use of immunohistochemistry for MyoD1 and myogenin

* Occasionally positive: [SATB2](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainssatb2.html), [S100](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsS100.html), [CD99](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/cdmarkerscd99.html) (focal), [CD30](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/cdmarkerscd30.html) (focal) ([Head Neck Pathol 2021;15:374](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/32504289), [Mod Pathol 2020;33:404](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/31383960))

Negative stains

* [ERG](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsERG.html), [SOX10](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainssox10.html), [CK18](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsck18.html), [CK20](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsck20.html), [h-caldesmon](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainscaldesmon.html)
* [INI1](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsini1.html) (retained)

Molecular / cytogenetics description

* Molecular testing is required to confirm the presence of *TFCP2* rearrangement, through use of a variety of molecular modalities ([Histopathology 2021;79:347](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/33382123))
  + Fluorescent in situ hybridization: dual color break apart probe can be used to confirm presence of *TFCP2* rearrangement
  + RNA next generation sequencing
* Characterized by *TFCP2* rearrangement, which is fused at the 5' end to either *FUS* or *EWSR1*
  + *FUS::TFCP2* (~66% of cases) ([Histopathology 2024;84:776](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/38114270))
  + *EWSR1::TFCP2* (~33% of cases) ([Histopathology 2024;84:776](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/38114270))
    - Associated more often with soft tissue location ([Mod Pathol 2020;33:404](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/31383960))
* Despite being a fusion associated sarcoma, these tumors have complex genetic profiles; these include cases of aneuploidy (with associated chromothripsis of 1p and 3p), *CDKN2A/B* inactivation, unbalanced translocations and gene amplification (including *MDM2*) ([Mod Pathol 2020;33:404](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/31383960))
* *ALK*
  + Majority of cases associated with *ALK* gene alteration, most commonly gene deletions - internal hemizygous, hemizygous or homozygous internal deletion ([Mod Pathol 2020;33:404](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/31383960))
    - Overexpression by IHC likely due to a truncated isoform that lacks part of or the entire extracellular domain of the receptor tyrosine kinase; this results in overexpression by an undescribed alternative promoter ([Pathology 2019;51:116](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/30477883), [Nat Commun 2024;15:51](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/38168093))
* *CDKN2A*
  + Concomitant homozygous deletion of *CDKN2A* present in virtually all cases ([Mod Pathol 2020;33:404](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/31383960))
    - Almost always codeleted with neighboring *MTAP* or *CDKN2B* genes ([Nat Commun 2024;15:51](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/38168093))
  + Amplification of *CDKN2A/B* reported in one case ([Histopathology 2024;84:776](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/38114270))
* Differentially expressed genes compared to other RMS subtypes
  + *TERT, IGFBP5, PAPPA2* overexpression ([Nat Commun 2024;15:51](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/38168093))
  + Genomic profile of *TFCP2* rearranged RMS more closely resembles undifferentiated sarcomas rather than other RMS subtypes ([Nat Commun 2024;15:51](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/38168093))

Sample pathology report

* Maxilla, anterior maxillectomy:
  + Rhabdomyosarcoma with *TFCP2* rearrangement (see comment)
  + Comment: There is a high grade spindle cell neoplasm with its epicenter in the maxillary bone. The tumor is composed of fascicles and whorls of spindle shaped cells with elongated nuclei and small to moderate amount of eosinophilic cytoplasm. The nuclei are hyperchromatic and show moderate nuclear pleomorphism. Mitotic rate is focally elevated. The tumor demonstrates variable cellularity with the intraosseous component demonstrating areas of high cellularity and marked nuclear pleomorphism. Occasional rhabdomyoblasts are present in the center of the tumor. There is extensive bone destruction and the tumor infiltrates into the adjacent soft tissues and the submucosal tissues in the left gingivolabial region. The tumor is focally seen in the submucosa of the respiratory epithelium lining the left nasal floor. In these areas, the tumor demonstrates an infiltrative growth pattern and mild nuclear pleomorphism. The tumor extends to margins at the left maxillary tuberosity and the gingival submucosal tissues around tooth 25. Immunohistochemistry (with working external controls) shows strong diffuse staining in malignant cells for desmin, MyoD1 and ALK1 (D5F3). There is patchy positive staining for AE1 / AE3. Malignant cells are negative for p40, high molecular weight cytokeratin and SOX10. Fluorescent in situ hybridization (dual color break apart probe) shows a split signal for *TFCP2*.

Differential diagnosis

* High grade malignancies with positive [cytokeratin](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsckgeneral.html) staining, either primary or metastatic
  + [Poorly differentiated squamous cell carcinoma (spindle cell)](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/oralcavityscc.html):
    - Older patients
    - Negative for myogenic markers
  + [Epithelioid sarcoma](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/softtissueepithelioidsarcoma.html):
    - Positive for [cytokeratins](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsckgeneral.html)
    - Aberrant staining for [INI1 / SMARCB1](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsini1.html) (loss)
    - Negative for [desmin](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsdesmin.html)
  + [Alveolar rhabdomyosarcoma](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/softtissuealvrhabdo.html):
    - Positive for [cytokeratins](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsckgeneral.html) (broad spectrum or low molecular weight) in roughly 50% of cases ([Mod Pathol 2008;21:795](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/18487991))
    - Negative for [ALK](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsalk.html)
* High grade sarcomatoid malignancies with rhabdomyoblastic differentiation
  + [Malignant peripheral nerve sheath tumor with rhabdomyoblastic differentiation](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/softtissuempnst.html) (malignant triton tumor) ([Mod Pathol 2020;33:404](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/31383960)):
    - Positive [S100](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsS100.html) and [SOX10](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainssox10.html) (patchy)
    - Positive [desmin](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsdesmin.html), [MyoD1](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsmyod1.html), [myogenin](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsmyogenin.html) in rhabdomyoblastic foci
    - Epithelioid variant may be positive for [cytokeratins](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsckgeneral.html); spindle cell variant is negative
    - Negative for [ALK](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsalk.html)
  + [Spindle cell / sclerosing rhabdomyosarcoma](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/softtissuesclerosingrhabdo.html):
    - Negative for [cytokeratins](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsckgeneral.html), [ALK](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsalk.html)
* High grade primary intraosseous malignancies
  + [Osteosarcoma of the jaw](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/mandiblemaxillaosteosarcoma.html):
    - Osteosarcoma in gnathic bones tends to arise in older patient cohort
    - Subtle cytological atypia
    - Production of osteoid matrix
    - Positive for [SATB2](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainssatb2.html)
* Small round blue cell tumors, e.g., [Ewing sarcoma](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/boneewing.html)
* Malignancies with [ALK](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsalk.html) positivity
  + [Inflammatory myofibroblastic tumor](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/softtissueinflammyofibro.html):
    - Positive for [CD30](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/cdmarkerscd30.html) (diffuse cytoplasmic)
    - Negative for [myogenin](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsmyogenin.html)
    - *ALK* rearrangement (not deletion)
  + [Anaplastic large cell lymphoma, ALK positive](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/lymphomanonBanaplastic.html):
    - Characteristic hallmark cells
    - Positive for [CD30](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/cdmarkerscd30.html) (strong in large cells), T cell markers
    - Negative for [cytokeratins](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsckgeneral.html), myogenic markers
* Rarely, can be mistaken for benign spindle cell neoplasms in paucicellular cases lacking pleomorphism, e.g., [ossifying fibroma](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/mandiblemaxillaossifyingfibroma.html) ([Int J Surg Pathol 2024;32:758](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/37545350))

Board review style question #1

Which immunohistochemical stain profile would be most consistent with a rhabdomyosarcoma with *TFCP2* rearrangement?

1. Positive: AE1 / AE3, INI1 (lost); negative: MyoD1, desmin, S100, SOX10
2. Positive: desmin, MyoD1, AE1 / AE3, ALK; negative: S100, SOX10, INI1 (retained)
3. Positive: desmin, MyoD1; negative: AE1 / AE3, ALK, S100, SOX10, INI1 (retained)
4. Positive: S100 / SOX10 (patchy), MyoD1 (patchy), desmin (patchy); negative: AE1 / AE3, INI1 (retained)

Board review style answer #1

**B.** Positive: desmin, MyoD1, AE1 / AE3, ALK; negative: S100, SOX10, INI1 (retained). Positive immunostaining for desmin, MyoD1, AE1 / AE3 and ALK are the most common positive immunostains for rhabdomyosarcoma (RMS) with *TFCP2* rearrangement. Answer A is incorrect because this immunoprofile is most consistent with epithelioid sarcoma. Loss of INI1 and negativity for desmin / MyoD1 are incompatible with RMS with *TFCP2* rearrangement. Answer C is incorrect because this immunoprofile is most consistent with an alternative variant of rhabdomyosarcoma (e.g., spindle cell rhabdomyosarcoma). RMS with *TFCP2* rearrangement shows frequent positivity for AE1 / AE3 and ALK. Answer D is incorrect because patchy immunostaining for desmin and MyoD1 can be found in rhabdomyoblastic foci within malignant peripheral nerve sheath tumor. SOX10 is negative in RMS with *TFCP2* rearrangement.

Board review style question #2

[![](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/imgau/mandiblemaxillarhabdomyosarcomaTFCP2MicroFletcher6.jpg)](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/imgau/mandiblemaxillarhabdomyosarcomaTFCP2MicroFletcher6.jpg)

A high grade malignancy with the appearance shown above is identified within the mandible of a 30 year old man. Which molecular finding is diagnostic of rhabdomyosarcoma with *TFCP2* rearrangement?

1. Fusion with *ERG*
2. Fusion with *FLI1*
3. Fusion with *FOXO1*
4. Fusion with *FUS* or *EWSR1*

Board review style answer #2

**D.** Fusion with *FUS* or *EWSR1* (*FUS::TFCP2*, *EWSR1::TFCP2*) is characteristically found in rhabdomyosarcoma with *TFCP2* rearrangement. Answer A is incorrect because *ERG* fusion (*EWSR1::ERG*) is characteristically found in Ewing sarcoma. Answer B is incorrect because *FLI1* fusion (*EWSR1::FLI1*) is characteristically found in Ewing sarcoma. Answer C is incorrect because *FOXO1* fusion (*PAX3::FOXO1*) is characteristically found in alveolar rhabdomyosarcoma.
