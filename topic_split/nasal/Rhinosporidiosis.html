Definition / general
  • Rhinosporidiosis is a chronic infectious disease triggered by Rhinosporidium seeberi (R. seeberi), a pathogen that is currently classified as a fungus-like parasite within the eukaryotic group mesomycetozoea (Am J Trop Med Hyg 2020;104:708, J Cytol 2012;29:246, BMJ Case Rep 2020;13:e236404)
  • Endemic to the tropical and subtropical regions of Southern India and Sri Lanka, including individuals who have migrated from these areas (Am J Trop Med Hyg 2020;104:708)
Essential features
  • Rhinosporidiosis is a chronic infectious disease of the upper respiratory tract (nasal cavity and nasopharynx) (Int J Surg Case Rep 2022;92:106807)
  • Characterized by development of polypoid masses caused by the sporulating organism Rhinosporidium seeberi (fungus-like parasite) (Am J Trop Med Hyg 2020;104:708, BMJ Case Rep 2020;13:e236404, IDCases 2020;21:e00901)
  • It is a benign lesion for which the gold standard test for diagnosis is histopathology (Am J Trop Med Hyg 2020;104:708, BMJ Case Rep 2020;13:e236404, Middle East Afr J Ophthalmol 2017;24:51)
Terminology
  • Acceptable: rhinosporidiosis
ICD coding
  • ICD-9: 117.0 - rhinosporidiosis
  • ICD-10: B48.1 - rhinosporidiosis
  • ICD-11: 1F56 - rhinosporidiosis
Epidemiology
  • Involves almost all the continents and has diverse geographical distribution
  • Highest incidence is reported in tropical and subtropical regions, with 90% of cases documented in India and Sri Lanka (Am J Trop Med Hyg 2020;104:708)
  • South America is the next endemic region
    • Marañao, a state in northern Brazil (Amazon region), is most affected, followed by Paraguay, where it is also considered endemic (Am J Trop Med Hyg 2020;104:708)
  • In the pediatric population, incidence is estimated to be 1.4% (Am J Trop Med Hyg 2020;104:708)
  • M:F = ~3:1 (Am J Trop Med Hyg 2020;104:708, J Cytol 2012;29:246)
  • M > F with nasal and urethral infections; F > M with conjunctival infections
  • It can affect people of all ages, usually those between 15 and 40 years (Am J Trop Med Hyg 2020;104:708, J Cytol 2012;29:246)
Sites
  • Most common sites involved: nasal cavity (inferior turbinate along the lateral nasal wall), nasopharynx or eyes (Am J Trop Med Hyg 2020;104:708, J Cytol 2012;29:246)
  • Infection may involve the mucosal surface of the larynx, tracheobronchial tree, esophagus, oropharynx, palpebral conjunctiva, ears, maxillary sinuses, penis and urethra (J Cytol 2012;29:246)
Pathophysiology
  • Infective organism was named after R. Seeber, who first described this lesion in Argentina
    • Based on the clinical presentation, he identified a causative agent as a possible etiological factor
    • Aquatic animals (e.g., fish and reptiles) are the most common hosts for R. seeberi
    • Humans, domestic mammals and birds are rare hosts (Int J Surg Case Rep 2022;92:106807)
  • R. seeberi infection is thought to occur by bathing in stagnant ponds (via water, dust or digital trauma) where the endospore is introduced into the nasal cavity and penetrates the mucosal epithelium (i.e., transepithelial infection) (Am J Trop Med Hyg 2020;104:708)
  • Infections are likely a result of the free endospores coming in contact with previously injured epithelium (Am J Trop Med Hyg 2020;104:708, Middle East Afr J Ophthalmol 2017;24:51)
  • Hematologic or lymphatic dissemination, direct inoculation or autoinoculation may cause skin lesions to appear at sites that are distant from the inoculation site (Am J Trop Med Hyg 2020;104:708)
  • Rhinosporidiosis affecting the lower nasal septum was initially attributed to digital trauma
    • For Muslims, the habit of removing the last drops of urine following micturition by cleaning the penile meatus with a stone may predispose them to urethral infection from contamination
  • There is no definitive known host; there are numerous cases in humans and it is known to affect birds and other mammals (Am J Trop Med Hyg 2020;104:708)
  • Concerning the immune response, certain data suggests a connection between the primary antigenic stimuli in the host and the endospores as well as the sporangia layers; this relationship is further influenced by the virulence that stems from genetic variability among different strains (Mycopathologia 1999;148:9)
Etiology
  • Emerging infectious disease that is frequently connected to farming or fishing activities, sand work and paddy cultivation (Am J Trop Med Hyg 2020;104:708, J Cytol 2012;29:246)
  • Thought to be a zoonotic organism, as rhinosporidiosis is seen in cattle, horses and mules (Wenig: Atlas of Head and Neck Pathology, 3rd Edition, 2015)
  • Transmitted to people bathing in contaminated water bodies (e.g., stagnant ponds) (BMJ Case Rep 2020;13:e236404)
  • In rare cases, presents with disseminated skin disease (Indian J Dermatol Venereol Leprol 2007;73:185)
  • There is no transmission from person to person (Wenig: Atlas of Head and Neck Pathology, 3rd Edition, 2015)

Clinical features
  • 4 clinical forms of the disease: nasal, ocular, cutaneous and disseminated (Am J Trop Med Hyg 2020;104:708)
  • Most common symptoms include nasal blockage / obstruction followed by epistaxis, bleed to touch, rhinorrhea, difficulty in breathing and swallowing (Trop Parasitol 2020;10:120)
  • Infection presents as a chronic, indolent condition
  • Causative organism does not seem to be contagious
  • Patients typically exhibit good health otherwise
Diagnosis
  • Clinical features such as a painless, friable, unilateral polypoid mass can provide valuable guidance for diagnosis, although histopathological findings remain the gold standard for definitive diagnosis (Am J Trop Med Hyg 2020;104:708)
Laboratory
  • Literature does not describe any pathognomonic findings for either chemical or hematological analysis (Am J Trop Med Hyg 2020;104:708)
  • R. seeberi has not been definitively cultured but is thought to be a blue-green algae (Ear Nose Throat J 2016;95:101)
Radiology description
  • Radiology on simple computerized tomography (CT) scan of the head usually reveals a nodular mass in the soft tissue of nostril without any local extension (Am J Trop Med Hyg 2020;104:708)
  • On contrast enhanced CT, rhinosporidiosis typically manifests as an enhancing lobulated mass within the inferior nasal cavity or as a polypoidal mass located in the oropharynx or nasopharynx (Indian J Radiol Imaging 2013;23:212)
    • In such cases, the bulbous end of the polypoidal lesion tends to exhibit relative hypoattenuation
    • Adjacent bone erosion frequently accompanies this presentation as a common finding
  • Contrast enhanced CT, however, plays a crucial role in determining both the site and extent of the disease, identifying involvement of bone, nasolacrimal duct and the tracheobronchial tree as well as detecting any associated complications; therefore, it serves as a valuable roadmap before surgery (Indian J Radiol Imaging 2013;23:212)

Prognostic factors
  • Usually a benign condition that exhibits slow growth of polypoid lesions and involvement of the nose, nasopharynx or eyes (Am J Trop Med Hyg 2020;104:708, J Cytol 2012;29:246)
  • High recurrence rate, requiring multiple surgeries and resulting in increased morbidity (BMJ Case Rep 2020;13:e236404)
Case reports
  • 10 year old Latin American boy with unilateral epistaxis of 5 months duration (Am J Trop Med Hyg 2020;104:708)
  • 12 year old boy with a fleshy pedunculated conjunctival mass (Int Med Case Rep J 2022;15:405)
  • 35 year old Nepalese man with recurrent nasopharyngeal rhinosporidiosis (IDCases 2020;21:e00901)
  • 35 year old man with a right nasal mass (Int J Surg Case Rep 2022;92:106807)
  • 38 year old man presented with pharyngeal rhinosporidiosis (BMJ Case Rep 2020;13:e236404)
Treatment
  • Surgical excision; may recur in up to 10% of cases, necessitating additional surgical excision (Wenig: Atlas of Head and Neck Pathology, 3rd Edition, 2015)
  • Antibiotics are not an effective treatment (Wenig: Atlas of Head and Neck Pathology, 3rd Edition, 2015)
  • Rhinosporidiosis has a high recurrence rate and requires multiple surgeries, so long term follow up is necessary (BMJ Case Rep 2020;13:e236404)
  • Anecdotally, dapsone treatment has also been reported (BMJ Case Rep 2020;13:e236404)

Gross description
  • Rhinosporidiosis affecting the nasal passage typically presents as either single or multiple polypoidal lesions, which are characterized by a granular, red appearance (Am J Trop Med Hyg 2020;104:708, J Cytol 2012;29:246, Middle East Afr J Ophthalmol 2017;24:51)
  • These lesions manifest as pedunculated or sessile, friable masses with a red to pink hue, often resembling neoplastic growths, thereby posing a clinical challenge for differentiation
  • In the cut section, the tissue exhibits a pink to purplish appearance with a glistening mucoid surface, while microcysts may be discernible within the submucosal stroma (Wenig: Atlas of Head and Neck Pathology, 3rd Edition, 2015)
  • Presence of numerous yellowish pinhead sized spots, indicative of underlying mature sporangia, is a distinctive gross characteristic; however, it is important to note that this appearance alone is not diagnostic (J Cytol 2012;29:246)
  • Nasopharyngeal lesions are often multilobed and less vascular (J Cytol 2012;29:246)

Microscopic (histologic) description
  • Rhinosporidiosis is characterized by intense acute and chronic inflammation and numerous large, round, thick walled sporangia that may rupture (Am J Trop Med Hyg 2020;104:708, Medicina (B Aires) 2023;83:639)
  • Hyperplasia with squamous metaplasia may be seen in overlying epithelium
  • Thick cyst walls are birefringent (Thompson: Head and Neck Pathology, 3rd Edition, 2018)
  • Largest, most mature cysts are arranged closest to the mucosal surface and are mostly absent in the overlying mucosa (Medicina (B Aires) 2023;83:639)
  • Mucosal and submucosal cysts (sporangia) range in size from 10 to 300 µm in diameter (Wenig: Atlas of Head and Neck Pathology, 3rd Edition, 2015)
  • On H&E, sporangia contain innumerable sporangiospores (i.e., endospores) of which 2 sizes may be seen: smaller spores ~1 - 2 µm in diameter and larger spores ~5 - 10 µm in diameter (Wenig: Atlas of Head and Neck Pathology, 3rd Edition, 2015)
  • Larger spores represent the more mature forms, typically congregating towards the center, while the smaller ones are more peripheral, resulting in a zonated appearance based on spore size (Wenig: Atlas of Head and Neck Pathology, 3rd Edition, 2015)
  • Additionally, smaller cystic structures, devoid of sporangiospores and ranging from 10 to 100 µm, are observed (Wenig: Atlas of Head and Neck Pathology, 3rd Edition, 2015)
    • Termed trophocysts, these structures are believed to arise from autoinfection via mature sporangiospores released from ruptured sporangia
    • Both sporangia and trophocysts feature eosinophilic walls that are several micrometers thick
  • In cases of disseminated rhinosporidiosis, single spores may be present in body fluids (e.g., urine)
  • Rhinosporidiosis can sometimes trigger granulomatous reaction

Cytology description
  • On aspiration, scattered basophilic rhinosporidial endospores are appreciated in a background of amorphous eosinophilic material (J Cytol 2012;29:246)
  • Fine needle aspiration (FNA) smear reveals globular sporangia containing spores and inflammatory cells (Case Rep Pathol 2012;2012:101832)

Positive stains
  • Gomori methanamine silver, digested periodic acid-Schiff, Verhoeff-Van Gieson and mucicarmine (J Cytol 2012;29:246, Trop Parasitol 2020;10:120)
  • May-Grünwald-Giemsa (MGG) (J Cytol 2012;29:246)
Negative stains
  • Does not stain with any immunohistochemistry
Sample pathology report
  • Nasal swelling, excision:
    • Rhinosporidiosis (see comment)
    • Comment: Polypoidal lesion characterized by presence of multiple spherules ranging in size, containing endospores highlighted on PAS and intense inflammatory reaction with associated squamous metaplasia favors diagnosis of rhinosporidiosis.
Differential diagnosis
  • Coccidioidomycosis:
    • Rhinosporidiosis is most commonly mistaken for coccidioidomycosis; however, morphology, clinical history of patient and histological presentation draw a clear distinction between the two
    • Coccidioidomycosis, induced by the dimorphic pathogenic fungi Coccidioides immitis and Coccidioides posadassi, is endemic to the southwestern United States as well as Central and South America (Infect Dis Clin North Am 2021;35:453)
    • R. seeberi differs from Coccidioides immitis in 2 ways (Int J Surg Case Rep 2022;92:106807)
      • Size: Coccidioides immitis is large, thick walled and spherical and contains endospores with a diameter of 20 - 80 µm compared to Rhinosporidium seeberi that are 5 - 10 µm in diameter
      • Mucicarmine does not stain Coccidioides immitis but it does stain Rhinosporidium seeberi
    • According to one case report, Rhinosporidium seeberi has very similar morphology to Coccidioides but its sporangia and endospores are larger than spherules (Am J Trop Med Hyg 2020;104:708)
  • Myospherulosis:
    • Also known as spherulosis or spherulocytosis, this condition entails a rare foreign body reaction in tissues, typically stemming from contact with petrolatum based products
    • Termed myospherulosis due to its location in skeletal muscle and histologic features
    • Diagnosis of myospherulosis is confirmed by histology and can be detected on imaging
    • Myospherulosis has been reported at many sites, including the nose, paranasal sinuses, eye, upper eyelid, oral cavity, lower lip, brain, liver, skin, breast and kidney (Head Neck Pathol 2016;10:221)
    • Cyst is composed of spherules which are damaged erythrocytes (J Clin Pathol 2004;57:155)
    • Cyst wall comprises fibrous tissue and is often accompanied by a lipogranulomatous reaction (J Clin Pathol 2004;57:155)
    • Numerous eosinophilic spherules, containing red blood cells, are observed within histiocytes that line the cyst wall (J Clin Pathol 2004;57:155)
    • Dermal nodules present as cystic cavities enclosed by a fibrous wall lined with histiocytes and multinucleated foreign body giant cells; adjacent adipose tissue exhibits lipogranulomatous inflammation surrounding the cavities
    • Histologically, the entire specimen consists of black spherules of varying size, admixed with amorphous eosinophilic debris (Head Neck Pathol 2016;10:221)
    • These spherules are negative for PAS and GMS
  • Tuberculosis verrucosa cutis (TVC):
    • Common cutaneous manifestation of paucibacillary tuberculosis observed in individuals with a moderate to high degree of immunity to Mycobacterium tuberculosis infection
    • Clinical appearance of TVC is generally typical, presenting with well defined warty plaques on the hands, knees, ankle and buttocks (Indian J Dermatol 2014;59:80)
    • Histopathological examination of the plantar skin with TVC reveals pseudocarcinomatous proliferation of the epidermis and diffuse tuberculous granuloma within the dermis, characterized by histiocytes and giant cells
    • Confirmatory diagnosis typically requires acid fast bacilli (AFB) culture or polymerase chain reaction (PCR) testing (Indian J Dermatol 2014;59:80)
  • Bacillary angiomatosis (BA):
    • Also known as epithelioid angiomatosis
    • Uncommon disease characterized by neovascular proliferation in the skin or internal organs
    • Presents as tumor-like mass from infection with Bartonella henselae or Bartonella quintana (Cureus 2023;15:e36844)
    • Most commonly, the disease affects the skin but the involvement of the respiratory tract, bone, lymph nodes and gastrointestinal tract has also been reported (Cureus 2023;15:e36844)
    • In immunocompromised patients, it presents as nodules or papules
    • Oral lesions are uncommon compared to cutaneous lesions and histopathology is crucial in distinguishing them from similar conditions
    • Histologically, oral lesions exhibit hyperplastic stratified squamous epithelium with intercellular edema, alongside connective tissue that displays lobular proliferation of capillaries lined by prominent endothelial cells (Cureus 2023;15:e36844)
    • Warthin-Starry stain reveals the presence of black rod shaped bacilli arranged in clusters; additionally, modified Brown and Brenn stain shows superficial microbial colonies (Cureus 2023;15:e36844)

Board review style question #1


An 8 year old child presented with nasal obstruction. On examination, a polypoidal lesion was seen in the nasal cavity. Microscopic examination shows multiple spherules and intense inflammatory reaction with associated squamous metaplasia (see image above). These spherules can be highlighted using which of the following special stains?

  1. Digested periodic acid-Schiff
  2. Elastin stain
  3. Masson trichome stain
  4. Rhodamine stain
  5. Von Kossa stain
Board review style answer #1
A. Digested periodic acid-Schiff is used to highlight the spores in magenta color. Answer B is incorrect because elastin stain is used for vessels and it significantly enhances the accurate identification of venous invasion in colorectal carcinomas (Int J Mol Sci 2023;24:15264). Answer C is incorrect because Masson trichome detects the extent of collagen fiber deposition (Bio Protoc 2020;10:e3629). Answer D is incorrect because rhodamine is a copper stain that is positive in hepatocytes, Kupffer cells and histiocytes. Answer E is incorrect because von Kossa staining method demonstrates mineralized tissues in vivo and in vitro (Histochem Cell Biol 2021;156:523).

Board review style question #2
Which of the following is considered the causative organism for rhinosporidiosis?

  1. Bartonella henselae
  2. Coccidioides immitis
  3. Haemophilus influenzae
  4. Mycobacterium tuberculosis
  5. Rhinosporidium seeberi
Board review style answer #2
E. Rhinosporidium seeberi. Rhinosporidiosis is caused by Rhinosporidium seeberi (Am J Trop Med Hyg 2020;104:708). Answer A is incorrect because Bartonella henselae causes bacillary angiomatosis via cat scratch and is stained by Warthin-Starry stain (StatPearls: Bacillary Angiomatosis [Accessed 7 May 2024]). Answer B is incorrect because coccidioidomycosis is the infection caused by breathing in the microscopic fungal spores of Coccidioides immitis from the air (J Am Acad Dermatol 2006;55:929, Infect Dis Clin North Am 1994;8:879). Mucicarmine does not stain Coccidioides immitis but it does stain Rhinosporidium seeberi (Int J Surg Case Rep 2022;92:106807). Answer C is incorrect because Haemophilus influenzae causes bacterial infection of the upper respiratory tract but not rhinosporidiosis (StatPearls: Haemophilus influenzae Infection [Accessed 7 May 2024]). Answer D is incorrect because Mycobacterium tuberculosis is the etiological agent of tuberculosis (Trends Microbiol 2018;26:555).
