Definition / general
  • Infrequent, well differentiated variant of squamous cell carcinoma
  • Commonly presents on the plantar surface of the foot
Essential features
  • Endophytic growth pattern with keratin filled cavities / invaginations
  • No to very minimal cytologic atypia
  • Intraepithelial and stromal neutrophils are abundant, often with keratin microabscesses
  • Microsequestra are frequently associated with bone invasion
Terminology
  • Name is derived from the latin word cunniculus, which means rabbit burrow (Head Neck Pathol 2019;13:652)
ICD coding
  • ICD-10
    • C06.9 - malignant neoplasm of mouth, unspecified
    • C00 - C14 - malignant neoplasms of lip, oral cavity and pharynx
  • ICD-11: 2B6E.0 - squamous cell carcinoma of other or ill defined sites in the lip, oral cavity or pharynx
Epidemiology
  • M > F
  • Fifth and sixth decades (Oral Surg Oral Med Oral Pathol Oral Radiol 2018;126:208)
  • Rare orally; within oral cavity most commonly presents on the hard palate, alveolar ridge or gingiva (J Cancer Res Ther 2022;18:266, Oral Surg Oral Med Oral Pathol Oral Radiol 2018;126:208)
Sites
  • Oral cavity
  • Plantar surface of the feet (Ann Diagn Pathol 2005;9:134)
  • Genitals
Pathophysiology
  • Unknown
  • No association with human papillomavirus (HPV)
Etiology
  • Tobacco use and alcohol consumption are suspected risk factors (Oral Surg Oral Med Oral Pathol Oral Radiol Endod 2002;94:601)
  • Chronic inflammation (Head Neck Pathol 2019;13:652)

Clinical features
  • Clinically, the growth pattern of carcinoma cuniculatum is indolent
  • Lesion often appears to be nodular and bluntly papillary (pebbly)
  • Surface of the lesions may have crypts / sinuses / invaginations that are filled with keratin (Oral Surg Oral Med Oral Pathol Oral Radiol 2018;126:208)
Diagnosis
  • Clinical examination of the oral cavity is the mode of initial identification of these lesions
  • Biopsy should be performed for histopathologic guidance in achieving a final diagnosis

Radiology description
  • Bone resorption
  • Radiographic features may mimic an infectious process such as osteomyelitis (Oral Surg Oral Med Oral Pathol Oral Radiol Endod 2002;94:601)

Prognostic factors
  • Locally aggressive with low metastatic potential
  • Has a favorable prognosis
  • Complete excision with negative margins is recommended management
  • Recurrence rate is low (Oral Surg Oral Med Oral Pathol Oral Radiol 2018;126:208)
Case reports
  • 56 year old man with a slow growing lesion on the anterior maxilla (Oral Surg Oral Med Oral Pathol Oral Radiol Endod 2002;94:601)
  • 74 year old woman with carcinoma cuniculatum of the right maxilla (J Craniofac Surg 2009;20:1270)
  • 82 year old African woman with a palatal mass (Oral Surg Oral Med Oral Pathol Oral Radiol 2018;126:208)
Treatment
  • Excision is general mode of treatment
  • Neck dissection is not always indicated due to low risk of metastatic spread (Oral Surg Oral Med Oral Pathol Oral Radiol Endod 2002;94:601)

Microscopic (histologic) description
  • Architecturally complex proliferation of well differentiated squamous epithelium that branches and forms keratin filled cysts or cavities
  • Cavities resemble a rabbit burrow
  • There is a predominantly endophytic growth pattern (Head Neck Pathol 2022;16:213)
  • Minimal or no cytologic atypia is observed
  • Rete pegs are broad and bulbous (Head Neck Pathol 2022;16:213)

Positive stains
  • Elevated expression of p53
  • Positive and increased expression of Ki67
    • Expression is weaker than in conventional oral squamous cell carcinoma or verrucous carcinoma (Int J Oral Med Sci 2012;10:336)
Negative stains
  • Negative for HPV within oral cavity as confirmed through immunohistochemistry with an antibody targeting a wide spectrum of subtypes, polymerase chain reaction (PCR) and in situ DNA hybridization for low and high risk subtypes (Oral Surg Oral Med Oral Pathol Oral Radiol Endod 2002;94:601, Head Neck Pathol 2012;6:130)

Sample pathology report
  • Mandibular gingiva, left, incisional biopsy:
    • Oral squamous cell carcinoma, well differentiated cuniculatum type
Differential diagnosis
  • Verrucous carcinoma:
    • Has an exophytic and endophytic component
    • Pushing, stromal invasion with bulbous rete processes
    • Sharp stromal / epithelial interface with lymphoplasmacytic inflammatory response
    • Keratinization is church spire-like (J Craniofac Surg 2009;20:1270)
    • Lack of substantial cytologic atypia
  • Papillary squamous cell carcinoma:
    • Exophytic
    • Thin, filiform, finger-like papillary projections
    • Cytologic atypia should be present
  • Squamous cell carcinoma, conventional type:
    • Lacks deep keratin cyst formation with branching
    • Cytologic atypia should be present

Board review style question #1
A 58 year old man presents with a slow growing mass on the mandibular alveolar ridge mucosa. Histopathologic examination reveals an endophytic proliferation of well differentiated squamous epithelium with little cytologic atypia that forms deep keratin filled cavities. Immunohistochemical analysis for HPV is negative. Which of the following is the most likely diagnosis?

  1. Carcinoma cuniculatum
  2. Keratocystic odontogenic tumor
  3. Papillary squamous cell carcinoma
  4. Verrucous carcinoma
Board review style answer #1
A. Carcinoma cuniculatum. Carcinoma cuniculatum displays minimal or no cytologic atypia and has keratin filled cavities. Answer D is incorrect because verrucous carcinoma, although it also has minimal cytologic atypia, does not have keratin filled sinuses and shows a church spire-like pattern of keratinization. Answer C is incorrect because in papillary squamous cell carcinoma, more atypia is present and deep keratin filled cavities are not a common feature. Answer B is incorrect because keratocystic odontogenic tumors exhibit a prominent basal cell layer with palisading cells and most commonly intraosseous lesions.

Board review style question #2
A 73 year old man presents with a pebbly mass of 3 months duration on the hard palate. An incisional biopsy was performed. Upon histopathologic examination, a diagnosis of carcinoma cuniculatum was confirmed. Which of the following is most strongly implicated in the development of oral carcinoma cuniculatum?

  1. Head and neck radiation therapy
  2. Human papillomavirus infection: HPV 16 and HPV 18
  3. Sun exposure
  4. Tobacco and alcohol use
Board review style answer #2
D. Tobacco and alcohol use. Tobacco use and alcohol consumption are most often found to be the risk factors associated with carcinoma cuniculatum. Answer B is incorrect because cases of oral carcinoma cuniculatum are almost always negative for HPV. Answer C is incorrect because excessive sun exposure is associated with premalignant or malignant conditions on the lips / skin and has not been found to be associated with oral carcinoma cuniculatum. Answer A is incorrect because although head and neck radiation is associated with the development of other types of oral cancer, an association with carcinoma cuniculatum has yet to be identified.

Board review style question #3


A 78 year old man presents with a slow growing mass of longstanding duration on the maxillary gingiva. Radiology highlights the underlying bone involvement through a unilocular, radiolucent lesion. Histopathologic examination reveals a diagnosis of carcinoma cuniculatum. Which of the following histologic features was most likely observed?

  1. Marked cellular atypia and high mitotic activity
  2. Multinucleated giant cells in the context of granulomatous inflammation
  3. No to very minimal cytologic atypia with deep, keratin filled invaginations and microsequestra
  4. Sheets of poorly differentiated squamous cells
Board review style answer #3
C. No to very minimal cytologic atypia with deep, keratin filled invaginations and microsequestra. Carcinoma cuniculatum is associated with minimal cellular atypia and deep, branching, keratin filled sinuses. Microsequestra is a result of the bone involvement. Answer A is incorrect because carcinoma cuniculatum does not have high mitotic activity or marked cellular atypia. Answer D is incorrect because carcinoma cuniculatum has well differentiated tumor cells. Answer B is incorrect because granulomatous inflammation is not a feature of carcinoma cuniculatum.
