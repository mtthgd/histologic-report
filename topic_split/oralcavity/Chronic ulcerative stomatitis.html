Definition / general
  • Chronic, rare, immune mediated disease that mainly affects mucous membranes
  • Autoantibodies target the ΔNp63α isoform of p63, a nuclear protein in basal keratinocytes of stratified squamous epithelium
Essential features
  • Clinically and histopathologically overlaps with erosive lichen planus (Head Neck Pathol 2019;13:386)
  • Highly refractory to corticosteroids and standard erosive lichen planus management
  • Direct immunofluorescence or indirect immunofluorescence testing is the only way to distinguish erosive oral lichen planus from chronic ulcerative stomatitis (CUS)
  • Any oral site may be involved, with the buccal mucosa, gingiva and tongue most commonly affected
  • Direct immunofluorescence: speckled, finely granular pattern (SES ANA pattern) of IgG antibody in basal and parabasal cell nuclei of keratinocytes (Oral Surg Oral Med Oral Pathol Oral Radiol Endod 2003;96:718)
  • Responds well to hydroxychloroquine (Oral Surg Oral Med Oral Pathol Oral Radiol Endod 2007;104:194)
Terminology
None
ICD coding
  • ICD-10: K12.1 - other forms of stomatitis
Epidemiology
  • Predominantly affects White females (> 90% of cases) (Oral Maxillofac Surg Clin North Am 2023;35:189)
  • Common in the fifth and sixth decades of life
Sites
  • Primarily affects the oral cavity, with the buccal mucosa (43.75%), gingiva (38.75%) and tongue (22.5%) most commonly involved
  • Other affected sites include the palate, lingual mucosa, lower lip and cheek (Oral Dis 2019;25:1465)
  • Extraoral involvement occurs in 22.5% of cases
Pathophysiology / etiology
  • Chronic ulcerative stomatitis autoantibodies target ΔNp63α, a nuclear protein normally found in basal keratinocytes of stratified squamous epithelium (Oral Dis 2008;14:383)
    • Autoantibodies bind to ΔNp63α, interfering with its DNA binding function and disrupting the transcription of genes essential for epithelial integrity
    • Autoantibody interference affects α6β4 integrin expression, a key component of hemidesmosomal attachment of basal cells to laminin 332 in the basement membrane, leading to loss of epithelial adhesion and migration
  • p63 gene (located on chromosome 3q27) belongs to the TP53 family, which includes p53 and p73, sharing functional domains involved in transcription regulation
  • ΔNp63α isoform is predominantly expressed in squamous epithelia, playing a crucial role in epithelial development and differentiation
  • It regulates epithelial differentiation, adhesion and proliferation, ensuring the integrity and survival of stratified squamous epithelial cells
  • Some theories suggest that chronic inflammation exposes intranuclear antigens, triggering a B cell response and autoantibody production, similar to mechanisms in oral lichen planus and other autoimmune diseases
  • Experimental and clinical data support chronic ulcerative stomatitis as an organ specific autoimmune disease, with hydroxychloroquine demonstrating efficacy by modulating B cell responses

Clinical features
  • Array of clinical presentations similar to oral lichen planus and autoimmune vesiculobullous diseases
  • Progressive, painful, nonhealing, ulcerative erosive lesions or vesicles with or without desquamative gingivitis (Oral Maxillofac Surg Clin North Am 2023;35:189)
  • Hypertrophic areas, especially on the dorsal tongue, mimicking hypertrophic lichen planus
  • Ulcers are surrounded by erythematous zones and streaky keratosis resembling oral lichen planus but lacking classic Wickham striae (Head Neck Pathol 2019;13:386)
  • Ulcers heal without scarring, with alternating periods of severity and migration across the oral mucosa
  • Patients may experience systemic symptoms, including fatigue, weight loss, dry mouth, mood changes and sleep disturbances
Diagnosis
  • Diagnosis made on routine H&E histological examination and biopsy for direct immunofluorescence (Oral Maxillofac Surg Clin North Am 2023;35:189)
  • Direct immunofluorescence is gold standard for diagnosis
  • Serum studies using indirect immunofluorescence on guinea pig esophagus substrates revealed high titers of circulating IgG antibodies bound to epithelial nuclei in the basal layer in a stratified epithelium specific antinuclear antibody (SES ANA) pattern
Laboratory
  • Enzyme linked immunosorbent assay (ELISA): detect specific IgG antibodies against ΔNp63α protein (80% sensitivity and 75% specificity) (Oral Dis 2010;16:151)

Prognostic factors
  • Overall prognosis is good
  • Lesions heal and regress uneventfully with appropriate treatment
  • Complications, mainly from medication adverse effects, may include retinopathy, neuromuscular issues and hematologic disorders (Oral Surg Oral Med Oral Pathol Oral Radiol Endod 2003;96:718, J Oral Pathol Med 2017;46:859)
Case reports
  • 40 - 81 year old White women presented with painful ulcers, mucosal irritation and oral dryness, primarily affecting the gingiva, tongue and buccal mucosa (Oral Surg Oral Med Oral Pathol Oral Radiol Endod 2007;104:194)
  • 54 year old White woman presented with a 2 - 3 year history of stomatitis and dry mouth (J Periodontol 2000;71:104)
Treatment
  • Hydroxychloroquine limits disease progression, keeping patients generally asymptomatic
    • Dosage of 200 mg/day helps improve the condition in the majority of cases (Oral Surg Oral Med Oral Pathol Oral Radiol Endod 2003;96:718)
    • Effective initially but not suitable for long term use due to gastrointestinal and ocular side effects

Microscopic (histologic) description
  • Ulcerated specimens often show atrophic epithelium with few sawtoothed rete ridges and eosinophilic coagulum along the basement membrane zone (Head Neck Pathol 2019;13:386)
  • Numerous eosinophilic Civatte bodies (necrotic keratinocytes) are present within the epithelium
  • Significant interface stomatitis with leukocytic exocytosis and partial basal cell layer destruction may be observed

Sample pathology report
  • Oral mucosa, incisional biopsy:
    • Routine histopathology and direct immunofluorescent antibody studies compatible with chronic ulcerative stomatitis (see comment)
    • Comment: On H&E, there is atrophy of the stratified squamous epithelium and a lichenoid pattern of infiltrate at the epithelium / connective tissue interface. Also noted is a focal subepithelial separation. Direct immunofluorescence shows a speckled and granular pattern at the basal and parabasal cell layers, with a nuclear distribution for IgG and negativity for C3, IgM, IgA and fibrinogen.
Differential diagnosis
  • Erosive lichen planus:
    • T cell mediated autoimmune disease
    • Epithelial atrophy with Civatte bodies
    • Subepithelial band of lymphocytes
    • Direct immunofluorescence shows shaggy fibrinogen deposition at the basement membrane zone (BMZ) or C3 at BMZ and IgM positive colloid bodies (Mod Pathol 2017;30:S54)
    • Clinically, lesions often respond to corticosteroid therapy (Oral Maxillofac Surg Clin North Am 2023;35:189)
  • Mucous membrane pemphigoid:
    • Subepithelial blistering disease, commonly presenting in the oral cavity (Oral Maxillofac Surg Clin North Am 2023;35:203)
    • Affects middle aged to older adults
    • Slight female predominance
    • Characterized by ulcers, with or without blood blister formation
    • Positive Nikolsky sign
    • Direct immunofluorescence reveals IgG, C3 or IgA positivity at the BMZ (Oral Maxillofac Surg Clin North Am 2023;35:203)
    • Clinically, lesions often respond to corticosteroid therapy
  • Lupus erythematosus:
    • Oral involvement occurs in ~25% of cases
    • Most cases have cutaneous involvement or are associated with systemic inflammatory disease
    • Histopathology shows a lichenoid pattern, often with perivascular inflammation (Mod Pathol 2017;30:S54)
    • Direct immunofluorescence reveals a linear band or continuous granular deposition of IgG, IgM, IgA or C3 at the basement membrane zone
  • Oral lichenoid drug reaction:
    • Oral mucosal immunoinflammatory disorder causing erythematous plaques, vesicles, ulcers or hyperkeratosis in response to medications
    • Onset varies after medication initiation
    • Commonly associated with NSAIDs, anticonvulsants, antihypertensives, antimalarials and antiretrovirals
    • Histologically, a lichenoid infiltrate may extend into the deep lamina propria and show perivascular inflammation
    • Infiltrate may be diffuse or mixed with plasma cells and occasionally eosinophils
    • Direct immunofluorescence may be negative or show shaggy fibrinogen deposition at the BMZ or C3 at BMZ and IgM positive colloid bodies (Mod Pathol 2017;30:S54)
    • Withdrawal of the causative agent may result in improvement within 2 - 8 weeks (Oral Maxillofac Surg Clin North Am 2023;35:189)
  • Oral lichenoid contact hypersensitivity reaction:
    • Oral mucosal immunoinflammatory disorder causing erythematous plaques, vesicles, ulcers or hyperkeratosis in response to a local agent
    • May present with pain, burning or itchiness
    • Commonly triggered by mouthwashes, foods, spices (especially cinnamon), gloves, rubber dams and dental materials
    • Histologically, lichenoid interface mucositis with mixed inflammation extending into the deep lamina propria and perivascular inflammation
    • Direct immunofluorescence may be negative or show features similar to oral lichen planus (Mod Pathol 2017;30:S54)
    • Resolution typically occurs after eliminating the offending agent
  • Oral graft versus host disease:
    • Clinical and histopathological presentations may resemble those of oral lichenoid lesions
    • Occurs as a result of allogeneic hematopoietic cell transplantation

Board review style question #1


Which direct immunofluorescence finding is most supportive of chronic ulcerative stomatitis?

  1. IgG in the basal and parabasal epithelial nuclei
  2. Intracellular IgG deposition
  3. Linear IgG deposition at the basement membrane zone
  4. Shaggy, linear deposition of fibrinogen at the basement membrane zone
Board review style answer #1
A. IgG in the basal and parabasal epithelial nuclei. Speckled, finely granular IgG staining in the basal and parabasal keratinocyte nuclei in a stratified epithelium, exhibiting a specific antinuclear antibody (SES ANA) pattern, is diagnostic for chronic ulcerative stomatitis. Answer B is incorrect because chronic ulcerative stomatitis (CUS) is characterized by IgG targeting nuclear proteins rather than intracellular components in general. Answer C is incorrect because this pattern is more characteristic of pemphigoid diseases, such as mucous membrane pemphigoid, rather than chronic ulcerative stomatitis. Answer D is incorrect because this finding is not consistent in every chronic ulcerative stomatitis case and is not diagnostic. Fibrinogen positivity alone is more typical of lichen planus.

Board review style question #2
Chronic ulcerative stomatitis is most commonly associated with the presence of autoantibodies directed against which of the following antigens?

  1. BP180 (collagen XVII)
  2. Desmoglein 1 and 3
  3. ΔNp63α
  4. p53
Board review style answer #2
C. ΔNp63α. Chronic ulcerative stomatitis autoantibodies target ΔNp63α, a nuclear protein normally found in basal keratinocytes of stratified squamous epithelium, leading to disruption of epithelial integrity. Answer B is incorrect because these are the target antigens in pemphigus vulgaris, not chronic ulcerative stomatitis (CUS). Answer A is incorrect because BP180 (collagen XVII) is a target antigen in bullous pemphigoid and mucous membrane pemphigoid, not chronic ulcerative stomatitis. Answer D is incorrect because p53 is a tumor suppressor protein involved in carcinogenesis, not an autoantibody target in chronic ulcerative stomatitis.
