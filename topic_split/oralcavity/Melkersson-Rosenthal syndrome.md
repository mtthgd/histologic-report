Definition / general

* Rare neuromucocutaneous syndrome characterized by recurrent orofacial swelling, relapsing facial paralysis and fissured tongue (lingua plicata)
* Cheilitis granulomatosa is its most common monosymptomatic form

Essential features

* Classic clinical triad of recurrent orofacial swelling, recurrent facial nerve paralysis and fissured tongue
* Histopathological examination of the swollen tissue shows granulomatous inflammation, lymphomononuclear inflammation and edema

Terminology

* Melkersson-Rosenthal syndrome (MRS): named after neurologists Ernst Melkersson and Curt Rosenthal, who described the classic symptoms of MRS
* Melkersson syndrome
* Cheilitis granulomatosa, also known as Miescher granulomatous cheilitis
* Orofacial granulomatous disease: not recommended; broad entity comprising noninfectious granulomatous involvement of lips, oral cavity and face
  + Causes may include sarcoidosis, Crohn's disease, cheilitis granulomatosa, etc.

ICD coding

* ICD-10: [G51.2](https://www.icd10data.com/ICD10CM/Codes/G00-G99/G50-G59/G51-/G51.2) - Melkersson syndrome
* ICD-11: [8B88.Y](https://icd.who.int/browse/2024-01/mms/en#1640393055%2Fother) - other specified disorders of facial nerve

Epidemiology

* Rare condition; estimated incidence of 0.08% ([Zhejiang Da Xue Xue Bao Yi Xue Ban 2021;50:148](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/34137235))
* Occurs frequently in second to third decades of life (average age: 25 years); incidence decreases with age
* Age range: 2 - 81 years
* M:F = 1:1.1 - 1.4 ([J Clin Neurosci 2013;20:993](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/23664134), [Curr Pediatr Rev 2018;14:196](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/29766816))
* More common in young adults and women

Sites

* Characterized by triad of nonpitting orofacial edema, fissured tongue and recurrent lower motor neuron facial paralysis ([Curr Pediatr Rev 2018;14:196](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/29766816))
* Orofacial edema: lips are most commonly affected; gingiva, buccal mucosa, cheeks, tongue and eyelids can also be affected
* Tongue swollen and fissured
* Recurrent lower motor neuron facial paralysis ([Curr Pediatr Rev 2018;14:196](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/29766816))

Pathophysiology

* Orofacial swelling is caused by fluid accumulation due to vasomotor and other autonomous disturbances
* Tissue edema, due to vasomotor disturbances, damages the nerve branches and can compress the facial nerve, leading to facial paralysis ([J Dermatol 1997;24:281](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/9198316))
* Later, the microgranulomas appear
* Repeated episodes may be followed by persistent orofacial swelling or facial paralysis

Etiology

* Not known completely
* Genetic factors, chronic infectious diseases, microbial factors (e.g., *Mycobacterium tuberculosis*, *Borrelia burgdorferi*, herpes simplex), allergic reactions and immune factors have been implicated ([Zhejiang Da Xue Xue Bao Yi Xue Ban 2021;50:148](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/34137235), [Curr Pediatr Rev 2018;14:196](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/29766816))
* *FATP1* (fatty acid transport protein 1) mutation has been reported ([J Eur Acad Dermatol Venereol 2017;31:e230](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/27862329))
* Genetic factors: certain familial cases have been reported in an autosomal dominant pattern ([Clin Genet 1979;15:189](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/761419))
* C1-INH complement deficiency may contribute to the orofacial swelling ([Dermatology 2008;217:114](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/18477847))

Clinical features

* Recurrent facial swelling is the most common feature and may be the first and only presentation (80 - 100% of cases) ([Allergy Asthma Clin Immunol 2021;17:8](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/33422123))
  + Often asymmetric or unilateral, sudden onset, nontender, nonpruritic, lasts for hours to weeks and can sometimes persist; resistant to antihistamines
* Recurrent facial nerve paralysis (50 - 60% of cases) ([Pan Afr Med J 2023;44:72](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/37159626))
  + Can occur months or years before or after the onset of facial edema
  + Can be unilateral or bilateral, partial or complete, resolves in initial episodes but later may become permanent
* Fissured tongue (30 - 80% of cases) ([Am J Otolaryngol 2009;30:33](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/19027510))
  + Also known as scrotal tongue, lingua plicata, furrowed tongue or cerebriform tongue
  + Swollen tongue with irregular clefts and protuberance; fissured tongue is the presence of grooves on the tongue about 2 mm deep and 15 mm long ([Odontol Revy Suppl 1976;36:1](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/186740))
* 8 - 25% of cases have all 3 components ([Braz J Otorhinolaryngol 2007;73:132](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/17505613))
* Other symptoms may include migraines, tinnitus, sudden deafness, dry mouth, altered taste, facial neuralgia, dysphagia, diverticulitis and uveitis

Diagnosis

* Classic clinical triad of recurrent orofacial swelling, recurrent facial paralysis and fissured tongue is seen in 8 - 25% of cases ([Braz J Otorhinolaryngol 2007;73:132](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/17505613))
* Classified as complete or incomplete (oligosymptomatic) by Hornstein ([J Dermatol 1997;24:281](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/9198316))
  + Complete: classic triad + some minor signs
  + Incomplete / oligosymptomatic
    - Recurrent orofacial swelling (supportive histology) + ≥ 2 minor signs (plicated tongue may or may not present) or
    - Recent or current facial palsy (≥ 1 episode) + recurrent orofacial swelling (insignificant histology) + ≥ 2 minor signs + plicated tongue
* Minor signs include orofacial hyperesthesia / dysesthesia, hemicrania-like frontal headache, cluster headache, unilateral hyperacusis, statoacoustic dysfunctions, recurrent pharyngeal neuralgia, recurrent dysphagia, recurrent dry eye, recurrent dry mouth, etc. ([J Dermatol 1997;24:281](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/9198316))
* Granulomatous cheilitis is the most common monosymptomatic form of Melkersson-Rosenthal syndrome ([Braz J Otorhinolaryngol 2007;73:132](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/17505613))
* If the criteria are not met, then the patient should follow up for any presentations of components of the syndrome later in life

Laboratory

* Except for histological examination, there are no specific laboratory findings
* Can help exclude other differential diagnoses

Radiology description

* No significant abnormality except for confirmation of edema ([Arch Ophthalmol 2000;118:227](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/10676788))
* Can help exclude other differentials

Prognostic factors

* Prognostic factors are not currently known
* Recurrences occur in most patients ([Int J Environ Res Public Health 2019;16:1289](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/30974872))
* Spontaneous recovery can occur ([Int J Environ Res Public Health 2019;16:1289](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/30974872))
* May lead to persistent symptoms in some cases ([Allergol Immunopathol (Madr) 1998;26:291](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/9934409))

Case reports

* 28 year old woman with Melkersson-Rosenthal syndrome ([J Clin Rheumatol 2021;27:e190](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/32015259))
* 29 year old woman with gingival granulomatous manifestation in Melkersson-Rosenthal syndrome ([Pan Afr Med J 2023;44:72](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/37159626))
* 51 year old woman with COVID-19 presented with Melkersson-Rosenthal syndrome ([Am J Emerg Med 2021;41:262.e5](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/32829989))

Treatment

* Treatment is difficult; no standardized therapy scheme exists
* Local intralesional / systemic corticosteroids, nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory agents and antibiotics are commonly used ([Curr Pediatr Rev 2018;14:196](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/29766816))
* Thalidomide was successful in one report ([Ann Allergy Asthma Immunol 2002;88:421](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/11995671))
* Surgery for facial nerve decompression, cosmetic surgery for persistent lip edema (cheiloplasty)

Microscopic (histologic) description

* Typical histology is seen in the biopsies from the swollen lips and other edematous tissue during acute episode
* Tuberculoid (lymphoepithelioid) granulomas in dermis or muscularis propria - epithelioid cell granulomas, surrounded by lymphocytes and other mononuclear cells, edematous connective tissue
  + Granulomas are few in number and small; larger granulomas are rare (seen in 5% of cases) ([J Dermatol 1997;24:281](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/9198316))
  + Adjacent to blood vessels and lymphatics; may be intralymphatic ([Arch Ophthalmol 2000;118:227](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/10676788))
* Less commonly, perivascular or paravascular lymphonodular plasmacytic inflammation with central lymphocytic nodules surrounded by plasma cells and histiocytes in an edematous connective tissue ([J Dermatol 1997;24:281](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/9198316))
* Nonspecific inflammation, fibrosis

Positive stains

* Monoclonal antibodies for the histiocytic markers [lysozyme](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainslysozyme.html) and [CD68 (KP1, PGM1)](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/cdmarkerscd68.html) in the epithelioid cells ([Arch Ophthalmol 2000;118:227](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/10676788), [Am J Dermatopathol 2011;33:594](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/21317610))
* Endothelial cells lining the lumens of the lymphatic vessels stain for [factor VIII](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsfactorviii.html) and [D2-40](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsD240.html) ([Arch Ophthalmol 2000;118:227](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/10676788))

Negative stains

* [PAS](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainspas.html), [Ziehl-Neelson](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsacidfast.html), [GMS](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsgms.html), [Warthin-Starry](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainswarthin.html) and [Fite-Faraco](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsacidfast.html), excluding fungi and mycobacteria
* [Brown-Hopps](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsgramstain.html) for bacteria ([Arch Ophthalmol 2000;118:227](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/10676788))

Polarization microscopy description

* No polarizable material seen ([Head Neck Pathol 2019;13:449](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/30627963))

Molecular / cytogenetics description

* Mutation affecting 9p11 gene has been reported ([Clin Genet 1994;45:323](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/7923865))
* *FATP1* (fatty acid transport protein 1) mutation has been reported ([J Eur Acad Dermatol Venereol 2017;31:e230](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/27862329))

Sample pathology report

* Lip, skin biopsy:
  + Granulomatous cheilitis, in favor of Melkersson-Rosenthal syndrome in the appropriate clinical setting (see comment)
  + Comment: Skin biopsy shows epidermis with basket weave orthokeratosis and mild dermoepidermal interface changes. Dermis shows multiple epithelioid cell granulomas with moderate lymphomononuclear inflammatory infiltrate and edema. A few of the granulomas are seen in the perivascular location. No necrosis / fungal elements are seen. Ziehl-Neelson, Fite-Faraco, PAS and GMS stains are negative.

Differential diagnosis

* [Crohn's disease](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/coloncrohns.html):
  + Labial swelling is rare; granulomatous inflammation in the intestine is a more prominent feature
* **Angioneurotic edema**:
  + Labial swelling occurs rapidly and is transient
  + Swelling of oral mucosa, tongue, larynx, joints
  + Other features of Melkersson-Rosenthal syndrome are lacking
* [Sarcoidosis](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/skinnontumorsarcoidosis.html):
  + Solid nodules on the gingiva, oral mucosa can occur
  + Epithelioid cell granulomas of other organs
  + Other features of sarcoidosis are present (raised serum angiotensin converting enzyme [ACE] levels)
  + Heerfordt syndrome, a subset of sarcoidosis, has preauricular edema, facial palsy and injury to other cranial nerves
* [Tuberculosis](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/lungnontumorTB.html):
  + Caseating epithelioid cell granulomas are classic, while in Melkersson-Rosenthal syndrome, the granulomas are nonnecrotizing and related edema is present
  + [Ziehl-Neelson](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsacidfast.html) stain is positive; other ancillary tests for tuberculosis should be done to rule out tuberculosis
* [Insect bite](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/skinnontumorarthropod.html):
  + Clinically, Melkersson-Rosenthal syndrome and insect bite may mimic each other; histology differentiates the 2 entities
  + Granulomas absent in insect bite

Board review style question #1

[![BRQ image](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/imgau/oralcavityMRsyndromekala01.jpg)](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/imgau/oralcavityMRsyndromekala01.jpg)

A 29 year old woman presented with recurrent orofacial swelling from the past few months. On examination, she had a fissured tongue and right sided facial weakness. The rest of the systemic examination was normal. A biopsy of the swollen lip was performed and a representative microphotograph is shown above. The cell counts were within normal limits and serum angiotensin converting enzyme (ACE) level was 7 U/L. Which of the following is correct about the disease condition?

1. Cytoplasmic antineutrophil cytoplasmic antibodies (cANCA) levels will be raised
2. Facial paralysis is due to vasomotor disturbances
3. Granulomas lead to the orofacial swelling
4. Nodular infiltrates will be seen in magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) of the head and neck region

Board review style answer #1

**B**. Facial nerve paralysis is due to vasomotor disturbances. The diagnosis is Melkersson-Rosenthal syndrome with facial nerve paralysis and orofacial swelling occurring because of the edema caused by vasomotor disturbances. Answer C is incorrect because the granulomas occur after orofacial swelling. Answer A is incorrect because cANCA levels are raised in granulomatosis with polyangiitis; it is unrelated to Melkersson-Rosenthal syndrome. Answer D is incorrect because radiology is near normal in Melkersson-Rosenthal syndrome. The presence of nodular infiltrates may indicate another diagnosis.
