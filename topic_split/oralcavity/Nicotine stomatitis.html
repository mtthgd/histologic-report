Definition / general
  • Common inflammatory reaction of palatal minor salivary gland orifices that results from smoking tobacco, electronic cigarettes, marijuana or frequent consumption of hot beverages
    • This inflammatory reaction is mounted in response to both heat and chemicals released from burning substances
Essential features
  • Clinically appears as papules with central red umbilications; the papules correspond to obstructed palatal minor salivary glands and the red umbilications correspond to their inflamed duct orifices (Oral Surg Oral Med Oral Pathol 1965;20:306)
    • Common finding in patients who smoke or frequently drink hot beverages
  • Keratosis and acanthosis of palatal epithelium with underlying patchy, chronic inflammation involving minor salivary glands and squamous metaplasia of salivary duct epithelium
  • Inflammatory changes of minor salivary gland ductal orifices resolve within 2 - 4 weeks of cessation of smoking or consumption of hot drinks (Br J Cancer 1971;25:403)
Terminology
  • Nicotine stomatitis was first documented in 1926 in a comprehensive study by Fordyce and MacKee, who both named the lesion mucous adenoma of the roof of the mouth (Arch Dermatol 1926;13:601)
  • In 1936, McCarthy referred to the lesions as smoker’s patches and vehemently stated that the use of tobacco was the most important etiologic factor of these lesions (Arch Derm Syphilol 1936;34:612)
  • Stomatitis nicotina was a term introduced by Thoma in 1937 (Arch Dermatol 1961;83:945)
    • Thoma postulated the condition was a periodic, desquamating leukoplakia arising from small cyst formation within the salivary tissue of the palate with inflamed duct orifices (Arch Dermatol 1961;83:945)
  • Smoker’s keratosis was a term coined in the dermatologic literature by Cooke in 1956 (Br J Dermatol 1956;68:151)
  • The designation of nicotine stomatitis was first introduced in the literature by Saunders in 1958, who stated this nomenclature was used by dental pathologists and oral surgeons; additionally, Saunders mentioned that papular leukoplakia was a frequently used synonym for this entity (Ann Otol Rhinol Laryngol 1958;67:618)
ICD coding
  • ICD-10: K13.24 - leukokeratosis nicotina palati
  • ICD-11: DA01.0Y - other specified disturbances of oral epithelium
Epidemiology
  • Predominantly occurs in male tobacco smokers in the third through fifth decades of life (Br J Cancer 1971;25:403)
Sites
  • Involves the minor salivary glands of the hard palate posterior to the rugae and the adjoining soft palate
Pathophysiology
  • Nicotine stomatitis is an inflammatory reaction to heat and chemical irritants from tobacco products and hot beverages
    • These irritants induce inflammation of the palatal mucosa and minor salivary gland ductal orifices, squamous metaplasia of salivary ducts and keratosis as well as acanthosis of the palatal mucosa (Tob Induc Dis 2024;22:10.18332/tid/189600)
Etiology
  • Smoking tobacco products, including conventional and reverse smoking of cigarettes and cigars (Br J Cancer 1971;25:403)
    • High temperatures of hot beverages have been observed to have a concerted effect (Tob Induc Dis 2024;22:10.18332/tid/189600)
      • In one case report, a woman who did not smoke but frequently drank hot beverages had palatal lesions that resembled nicotinic stomatitis, which further suggests heat as an etiologic factor (Oral Surg Oral Med Oral Pathol 1990;70:597)
  • This inflammatory change is also noted in those who use electronic cigarettes and smoke marijuana (Acta Odontol Scand 2018;76:226, Cureus 2023;15:e48868)
Clinical features
  • Lesions are asymptomatic papules with central red umbilications of the hard palate posterior to the palatal rugae and may extend onto the soft palate
  • Dried mud appearance of the palatal mucosa may also be seen
Diagnosis
  • Papules with central red umbilications of the hard and soft palate may be detected as an incidental finding
Prognostic factors
  • Prognosis is excellent; lesions resolve within 2 - 4 weeks of smoking cessation or cessation of routine hot beverage consumption (Br J Cancer 1971;25:403)
Case reports
  • 27 year old man with a history of smoking presented with a chief complaint of reddish spots on the back of his throat (Cureus 2023;15:e48868)
  • 28 year old man presented with chief complaint of white spots on his palate (J Dentomaxillofac Sci 2018;3:58)
  • 50 year old man with a history of smoking tobacco in the form of beedi (form of tobacco smoking without filter, a common practice in parts of Asia) (J Dent Oral Disord 2017;3:1055)
Treatment
  • No treatment is required; lesions resolve within 2 - 4 weeks of smoking cessation or cessation of routine hot beverage consumption (Br J Cancer 1971;25:403)

Microscopic (histologic) description
  • Keratosis and acanthosis of surface epithelium (Br J Cancer 1971;25:403)
  • Mild, patchy, chronic inflammation of the connective tissue stroma and minor seromucous glands
    • Inflammatory excretion may be seen within the lumina of striated salivary ducts (Br J Cancer 1971;25:403)
  • Squamous metaplasia of ductal epithelium near the orifice may be seen in instances of papular elevation (Oral Surg Oral Med Oral Pathol 1965;20:306)

Positive stains
  • Immunohistochemistry is often unneeded for diagnosis
    • CAM 5.2 and SOX10 may be used to highlight metaplastic salivary ducts (Head Neck Pathol 2020;14:593, Mod Pathol 2013;26:1041)
Sample pathology report
  • Posterior hard palatal mucosa, left incisional biopsy:
    • Hyperkeratosis, squamous ductal metaplasia and chronic sialadenitis (see comment)
    • Comment: The histologic findings are consistent with nicotine stomatitis.
Differential diagnosis
  • Subacute necrotizing sialadenitis:
    • Extensive acinar necrosis
    • Foci of mixed inflammation, including neutrophils, eosinophils and mononuclear inflammatory cells
    • No ductal squamous metaplasia (Head Neck Pathol 2016;10:425)
  • Necrotizing sialometaplasia:
    • Pseudoepitheliomatous hyperplasia of the surface epithelium may be seen
    • Ulceration of the surface epithelium with underlying acute inflammation is often observed
    • Lobular architecture of minor salivary glands is usually preserved and squamous ductal metaplasia is often noted in mature lesions
  • Dysplasia:
    • Basal hyperplasia and bulbous, tear dropped shaped rete ridges
    • Nuclear and cellular pleomorphism
    • Increased mitotic activity, increased N:C ratio, dyskeratosis and glassy appearing cytoplasm
    • Dysplastic changes of overlying surface epithelium may track down nearby salivary ducts
  • Erythematous candidiasis:
    • Spongiosis and mild acanthosis of surface epithelium
    • Acute and chronic inflammation with transmigratory neutrophils forming microabscesses in the stratum granulosum
    • Pseudohyphae are seen oriented perpendicular to the surface epithelium and are embedded in the stratum corneum
  • Herpetic stomatitis:
    • Intraepidermal blisters with discohesive epithelium that burst, ulcerate and often coalesce
      • Recurrent episodes are typically unilateral along affected dermatome
    • Involved keratinocytes exhibit cytopathic effect, including margination of chromatin, nuclear inclusions and multinucleation
      • Nuclear inclusions are small, eosinophilic deposits with clear halation

Board review style question #1


A 40 year old man with a social history significant for cigarette smoking presents with umbilicated, red papules of the palate. What is the most likely diagnosis based on the photomicrograph?

  1. Dysplasia
  2. Erythematous candidiasis
  3. Nicotine stomatitis
  4. Subacute necrotizing sialadenitis
Board review style answer #1
C. Nicotine stomatitis. The photomicrograph reveals keratosis and acanthosis of the palatal epithelium with underlying patchy, chronic inflammation of the connective tissue stroma and minor mucous gland, in addition to squamous metaplasia of salivary ducts. Answer A is incorrect because epithelial exhibits maturational changes of the surface epithelium including cellular and nuclear pleomorphism, nuclear hyperchromasia, increased mitotic activity and architectural changes, particularly tear drop shaped rete ridges. Answer B is incorrect because erythematous candidiasis exhibits spongiosis of the surface epithelium and transmigratory neutrophils across the surface epithelium forming microabscesses in response to pseudohyphae perpendicularly oriented relative to the surface epithelium. Answer D is incorrect because subacute necrotizing sialadenitis does not exhibit squamous metaplasia of salivary ducts and exhibits extensive necrosis of salivary acini.
