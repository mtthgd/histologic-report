Definition / general

* Ovarian torsion is a medical emergency involving the twisting of the ovary or fallopian tube on its ligaments
* This leads to obstructed blood flow to the ovary, potentially resulting in necrosis and infertility if left untreated

Essential features

* Risk factor: 0varian mass, mostly benign neoplasms or cysts (Clin Exp Obstet Gynecol. 2004;31(1):34-38)
* Characterized microscopically by hemorrhagic infarction with associated neoplasms / cysts
* Thorough sampling is needed to exclude malignancy

Terminology

* Synonyms: adnexal torsion

ICD coding

* ICD-10: [N83.53](https://www.icd10data.com/ICD10CM/Codes/N00-N99/N80-N98/N83-/N83.53) - torsion of ovary, ovarian pedicle and fallopian tube
* ICD-11: [GA18.5](https://icd.who.int/browse/2024-01/mms/en#732377941) - torsion of ovary, ovarian pedicle or fallopian tube

Epidemiology

* Occurs in women of all ages, most commonly during reproductive years
* Can also affect children, neonates and even fetuses in utero, often associated with ovarian cysts (Obstet Gynecol Surv. 1991;46(7):407-414)

Sites

* Ovary and fallopian tubes
* Right side is more prone to torsion than left, likely due to the longer right utero-ovarian ligament (J Ultrasound Med. 2001;20(10):1083-1089)

Pathophysiology

* Ovary is suspended by the utero-ovarian ligament and the infundibulopelvic (suspensory) ligament, which contains the ovarian vessels
* Ovarian torsion occurs when the ovary twists around these ligaments, obstructing blood flow
  + Lymphatic and venous outflow is initially impeded, causing edema, enlargement and further vascular compression
  + Arterial inflow is eventually compromised, leading to ischemia, necrosis and hemorrhage

Etiology

* Risk factor: ovarian mass (found in over 85% of torsion cases), particularly those > 5 cm in diameter
  + Masses predispose the ovary to rotate on its ligament and become fixed in a twisted position
  + Most are benign neoplasms or cysts
  + Pregnancy and ovulation induction increase the risk by enlarging ovarian follicles
* Torsion can still occur in a normal ovary
* References: Clin Exp Obstet Gynecol. 2004;31(1):34-38, Gynecol. 2007;109(2 Pt 1):355-359, Ann Emerg Med. 2001;38(2):156-159

Clinical features

* Most patients present with a sudden onset of abdominal or pelvic pain (Ann Emerg Med. 2001;38(2):156-159)
* Other symptoms include nausea, vomiting and fever

Diagnosis

* Diagnosis is based on clinical history, imaging and surgical evaluation
* Ultrasound with doppler flow is the primary imaging tool for diagnosis (see [Radiology description](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/ovarytubetorsion.html#ovarytubetorsionradiology)) (Ultrasound Obstet Gynecol. 2020;56(6):934-943)
* Definitive diagnosis is confirmed through direct visualization during surgery

Laboratory

* No abnormalities in many patients
* Leukocytosis is present in 10 - 20% of patients (Ann Emerg Med. 2001;38(2):156-159, Emerg Med Australas. 2005;17(3):231-237)
* HCG testing is recommended, as pregnancy is a risk factor

Radiology description

* Ultrasound findings (Ultrasound Obstet Gynecol. 2020;56(6):934-943)
  + Ovary may appear enlarged due to edema
  + Free fluid in the pouch of Douglas is often observed
  + Doppler flow may be normal, decreased or absent; normal flow does not rule out torsion due to incomplete occlusion, intermittent torsion or collateral blood supply
  + Whirlpool sign indicates twisted ovarian vessels

Prognostic factors

* There is no specific timeframe after symptom onset that reliably predicts ovarian necrosis
* A dark, enlarged ovary is typically due to vascular congestion, not necrosis and is likely still viable (J Pediatr Surg. 2004;39(5):750-753)
* Most patients undergoing adnexal sparing surgery demonstrate normal ovaries with follicular development on follow up (Fertil Steril. 1993;60(6):976-979)
* Prompt intervention improves ovarian salvage rates

Case reports

* 2 year old girl with left ovarian torsion underwent detorsion and showed a normal ovary on follow up imaging (Int J Reprod Biomed. 2023;21(4):355-358)
* 34 year old woman with a twin pregnancy at 32 weeks developed ovarian torsion (J Gynecol Obstet Hum Reprod. 2021;50(6):102117)
* 63 year old woman with a 16 cm right ovarian mass that underwent torsion and was found to be a serous cystadenoma (Cureus. 2023;15(10):e47693)

Treatment

* Detorsion or adnexal sparing surgery (with or without ovarian cystectomy) is the preferred approach when feasible (Hum Reprod. 2003;18(12):2599-2602, Am J Obstet Gynecol. 1993;168(6 Pt 1):1791-1795)
* Salpingo-oophorectomy is indicated for nonviable ovaries, suspected malignancy or in postmenopausal women with an ovarian mass

Microscopic (histologic) description

* Early features
  + Hemorrhage and vascular congestion / thrombi
* Developed features
  + Hemorrhagic infarction with edema, acute hemorrhage and inflammatory cells
  + Ovarian parenchyma may show ghost cells
* Associated ovarian cyst / neoplasms
  + Teratoma
  + Cystadenomas
  + Functional cysts
  + Endometrioma
  + Fibroma / thecoma
  + Malignancy
* Recommendation: thorough sampling to exclude malignancy
* Reference: Nucci, Marisa R. Gynecologic Pathology: Foundations in Diagnostic Pathology, 2nd edition, 2017, pp. 545-575, Nucci, Marisa R. Diagnostic Pathology: Gynecological, 2nd edition.
