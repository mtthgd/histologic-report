Definition / general
  • Chronic inflammatory lesion defined by a mixed infiltrate of T cells, eosinophils and histiocytes within the vessel wall of ≥ 1 chorionic or stem villous vessel
  • Orientation of inflammation is frequently toward the maternal space
  • Primarily found in third trimester placentas
Essential features
  • Incidental chronic inflammatory lesion composed primarily of fetal eosinophils and CD3+ T lymphocytes within the wall of ≥ 1 chorionic plate, stem or umbilical cord fetal vessel
  • Most commonly involves a single chorionic plate vessel with migration of the inflammatory infiltrate away from the amniotic cavity
  • No known clinical significance (in isolation) and no evidence of recurrence
  • Can be associated with chronic villitis of unknown etiology
  • Etiology and pathogenesis is presently unknown
Epidemiology
  • Incidence appears to be increasing
    • 10 fold increase from 0.11% in 2010 to 1.51% in 2021 (Pediatr Dev Pathol 2023;26:281)
  • True incidence is unknown due to its focal nature and standard placental sampling
    • Sampling generally conforms with the Amsterdam placental workshop consensus (Arch Pathol Lab Med 2016;140:698)
Sites
  • Chorionic plate or stem villous fetal vessels
    • Often an isolated finding
    • Multifocality can occur
  • Rare umbilical cord involvement
    • Usually in multifocal cases (Pediatr Dev Pathol 2023;26:281)
Pathophysiology
  • Exact pathophysiology is unknown at this time
  • Considered a T helper type 2 immune response due to the simultaneous presence of T cells and eosinophils (Pediatr Dev Pathol 2002;5:326)
  • Inflammatory cells are fetal in origin; however, the stimulus to which the cells are responding is unclear (Pediatr Dev Pathol 2015;18:305)
  • Hypotheses include a response to infection, an alloimmune process or a localized response to a vascular thrombo-occlusive process (Pediatr Dev Pathol 2013;16:278)
Etiology
  • Unknown at this time
Clinical features
  • Asymptomatic
Diagnosis
  • Placental histopathology
    • Identification of a mixed T cell and eosinophilic chronic inflammatory cell infiltrate within the vessel wall of ≥ 1 chorionic, umbilical or stem villous vessel in the absence of associated neutrophil polymorphs
  • Currently no discernable laboratory, radiological or macroscopic diagnostic features
Prognostic factors
  • Incidental finding
  • No indication of recurrence (Pediatr Dev Pathol 2023;26:281)
Case reports
  • 30 year old woman with multifocal eosinophilic / T cell chorionic vasculitis observed in only 1 territory of a dichorionic diamniotic (DCDA) twin pregnancy (Int J Mol Sci 2023;24:9207)
  • 33 year old woman with eosinophilic / T cell chorionic vasculitis in the placenta following fetal death in utero at 34 weeks gestation (Pediatr Dev Pathol 2010;13:393)
  • 34 year old woman with eosinophilic / T cell chorionic vasculitis following spontaneous delivery of a live born infant at 37 weeks 1 day gestation (Malays J Pathol 2023;45:145)
  • 36 year old woman with co-occurrence of eosinophilic / T cell chorionic vasculitis and amniotic fluid infection (Pediatr Dev Pathol 2022;25:485)
  • 4 pregnancies with eosinophilic / T cell chorionic vasculitis including both single and multiple gestations (Fetal Pediatr Pathol 2015;34:73)
Gross description
  • Grossly normal
Microscopic (histologic) description
  • Inflammatory infiltrate within the wall of large caliber fetal vessels consisting primarily of eosinophils and lymphocytes
  • Admixed histiocytes can be present
  • Inflammation is commonly orientated away from the amniotic fluid
    • Can be omnidirectional
  • Frequently limited to a single chorionic vessel
    • Multifocality is being increasingly observed
    • Involvement of stem villous or umbilical cord vessels may occur
  • Increased incidence of villitis of unknown etiology in placentas with eosinophilic / T cell chorionic vasculitis
  • May occur in the presence of amniotic fluid infection
    • When concomitant in the same placenta, one pattern or the other predominates in each involved fetal vessel (Pediatr Dev Pathol 2022;25:485)
  • No acute inflammation or necrosis of the vessel wall

Positive stains
  • Distinct H&E appearance so IHC is generally not necessary
  • CD3, CD5, CD8 (T lymphocyte cell markers) (Int J Mol Sci 2023;24:9207)
  • CD4 (marker of T helper cells, granulocytes, macrophages) (Int J Mol Sci 2023;24:9207)
  • CD25 (Treg cell marker) (Int J Mol Sci 2023;24:9207)
  • CD68 (histiocytic marker) (Int J Mol Sci 2023;24:9207)
Negative stains
  • CD15 (highlights human myeloid cells including neutrophils) (Pediatr Dev Pathol 2022;25:485)
  • CD20 (B lymphocyte cell marker): generally negative; however, rare CD20 positive lymphocytes are sometimes seen (Int J Mol Sci 2023;24:9207)
Sample pathology report
  • Placenta, term vaginal delivery:
    • Mature placenta (weight = fiftieth percentile for gestational age); eosinophilic / T cell chorionic vasculitis (see comment)
    • Comment: Eosinophilic / T cell chorionic vasculitis is an idiopathic lesion defined by a mixed infiltrate of T cells, eosinophils and histiocytes within the vessel wall of ≥ 1 chorionic or stem villous vessel. It can be associated with other chronic inflammatory conditions of the placenta, such as villitis of unknown etiology (not observed in this placenta). There are currently no known specific clinical associations.
Differential diagnosis
  • Acute chorioamnionitis:
    • Acute inflammation (neutrophils) within the chorion or amnion of the extraplacental membranes or chorionic plate (maternal inflammatory response), with or without extravasation of acute inflammatory cells from the umbilical cord or chorionic plate vessels (fetal inflammatory response [FIR])
    • Eosinophils can be seen accompanying neutrophils as part of the FIR (Pediatr Dev Pathol 2013;16:278)
    • Inflammation is primarily oriented towards the amniotic fluid
    • Frequently multifocal
    • No stem villous involvement
Board review style question #1
 


The entity shown above was identified in the placenta of a female infant delivered via normal vaginal delivery at 38 weeks gestation to a 35 year old primipara. Which of the following pathologies is most likely to also be present in the placenta?

  1. Acute villitis
  2. Chorangiosis
  3. Chronic villitis
  4. Delayed villous maturation
Board review style answer #1
C. Chronic villitis. Chronic villitis, chronic deciduitis, chronic chorioamnionitis and eosinophilic / T cell chorionic vasculitis often appear together in a single placenta (sometimes referred to as the chronic inflammatory tetrad). If a pathologist identifies 1 of the 4 entities, they should look carefully for the other 3 diagnoses (Semin Perinatol 2015;39:20). Answer A is incorrect because acute villitis typically involves acute inflammatory cells like neutrophils and is generally associated with bacterial infections. Answer B is incorrect because chorangiosis refers to an increased number of capillaries in the chorionic villi and is associated with conditions like chronic hypoxia. Answer D is incorrect because delayed villous maturation is a placental maturation disorder often seen in cases of maternal diabetes or other conditions affecting fetal growth.

Board review style question #2
How is eosinophilic / T cell chorionic vasculitis diagnosed?

  1. Histological examination of the placenta
  2. Macroscopic placental examination
  3. Maternal complete blood count (CBC)
  4. Third trimester ultrasound scan
Board review style answer #2
A. Histological examination of the placenta. At present, there are no recognized laboratory, radiological or macroscopic features that indicate the presence of eosinophilic / T cell chorionic vasculitis. Diagnosis can only be made at present by histological identification of a mixed T cell, eosinophilic and histiocytic infiltrate within the vessel wall of ≥ 1 chorionic, umbilical or stem villous vessel in the absence associated neutrophil polymorphs. Answer B is incorrect because macroscopic examination involves visually inspecting the placenta for gross abnormalities, such as size, shape and visible lesions or infarctions. However, eosinophilic / T cell vasculitis requires microscopic evaluation to identify specific cellular infiltrates and detailed vascular changes that cannot be seen with the naked eye. Answer C is incorrect because while a CBC can provide information about the overall health of the mother, including white blood cell counts and the presence of eosinophilia (an increased number of eosinophils), it does not provide direct information about the cellular composition or specific inflammatory processes within the placenta. Diagnosing eosinophilic / T cell chorionic vasculitis requires examination of placental tissue. Answer D is incorrect because ultrasound scans are used to assess the placenta and fetus's physical and functional status, such as placental location, blood flow and fetal growth. While they can identify some placental abnormalities, they cannot detect specific cellular infiltrates or the detailed histopathological changes characteristic of eosinophilic / T cell vasculitis. Ultrasound is not capable of providing the microscopic detail necessary for this diagnosis.
