Definition / general

* Pleural mesothelioma in situ (PMIS) is a preinvasive lesion where neoplastic mesothelial cells proliferate as a single layer on the pleural surface, without invasion into underlying tissues
* PMIS is a precisely defined clinical pathologic entity that requires demonstration of loss of nuclear BAP1 or cytoplasmic MTAP staining by IHC or *CDKN2A* homozygous deletion by FISH, with droplet digital polymerase chain reaction (ddPCR) as a potential alternative diagnostic tool

Essential features

* Preinvasive: neoplastic mesothelial cells proliferate as a single layer on the pleural surface without invasion
* Molecular alterations are key: requires demonstration of loss of nuclear BAP1 or cytoplasmic MTAP staining by IHC or homozygous deletion of *CDKN2A* by fluorescent in situ hybridization (FISH)
* Asbestos exposure: strong causal association with asbestos exposure
* High risk of progression: PMIS is a precursor lesion to mesothelioma
* Diagnosis requires adequate tissue and correlation with clinical presentation and radiology: small biopsies and cytology samples are not sufficient for diagnosis; multiple thoracoscopic biopsies are needed

ICD coding

* ICD-O: [9050/2](https://seer.cancer.gov/icd-o-3/sitetype.icdo3.20220429.pdf#page=152) - mesothelioma, in situ

Epidemiology

* PMIS usually occurs in older individuals, with the median age of diagnosis being 74 years
* Based on a small number of published cases, the true prevalence of PMIS in different age groups is unknown
* Slight predilection for men
* No known association between PMIS and any particular geographic location
* Strongest causal connection for PMIS is asbestos exposure
* References: [Mod Pathol 2020;33:297](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/31375770), [Mod Pathol 2022;35:51](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/34465883), [Pathol Int 2009;59:537](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/19627537), [Front Oncol 2020;10:579327](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/33304846), [Dis Markers 2017;2017:1310478](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/29085180)

Sites

* Mesothelioma in situ can develop on the surfaces of the pleura, peritoneum, pericardium and tunica vaginalis (i.e., any body surface with mesothelial lining)
* Most common location for mesothelioma in situ is the pleura (i.e., PMIS, as is the case for mesothelioma as well)
* References: [Mod Pathol 2020;33:297](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/31375770), [Mod Pathol 2022;35:51](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/34465883)

Pathophysiology

* PMIS is thought to arise from mesothelial hyperplasia
* PMIS is the only known precursor lesion to mesothelioma
* Known molecular pathways underpinning PMIS are the BAP1 / ASXL1 / PRC2 pathway and the Hippo pathway (with other less understood pathways like p53, PI3K / AKT / mTOR and MAPK playing a not yet fully elucidated role)
* Loss of functions of BAP1 can lead to dysregulation of PRC2, which may contribute to the development of PMIS
* *MTAP* is a tumor suppressor gene located on chromosome 9p21; it is frequently codeleted with *CDKN2A* (suppressor gene located on chromosome 9p21.3) in mesothelioma, including PMIS
* *CDKN2A* is a tumor suppressor gene that encodes 2 proteins, p16INK4A and p14ARF
  + These proteins regulate the Rb and p53 cell cycle pathways, respectively
  + Loss of *CDKN2A* function can lead to dysregulation of these pathways, which may contribute to the development of mesothelioma in situ
* In the BAP1 / ASXL1 / PRC2 pathway, *CDKN2A* may play a role in regulating the expression of BAP1
* In the Hippo pathway, Merlin may interact with *CDKN2A* to regulate the activity of the pathway
* References: [Mod Pathol 2020;33:297](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/31375770), [Mod Pathol 2022;35:51](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/34465883), [Pathol Int 2009;59:537](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/19627537), [Front Oncol 2020;10:579327](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/33304846), [Dis Markers 2017;2017:1310478](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/29085180), [Cancer Res 2019;79:4113](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/31151962)

Etiology

* Strongest causal connection for PMIS is asbestos exposure
* Most patients with PMIS have a history of asbestos exposure
* Other potential risk factors for PMIS include exposure to erionite, radiation therapy and certain genetic pathogenic variants
* References: [Expert Rev Mol Med 2012;14:e12](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/22622048), [Clin Cancer Res 2003;9:2108](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/12796375), [Cancer Res 2015;75:264](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/25488749)

Clinical features

* Asbestos exposure: the vast majority of patients with PMIS have a history of asbestos exposure ([J Thorac Oncol 2023;18:1233](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/37356802), [Mod Pathol 2022;35:51](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/34465883), [Mod Pathol 2020;33:297](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/31375770))
* Pleural effusion is a common presenting symptom of PMIS ([J Thorac Oncol 2023;18:1233](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/37356802), [Mod Pathol 2022;35:51](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/34465883))
* Pleural plaques are areas of thickened pleura that are often seen in patients with asbestos exposure; they are not specific for PMIS but may be a clue to the diagnosis ([J Thorac Oncol 2023;18:1233](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/37356802), [Mod Pathol 2022;35:51](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/34465883))
* Long latency period: the latency period between asbestos exposure and the development of PMIS can be many years, even decades ([J Thorac Oncol 2023;18:1233](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/37356802), [Mod Pathol 2022;35:51](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/34465883))
* Progression to mesothelioma: PMIS is a precursor lesion to mesothelioma; the time to progression can vary but it is typically several years ([J Thorac Oncol 2023;18:1233](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/37356802), [Mod Pathol 2022;35:51](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/34465883))

Diagnosis

* PMIS is suspected in patients with recurrent pleural effusion(s), with known asbestos exposure, with or without pleural plaques, after irradiation and with a known familial burden ([J Thorac Oncol 2023;18:1233](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/37356802), [Mod Pathol 2022;35:51](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/34465883))
* No mass lesions are identified on imaging (computed tomography [CT], position emission tomography CT [PET CT]) or thoracoscopy ([Histopathology 2018;72:1033](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/29350783))
* Thoracoscopic evaluation with multiple large biopsy samples (100 - 200 mm2), from different pleural areas, is required for diagnosis ([Pathology 2021;53:446](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/33775406))
* Small biopsy / cytology samples are not adequate for diagnosis ([J Thorac Oncol 2020;15:899](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/32307274))
* Diagnosis of PMIS is made based on a pathological characteristic and correlation with clinical and radiological presentation ([J Thorac Oncol 2023;18:1233](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/37356802), [Mod Pathol 2022;35:51](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/34465883))
* Multidisciplinary team (MDT) discussion is needed for diagnosis ([J Thorac Oncol 2023;18:1233](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/37356802), [Mod Pathol 2022;35:51](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/34465883), [Mod Pathol 2020;33:297](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/31375770))

Radiology description

* Imaging is not definitive but some findings can raise suspicion
  + Chest Xray: may show pleural effusion or pleural thickening
  + CT scan: may show pleural effusion
  + PET scan: may show increased uptake in the pleura
* References: [Histopathology 2018;72:1033](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/29350783), [Pathology 2020;52:635](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/32829890), [Arch Pathol Lab Med 2024;148:1251](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/38586983), [Radiographics 2023;43:e220128](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/36757881)

Prognostic factors

* Disease progression observed in 44% of cases over time ([Mod Pathol 2020;33:297](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/31375770))
  + 15% progress within 6 - 12 months
  + 30% progress within 1 - 2 years
  + 35% progress after > 4 years
* Natural history and optimal management remain poorly understood due to limited long term follow up data ([J Natl Cancer Inst 2013;105:293](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/23355760))
* Time of progression to invasive disease is variable and unpredictable ([Mod Pathol 2020;33:297](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/31375770))
* Requires careful correlation between clinical, radiologic and pathologic findings for risk assessment ([J Natl Cancer Inst 2013;105:293](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/23355760))

Case reports

* 43 year old woman with a history of multifocal left breast invasive ductal carcinoma ([JTO Clin Res Rep 2022;3:100356](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/35814862))
* 57 year old man presented with cough and B symptoms (fever, night sweats, weight loss) for more than 3 months ([Acta Cytol 2021;65:99](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/32814330))
* 72 year old man with a history of asbestos exposure presented with pleural effusion ([Diagn Pathol 2023;18:126](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/38017544))

Treatment

* No established treatment protocols ([Radiographics 2023;43:e220128](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/36757881))

Microscopic (histologic) description

* Layer of flat or cuboidal cells
* No cytological atypia or if present it should be minimal
* No invasion into the surrounding tissue
* Bland nuclear features, with some cells displaying a prominent nucleolus
* Usually without mitotic activity
* Small papillary projections are sometimes present
* Reference: [Mod Pathol 2020;33:297](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/31375770)

  + Mesothelioma in situ (see comment)
  + Comment: Single layer of mesothelial cells shows loss of nuclear staining with BAP1. No histological features of invasion.

Differential diagnosis

* [Reactive mesothelial proliferation](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/pleuraperitmesothelialhyper.html):
  + Distinguished from PMIS by IHC staining for [BAP1](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsbap1.html) or [MTAP](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsmtap.html)
  + FISH is used for the detection of *CDKN2A* homozygous deletion
* Surface spread of adjacent [invasive mesothelioma](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/pleuramesothelioma.html):
  + Very difficult to differentiate from PMIS
  + Correlation with imaging is extremely important
* [Well differentiated papillary mesothelial tumor](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/peritoneumwelldiffpapillarymeso.html):
  + Difficult to differentiate from PMIS
  + Thoracoscopic findings of small pleural nodules help exclude PMIS

Board review style question #1

What is the role of fluorescence in situ hybridization (FISH) in diagnosing mesothelioma in situ?

1. Confirms *KRAS* mutation
2. Demonstrates *ALK* rearrangement
3. Detects *EGFR* amplification
4. Identifies *CDKN2A* deletion

Board review style answer #1

**D**. Identifies *CDKN2A* deletion. In the context of PMIS, FISH plays a crucial role in identifying homozygous deletion of the *CDKN2A* gene, a tumor suppressor gene located on chromosome 9p21.3. Homozygous deletion of *CDKN2A* is a frequent genetic alteration in PMIS and is considered a diagnostic criterion for this preinvasive lesion. Answer C is incorrect because *EGFR* amplification is more commonly associated with lung adenocarcinoma and is not a typical finding in PMIS. Answer B is incorrect because *ALK* rearrangements are characteristic of a subset of lung adenocarcinomas and are not relevant to the diagnosis of PMIS. Answer A is incorrect because *KRAS* mutations are more frequently observed in colorectal and lung adenocarcinomas and are not a primary diagnostic feature of PMIS.

Board review style question #2

[![](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/imgau/pleuramesoinsituBrcic04.jpg)](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/imgau/pleuramesoinsituBrcic04.jpg)

A 74 year old man with a history of asbestos exposure presents with pleural effusion. A pleural biopsy reveals atypical cells confined to the surface without stromal invasion. Which immunohistochemical panel would be most appropriate to confirm mesothelial origin and neoplastic process?

1. Calretinin, CK5/6, BAP1
2. Calretinin, WT1, CK8/18
3. TTF1, claudin4, CK7
4. WT1, TTF1, D2-40

Board review style answer #2

**A**. Calretinin, CK5/6, BAP1. Calretinin is a highly sensitive marker for mesothelial cells, helping to confirm the origin of the atypical cells. CK5/6 is also usually expressed in mesothelial cells, which supports the mesothelial origin in this scenario. Loss of nuclear BAP1 expression is seen in mesothelioma and can be a helpful marker in differentiating mesothelioma from reactive mesothelial proliferations (100% specificity). Answer B is incorrect because while calretinin, WT1 and CK8/18 markers can be positive in mesothelioma, they lack the specificity of the combination in answer A and cannot prove malignancy. WT1 can be expressed in a variety of tumors and CK8/18 can be seen in reactive mesothelial cells. Answer C is incorrect because this panel is more suggestive of lung adenocarcinoma. TTF1 is a marker of lung and thyroid origin while claudin4 and CK7 can be expressed in lung adenocarcinoma. Answer D is incorrect because this panel is not specific for mesothelial differentiation. As mentioned, WT1 can be seen in various tumors, TTF1 points towards lung or thyroid origin and while D2-40 can stain mesothelial proliferation, the use of it in this panel does not offer diagnostic specificity.
