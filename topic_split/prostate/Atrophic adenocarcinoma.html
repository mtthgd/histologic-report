Definition / general
  • Infiltrative proliferation of malignant acini that are well formed punched out glands (Gleason pattern 3), architecturally resembling atrophic benign prostate gland
  • Usually sporadic, also associated with radiation and androgen deprivation therapy
Essential features
  • Disorganized infiltration of glands, disrupting the partially lobulated structure of atrophic benign glands
  • Reduced amount of cytoplasm (the main reason why this type of prostatic adenocarcinoma mimics atrophy)
  • Stromal changes associated with benign atrophy are usually absent
  • Mixed with conventional or other variants of prostatic adenocarcinoma; pure atrophic type prostatic adenocarcinoma has not been described
Terminology
  • Despite it being a recognized subtype of prostatic acinar adenocarcinoma, there is no definitive terminology
  • Prostatic adenocarcinoma, atrophic subtype; adenocarcinoma of the prostate with atrophic features can be used (it is still unclear whether to mention specific subtypes of prostatic acinar adenocarcinoma)
ICD coding
  • Same as prostatic adenocarcinoma; no specific code
Epidemiology
  • Mean age is 60 (51 - 73 years) (Am J Surg Pathol 1997;21:931)
  • Incidence is 2% in needle biopsies and 16% in prostatectomies (Am J Clin Pathol 1998;109:695, Am J Surg Pathol 1997;21:931)
  • Rarely, it can be seen in transurethral resections (Am J Surg Pathol 1997;21:289)
Sites
  • Prostate
Pathophysiology
  • Thought to be similar to conventional or subtypes of prostatic acinar adenocarcinoma
  • Can be present after radiation or androgen deprivation therapy, although not a requisite
Etiology
  • Possibly similar to the etiology of prostatic acinar adenocarcinoma

  • No known macroscopic or clinicopathologic features since its pure form has not been described

Laboratory

Microscopic (histologic) description
  • Well formed, occasionally distorted acini with cells containing scant cytoplasm and infiltrative growth pattern
  • Lack of basal cells
  • Cells can look flattened or attenuated but mostly preserve malignant cytologic features including
    • Presence of enlarged nucleoli
    • Increased nuclear size
    • Macronucleoli
  • Pink amorphous secretions, intraluminal mucin and crystalloids can be seen
  • References: Am J Clin Pathol 1998;109:695, Am J Surg Pathol 1997;21:931, Am J Surg Pathol 1997;21:289)

Sample pathology report
  • Prostate, radical prostatectomy or prostate, core biopsy:
    • Adenocarcinoma of the prostate, prognostic grade group 1 (Gleason score 3+3=6 involving 10 [1 mm in length] of 1 core) (see comment)
    • Comment: This tumor focally shows atrophic features.
Differential diagnosis
  • Benign atrophy:
    • Preservation of lobular architecture with distinct fibrous stroma, presence of basal cells but may focally be absent, small and indistinct nucleoli
  • Postatrophic hyperplasia:
    • Usually with a central, dilated atrophic gland surrounded by clustered smaller glands within a fibrotic / sclerotic stroma but does not have an infiltrative appearance
    • May have prominent nucleoli
Additional references

Board review style question #1


What is the most important feature in distinguishing atrophic type prostatic adenocarcinoma from benign prostatic atrophy?

  1. Absence of basal cells
  2. Infiltrative pattern
  3. Malignant cytopathologic features in nuclei
  4. Preservation of lobulation
  5. Reduced cytoplasm
Board review style answer #1
A. Absence of basal cells. This can be seen on the left side of the core biopsy because it is a requisite in prostatic adenocarcinoma and it is still the most important feature of atrophic type prostatic adenocarcinoma. Answers B, C, D and E are incorrect because preservation of lobulation, malignant cytopathologic features in nuclei, reduced cytoplasm and infiltrative pattern can be seen in benign prostatic atrophy.
