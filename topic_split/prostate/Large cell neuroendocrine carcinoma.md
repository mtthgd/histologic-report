Definition / general

* High grade prostatic carcinoma composed of large cells with diffuse neuroendocrine features
* Neuroendocrine differentiation of prostate carcinoma is classified as follows ([Am J Surg Pathol 2014;38:756](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/24705311), [Endocr Pathol 2016;27:123](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/26885643)):
  + Usual prostatic adenocarcinoma with neuroendocrine differentiation
  + Prostatic adenocarcinoma with Paneth cell neuroendocrine differentiation
  + Carcinoid tumor
  + Small cell carcinoma
  + **Large cell neuroendocrine carcinoma**
  + Mixed (small or large cell) neuroendocrine carcinoma with usual prostatic adenocarcinoma

Essential features

* Composed of tumor cells larger than those in small cell (neuroendocrine) carcinoma, also with slightly more cytoplasm ([Am J Clin Exp Urol 2014;2:273](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/25606573), [Am J Clin Exp Urol 2014;2:337](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/25606580)); however the size cut-off point is not well established
* This tumor is similar to small cell carcinoma ([Am J Surg Pathol 2008;32:65](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/18162772)) in the following features:
  + high grade, with no prominent glandular differentiation
  + diffuse, not focal neuroendocrine features
  + high rates of mitosis and apoptosis
  + poor prognosis
* Can be pure or mixed with conventional high grade prostatic adenocarcinoma, either acinar or ductal type
* Paraneoplastic syndromes such as Cushing syndrome (ACTH producing tumor induced) may be present but are rare

Epidemiology

* Same as prostatic adenocarcinoma

Etiology

* Unknown
* May occur after androgen deprivation therapy for prostate cancer ([Can J Urol 2015;22:7752](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/25891342))
* However, many cases develop de novo or with untreated high grade prostatic adenocarcinoma ([Am J Surg Pathol 2008;32:65](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/18162772))

Clinical features

* Similar to prostatic adenocarcinoma
* Although the serum PSA may be elevated, many cases do not show significant elevations, particularly considering the high tumor volume
* Other symptoms and signs of advanced prostate cancer may occur
* Serum neuroendocrine markers such as chromogranin may be elevated

Diagnosis

* Tissue diagnosis with histology is essential, either from needle core biopsy, transurethral resection or prostatectomy
* Cytology diagnosis of a primary tumor is not generally accepted as an initial diagnosis, but may be used for metastatic sites

Prognostic factors

* Often advanced disease at the time of diagnosis
* High volume disease is associated with poorer prognosis

Case reports

* 69 year old man with prostate needle core biopsy ([Individual Case Report [pdf]](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/site/prostatelargecellNEcarcyangcasereport.pdf))

Treatment

* There is very limited treatment experience, because it is rare and probably underreported
* Generally requires systemic therapy similar to small cell carcinoma, but may not respond well to this treatment or to that for conventional prostatic adenocarcinoma

Gross description

* Usually large tumor nodule(s) with a high volume of disease in the prostate

Microscopic (histologic) description

* Large islands or sheets of tumor cells
* Large tumor cells with prominent neuroendocrine features such as salt and pepper chromatin and small nucleoli (see fig 1A)
* High grade features such as lack of glandular formation (see fig 2A), frequent mitoses and apoptotic bodies and tumor necrosis are frequent findings

Positive stains

* At least one of these neuroendocrine markers should be positive: [chromogranin A](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainschromogranin.html), [synaptophysin](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainssynaptophysin.html), [neuron specific enolase](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsNSE.html), [CD56](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/cdmarkerscd56.html)
* **May be positive**:
  + [TTF1](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsttf1.html) positive in less than 50% cases
  + [AMACR](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsamacr.html) positive, but may be focal or weaker than prostatic adenocarcinoma
  + Prostate markers such as [PSA](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainspsa.html), PSMA, NKX3.1 prostein (P501S) are negative in most cases, but they can be focally positive in a small subset of tumors

Negative stains

* Urothelial markers such as [GATA3](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsgata3.html), [p63](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsp63.html) and high molecular weight cytokeratins such as [CK5](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsck5.html) / [6](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsck6.html)
* [CD99](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/cdmarkerscd99.html)

Differential diagnosis

* [Carcinoid tumor](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/prostatecarcinoid.html)
* [PNET / Ewing sarcoma](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/boneewing.html)
* **Prostatic adenocarcinoma with focal neuroendocrine differentiation**
* **Urothelial carcinoma with neuroendocrine differentiation**
