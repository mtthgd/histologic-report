Definition / general

* Folliculitis and perifolliculitis with a predilection for the posterior neck and occiput
* Prolonged follicular and perifollicular inflammation leads to scarring alopecia and dense fibrotic scar formation

Essential features

* Early neutrophilic inflammation damages hair follicles and adjacent sebaceous glands
* Prolonged damage to hair follicles releases naked hair shafts
* Naked hair shafts act as foreign bodies and trigger late granulomatous inflammation
* Prolonged inflammation leads to hypertrophic plaque-like scars

Terminology

* Acne keloidalis is a misnomer, as it is not associated with acne vulgaris nor does it cause true keloid formation
* Synonyms: acne keloidalis nuchae, folliculitis keloidalis, folliculitis keloidalis nuchae, folliculitis nuchae, sycosis framboesiformis, dermatitis papillaris capillitii

ICD coding

* ICD-10: [L73.0](https://www.icd10data.com/ICD10CM/Codes/L00-L99/L60-L75/L73-/L73.0) - acne keloid

Epidemiology

* Most common in young Black men, especially those of African descent ([Int J Dermatol 2007;46:482](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/17472675), [Am Fam Physician 2013;87:859](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/23939568))
* However, not limited to Black men and can also affect other races and women ([J Natl Med Assoc 2005;97:736](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/15926654), [Cureus 2024;16:e59119](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/38803744), [JAAD Case Rep 2023;38:70](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/37600726))
* M:F = 20:1 ([Dermatol Clin 2003;21:645](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/14717405))

Sites

* Predilection for the posterior neck and occiput with cranial spread as the condition progresses

Pathophysiology

* Initial neutrophilic folliculitis involving follicular isthmus, infundibulum or adjacent sebaceous glands ([Arch Dermatol 2000;136:479](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/10768646))
* Early damage and loss of sebaceous glands ([J Am Acad Dermatol 2011;65:597](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/21669475))
* Prolonged inflammation leads to destruction of follicular wall, leading to hair fragments and naked hair shafts ([Arch Dermatol 2000;136:479](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/10768646))
* Late stage granulomatous inflammation with development of hypertrophic fibrosis without true keloid formation and possible formation of sinus tracts ([Clin Cosmet Investig Dermatol 2016;9:483](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/28008278), [J Invest Dermatol 2024;144:1491](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/38218364))

Etiology

* Causes are not well understood but suspected contributing factors include
  + Autoimmune reaction to follicular antigens
  + Autoimmune reaction to chemical or medication exposure
  + Trauma to the scalp or occlusion of follicles
  + Bacterial infection
* Lesions rarely occur in patients prior to puberty, suggesting a hormonal component to this entity ([Clin Cosmet Investig Dermatol 2016;9:483](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/28008278))

Clinical features

* Early lesions present as small papules and pustules over the posterior neck or occipital scalp and progressively extend cranially with disease progression ([StatPearls: Acne Keloidalis Nuchae [Accessed 14 February 2025]](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/29083612))
* Lesions may be pruritic or painful and may bleed even with mild irritation to the area, such as during a haircut ([Clin Cosmet Investig Dermatol 2016;9:483](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/28008278))
* Fibrous scars develop over areas of folliculitis, coalescing into large firm papules and plaques
* Sinus tracts may develop in advanced cases ([StatPearls: Acne Keloidalis Nuchae [Accessed 14 February 2025]](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/29083612))

Diagnosis

* Diagnosis typically made clinically ([StatPearls: Acne Keloidalis Nuchae [Accessed 14 February 2025]](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/29083612), [BMJ Case Rep 2017;2017:bcr2017222222](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/28942416))
  + Examination of scalp and neck for papules and pustules that coalesce
  + Examination for fibrotic plaques in areas of hair loss
* Rule out secondary infection with bacterial cultures ([StatPearls: Acne Keloidalis Nuchae [Accessed 14 February 2025]](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/29083612))
* Skin biopsy typically not required

Prognostic factors

* Early treatment of lesions leads to reduced scarring ([Clin Cosmet Investig Dermatol 2016;9:483](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/28008278))
* Recurrence is common and may be less responsive to therapy for lesions that are not treated early ([Dermatol Ther (Heidelb) 2016;6:363](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/27432170))
* Surgical removal of follicles is an effective treatment for lesions that do not respond to medical therapy and helps prevent recurrence

Case reports

* 38 year old Caucasian woman with concurrent metabolic syndrome and autoimmune thyroiditis ([Cureus 2024;16:e59119](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/38803744))
* 39 year old Pacific Asian American man with disease onset at 10 years of age ([JAAD Case Rep 2021;13:90](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/34179325))
* 41 year old woman with a 10 year disease history, treated with JAK inhibitor ([JAAD Case Rep 2023;38:70](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/37600726))

Treatment

* Medical therapy with topical or intralesional corticosteroids may be beneficial in mild to moderate cases without significant fibrotic scarring ([StatPearls: Acne Keloidalis Nuchae [Accessed 14 February 2025]](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/29083612), [Dermatol Ther (Heidelb) 2016;6:363](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/27432170), [Cutis 2020;105:223](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/32603383))
* Systemic antibiotics if a secondary bacterial infection is suspected ([Dermatol Ther (Heidelb) 2016;6:363](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/27432170))
* Phototherapy, laser or surgical resection of fibrotic plaques for more advanced cases ([Dermatol Ther (Heidelb) 2016;6:363](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/27432170), [J Cutan Aesthet Surg 2024;17:25](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/38736851), [Cutis 2020;105:223](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/32603383))

Microscopic (histologic) description

* Early stage lesions: neutrophilic folliculitis and perifolliculitis involving adnexal structures ([Am J Dermatopathol 1990;12:109](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/2331046))
  + Can destroy sebaceous glands and adnexal structures
* Late stage lesions: granulomatous folliculitis and perifolliculitis, often also presenting with plasmacytic infiltrate ([Am J Dermatopathol 1990;12:109](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/2331046), [Arch Dermatol 2000;136:479](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/10768646))
  + Areas of coalescing hypertrophic fibrosis
  + Presence of naked hair shafts ([J Dermatol 2020;47:41](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/31646683))
  + Presence of multinucleated foreign body giant cells
  + Presence of hemosiderin

Sample pathology report

* Skin, posterior scalp, shave:
  + Acne keloidalis (see comment)
  + Comment: Granulomatous folliculitis with the presence of naked hair shafts and hypertrophic dermal fibrosis. The findings are consistent with acne keloidalis.

Differential diagnosis

* [Central centrifugal scarring alopecia](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/skinnontumorcentralcentrifugalalopecia.html):
  + Typically appears at the vertex of the scalp
  + Primarily lymphocytic inflammation
  + Sparing of vellus hairs and dilated eccrine ducts
  + Scarring alopecia with premature desquamation of the inner root sheath
* [Folliculitis decalvans](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/skinnontumorfolliculitisdecalvans.html):
  + Predilection for scalp vertex
  + Less extensive scarring
* [Infectious folliculitis](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/skinnontumorfolliculitis.html):
  + Identification of infectious organisms (e.g., bacteria, fungi) as the primary cause
* [Acne vulgaris](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/skinnontumorfolliculitis.html) / **acne conglobata**:
  + Dilated follicles with associated sebaceous glands
  + Comedones with keratin, sebum, and bacterial contents
  + Mixed inflammation with neutrophils, lymphocytes and histiocytes
* [Hidradenitis suppurativa](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/skinnontumorhidradenitis.html):
  + Affects intertriginous areas
  + Follicular plugging leading to folliculitis and follicle rupture
  + Mixed inflammation with abscess formation, fibrosis and sinus tract formation

Board review style question #1

[![](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/imgau/skinnontumoracnekeloidalisPlaza07.jpg)](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/imgau/skinnontumoracnekeloidalisPlaza07.jpg)

A patient presents with pruritic papules and pustules on the posterior side of the neck. Conservative treatment with topical corticosteroids is administered. The lesions are nonresponsive and progress as a large coalescing scar with sinus tracts. What is the most likely diagnosis?

1. Acne keloidalis
2. Acne vulgaris
3. Folliculitis decalvans
4. Hidradenitis suppurativa

Board review style answer #1

**A**. Acne keloidalis. Early stage acne keloidalis typically presents as pruritic papules and pustules that coalesce into larger plaques with alopecia, fibrotic scarring and formation of abscesses or sinus tracts. Histology shows mixed inflammation with multinucleated giant cells surrounding naked hair shafts. While the microscopic findings are consistent with acne keloidalis, this entity can be diagnosed clinically without histologic examination.
Answer B is incorrect because while acne vulgaris may present with pustules on the posterior neck, it does not typically cause alopecia or hypertrophic fibrous scarring.
Answer D is incorrect because while hidradenitis suppurativa may form abscesses, sinus tracts and fibrous scarring in later stages, this entity generally affects the axilla, inguinal, anogenital and inframammary regions.
Answer C is incorrect because while folliculitis decalvans may have neutrophilic inflammation, additional features of this entity include polytrichia. Furthermore, fibrous scarring is typically not as extensive nor do sinus tracts typically form.

Board review style question #2

A patient presents with hair loss near the occiput and a punch biopsy is performed. Which of the following findings is consistent with acne keloidalis?

1. Lichenoid inflammation
2. Naked hair shafts with foreign body type multinucleated giant cells
3. Premature desquamation of inner root sheath
4. Mucin deposition in outer root sheath

Board review style answer #2

**B**. Naked hair shafts with foreign body type multinucleated giant cells. Inflammation in acne keloidalis can lead to the destruction of the follicular wall, leading to the presence of hair fragments, naked hair shafts, and granulomatous inflammation with foreign body type multinucleated giant cells
Answer A is incorrect because lichenoid inflammation can be seen in lichen planopilaris. Early inflammation in acne keloidalis is neutrophilic and not lymphocytic and late stage lesions show granulomatous inflammation.
Answer C is incorrect because premature desquamation of the inner root sheath is characteristic of central centrifugal cicatricial alopecia, and not acne keloidalis.
Answer D is incorrect because mucin deposition in the outer root sheath is characteristic of alopecia mucinosa and not acne keloidalis.
