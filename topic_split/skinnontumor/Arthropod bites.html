Definition / general
  • Arthropod bite reactions are common, self limiting, inflammatory dermatologic reactions, observed in all age groups, that occur secondary to bites, stings or injury by arthropods; common assailants include fleas, bed bugs, scabietic mites, mosquitos, ants, ticks and spiders
Essential features
  • Common, self limiting, inflammatory eruptions most frequently affecting upper and lower extremities of all age groups
  • Histopathologically, they most often demonstrate an urticarial hypersensitivity dermatitis inflammatory pattern presenting with superficial and deep perivascular dermal lymphocytic infiltrate with increased eosinophils; the epidermis may show minimal / focal spongiosis or slight hyperkeratosis but is often otherwise normal
  • Arthropod bite reactions, urticaria and urticarial pattern drug reactions commonly have identical histopathologic features and are largely indistinguishable by microscopic examination alone
Terminology
  • Common names: arthropod assault, arthropod bite reaction, hypersensitivity dermatitis, dermal hypersensitivity reaction, dermal hypersensitivity reaction to an arthropod bite, urticarial dermatitis, urticarial dermatitis secondary to an arthropod bite
ICD coding
  • ICD-10
    • W57.XXXA - bitten or stung by nonvenomous insect and other nonvenomous arthropods, initial encounter
    • L30.9 - dermatitis, unspecified

Sites
  • May affect any anatomic cutaneous site; however, the most commonly reported are exposed skin sites on the upper and lower extremities
  • Importantly, while the bite itself may be present at one anatomic site, involvement of more distant anatomic sites is not uncommon
Pathophysiology
Not applicable
Etiology
  • Most commonly arises secondary to injury by bite or sting from insects, fleas, ticks, wasps and bees (J Cutan Pathol 2009;36:26)
  • Bite or injury stimulates what is believed to be a hypersensitivity reaction within the skin that may be limited to the site of injury or more widespread

Clinical features
  • Manifest with variable clinical findings on physical exam, most often as urticarial dermatitis with erythema, papules and plaques typically lasting > 24 hours in duration (Arch Dermatol 2006;142:29)
  • Can have identical clinical exam findings to urticarial dermatitis of a variety of etiologies, including drug reactions and urticarial vasculitis (Arch Dermatol 2006;142:29)
  • Can be challenging to diagnose clinically (particularly if a recent history of an arthropod bite has not been documented in medical history) due to variable clinical exam findings such as vesicles, pustules, macules, urticaria, bullae, nodules, erythema (only), pruritis or mere signs of external trauma, such as erosions and excoriations (J Cutan Pathol 2009;36:26)
Diagnosis
  • Most often diagnosed clinically with a thorough medical history and clinical skin exam on patient intake
  • In some instances when the diagnosis is clinically unclear, a punch biopsy of the affected skin site is performed to clarify the diagnosis

Prognostic factors
  • Self limited, with most cases resolving without complications
  • In patients with known pre-existing inflammatory dermatoses, bites may trigger what is known as an id reaction, which is a flare of a patient's known inflammatory dermatologic condition (for example, eczema) in a different area of the skin caused by generalized activation of the immune system within the skin from the recent arthropod bite reaction
Case reports
  • 35 year old pregnant woman with pustules and vesicles (J Cutan Med Surg 2018;22:527)
  • 61 year old woman with hypothyroidism (BMJ Case Rep 2018;11:e228079)
  • 63 year old woman with pancreatic adenocarcinoma (JGH Open 2022;6:280)
  • 85 year old woman with Helicobacter pylori infection (JAAD Case Rep 2020;6:156)
Treatment
  • Seldom requires treatment as many reactions are self limited
  • Clinical symptoms, in some patients, are managed with low potency topical corticosteroids ointments or creams applied to the affected site (Emerg Med Clin North Am 2017;35:355)
  • Some patients with more widespread reactions may be managed with systemic corticosteroids or ultraviolet light therapy

Microscopic (histologic) description
  • Skin biopsies for arthropod bite reactions most often demonstrate features of an urticarial hypersensitivity dermatitis or dermal hypersensitivity reaction: a mild or moderate superficial and deep perivascular lymphocytic infiltrate with increased eosinophils within the reticular dermis and often times minimal epidermal changes (Chang: Allergic and Immunologic Diseases, 1st Edition, 2022)
  • Some cases may include focal spongiosis or parakeratosis within the overlying epidermis or focal subepidermal or intraepidermal serum filled vesicles
  • Histopathologic findings of an arthropod bite may also be observed in urticaria and an urticarial reaction to a medication / drug; clinical correlation is required to appropriately distinguish between these 3 entities

Arthropod bite reaction: 5 minute pathology pearls

Sample pathology report
  • Skin, punch biopsy:
    • Urticarial hypersensitivity dermatitis (see comment)
    • Comment: The differential diagnosis for this histopathologic pattern in the appropriate clinical context includes urticaria, an urticarial hypersensitivity reaction to an arthropod bite (including scabies) and an urticarial reaction to a medication / drug.
Differential diagnosis
  • Spongiotic dermatitis:
    • Often manifests with well developed (as opposed to focal / minimal) epidermal spongiosis with or without parakeratosis
  • Urticarial vasculitis:
    • Features of an urticarial hypersensitivity dermatitis with the additional findings of perivascular neutrophils, leukocytoclasis and fibrinoid vascular necrosis
  • Fixed drug eruption:
    • Mixed perivascular inflammatory infiltrate (neutrophils as well as eosinophils) with the additional features of small vessel vasculitis and focal vacuolar interface alteration
Additional references
  • Ann Dermatol 2017;29:143, Biomedicines 2023;11:1096, Pediatr Ann 2020;49:e124
Board review style question #1
A 3 year old boy presents to his pediatric dermatologist with pruritic pink papules on his right forearm days after a family camping trip. He is otherwise healthy and well. A punch biopsy shows mildly dense superficial and deep perivascular dermal lymphocytic inflammation with increased eosinophils and overlying epidermis that is unremarkable. Which of the following is the best diagnosis?

  1. Pityriasis rosea
  2. Psoriasis
  3. Spongiotic dermatitis
  4. Urticarial hypersensitivity dermatitis secondary to arthropod bite
Board review style answer #1
D. Urticarial hypersensitivity dermatitis secondary to arthropod bite. The clinical presentation, biopsy showing inflammation with abundance of eosinophils, in addition to the history of camping makes this the most likely diagnosis.

Answer C is incorrect because while spongiotic dermatitis is a common entity, the overlying epidermis in this biopsy notably lacks spongiosis, making spongiotic dermatitis not a best fit diagnosis. Spongiotic dermatitides are characterized by epidermal spongiosis, variable parakeratosis and a superficial perivascular dermal inflammatory infiltrate consisting of lymphocytes and a few eosinophils. Answer B is incorrect because although psoriasis commonly involves the forearms and distal lower extremities, the clinical context of these lesions arising shortly after a camping trip would suggest a more acute diagnosis rather than a chronic disease. Furthermore, most presentations of classic psoriasis are characterized by regular psoriasiform epidermal hyperplasia with parakeratosis, neutrophil microabscesses in the cornified layer and scarce or inconspicuous dermal eosinophils (unlike arthropod bite reaction). Answer A is incorrect because pityriasis rosea often presents with fairly characteristic epidermal changes, including alternating mounds of parakeratosis, focal spongiosis and irregular psoriasiform epidermal hyperplasia. Since the biopsy findings in this case lack any epidermal changes, pityriasis rosea is not the best fit diagnosis.

Board review style question #2
Which of the following is the best first line treatment for an arthropod bite reaction in the absence of systemic involvement?

  1. Oral antihistamines
  2. Systemic corticosteroids
  3. Low potency topical corticosteroids
  4. Ultraviolet light therapy
Board review style answer #2
C. Low potency topical corticosteroids is the best first line treatment for a typical, localized arthropod bite reaction. These reactions are usually self limiting and resolve without further treatment. Answer A is incorrect because although oral antihistamines can be helpful for symptom management, they are recommended for moderate to severe reactions. Answers B and D are incorrect because systemic corticosteroids and UV light therapy are recommended for systemic involvement and more widespread reactions.
