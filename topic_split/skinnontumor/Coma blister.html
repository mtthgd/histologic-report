Definition / general
  • Self limited cutaneous condition marked by tense, fluid filled blisters that usually occur at sites of maximal pressure in patients with impaired consciousness (Am J Dermatopathol 2013;35:381)
Essential features
  • Self limiting tense, fluid filled blisters at pressure sites in patients with impaired consciousness, appearing within 24 - 72 hours (Case Rep Dermatol 2009;1:66)
  • Various factors like local pressure, hypoxia, drug toxicity in sweat, immune responses and vasomotor changes are linked to the formation of coma blisters, though their exact cause remains unclear (Curr Drug Res Rev 2019;11:21)
  • Subepidermal bullae and focal necrosis of epidermis, dermis, subcutaneous tissue and all epidermal appendages (Case Rep Dermatol 2009;1:66)
  • Necrosis of the eccrine sweat glands is the most notable and significant alteration (Case Rep Dermatol 2009;1:66)
Terminology
  • Pressure induced necrosis or pressure necrosis (JAAD Case Rep 2018;4:365)
  • Coma bullae (Neurologia (Engl Ed) 2020;35:512)
ICD coding
  • ICD-10: L13.8 - other specified bullous disorders
  • SNOMED CT: 339008 - blister
Epidemiology
  • Relatively well documented in adults and very rarely described in children (J Child Neurol 2013;28:1677)
  • Associated with various causes of impaired consciousness
  • Implicated drugs include barbiturate, methadone, hydrocodone, diazepam, amitriptyline hydrochloride, clorazepate dipotassium, meprobamate, imipramine hydrochloride, acetyl-bromo-diethyl acyl-carbamide and glutethimide (JAMA 1970;213:253, Arch Dermatol 1970;101:369, Arch Intern Med 1977;137:1207, Arch Dermatol 1979;115:499)
  • Have also been observed in individuals with diabetic ketoacidosis, carbon monoxide poisoning, autoimmune disorders such as granulomatosis with polyangiitis (Wegener), central nervous system (CNS) diseases such as patients comatose from head trauma, following cerebral vascular accidents and in viral encephalitis, glioblastoma, as an antemortem complication of bilateral prefrontal lobotomy and on the hemiplegic side following a cerebral vascular accident (J Am Acad Dermatol 1992;27:269, Ann Emerg Med 1985;14:603, J Invest Dermatol 1973;60:312, Br Med J 1958;1:435, Br Med J 1965;1:1495, Br Med J 1953;1:291, J Neurol Neurosurg Psychiatry 1950;13:106, Arch NeurPsych 1945;53:262, Lancet 1967;1:811)
Sites
  • Typically form on areas subjected to pressure, such as fingers and toes, elbows, knees, ankles and heels (Actas Dermosifiliogr 2017;108:81)
Pathophysiology
  • Exact pathophysiology of coma blisters is still unclear but hypoxia and external pressure seem to be the main contributing factors (Case Rep Dermatol 2009;1:66)
  • Pressure related ischemia is believed to directly result in tissue damage, with the extent and severity dependent on the level and duration of pressure (Case Rep Dermatol 2009;1:66)
  • Local anoxia in specific areas leads to the formation of a necrotic bulla and the selective destruction of highly metabolic cells, like eccrine sweat glands (Case Rep Dermatol 2009;1:66, Cutis 1990;45:423)
Etiology
  • Numerous factors are believed to contribute to the development of coma blisters, including local pressure or friction, widespread hypoxia and tissue ischemia, direct skin and eccrine gland toxicity from drugs excreted through sweat, immune mechanisms and vasomotor alterations in comatose states; however, the precise cause of these blisters is still debated (Curr Drug Res Rev 2019;11:21)
  • Drug overdose is the most frequent predisposing event; however, lesions cannot be related specifically to the toxic effects of any medication (Case Rep Dermatol 2009;1:66)
Clinical features
  • Tense blisters arise in red macules or patches in patients with decreased consciousness (Neurologia (Engl Ed) 2020;35:512)
  • Size ranges up to 4 - 5 cm; typically contain clear fluid and seldom have blood (Neurologia (Engl Ed) 2020;35:512)
  • Blisters generally emerge 24 hours following the start of drug treatment and 48 - 72 hours after onset of coma (Neurologia (Engl Ed) 2020;35:512)
Diagnosis
  • Diagnosis can be made on clinical signs and symptoms alone
  • Histopathological analysis is considered the hallmark of coma blister diagnosis (Curr Drug Res Rev 2019;11:21)
Prognostic factors
  • Skin lesions typically heal within 2 - 4 weeks but early recognition and treatment of the underlying condition is crucial (Mayo Clin Proc 2011;86:e5)
Case reports
  • 24 year old woman who was found unconscious in her home developed asymptomatic skin lesions located mainly on bony prominences (Actas Dermosifiliogr 2017;108:81)
  • 29 year old man who developed multiple tense hemorrhagic blisters on the extremities 2 days after the onset of coma (Case Rep Dermatol 2009;1:66)
  • 67 year old woman who presented with the first case of coma bullae associated with an anomalous brachioradial artery (high origin with tortuous anastomoses) (Clin Cosmet Investig Dermatol 2023;16:1857)
  • 68 year old man who developed atypical coma bullae after drug induced unconsciousness, with blisters in unusual locations and subsequent eschar formation (Cureus 2024;16:e58646)
Treatment
  • Self limited lesions that heal spontaneously over 1 or 2 weeks (Case Rep Dermatol 2009;1:66)
  • If treatment is needed, a possible option is topical antibiotics, which can help to prevent secondary infections (Clin Toxicol (Phila) 2007;45:808)
  • Offloading pressure prone sites

Microscopic (histologic) description
  • Subepidermal bullae, focal necrosis of epidermis, dermis, subcutaneous tissue and all epidermal appendages (Case Rep Dermatol 2009;1:66, Acta Derm Venereol 2003;83:236)
  • Necrosis of the eccrine sweat gland and sweat duct is the most notable and significant alteration, though its absence does not rule out the diagnosis (Case Rep Dermatol 2009;1:66, Acta Derm Venereol 2002;82:378)
  • Absence of inflammatory infiltrate in the epidermis and the presence of thrombosis in dermal vessels suggest a nondrug etiology (Case Rep Dermatol 2009;1:66)

Immunofluorescence description
  • Usually negative results
  • Some patients display immunoglobulin and complement deposition in the walls of dermal vessels and keratinocytes; however, these are nonspecific (Neurologia (Engl Ed) 2020;35:512)

Coma blister (pressure induced ischemia of skin blister)

Sample pathology report
  • Skin, left upper arm, punch biopsy:
    • Necrosis of epidermis and eccrine epithelium with subepidermal bulla, consistent with coma bulla (see comment)
    • Comment: The features are consistent with coma bullae, pending clinical correlation. There is limited vascular thrombosis but primary vasculopathy is not favored. While full epidermal necrosis is present, toxic epidermal necrolysis is not favored. The features do not support fixed drug eruption.
    • Clinical information: Bullous fixed drug eruption, burn, trauma. Dusky erythematous plaques with bullae and vesicles within on left upper arm, left lateral knee, left lateral foot. Also, with 2 bullae on right dorsal fingers suspicious for trauma.

  • Skin, left thigh, punch biopsy:
    • There is no significant inflammation or dyskeratosis to support contact dermatitis, an immunobullous disorder, Stevens-Johnson syndrome (SJS) or toxic epidermal necrolysis (TEN); there is a suggestion of subtle eccrine gland necrosis, supporting the possibility of pressure necrosis (e.g., coma blister)
    • Negative direct immunofluorescence study
    • Microscopic description: Sections show a subepidermal split and very sparse lymphocytes in the underlying dermis. There is a suggestion of limited dyskeratosis within eccrine glands.
    • Clinical information: ICU patient found down, admitted for unresponsiveness, found to have septic shock. Has pink macules, some are edematous as well as fluid filled blisters and erosions on trunk and extremities. Positive Nikolsky sign. No oral involvement. Rule out staphylococcal scalded skin syndrome, less likely Stevens-Johnson syndrome / toxic epidermal necrolysis and pemphigus.
Differential diagnosis
  • Friction blister:
    • Found in areas that undergo repetitive friction
    • Intraepidermal blister cavity just below the granular layer (Curr Drug Res Rev 2019;11:21)
  • Edema blister:
    • Subepidermal blister and orthokeratosis of the stratum corneum (Cureus 2017;9:e1780)
  • Epidermolysis bullosa acquisita:
    • Subepidermal blister without keratinocyte necrosis
    • Linear IgG deposits along the basement membrane zone, more likely without concomitant C3 deposition (J Am Acad Dermatol 1992;27:674)
  • Dermatitis herpetiformis:
    • Subepidermal blistering disease due to gluten exposure with neutrophilic microabscesses and granular IgA deposits in the dermal papillae
  • Linear IgA bullous disease:
    • Subepidermal bullae or vesicles with neutrophils
    • Linear deposits of IgA along the basement membrane
  • Bullous pemphigoid:
    • Subepidermal blister
    • Superficial perivascular mixed inflammatory infiltrate with eosinophils in the dermis and blister cavity
    • Linear C3 > IgG deposition along the basement membrane zone
Board review style question #1


A 56 year old woman with a history of chronic alcoholism is found unconscious at home and brought to the emergency department. After 48 hours, she develops multiple tense, fluid filled blisters on her heels and elbows. The blisters are 2.5 cm in diameter, contain clear fluid and are located over bony prominences. A skin biopsy is obtained revealing subepidermal bullae with necrosis of epidermis and eccrine epithelium. Direct immunofluorescence is negative. The patient remains comatose. Laboratory tests confirm high levels of barbiturates in her system. Which of the following is the most likely diagnosis?

  1. Bullous pemphigoid
  2. Coma blister
  3. Edema blister
  4. Epidermolysis bullosa acquisita
  5. Friction blister
Board review style answer #1
B. Coma blister. The histologic findings were subepidermal bullae with necrosis of epidermis and eccrine epithelium. Answer C is incorrect because although edema blister shows subepidermal edema, there is no eccrine gland necrosis. Answer E is incorrect because friction blister shows an intraepidermal blister. Answer A is incorrect because bullous pemphigoid would show an inflammatory infiltrate with eosinophils. Answer D is incorrect because both bullous pemphigoid and epidermolysis bullosa acquisita lack confluent epidermal necrosis (except possibly in late lesions); both would show a positive direct immunofluorescence of linear deposits along the epidermal basement membrane.

Board review style question #2
A 55 year old man with a history of diabetes and hypertension is admitted to the hospital after being found unconscious for an unknown duration. He presents himself with several bullous lesions on his back and buttocks. A skin biopsy reveals subepidermal bullae with necrotic keratinocytes and minimal inflammatory infiltrate. Perilesional direct immunofluorescence (DIF) biopsy was completed. Which of the following findings would most likely support the diagnosis of coma blister?

  1. Granular IgA in dermal papillae
  2. Linear IgA along the basement membrane
  3. Linear IgG and C3 deposits along the basement membrane
  4. Linear IgG deposits along the basement membrane
  5. Negative DIF results
Board review style answer #2
E. Negative DIF results. Coma blisters are characterized histologically by subepidermal bullae with necrotic keratinocytes and minimal inflammatory infiltrate. DIF results for coma blisters are typically negative for IgG, IgA, IgM and C3 at the basement membrane zone. Answers A, B, C and D are incorrect because a negative direct immunofluorescence (DIF) result differentiates coma blisters from immunobullous disorders such as pemphigoid (linear IgG and C3), linear IgA bullous dermatosis (linear IgA), epidermolysis bullosa acquisita (linear IgG) and dermatitis herpetiformis (granular IgA in dermal papillae).
