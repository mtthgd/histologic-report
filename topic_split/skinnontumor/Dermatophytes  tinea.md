Definition / general

* Superficial cutaneous mycoses classified by location of infection
* Common genera involved (*Trichophyton*, *Epidermophyton*, *Microsporum* and others) ([J Fungi (Basel) 2023;9:669](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/37367605))
* Dermatophytes typically reside in keratinized tissue (i.e., the stratum corneum, nails and hair shaft) and rarely show invasion ([Mycoses 2021;64:340](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/33217082))

Essential features

* Tinea is diagnosed by identification of fungal organisms consistent with dermatophytes by microscopic examination of skin scrapings with KOH or by skin biopsy; periodic acid-Schiff (PAS) or Grocott-Gomori silver (GMS) special stains may be used on skin biopsies to help identify organisms
* Diagnosis classified by body site of infection

Terminology

* Classified by primary site of infection ([Am Fam Physician 2014;90:702](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/25403034), [JAAD Int 2023;13:104](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/37744668), [Mycopathologia 2010;170:143](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/20411336))
  + Majocchi granuloma: dermatophytosis of hair follicles outside of the scalp and beard area
  + Tinea barbae: dermatophytosis of the beard area, characteristically involving hair follicles
  + Tinea capitis: dermatophytosis of the scalp, characteristically involving hair follicles
  + Tinea corporis: dermatophytosis involving the trunk, neck, arms and legs
  + Tinea cruris: dermatophytosis of the groin
  + Tinea faciei: dermatophytosis of the face
  + Tinea favosa: variant of tinea capitis; due to *T. schoenleinii*
  + Tinea incognito: variant of tinea exacerbated by administration of topical / systemic corticosteroids
  + Tinea manuum: dermatophytosis of the hands
  + Tinea pedis: dermatophytosis of the feet
  + Tinea unguium (onychomycosis): dermatophytosis of the nails
  + Kerion: profound scalp inflammatory reaction associated with tinea capitis, especially by *Microsporum canis* infection

Epidemiology

* Affects 20 - 25% of the population worldwide ([J Fungi (Basel) 2023;9:669](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/37367605))
* More common in subtropical regions ([J Fungi (Basel) 2021;8:39](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/35049979))
* Most common organisms found in humans are *Trichophyton*, *Microsporum*, *Epidermophyton* and *Nannizzia* ([J Fungi (Basel) 2023;9:669](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/37367605))
* Tinea capitis is more common in children
  + Afro-Caribbean and Afro-American children in the United States have higher rates of infection; however, a host of epidemiologic factors can affect transmission ([Am J Clin Dermatol 2005;6:203](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/16060708))
* Immunocompromised patients may have extensive / recurrent disease ([J Fungi (Basel) 2021;8:39](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/35049979))
* Tinea favosa is endemic in Nigeria, Ethiopia, Western China, Iran and some regions in India ([StatPearls: Favus [Accessed 26 September 2024](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/32644450))
* *Trichophyton indotineae* is an emerging dermatophyte that is sexually transmitted and extremely recalcitrant to therapy ([J Am Acad Dermatol 2024;91:315](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/38574764), [J Clin Med 2024;13:3558](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/38930086))

Sites

* See [Terminology](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/skinnontumorfungisuperficialinfections.html#dermtineaterms)

Pathophysiology

* Fungal cell wall adhesins aid in inoculation and proteases digest host keratin ([J Fungi (Basel) 2021;8:39](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/35049979))
* Host immune responses include innate, humoral and cell mediated ([J Fungi (Basel) 2021;8:39](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/35049979))

Etiology

* Involves a group of closely related fungi that infect keratinized tissues such as the skin, hair and nails
* Spreads via direct contact with infected individuals, animals or contaminated surfaces

Clinical features

* Scaly, erythematous patches / plaques, often with an annular morphology ([J Fungi (Basel) 2023;9:669](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/37367605))
* May present with pustules / vesicles ([J Fungi (Basel) 2023;9:669](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/37367605))
* Onychomycosis may affect 1 or more nails and is characterized by onycholysis, hyperkeratosis, accumulation of subungual debris and nail discoloration ([J Fungi (Basel) 2023;9:669](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/37367605))

Diagnosis

* Common methodologies
  + Wood lamp fluorescence ([Pathogens 2022;11:957](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/36145389))
  + Potassium hydroxide (KOH) preparation
  + Skin biopsy
* Other methodologies
  + Culture on dermatophyte test medium and Sabouraud dextrose agar (SDA)
  + Various other media can be used to differentiate *Trichophyton* species ([Pathogens 2022;11:957](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/36145389))
  + Polymerase chain reaction may be used for diagnosis ([Pathogens 2022;11:957](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/36145389))

Prognostic factors

* Most localized infections are treated effectively with topical or oral antifungal medications ([J Am Acad Dermatol 2000;43:S104](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/11044285))
* Half of patients with invasive dermatophytosis are immunosuppressed ([Mycoses 2021;64:340](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/33217082))
* Overall mortality rate is ~8% in invasive dermatophytosis and is higher in disseminated infection ([Mycoses 2021;64:340](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/33217082))

* 78 year old woman with tinea capitis due to *T. rubrum* ([Actas Dermosifiliogr (Engl Ed) 2021 Apr 24 [Epub ahead of print]](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/33901473))

Treatment

* Topical antifungal therapy for most localized infections ([J Fungi (Basel) 2021;8:39](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/35049979))
* Oral antifungal medications may be required for more extensive infections, including ([J Fungi (Basel) 2021;8:39](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/35049979), [Indian Dermatol Online J 2016;7:77](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/27057486))
  + Large body surface area of involvement or overlapping body regions (e.g., tinea cruris, tinea corporis and tinea pedis)
  + Infection failing to respond to repeated and different topical agents
  + Tinea capitis
  + Tinea affecting the nails
  + Tinea pedis with extensive sole, heel or dorsal involvement or blistering

Microscopic (histologic) description

* Fungal hyphae and spores (1 - 2 microns in size) within the stratum corneum or within follicles
* In tinea capitis
  + Endothrix: fungi invade the hair shaft and grow within it
  + Ectothrix: hyphae do not invade the hair shaft but degrade the cuticle and grow on the surface of the hair
* Soft clues ([Ann Dermatol 2014;26:286](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/24882998))
  + Neutrophils within the stratum corneum and epidermis with parakeratosis
  + Compact orthokeratosis
  + Sandwich sign: orthokeratosis or parakeratosis alternating in layers with basketweave stratum corneum
  + Epidermal spongiosis
  + Variable inflammatory response (lymphocytic, neutrophilic, histiocytic)

Differential diagnosis

* Clinical differential will vary widely depending on location and will include other annular and scaly eruptions ([Am Fam Physician 2014;90:702](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/25403034))
  + [Atopic dermatitis](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/skinnontumoracuteeczematousdermatitis.html), **nummular dermatitis**, [allergic contact dermatitis](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/skinnontumorallergiccontact.html):
    - Spongiosis, superficial perivascular lymphocytic infiltrate; may have eosinophils
    - Negative fungal special stains
  + [Psoriasis](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/skinnontumorpsoriasis.html):
    - Psoriasiform hyperplasia, confluent parakeratosis, diminished granular layer and collections of neutrophils in the stratum corneum
    - Negative fungal special stains
  + [Pityriasis rosea](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/skinnontumorpityriasisrosea.html):
    - Spongiotic dermatitis with superficial perivascular lymphocytic infiltrate
    - Few extravasated red blood cells
    - Negative fungal special stains
  + [Granuloma annulare](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/skinnontumorgranulomaannulare.html):
    - Palisading granulomas in the dermis surrounding degenerated collagen and central mucin deposition
    - Mild perivascular lymphocytic infiltrate
  + [Annular / gyrate erythemas](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/skinnontumorerythemaannularecentrifugum.html):
    - Superficial and deep perivascular dermatitis with a coat sleeve appearance of lymphocytes hugging vessels
    - Negative fungal special stains
  + [Syphilis](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/skinnontumorsyphilis.html):
    - Psoriasiform and lichenoid dermatitis with increased collections of plasma cells
    - Positive [treponema](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainstreponemaihc.html) immunohistochemical stain or [Warthin-Starry silver stain](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainswarthin.html)
  + If pustular, one may consider the following
    - [Impetigo](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/skinnontumorimpetigo.html):
      * Subcorneal neutrophils
      * May have acantholysis
      * [Gram stain](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsgramstain.html) may show bacterial organisms
    - [Pustular psoriasis](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/skinnontumorpsoriasis.html):
      * Subcorneal neutrophils, in addition to the features of [psoriasis](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/skinnontumorfungisuperficialinfections.html#skinNTtineaDDxpsoriasis)
    - [Arthropod bites](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/skinnontumorarthropod.html):
      * Superficial and deep, wedge shaped mixed inflammatory infiltrate (lymphocytes, histiocytes, and eosinophils)
    - [Candidiasis](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/skinnontumorfungicandida.html):
      * See [below](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/skinnontumorfungisuperficialinfections.html#skinNTtineaDDxcandidiasis)
* In cases where fungal organisms are identified, the differential diagnosis includes
  + [Pityrosporum (tinea) versicolor](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/skinnontumorfungitineaversicolor.html):

    [![](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/imgau/skinNMTdermtineachung09.jpg)](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/imgau/skinNMTdermtineachung09.jpg)

    - Organisms present in basketweave stratum corneum usually without inflammatory reaction and will have shorter hyphae with numerous yeast forms that are readily identifiable on routine H&E slides
  + [Candidiasis](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/skinnontumorfungicandida.html):
    - Nonseptate pseudohyphae
    - Infection often in intertriginous areas or mucous membranes

Board review style question #1

[![](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/imgau/skinNMTdermtineachung10.jpg)](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/imgau/skinNMTdermtineachung10.jpg)

A patient presents with multiple pruritic, scaly patches on the back. A skin biopsy was performed and a PAS special stain is shown above. What is the best diagnosis?

1. Candidiasis
2. Tinea corporis
3. Tinea cruris
4. Tinea versicolor

Board review style answer #1

**B**. Tinea corporis. The sections show PAS positive hyphae in the stratum corneum, without yeast. Answer A is incorrect because candidiasis will have nonseptate pseudohyphae. Answer C is incorrect because while the etiology / morphology of organisms is the same, tinea cruris is classified as a superficial infection involving the groin. Answer D is incorrect because tinea versicolor is a superficial mycosis due to *Malessezia* spp. and morphologically will have short hyphae and yeast organisms.

[Comment Here](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/)

Reference: [Dermatophytes / tinea](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/skinnontumorfungisuperficialinfections.html)

Board review style question #2

Which of the following is true regarding dermatophyte infections?

1. *Candida* is most frequently identified in cases of tinea cruris
2. Invasive spread of organisms is impossible due to the superficial nature of cutaneous dermatophyte infections
3. Morphologic features include abundant budding yeast
4. Most commonly due to *Trichophyton*, *Epidermophyton*, *Microsporum* and *Nannizzia* organisms

Board review style answer #2

**D**. Most commonly due to *Trichophyton*, *Epidermophyton*, *Microsporum* and *Nannizzia* organisms. These are the most common organisms to cause dermatophytosis. Answer A is incorrect because *Candida* is not a dermatophyte. Answer B is incorrect because invasive dermatophytosis has been reported in immunosuppressed patients. Answer C is incorrect because tinea is not characterized by budding yeast.
