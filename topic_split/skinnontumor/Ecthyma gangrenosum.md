Definition / general

* Ecthyma gangrenosum is a rare, necrotizing ulcerative disease caused by cutaneous bacterial infection
* Mainly associated with *Pseudomonas aeruginosa* bacteremia

Essential features

* Necrosis of dermal vessels secondary to direct invasion of vascular wall by microorganisms
* Amphophilic bacilli or cocci shaped bacteria surrounding vessels
* Clinically, ulcerated plaques with central black eschar and surrounding erythema
* Potentially fatal
* References: [Eur J Clin Microbiol Infect Dis 2015;34:633](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/25407372), [J Med Case Rep 2008;2:14](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/18211707)

ICD coding

* ICD-10: [L08.0](https://www.icd10data.com/ICD10CM/Codes/L00-L99/L00-L08/L08-/L08.0) - pyoderma
* ICD-11: [1B73.2](https://icd.who.int/browse/2024-01/mms/en#1634854510) - ecthyma gangrenosum

Epidemiology

* Almost exclusively occurs in patients who are immunocompromised ([Eur J Clin Microbiol Infect Dis 2015;34:633](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/25407372), [Am J Dermatopathol 2017;39:637](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/27759698))

Sites

* Majority of cases involve gluteal / perianal area or lower extremities (65.8%) ([Eur J Clin Microbiol Infect Dis 2015;34:633](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/25407372))
* Reports of involvement on trunk, upper extremities, mucosal surfaces and head and neck
* References: [Medicine (Baltimore) 2017;96:e5507](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/28079790), [Eur J Clin Microbiol Infect Dis 2015;34:633](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/25407372)

Pathophysiology

* Bacteremic form: hematogenous dissemination of microorganisms to the dermal vessels ([Int J Dermatol 2023;62:1359](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/37700568))
  + Invasion of bacteria in the media and adventitia of arteries and veins
  + Ischemic necrosis of surrounding skin
* Nonbacteremic form: iatrogenic inoculation of pathogen directly into the skin ([Int J Dermatol 2023;62:1359](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/37700568))
* Bacterial toxins and enzymes propagate vascular and cutaneous tissue destruction ([Actas Dermosifiliogr (Engl Ed) 2021;112:1](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/33045208))

Etiology

* *Pseudomonas aeruginosa* is the most common causative organism (74% of cases)
* Identical cutaneous features described with several other bacteria ([Eur J Clin Microbiol Infect Dis 2015;34:633](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/25407372), [Am J Dermatopathol 2017;39:637](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/27759698), [Medicine (Baltimore) 2017;96:e5507](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/28079790))
  + *Escherichia coli*
  + *Staphylococcus aureus*
  + *Streptococcus pyogenes*
  + *Aeromonas hydrophila*
  + *Citrobacter freundii*
  + *Klebsiella pneumoniae*
  + Other *Pseudomonas* species
  + *Morganella morganii*
  + *Burkholderia cepacia*
  + *Chromobacterium violaceum*
  + *Neisseria gonorrhoeae*
* Controversial whether the definition of ecthyma gangrenosum should include nonbacterial causes ([Mycopathologia 2016;181:759](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/27324372))
  + Lesions appear clinically similar
  + Some authors refer to as ecthyma gangrenosum-like infections
  + Debate over potential viral etiologies ([Eur J Clin Microbiol Infect Dis 2015;34:633](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/25407372))
* Less common fungal causes ([Eur J Clin Microbiol Infect Dis 2015;34:633](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/25407372), [Am J Dermatopathol 2017;39:637](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/27759698), [Medicine (Baltimore) 2017;96:e5507](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/28079790))
  + *Mucor*
  + *Candida albicans*
  + *Fusarium*
* Infection most likely in immunocompromised patients with ([Eur J Clin Microbiol Infect Dis 2015;34:633](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/25407372), [Am J Dermatopathol 2017;39:637](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/27759698), [Medicine (Baltimore) 2017;96:e5507](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/28079790))
  + Neutropenia
  + Immunodeficiency
  + Hypogammaglobulinemia
  + Additional risk factors, such as hematologic malignancy, diabetes, malnutrition, burn wounds

Clinical features

* Erythematous macules rapidly progress to hemorrhagic vesicles or bullae
* Bullae rupture and evolve to gangrenous ulcers
  + Ulcers noted to have central necrosis with black eschar surrounded by an erythematous border
* Single or multiple lesions
* Localized or widespread
* Lesions can occur in variable stages of progression
* Initially painless in macule stage
* Evolve rapidly within 12 hours to 5 days
* Clinical signs of infection (e.g., fever, leukocytosis, etc.)
* References: [Indian Dermatol Online J 2015;6:336](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/26500865), [Actas Dermosifiliogr (Engl Ed) 2021;112:1](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/33045208)

Diagnosis

* Skin biopsy with tissue culture of the lesion(s) is diagnostic
  + Deep skin biopsy with subcutaneous tissue
* Blood cultures for identification of the causative organism
* References: [Eur J Clin Microbiol Infect Dis 2015;34:633](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/25407372), [Am J Dermatopathol 2017;39:637](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/27759698)

Prognostic factors

* Mortality rates vary between 35 and 90%
* Higher mortality rate associated with neutropenia ([J Cutan Med Surg 2017;21:425](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/28470091))
* Better prognosis in nonbacteremic ecthyma gangrenosum ([StatPearls: Ecthyma Gangrenosum [Accessed 10 December 2024]](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/30521198))

Case reports

* 1 month old girl with periocular ecthyma gangrenosum from *Pseudomonas septicemia* ([IDCases 2023;34:e01894](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/37736020))
* 7 year old girl with acute lymphoblastic leukemia and ecthyma gangrenosum ([JPRAS Open 2024;40:215](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/38681531))
* 19 year old man with underlying chronic kidney disease and ecthyma gangrenosum secondary to methicillin resistant *Staphylococcus aureus* (MRSA) ([IDCases 2020;23:e01026](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/33384925))
* 67 year old man with chronic idiopathic neutropenia and ecthyma gangrenosum ([Cureus 2022;14:e21225](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/35186525))
* 77 year old woman with documented progression of ecthyma gangrenosum from macule stage to hemorrhagic blister to necrotic ulcer ([Clin Case Rep 2021;9:1787](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/33768939))

Treatment

* Treatment of underlying bacterial infection with antibiotics directed at causative organism
* Extensive necrosis may require surgical debridement ([J Cutan Med Surg 2017;21:425](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/28470091))

Microscopic (histologic) description

* Epidermal necrosis
* Often epidermal ulceration
  + May see subepidermal hemorrhagic blister
* Necrosis of deep dermal vessels
  + Variable dermal hemorrhage
  + Vascular occlusion of dilated blood vessels in the dermis
* Sparse dermal inflammatory infiltrate with neutrophils or lymphocytes
  + May appear as neutrophilic vasculitis of small and medium vessels with minimal leukocytoclasia
* Basophilic granular material within or around involved vessels
  + May appear as a light blue haze surrounding vessels
  + Represents the accumulation of the pathogenic microorganism in the vasculature
  + Basophilic material may be dispersed among dermal collagen or adipocytes of the subcutis
  + Notably minimal inflammatory infiltrate around vessels
  + Stain poorly on H&E
* References: [Am J Dermatopathol 2017;39:637](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/27759698), [Actas Dermosifiliogr (Engl Ed) 2021;112:1](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/33045208)

Positive stains

* Brown-Brenn modified [Gram](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsgramstain.html) stain variably positive
  + May be falsely negative on H&E slides for [Gram](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsgramstain.html) negative bacteria
* Reference: [Acta Histochem 2023;125:151992](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/36566583)

Negative stains

* [Von Kossa](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainscalcium.html) and [alizarin red](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainscalcium.html) are negative
* [PAS](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainspas.html) and [GMS](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsgms.html) negative (unless caused by fungi)
* [AFB](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsacidfast.html), [Fite](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsacidfast.html) negative (unless caused by atypical mycobacteria)
* References: [Am J Dermatopathol 2017;39:637](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/27759698), [Actas Dermosifiliogr (Engl Ed) 2021;112:1](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/33045208)

Sample pathology report

* Skin, punch biopsy:
  + Suggestive of ecthyma gangrenosum (see comment)
  + Comment: The sections demonstrate an ulcer with epidermal and dermal necrosis. There is a dermal infiltrate of neutrophils with minimal leukocytoclasia. There is a perivascular and dermal blue haze suggestive of bacteria. A Brown-Brenn stain confirms the presence of Gram negative bacteria in the specimen. A PAS and AFB stain are negative for fungal hyphae and acid fast bacilli respectively. All control slides stain appropriately.
  + These findings are consistent with an infection such as ecthyma gangrenosum. Tissue cultures are recommended.

Differential diagnosis

* [Hyalohyphomycosis (e.g., fusariosis, aspergillosis)](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/microbiologyhyalinemolds.html) ([Clin Microbiol Infect 2004;10:67](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/14748803), [J Cutan Pathol 2022;49:917](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/34632617)):
  + Angioinvasive fungi cause vascular occlusion and necrosis in skin and other organs
  + Painful ecthyma gangrenosum-like lesions, targetoid lesions, retiform purpura or subcutaneous nodules
  + Often febrile patients with sinopulmonary infection
  + Epidermal and dermal necrosis
  + Dermal microabscesses
  + [PAS](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainspas.html) positive
  + Acute branching septate hyphae throughout dermis and subcutaneous vessels
* [Disseminated strongyloidiasis](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/parasitologystrongyloides.html):
  + Pulmonary and intestinal symptoms due to travel of the larvae
  + Eosinophilia
  + Serpiginous urticarial tracts or disseminated purpura
  + Perivascular inflammatory infiltrate with eosinophils
  + If larvae are seen, they are located between the dermal collagen or in the lumina of vessels
* **Lucius phenomenon**, [diffuse lepromatous leprosy](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/skinnontumorleprosy.html):
  + Multiple painful hemorrhagic cutaneous ulcers
  + Necrotizing vasculitis of small and medium dermal vessels
  + Foamy macrophages filled with *Mycobacterium lepromatosis* bacilli in the dermis and subcutaneous tissue
  + [Fite](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsacidfast.html) stain positive
* [Calciphylaxis](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/skinnontumorcalciphylaxis.html):
  + Painful, chronic necrotic ulcers
  + Calcification of small to medium cutaneous vessels, fibrointimal hyperplasia and microthrombi in dermal / subcutaneous vessels
  + [Von Kossa](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainscalcium.html) and [alizarin red](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainscalcium.html) are positive
* **Coagulopathies**:
  + [Factor V Leiden mutation](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/coagulationactivatedproteinC.html), [protein C](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/coagulationproteinCdef.html) and [S deficiency](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/coagulationproteinSdef.html), [warfarin induced skin necrosis](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/coagulationotheranticoagulants.html#warfarin), purpura fulminans, etc.
  + Thrombosed vessels within the dermis
  + Absent to mild perivascular inflammatory infiltrate
* **Hypercoaguable state disorders** (**heparin necrosis**, [thrombotic thrombocytopenic purpura (TTP)](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/hematologyTTP.html), [paroxysmal nocturnal hemoglobinuria (PNH)](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/hematologyPNH.html), [thrombocytosis secondary to myeloproliferative disorders](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/myeloproliferativeET.html), etc.):
  + Blood extravasation and occluded dermal vessels with white thrombi
  + [CD61](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/cdmarkerscd61.html) confirms presence of abundant platelets in thrombi
  + Absent inflammatory infiltrate
* [Toxic epidermal necrolysis (TEN)](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/skinnontumorstevensjohnsonsyndrome.html):
  + Severe adverse cutaneous eruption that is often drug induced
  + Atypical targetoid macules progress to erythematous, purpuric cutaneous and mucosal plaques
    - Late lesions with central epidermal necrosis or blister
  + Apoptotic keratinocytes
  + Full thickness epidermal necrosis and subepidermal bullae
* [Small vessel cutaneous vasculitis](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/skinnontumorvasculitis.html) (also known as neutrophilic / leukocytoclastic vasculitis):
  + Prominent leukocytoclasia
  + Variable levels of perivascular fibrin deposits
  + May lack occlusive luminal thrombi
  + Lack of intraepidermal pustules / epidermal necrosis
* References: [Am J Dermatopathol 2017;39:637](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/27759698), [Actas Dermosifiliogr (Engl Ed) 2021;112:1](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/33045208)

A 76 year old man with acute myeloid leukemia presents to the hospital with a high fever and a necrotic skin ulcer on the right thigh 10 days after an allogeneic bone marrow transplant. A skin punch biopsy is shown. What is the diagnosis?

1. Aspergillosis
2. Calciphylaxis
3. Ecthyma gangrenosum
4. Purpura fulminans
5. Small vessel cutaneous vasculitis

Board review style answer #1

**C**. Ecthyma gangrenosum is a necrotizing, ulcerative disease most frequently caused by the invasion of dermal vessels with microorganisms in immunocompromised patients with bacteremia. This condition is associated with a high rate of mortality. The slides above show a light blue haze of basophilic granular material around dermal vessels, which is suggestive of bacteria infiltration. There is minimal inflammatory infiltrate. Answer B is incorrect because calciphylaxis would show fibrointimal hyperplasia with perivascular calcium deposition; ulcers are also chronic. Answer E is incorrect because small vessel vasculitis would feature leukocytoclasia with nuclear debris and prominent perivascular inflammation. Answer A is incorrect because aspergillosis would demonstrate hyphae around vessels and throughout the dermis. Answer D is incorrect because purpura fulminans demonstrates intravascular, fibrin rich thrombi without perivascular haze or inflammation.

Board review style question #2

Which of the following is true regarding ecthyma gangrenosum?

1. Microscopically features a perivascular infiltrate with eosinophils
2. Microscopically features a perivascular red haze
3. Microscopically features platelet rich thrombi
4. Most cases of ecthyma gangrenosum are caused by *Pseudomonas aeruginosa* bacteremia
5. Stains perivascularly with von Kossa

Board review style answer #2

**D**. Most cases of ecthyma gangrenosum are caused by *Pseudomonas aeruginosa* bacteremia. Ecthyma gangrenosum is most often caused by bacterial invasion of cutaneous dermal vessels by *Pseudomonas aeruginosa* in immunocompromised patients with bacteremia. Answer B is incorrect because histologically, basophilic granular material within or around the affected vessels presents as a blue haze encircling the vessels (not red). Answers A, C and E are incorrect because ecthyma gangrenosum does not have calcium deposits as in calciphylaxis, eosinophils as in disseminated strongyloidiasis or platelet rich thrombi as in platelet plugging disorders.
