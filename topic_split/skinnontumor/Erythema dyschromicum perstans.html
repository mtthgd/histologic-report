Definition / general
  • Erythema dyschromicum perstans (EDP) is an acquired macular hyperpigmentation disorder (Indian Dermatol Online J 2019;10:639)
Essential features
  • Gray or blue-brown macules or patches on physical examination (Int J Womens Dermatol 2018;4:216)
  • Microscopy shows epidermal basal vacuolization, papillary dermal melanophages and mild to moderate dermal lymphocytic infiltration on histopathological examination (Indian Dermatol Online J 2019;10:639)
  • Progress, asymptomatic macules and patches on the trunk, neck and proximal upper extremities (Dermatol Online J 2019;25:13030)
Terminology
  • Ashy dermatosis
  • Cinderella dermatosis
ICD coding
  • ICD-10: L81.4 - other melanin hyperpigmentation
  • ICD-11: ED60.Y - hypermelanosis of other specified aetiology
Epidemiology
  • Worldwide but most common in Latin America and Asia and India (Int J Dermatol 2004;43:230, Indian Dermatol Online J 2021;12:663)
  • F > M (Int J Dermatol 2004;43:230)
  • First to third decade (Indian Dermatol Online J 2013;4:30)
Sites
  • Trunk
  • Neck
  • Proximal of upper extremities (Dermatol Online J 2019;25:13030)
Pathophysiology
  • Unknown (Dermatol Online J 2019;25:13030)
Etiology
  • Unknown (JAAD Case Rep 2020;6:797)
  • Cell mediated immunity to ingested agent or contact (JAAD Case Rep 2020;6:797)
  • Drug (penicillin, benzodiazepines, omeprazole)
  • HIV and HCV (as potential etiological factors) (Acta Derm Venereol 2000;80:78)
  • Distinguishing features
    • Most common areas of involvement in EDP are the face / neck, back and abdomen while a large proportion of lichen planus pigmentosus (LPP) patients have lesions on the face / neck, flexors and upper extremities
    • In addition, there is a slightly higher predilection for sun exposed areas in LPP (Biomed Res Int 2019;2019:5829185)
    • Considerable similarity between LPP and EDP in histopathology (Indian J Dermatol 2016;61:482)
Clinical features
  • Ash (blue, brown or gray) colored macules and patches on the trunk, neck and proximal upper extremities, which are progressive and asymptomatic (Dermatol Online J 2019;25:13030)
Diagnosis
  • Clinical and histopathological findings
  • Dermoscopic evaluation (Indian Dermatol Online J 2019;10:639)
    • Main dermoscopic findings: brown to gray dots and globules
    • Other dermoscopy findings: brown and pinkish-brown background and different patterns of vessels (Indian Dermatol Online J 2019;10:639)
Case reports
  • 8 year old girl with areas of ashy pigmentation distributed symmetrically (Indian Dermatol Online J 2013;4:30)
  • 17 year old boy with widespread, symmetric, brown-gray, ovoid macules and patches on his lower back and superior buttocks (Int J Womens Dermatol 2018;4:216)
  • 27 year old man with progressive cutaneous pigmentary changes on his left back, flank and abdomen (Ann Dermatol 2009;21:432)
  • 38 year old HIV+ man with asymptomatic skin eruption on the buttocks and sacral region (Acta Derm Venereol 2000;80:78)
  • 57 year old woman with asymptomatic gray-blue macules on the trunk and oral mucosa (An Bras Dermatol 2017;92:17)
  • 75 year old woman with increasing number of asymptomatic cutaneous lesions on her trunk (Australas J Dermatol 2019;60:e232)
Treatment
  • No definite effective treatment (Acta Derm Venereol 2000;80:78)
  • Antibiotics (Acta Derm Venereol 2000;80:78)
  • Isoniazid (Acta Derm Venereol 2000;80:78)
  • Griseofulvin (Acta Derm Venereol 2000;80:78)
  • Oral corticosteroids (Int J Womens Dermatol 2018;4:216)
  • Dapsone and clofazimine (Dermatol Online J 2019;25:13030)
  • UV light (Dermatol Online J 2019;25:13030)
  • Isotretinoin (JAMA Dermatol 2016;152:841)
  • Laser therapy (Lasers Surg Med 2017;49:60)

Microscopic (histologic) description
  • Patchy basal layer damage with superficial infiltrate of lymphocytes (Indian Dermatol Online J 2019;10:639)
  • Occasional Civatte and colloid bodies (Arch Dermatol 1981;117:701)
  • Prominent pigment incontinence (deeper in dermis than postinflammatory hyperpigmentation) (J Dermatol 2015;42:1053)
  • Perivascular lymphocytes (Arch Dermatol 1981;117:701)

Sample pathology report
  • Skin of trunk, biopsy:
    • Erythema dyschromicum perstans (ashy dermatosis) (see comment)
    • Comment: In the appropriate clinical setting, the prominent superficial melanin pigment incontinence is in favor of this diagnosis.
Differential diagnosis
  • Melasma (Dermatol Reports 2011;3:e46):
    • Mild lymphocytic infiltration
    • Increase in epidermal melanin but no increase in number of melanocytes
    • History of increase to sun exposure; variation in internal estrogen hormone profile and age related changes, clinical presentation on face is more in favor of melasma (J Cutan Aesthet Surg 2024;17:112)
  • Lichen planus pigmentosus (LPP) (Dermatol Reports 2011;3:e46):
    • Melanophages and vacuolization of the basement membrane zone
    • Distinguishing features
      • Hypergranulosis is usually present in LPP but is absent in ashy dermatosis
      • Most common areas of involvement in EDP are the face / neck, back and abdomen while a large proportion of LPP patients have lesions on the face / neck, flexors and upper extremities
      • In addition, there is a slightly higher predilection for sun exposed areas in LPP (Biomed Res Int 2019;2019:5829185)
      • Considerable similarity between LPP and EDP in histopathology (Indian J Dermatol 2016;61:482)
  • Various disorders with postinflammatory hyperpigmentation (Dermatol Online J 2019;25:13030):
    • Epidermal melanosis
    • Pigment incontinence
    • Increase pigment of skin following a previous inflammatory rash; increase or normal amount of melanin at the basal layer; often with prominent melanin incontinence
    • Post inflammatory hyperpigmentation differs in the history; clinical presentation; dermoscopy feature and histology finding (Dermatol Online J 2019;25:13030)
  • Minocycline pigmentation (Indian J Dermatol Venereol Leprol 2013;79:367):
    • Seen as perivascular granular brown pigment
    • Stains positive with Perls and Fontana-Masson
  • Argyria (Indian J Dermatol Venereol Leprol 2013;79:367):
    • Due to prolonged contact with silver salt or silver jewelry or from penetration of acupuncture needle
    • Does not accentuate on wood lamp examination
    • Histopathology
      • Brown-black granules deposited in a band-like fashion along the basement membrane of the eccrine gland; occasionally similar granules may be seen in the papillary dermis, around pilosebaceous unit, blood vessel wall and arrector pili muscle
  • Ochronosis (Indian J Dermatol Venereol Leprol 2013;79:367):
    • Exogenous ochronosis presents as a blue-black discoloration secondary to prolonged use of skin bleaching creams containing hydroquinone
    • Endogenous ochronosis (alkaptonuria) is an autosomal recessive disorder
    • Histopathology of both
      • Early lesion: swollen and basophilic collagen fiber
      • Older lesion: yellow-brown ochronotic pigment appearing as banana shaped bodies in the dermis
      • Pigment granule in epithelium and basement membrane of sweat gland
      • Melanophages may be observed in upper dermis
  • Pigmentary stage of incontinentia pigmenti:
    • Many melanophages in the upper dermis
    • Almost exclusively in females (Neurol Clin 1987;5:361)
    • Hyperpigmentation along Blaschko lines (Indian Dermatol Online J 2020;12:371)
    • Initial lesions usually present at birth (BMJ Case Rep 2011;2011:bcr0120113708)
    • Association with abnormalities of CNS, eyes, hair, nails and teeth (An Bras Dermatol 2014;89:26)
  • Lichenoid drug reaction:
    • Lichenoid interface infiltrate with basal cell squamatization and Civatte bodies, eosinophils possible
  • Macular mastocytosis:
    • Mast cells and edema are seen in the dermis
  • Lichen planus:
    • Hyperkeratosis without parakeratosis
    • Hypergranulosis
    • Dense superficial dermal band of lymphocytes causing alteration along the dermoepidermal junction
Additional references
  • Patterson: Weedon's Skin Pathology, 5th Edition, 2020, Kerl: Diagnostic Cutaneous Pathology, 2017, Bolognia: Dermatology, 5th Edition, 2024
Board review style question #1


A 25 year old man presented with asymptomatic, gray colored patches on his trunk and arms. What is the most probable diagnosis considering the histopathology shown?

  1. Erythema dyschromicum perstans
  2. Fixed drug eruption
  3. Lichen planus
  4. Urticaria pigmentosa
Board review style answer #1
A. Erythema dyschromicum perstans (also known as ashy dermatosis) presents with asymptomatic gray or blue colored patches on the body and proximal limbs. Answer B is incorrect because fixed drug eruption is mainly solitary on the lip, hand or genitalia with histopathology showing marked basal layer damage with dermal eosinophils. Answer C is incorrect because lichen planus mainly presents with violaceus pruritic papules and plaques. On histopathology, there is a lichenoid infiltrate. Answer D is incorrect because urticaria pigmentosa shows perivascular or diffuse dermal mast cells often with eosinophils. Dermal edema or subepidermal blister formation sometimes is present.

Board review style question #2
Which of the following factors has the most probable role in the etiopathogenesis of erythema dyschromicum perstans?

  1. Cell mediated immunity to an ingested agent
  2. Hepatitis A infection
  3. Humoral immunity
  4. Sun exposure
Board review style answer #2
A. Cell mediated immunity to ingested agent. Cell mediated immunity has a known role in the disease etiology of erythema dyschromicum perstans. Answer C is incorrect because there is no known role for humoral immunity in this condition. Answer B is incorrect because although HIV has a possible role, hepatitis A virus does not. Answer D is incorrect because there is no known role for sun exposure and the patches are mainly in sun protected areas.
