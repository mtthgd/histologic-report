Definition / general

* Interface dermatitis is a tissue reaction pattern characterized by the destruction of basal keratinocytes, which appears in the form of cell death or vacuolar change

Essential features

* Interface dermatitis is an inflammatory tissue reaction pattern characterized by lymphocyte mediated damage to epidermal basal keratinocytes, which manifests as cell death or vacuolar change
* Dyskeratotic keratinocytes or intracytoplasmic vacuoles are present in the basal epidermis
* Key features to narrow the differential diagnosis for interface dermatoses
  + Type of basal cell damage
    - Vacuolar (cell poor): erythema multiforme (EM) (prototypical example of each pattern), Stevens-Johnson syndrome / toxic epidermal necrolysis (SJS / TEN), pityriasis lichenoides, acute graft versus host reaction, fixed drug eruption
      * Lupus erythematosus and dermatomyositis are also important examples of vacuolar interface change but are topics more thoroughly discussed elsewhere
    - Lichenoid (cell rich): lichen planus (prototypical example of each pattern), lichenoid drug eruption, lichen planus-like keratosis, lichen striatus, lichen nitidus
  + Distribution of the dermal inflammatory infiltrate
    - Superficial only: erythema multiforme, SJS / TEN, lichen planus, lichenoid keratosis, lichen nitidus
    - Superficial to deep: drug associated eruptions (lichenoid drug eruption, fixed drug), pityriasis lichenoides, graft versus host reaction, lichen striatus
  + Prominent pigment incontinence
    - Lichen planus, lichenoid reactions in darker pigmented patients, some drug associated eruptions
  + Satellite cell necrosis (lymphocyte assisted apoptosis)
    - Erythema multiforme, acute graft versus host reaction, some drug associated eruptions

Terminology

* Lichenoid reaction pattern
* Lichenoid tissue reaction
* Lichenoid interface dermatitis
* Vacuolar interface dermatitis
* Vacuolar change

Epidemiology

**Vacuolar**

* Lymphocyte predominant dermal inflammatory infiltrate
  + Erythema multiforme
    - Predilection for young men; median age of 24 years ([Arch Dermatol 2002;138:1019](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/12164739))
  + Stevens-Johnson syndrome / toxic epidermal necrolysis
    - M:F = 1:1.5 ([Medicina (Kaunas) 2021;57:895](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/34577817))
    - Predilection for older individuals; median age of 45 years ([Arch Dermatol 2002;138:1019](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/12164739))
  + Pityriasis lichenoides
    - Predilection for young men, 10 - 30 years old
    - Children also
  + Graft versus host disease (GVHD)
    - Patients who have received allogenic hematopoietic stem cell transplantation (HSCT) or rarely solid organ transplantation or blood transfusion ([Transfus Apher Sci 2022;61:103402](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/35288056))
    - Acute cutaneous GVHD occurs in 30 - 50% of patients within 100 days after allogenic HSCT ([Bone Marrow Transplant 1998;22:755](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/9827972))
    - Chronic GVHD develops in 30 - 40% of all long lived HSCT survivors ([Blood 2015;125:606](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/25398933))
    - Most (77%) cases of chronic GVHD have a history of acute GVHD, with an increasing likelihood with an increasing histologic grade of acute GVHD ([Bone Marrow Transplant 1998;22:755](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/9827972))
    - Risk of GVHD is increased in human leukocyte antigen (HLA) disparity, peripheral blood and bone marrow grafts, donor - recipient sex mismatch and donors who have been previously pregnant or transfused as well as underlying disease and older age of recipient ([Br J Haematol 1987;67:397](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/3322360), [J Clin Oncol 2008;26:5728](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/18981462))
* Eosinophilic component in the dermal inflammatory infiltrate
  + Fixed drug eruption (FDE)
    - M = F
    - Patients taking antibiotics or other drugs (see [Diagrams / tables](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/skinnontumorinterfacedermatitis.html#interfacedermatitisdiagrams))
    - Predilection for ages 20 - 40 years old
    - Generalized bullous FDE occurs

**Lichenoid**

* Lymphocyte predominant dermal inflammatory infiltrate
  + Lichen planus (LP)
    - M = F, though oral LP and lichen planopilaris are more common in female patients ([Cutis 2003;72:63](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/12889717))
    - Age of onset is typically 30 - 50 years old ([J Am Acad Dermatol 2019;81:1397](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/31004729))
  + Lichenoid drug eruption
    - Adults, ~50 years old ([Indian J Dermatol 2024;69:137](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/38841229))
    - Patients taking antihypertensives or other photosensitizing drugs (see [Diagrams / tables](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/skinnontumorinterfacedermatitis.html#interfacedermatitisdiagrams))
  + Lichen striatus
    - M Children, ~5 months - 15 years old ([Pediatr Dermatol 2004;21:197](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/15165195))
  + Lichen planus-like keratosis
    - M:F = 1:2
    - Adults, wide age range of 30 - 80 years old
    - More common in White patients
* Eosinophilic component in the dermal inflammatory infiltrate
  + Lichenoid drug eruption
    - Adults, ~50 years old ([Indian J Dermatol 2024;69:137](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/38841229))
    - Patients taking antihypertensives or other photosensitizing drugs (see [Diagrams / tables](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/skinnontumorinterfacedermatitis.html#interfacedermatitisdiagrams))
* Histiocytic component in the dermal inflammatory infiltrate
  + Lichen nitidus
    - M > F
    - Childhood, mean age of 9 years ([Pediatr Dermatol 2019;36:189](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/30746752))

Sites

**Vacuolar**

* Erythema multiforme
  + Mucous membranes involved in 2 - 10% of individuals ([StatPearls: Erythema Multiforme [Accessed 3 February 2025]](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/29261983))
  + Skin, face and symmetrically distributed lesions on the hands and feet
* Stevens-Johnson syndrome / toxic epidermal necrolysis
  + Systemic symptoms are typically present in TEN and SJS / TEN overlap and usually present in SJS
    - Prodrome of fever, malaise, sore throat and cough ([Adv Wound Care (New Rochelle) 2020;9:426](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/32520664))
  + Multiple mucous membranes often involved (oral, ocular, genital)
  + Skin of the face and trunk most common
    - Body surface area involvement determines classification ([Arch Dermatol 1993;129:92](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/8420497))
      * SJS: SJS / TEN: 10 - 30%
      * TEN: > 30%
* Pityriasis lichenoides
  + Skin of the anterior trunk, flexor surfaces of the proximal extremities
* Graft versus host disease
  + Skin (epidermis to deep fascia), gastrointestinal tract, liver, oral mucosa, lung, kidney
  + Acute: skin of face, trunk, extremities, acral
  + Chronic: skin of oral mucosa, trunk, anogenital, acral, eyes (keratitis sicca)
* Fixed drug eruption
  + Recurs at the same site of mucous membranes and skin rash with repeated drug intake
  + Can occur anywhere but sites of predilection include the lip, sacral region and genitalia ([Arch Dermatol 1984;120:520](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/6231004))
  + Palmar and plantar skin can also be involved ([Arch Dermatol 1984;120:520](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/6231004))

**Lichenoid**

* Lichen planus
  + Mucous membranes, oral and genital
  + Skin, nails, flexor surfaces of the wrist, trunk, thighs, ankles
  + Hypertrophic LP is usually confined to the shins
  + Terminal hairs of the scalp are the main site of involvement in lichen planopilaris ([Int J Womens Dermatol 2018;4:180](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/30175224))
  + Palms and soles (palmoplantar LP) ([Br J Dermatol 2000;142:310](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/10730766))
  + Esophageal involvement may occur ([World J Gastroenterol 2022;28:5893](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/36405107))
* Lichenoid drug eruption
  + Mucous membranes, oral and genital
  + Skin, photo distributed sites (face, forearms) or generalized
* Lichen striatus
  + Skin, along the lines of Blaschko
  + Usually involves the length of 1 extremity ([J Cutan Pathol 1995;22:18](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/7751473))
  + Nail changes may manifest as longitudinal fissures ([Int J Dermatol 2015;54:1255](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/26474189))
* Lichen planus-like keratosis
  + Skin, chest, back
* Lichen nitidus
  + Skin, upper extremities, genitalia, abdomen, chest
  + Generalized lichen nitidus involves the entire body ([JAMA Dermatol 2018;154:367](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/29344609))
  + Nail changes occur

Pathophysiology

* Basal cell damage in interface dermatitis is primarily mediated by apoptotic mechanisms, more so than epidermal necrosis ([Am J Dermatopathol 1979;1:133](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/44963), [Adv Dermatol 1990;5:243](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/2204375))
* Apoptosis of the basal keratinocytes occurs by an active budding process that involves single cells condensing and fragmenting into small bodies (Civatte / colloid bodies)
* Colloid bodies in the papillary dermis appear to trap immunoglobulins nonspecifically, particularly IgM ([Am J Dermatopathol 2002;24:130](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/11979072))
* Cytotoxic T lymphocyte attack on the basal epidermal keratinocytes is thought to be mediated by increased ICAM1 (intracellular adhesion molecule 1) expression on basal keratinocytes, secondary to cytokine (TNFα and interferon γ [IFNγ]) release by the mononuclear inflammatory infiltrate in interface dermatitis (subacute cutaneous lupus erythematosus [SCLE], erythema multiforme and lichen planus) ([J Invest Dermatol 1995;105:71S](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/7616001))
* Increased IKKB (a subunit of the IKB kinase complex required for NFκB activation) activity resulted in interface dermatitis in transgenic mice, a finding dependent on inflammatory cytokines, macrophages and other CD45+ infiltrating cells in the skin and independent of T and B lymphocytes ([J Invest Dermatol 2010;130:1598](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/20200541))
* Type 1 IFN system is involved in cutaneous autoimmune diseases characterized by interface dermatitis (cutaneous lupus erythematosus, lichen planus) ([J Invest Dermatol 2008;128:2392](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/18418411))
* IFNγ and FasL / Fas exposure induces the histologic hallmarks of acute cutaneous GVHD in a reconstructed human epidermis model ([Am J Dermatopathol 2011;33:244](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/21430507))

Etiology

**Vacuolar**

* Erythema multiforme
  + Associated with recent or recurrent herpes simplex infection elsewhere in the body (no viral infection of the EM lesions themselves) as well as *Mycoplasma pneumoniae*, Epstein-Barr virus (EBV) ([Arch Dermatol 2002;138:1019](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/12164739))
  + Infections are the most common trigger for EM ([Am Fam Physician 2019;100:82](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/31305041))
  + See [Diagrams / tables](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/skinnontumorinterfacedermatitis.html#interfacedermatitisdiagrams) for list of some associations / causative triggers for EM
* Stevens-Johnson syndrome / toxic epidermal necrolysis
  + Associated with medications; anti-epileptic drugs, antibiotics and NSAIDs
  + See [Diagrams / tables](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/skinnontumorinterfacedermatitis.html#interfacedermatitisdiagrams) for list of drugs causing SJS / TEN
  + Those most at risk include those with hematologic malignancies, central nervous system cancer and infections with human immunodeficiency virus (HIV), tuberculosis and mycoplasma but no HSV association was found ([J Invest Dermatol 2016;136:1387](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/27039263))
* Pityriasis lichenoides
  + Is a spectrum of disease, with an acute form (pityriasis lichenoides et varioliformis acuta [PLEVA]) and a chronic form (pityriasis lichenoides chronica [PLC])
  + Lesions of both PLEVA and PLC can coexist on the same patient
    - PLEVA has been referred to as Mucha-Habermann disease but recently this terminology applies to the febrile ulceronecrotic subtype of PLEVA only ([J Am Acad Dermatol 2006;55:557](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/17010734))
      * Why some progress to the febrile ulceronecrotic Mucha-Habermann disease (FUMHD) variant is unknown
  + Associated with HIV1, varicella-zoster virus, as well as various medications and immunizations ([Int J Dermatol 1997;36:104](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/9109005), [Clin Exp Dermatol 2018;43:703](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/29797478))
  + May progress to mycosis fungoides or another lymphoma ([Br J Dermatol 2005;152:1327](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/15949002))
  + Has clinicopathologic overlap with lymphomatoid papulosis ([Br J Dermatol 2005;152:1327](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/15949002))
* Graft versus host disease
  + Acute
    - Interaction of donor T lymphocytes with host major histocompatibility complex (MHC) or host minor histocompatibility antigen (miH) ([N Engl J Med 2017;377:2167](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/29171820))
    - Mismatches in MHC class or minor histocompatibility antigens are associated with increased risk of severe acute GVHD
    - Th2 predominant cytokines / chemokines and increase in IL22 producing CD4+ T cells, with an elevated thymic stromal lymphopoietin (a cytokine inducing Th2 differentiation) at day 20 - 30 post-HSCT in the skin of patients who later developed acute GVHD ([Blood 2014;123:290](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/24255916))
  + Late onset acute GVHD
    - Mean levels of epidermal growth factor are 3 fold higher than in classic acute GVHD and not increased in chronic GVHD ([Blood 2016;128:2350](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/27625357))
  + Chronic
    - Chronic lichenoid has mixed Th1 / Th17 with upregulated IFNγ and IL17 producing CD8+ T cells ([Blood 2014;123:290](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/24255916))
* Fixed drug eruption
  + Occurs anytime from same day to 45 days after causative drug intake ([Indian J Dermatol 2018;63:332](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/30078879))
  + See [Diagrams / tables](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/skinnontumorinterfacedermatitis.html#interfacedermatitisdiagrams) for list of drugs causing fixed drug eruption

**Lichenoid**

* Lichen planus
  + Prototypical lichenoid interface dermatitis reaction pattern
  + Associated with autoimmune disorders such as primary biliary cholangitis, autoimmune thyroiditis, hypothyroidism, ulcerative colitis and alopecia areata ([J Am Acad Dermatol 1983;9:540](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/6355216), [Int J Trichology 2015;7:87](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/26180460), [J Am Acad Dermatol 2014;70:889](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/24569115), [Arch Dermatol 1991;127:688](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/2024987))
  + Also associated with hepatitis C infection and dyslipidemia ([Br J Dermatol 1996;134:715](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/8733378), [Int J Dermatol 2016;55:e295](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/26873870))
* Lichenoid drug eruption
  + Occurs 15 days - 6 months after causative drug intake ([Indian J Dermatol 2024;69:137](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/38841229))
  + See [Diagrams / tables](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/skinnontumorinterfacedermatitis.html#interfacedermatitisdiagrams) for list of drugs producing a lichenoid reaction pattern
* Lichen striatus
  + Unknown etiology
  + Reported after viral infections, certain vaccinations
  + Associated with atopic disease ([Pediatr Dermatol 2004;21:197](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/15165195))
* Lichen planus-like keratosis
  + Usually represents the attempted cell mediated immune rejection of a variety of epidermal lesions (solar lentigo, seborrheic keratosis) ([Am J Surg Pathol 1993;17:259](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/8434706))
* Lichen nitidus
  + Unknown etiology
  + Associated with Down syndrome and Russell-Silver syndrome ([Indian J Dermatol Venereol Leprol 2009;75:627](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/19915257), [Pediatr Dermatol 2013;30:150](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/22329413))

Clinical features

**Vacuolar**

* Erythema multiforme
  + Pleomorphic eruption characterized by a few to numerous urticarial plaques
  + Erythematous, raised bullseye or targetoid lesions
    - Lesions have a predilection for the extremities and the face
    - Unlikely to involve multiple mucous membranes, such as oral, ocular or genital
  + Demonstrates a high rate of recurrence, a distinguishing feature from SJS / TEN ([Arch Dermatol 2002;138:1019](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/12164739))
* Stevens-Johnson syndrome / toxic epidermal necrolysis
  + Ill defined erythematous macules with purpuric centers, with minimal (SJS) to widespread (TEN) coalescing of individual lesions
  + Hemorrhagic erosions of the mucous membranes
  + Positive Nikolsky sign with blistering and detachment of epidermis with light pressure ([Adv Wound Care (New Rochelle) 2020;9:426](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/32520664))
  + Classically mucocutaneous tenderness where the skin pain is out of proportion with the clinical appearance ([Open Access Maced J Med Sci 2018;6:730](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/29731949))
* Pityriasis lichenoides
  + Acute presentation is a sudden onset of numerous erythematous 3 - 15 mm papulovesicles and papules with hemorrhagic and necrotic crusts that resolve to leave varioliform scars ([Cureus 2020;12:e8725](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/32699720))
  + Waxing and waning course over months to years
  + Can progress to febrile ulceronecrotic Mucha-Habermann disease
    - Systemic involvement along with cutaneous lesions
    - Necrotic papules to large ulcers with necrotic crusts, hemorrhagic bullae and pustules
    - Systemic symptoms include high fever, lymphadenopathy, arthritis or bacteremia, with mucosal, gastrointestinal and pulmonary involvement ([Indian J Dermatol 2017;62:675](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/29263553))
* Graft versus host disease
  + Cutaneous and systemic manifestations (hepatic dysfunction, gastrointestinal symptoms)
  + Acute cutaneous
    - Develops within 100 days posttransplant
    - Erythematous maculopapular rash
    - With progression, there can be bullae and epidermal necrosis
    - Severe cases resemble toxic epidermal necrolysis
    - Late acute GVHD may arise > 100 days posttransplant, after the cessation or tapering of immunosuppressive drugs ([Yeung: Pathology of Graft vs. Host Disease, 1st Edition, 2019](https://www.amazon.com/exec/obidos/ASIN/3319420984/pathologyoutl-20))
  + Chronic cutaneous
    - Develops > 100 days posttransplant
    - Early lichenoid phase mimics lichen planus clinically and histologically, including Wickham striae of oral mucosa
    - Late chronic change clinically and histologically mimics lichen sclerosus, morphea or systemic sclerosis ([Yeung: Pathology of Graft vs. Host Disease, 1st Edition, 2019](https://www.amazon.com/exec/obidos/ASIN/3319420984/pathologyoutl-20))
    - Scaly, erythematous hyperkeratotic papules
  + Fixed drug eruption
    - Oral ingestion of certain drugs is associated with a recurrent reddish-brown patch at the same location, normally presents as localized FDE
    - Vesicles and bullae can rupture to leave superficial ulcers
    - Blistering and erosions involving > 10% of body surface area and at least 3 of 6 different sites (head and neck, anterior trunk, back, upper extremities, lower extremities, genitalia) warrants a diagnosis as generalized bullous fixed drug eruption ([J Am Acad Dermatol 2014;70:539](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/24388722))
  **Lichenoid**
  + Lichen planus
    - Multiple purple, pruritic, polygonal, planar, papules and plaques (6 Ps)
    - Frequently bilateral and symmetric
    - Wickham striae, a network of fine white lines seen on the surface of lesions
    - May exhibit the Koebner phenomenon, where new skin lesions appear on previously unaffected skin, secondary to trauma
    - Hypertrophic variant clinically may appear verrucous ([Dermatol Reports 2014;6:5113](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/25386324))
    - Many variants of LP: atrophic, hypertrophic, annual, linear, ulcerative (erosive), oral, erythema dyschromicum perstans (lichen planus pigmentosus), actinicus, planopilaris, pemphigoides, bullous, keratosis lichenoides chronica and lupus erythematosus - lichen planus overlap syndrome
  + Lichenoid drug eruption
    - Eruption can mimic lichen planus, though it can have marked desquamation ([Eur J Case Rep Intern Med 2020;7:002103](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/33457371))
    - Pronounced postinflammatory hyperpigmentation
    - After (ranges from 1 month to a year or more) oral intake of a wide range of possible drugs (see [Diagrams / tables](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/skinnontumorinterfacedermatitis.html#interfacedermatitisdiagrams))
  + Lichen striatus
    - Discrete flesh colored small papules that eventually coalesce to form an erythematous band-like, linear papular eruption along Blaschko lines, usually unilateral
  + Lichen planus-like keratosis
    - Solitary, discrete, violaceous purpura of sudden onset and short duration, mildly pruritic
    - 3 mm - 1.8 cm in diameter ([J Cutan Pathol 2002;29:291](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/12100630))
    - Clinically, may mimic basal cell carcinoma or squamous cell carcinoma in situ
  + Lichen nitidus
    - Multiple, round, 1 - 3 mm, flesh colored papules
    - Usually asymptomatic

Prognostic factors

**Vacuolar**

* Erythema multiforme
  + Self limiting disease, spontaneous remission within a few weeks ([Arch Dermatol 1996;132:711](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/8651727))
  + Recurrent EM, in contrast, occurs with a mean frequency of 6 attacks/year (range: 2 - 24), with a mean duration of 9.5 years (range: 2 - 36) ([Br J Dermatol 1993;128:542](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/8504046))
  + Recalcitrant cases of recurrent EM were associated with severe oral mucosal involvement, the inability to identify a specific cause, a lack of improvement with continuous antiviral therapy, near continuous corticosteroid therapy for at least 1 year, hospitalization and immunosuppressive therapy with 2 or more agents ([J Am Acad Dermatol 2010;62:45](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/19665257))
* Stevens-Johnson syndrome / toxic epidermal necrolysis
  + Life threatening disease
  + Higher mortality in Medicaid and Medicare insurance patients as well as those > 60 years of age with non-Hodgkin lymphoma, leukemia, septicemia, tuberculosis, pneumonia and renal failure ([J Invest Dermatol 2016;136:1387](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/27039263))
  + SJS: 4.8% mortality
  + SJS / TEN: 19.4% mortality
  + TEN: 14.8% mortality ([J Invest Dermatol 2016;136:1387](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/27039263))
* Pityriasis lichenoides
  + Self limiting disease, duration between months and several years
  + Resolution leaves varioliform or postinflammatory hyper or hypopigmented scars ([J Eur Acad Dermatol Venereol 2019;33:2039](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/31318465))
  + Life threatening in the febrile ulceronecrotic Mucha-Habermann disease variant
    - 15% mortality rate ([Indian J Dermatol 2017;62:675](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/29263553))
* Graft versus host disease
  + Worse prognosis is associated with late acute GVHD, overlap syndrome (concurrent acute and chronic GVHD) and multiple organ systems involved (GI, liver, pulmonary, etc.) ([Yeung: Pathology of Graft vs. Host Disease, 1st Edition, 2019](https://www.amazon.com/exec/obidos/ASIN/3319420984/pathologyoutl-20))
  + Numerous clinical staging systems to help in risk stratification (International Bone Marrow Transplant Registry [IBMTR]) and Glucksberg grade ([Br J Haematol 1997;97:855](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/9217189))
* Fixed drug eruption
  + Repeat ingestion of the same drug can cause an increase in number and size of lesions ([Int J Dermatol 1998;37:833](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/9865869))
  + Generalized bullous FDE may have clinicopathologic overlap with SJS/TEN; one study reported a mortality rate of 22% in these cases ([Br J Dermatol 2013;168:726](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/23413807))
  + Localized FDE has a spontaneous resolution, with residual postinflammatory pigmentary alteration ([Am J Clin Dermatol 2020;21:393](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/32002848))

**Lichenoid**

* Lichen planus
  + Cutaneous lichen planus is usually a self limiting disorder
  + Palm and sole lesions (palmoplantar LP) are especially painful and therapy resistant
  + Oral lichen planus
    - Malignant transformation is more likely in smokers, alcoholics and hepatitis C virus (HCV) infected patients ([Oral Oncol 2017;68:92](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/28438300))
  + Hypertrophic lichen planus ([Int J Dermatol 2016;55:e295](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/26873870))
    - Squamous cell carcinoma may arise from these lesions ([Dermatol Surg 2015;41:1411](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/26551772))
* Lichenoid drug eruption
  + Usually, complete resolution weeks to months after discontinuing the offending drug
  + Patients who require treatment are those who cannot discontinue the drug, those with intractable symptoms, extensive generalized skin involvement and persistence of the lesions several months after discontinuing the offending agent ([J Eur Acad Dermatol Venereol 2023;37:965](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/36652271))
* Lichen striatus
  + Spontaneous regression, mean duration of 6 months ([Pediatr Dermatol 2004;21:197](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/15165195))
* Lichen planus-like keratosis
  + Benign, spontaneous resolution or frozen off
* Lichen nitidus
  + Spontaneous remission
  + Palmar lichen nitidus can persist or be refractory to usual treatments ([Indian J Dermatol 2015;60:308](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/26120166))

Treatment

**Vacuolar**

* Erythema multiforme
  + Symptomatic treatment is the key ([Br J Dermatol 2018;179:1009](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/29957881))
  + Topical corticosteroids
  + Medicated mouthwash for oral involvement
  + Extensive oral involvement may require systemic glucocorticoids but it is unknown if this changes the outcome ([Br J Dermatol 2018;179:1009](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/29957881))
* Stevens-Johnson syndrome / toxic epidermal necrolysis
  + Multidisciplinary care in a specialized burn unit
  + Withdrawal of the culprit drug
  + Systemic treatments (systemic corticosteroids, intravenous immunoglobulin [IVIg], cyclosporine) may or may not be beneficial ([Biomedicines 2022;10:2105](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/36140207))
* Pityriasis lichenoides
  + Treatments include phototherapy, erythromycin, topical corticosteroids, low dose methotrexate ([J Eur Acad Dermatol Venereol 2019;33:2039](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/31318465))
  + Febrile ulceronecrotic variant (FUMHD)
    - IV methotrexate use has been reported to be effective ([J Am Acad Dermatol 1994;30:261](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/8288786))
* Graft versus host disease
  + Prophylaxis
    - Standard is calcineurin inhibitors (cyclosporine, tacrolimus) and methotrexate
    - Antithymocyte globulin (ATG) given before hematopoietic cell transplantation significantly reduces the risk of grade 3 or 4 acute GVHD
    - Other immunosuppressants may be added (mycophenolate mofetil [MMF], sirolimus)
    - Extracorporeal photopheresis ([Transfus Med Hemother 2020;47:214](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/32595426))
  + Acute
    - Topical corticosteroids if only skin involvement ([Nat Rev Dis Primers 2023;9:27](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/37291149))
    - Systemic treatment for grade 2 - 4 GVHD, which includes calcineurin inhibitors plus methylprednisolone ([Int J Hematol 2015;101:452](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/25864189))
    - Secondary therapy includes Janus associated kinase inhibitor ruxolitinib ([PLoS One 2022;17:e0271979](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/35905125))
    - ATG, sirolimus, etc. ([Bone Marrow Transplant 2017;52:438](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/27819684))
  + Chronic
    - Methylprednisolone / prednisolone and calcineurin inhibitor ([Best Pract Res Clin Haematol 2008;21:271](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/18503992))
* Fixed drug eruption
  + Discontinuation of offending drug
  + Medium to high potency topical corticosteroids, systemic antihistamines ([Am J Clin Dermatol 2020;21:393](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/32002848))
  + Generalized bullous FDE requires systemic treatments, similar to SJS / TEN, such as systemic corticosteroids ([Br J Dermatol 2013;168:726](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/23413807))

**Lichenoid**

* Lichen planus
  + First line therapy
    - Topical corticosteroid (high or super high potency for extremities / trunk, low or mid potency for intertriginous / facial skin) ([N Engl J Med 2012;366:723](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/22356325))
    - Intralesional corticosteroid for thick plaques of hypertrophic lichen planus ([Int J Dermatol 2009;48:682](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/19570072))
  + Second line therapy
    - Systemic corticosteroids, phototherapy (such as PUVA, narrowband ultraviolet B) and oral acitretin (oral retinoid) ([J Am Acad Dermatol 2019;81:1397](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/31004729), [J Am Acad Dermatol 1991;24:434](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/1829465))
* Lichenoid drug eruption
  + Discontinue offending drug
  + Topical corticosteroid ([Cancer Treat Res Commun 2022;30:100506](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/34990901))
  + Systemic corticosteroids
  + Systemic retinoids
* Lichen striatus
  + Mild or medium strength topical steroids may be used but do not appear to shorten disease duration or prevent postinflammatory hypopigmentation ([Pediatr Dermatol 2004;21:197](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/15165195))
* Lichen planus-like keratosis
  + Lesions can be removed by cryotherapy (liquid nitrogen) ([Cureus 2021;13:e13292](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/33732557))
* Lichen nitidus
  + Systemic and topical corticosteroids
  + Antihistamines, antituberculous drugs, antifungals ([Int J Dermatol 2006;45:615](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/16700804))
  + Narrowband ultraviolet B phototherapy has been reportedly successful in generalized lichen nitidus ([J Am Acad Dermatol 2006;54:545](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/16488316))

Microscopic (histologic) description

* Interface dermatitis is characterized by epidermal basal cell damage, which manifests as basal keratinocyte vacuoles and dyskeratotic / apoptotic keratinocytes
* Vacuolar pattern has a sparse inflammatory infiltrate
* Lichenoid pattern has a moderate to dense, band-like lymphocytic inflammatory infiltrate closely approximating the basal epidermis and dyskeratotic / apoptotic keratinocytes
* Dyskeratotic / apoptotic keratinocytes appear as
  + Shrunken, eosinophilic keratinocytes with pyknotic / absent nuclei scattered about the basal layer of the epidermis
  + Colloid bodies, which are brightly eosinophilic apoptotic keratinocytes that have been extruded into the papillary dermis

**Vacuolar**

* Lymphocyte predominant dermal inflammatory infiltrate
  + Ballooning and individual necrotic keratinocytes
    - Normal epidermal stratum corneum
      * Erythema multiforme
        + Apoptotic keratinocytes within and above the basal layer, in the form of single cells or full thickness necrosis
        + Ragged subepidermal split, where dyskeratotic keratinocytes form the roof of subepidermal vesicle
        + An acrosyringial concentration of keratinocyte necrosis has been reported in drug induced EM ([J Cutan Pathol 1997;24:235](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/9138115))
        + Mild to moderate lymphocytic interface obscuring inflammatory infiltrate
        + Usually, absent to occasional eosinophils ([Arch Pathol Lab Med 1989;113:36](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/2910224))
        + Cannot distinguish from SJS / TEN without clinical data
      * Stevens-Johnson syndrome / toxic epidermal necrolysis
        + Subepidermal bulla with overlying partial to full thickness epidermal necrosis
        + Individual cell necrosis may take the form of lymphocyte associated apoptosis (i.e., satellite cell necrosis)
        + Inflammatory infiltrate may be denser than in EM
    - Epidermal parakeratosis
      * Pityriasis lichenoides
        + Acute form has confluent mounded parakeratosis containing lymphocytes / neutrophils or serum
        + Epidermal necrosis can range from single dyskeratotic cells to full thickness (the latter representing the ulcerated papules seen clinically)
        + Intravascular fibrin deposition may be present
        + Wedge shaped or band-like lymphocytic infiltrate with lymphocyte exocytosis, red cell extravasation and spongiosis
        + Superficial and deep perivascular infiltrates in PLEVA, only superficial in PLC
        + Lymphocytes can have an atypical morphology, with reduced expression of pan T cell markers and have a positive T cell receptor rearrangement
        + There can be scattered large atypical CD30+ cells, which lends consideration to lymphomatoid papulosis ([Int J Dermatol 2003;42:26](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/12581137))
        + Clinical correlation is necessary for diagnosis of the febrile ulceronecrotic variant (FUMHD)
        + Chronic form is a milder version of acute, with a lack of ulceration
    - Prominent epidermal granular layer
      * Graft versus host disease
        + Acute
          - Keratinocyte apoptosis in basilar and lower spinous layers, especially in the tips of epidermal rete, pilosebaceous units and eccrine coils ([Yeung: Pathology of Graft vs. Host Disease, 1st Edition, 2019](https://www.amazon.com/exec/obidos/ASIN/3319420984/pathologyoutl-20))
          - Apoptosis can involve all levels of the epidermis
          - Satellite cell necrosis (lymphocytes surrounding intraepidermal apoptotic keratinocytes)
          - Diffuse vacuolization of the interface
          - Melanophages and red blood cell (RBC) extravasation may be present
          - Sparse, superficial lymphocytic infiltrate
          - Occasional eosinophils may be present
          - Grading for acute GVHD, by Horn ([J Cutan Pathol 1994;21:385](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/7868748))
            * Grade 0: normal skin
            * Grade 1: basal vacuolar change
            * Grade 2: dyskeratotic cells in the epidermis or follicle, dermal lymphocytic infiltrate
            * Grade 3: fusion of basilar vacuoles to form clefts and macrovesicles
            * Grade 4: separation of epidermis from dermis
        + Chronic
          - Early lichen planus-like inflammatory phase
            * Features of lichen planus: epidermal orthohyperkeratosis, hypergranulosis, acanthosis and band-like lichenoid inflammation
            * Also, vacuolar interface change of eccrine coils ([Yeung: Pathology of Graft vs. Host Disease, 1st Edition, 2019](https://www.amazon.com/exec/obidos/ASIN/3319420984/pathologyoutl-20))
          - Morphea-like: localized thickening and homogenization of collagen throughout the dermis with overlying vacuolar interface change ([Arch Dermatol 2008;144:1229](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/18794477))
          - Lichen sclerosus-like: homogenization or sclerosis of papillary dermal collagen with overlying vacuolar interface change, with melanophages and an underlying sparse lichenoid infiltrate
          - Fasciitis: thickening of fascial septa with adjacent inflammation with or without sclerosis in the subcutis
          - A few eosinophils may be present
* Eosinophilic and neutrophil predominant dermal inflammatory infiltrate
  + Ballooning and individual necrotic keratinocytes
    - Fixed drug eruption
      * Classic triad of features are
        + Interface alteration with overlapping lichenoid and vacuolar pattern
        + Papillary dermal fibrosis with melanophages / pigment incontinence
        + Dermal eosinophils
      * Neutrophil component may be prominent in acute lesions and take the form of subcorneal or intraepidermal microabscesses, a dermal neutrophilic infiltrate or a perivascular inflammatory infiltrate

**Lichenoid**

* Lymphocytes predominate
  + Jagged epidermal hyperplasia, wedged hypergranulosis, compact orthokeratosis
    - Lichen planus
      * Interface obscuring band-like lymphocytic infiltrate at the dermoepidermal junction (DEJ); eosinophils and plasma cells are rare
      * Numerous colloid bodies at the DEJ
      * Jagged epidermal hyperplasia with sawtooth appearance in the lower epidermis
      * Acanthotic or atrophic epidermis
      * Wedged hypergranulosis is the histologic correlate to Wickham striae
      * Clefting artifact between the epidermis and dermis, secondary to basal damage (Caspary-Joseph spaces) ([Int J Dermatol 1977;16:842](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/340394))
      * Lichen planopilaris
        + Peri-infundibular and peri-isthmic (bulge region of the hair follicle) band-like inflammatory infiltrate, that abuts the basilar follicular epithelium ([J Cutan Pathol 2005;32:675](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/16293179))
        + Perifollicular mucin ([J Am Acad Dermatol 2008;59:91](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/18571598))
        + Late stage there is a loss of hair follicles and sebaceous glands that are replaced by bands of fibrous tissue without elastic fibers ([J Cutan Pathol 2000;27:147](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/10728818))
      * Hypertrophic variant
        + Markedly acanthotic epidermis
        + Dermal infiltrate is concentrated near the tips of the rete ridges
        + Margins contain changes of lichen simplex chronicus (acanthotic epidermis, hypergranulosis, compact orthokeratosis, stratum lucidum)
        + Overlying squamous epithelium can progress to squamous cell carcinoma, usually well differentiated ([Int J Dermatol 2022;61:1527](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/35766459))
        + Dermal eosinophils are usually present, in contrast to conventional LP
  + Eosinophils sometimes, focally thinned epidermis, focal parakeratosis
    - Lichenoid drug eruption
      * Eosinophils, plasma cells
      * Pigment incontinence
    - Photolichenoid eruption
      * Deep extension of the infiltrate
  + Individual necrotic keratinocytes, ballooning, spongiosis, pallor in the upper part of epidermis and parakeratosis
    - Pityriasis lichenoides
      * See [Epidermal parakeratosis](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/skinnontumorinterfacedermatitis.html#interfacedermatitisepidermal) above
  + Lymphocytes along adnexal structures in the reticular dermis, individual necrotic keratinocytes
    - Lichen striatus
      * Distinguishing feature is deep perivascular and perieccrine lymphocytic inflammatory infiltrate
      * Epidermis will show spongiosis, most likely the subacute type (which is characterized by parakeratosis and acanthosis)
      * Lichenoid epidermal interface alteration
      * Intraepidermal vesicles filled with Langerhans cells may be present ([J Cutan Pathol 1995;22:18](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/7751473))
    - Graft versus host disease
      * Similar to vacuolar; see [Graft versus host disease](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/skinnontumorinterfacedermatitis.html#interfacedermatitisGVHD) above
  + Necrotic keratinocytes, focal parakeratosis, residuum of solar lentigo sometimes
    - Lichen planus-like keratosis
      * Adjacent solar lentigo or seborrheic keratosis, large cell acanthoma or a verruca
      * Epidermal atrophy
      * Plasma cells, eosinophils and neutrophils may be present in the brisk inflammatory infiltrate
      * Pigment incontinence may be prominent
      * Papillary dermal fibrosis
      * Atypical melanocytic proliferation may be present, especially in sun damaged skin ([Hum Pathol 2003;34:706](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/12874767))
      * Can rarely have histologic features of mycosis fungoides (Pautrier microabscesses, epidermotropism, the tagging of lymphocytes along the basal layer) ([J Cutan Pathol 2002;29:291](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/12100630))
* Histiocyte predominant dermal inflammatory infiltrate
  + Discrete foci in papillary dermis
    - Lichen nitidus
      * Ball and claw configuration where a well circumscribed inflammatory infiltrate (ball) expands the dermal papillae, pushing the finger-like epidermal rete to the periphery (claw)
      * Inflammatory infiltrate is very focal - limited to 1 or 2 adjacent dermal papillae
      * Infiltrate contains lymphocytes, histiocytes, melanophages, with or without granulomas / multinucleated giant cells
      * Lichenoid interface alteration
      * Epidermis with mild hyperkeratosis
      * No deep component

Electron microscopy description

* Epidermal basal cell damage in interface dermatitis is primarily mediated by apoptotic mechanisms ([Clin Exp Dermatol 1980;5:425](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/6973424), [Am J Dermatopathol 1979;1:133](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/44963))
  + Apoptosis occurs in the form of scattered single cells without adjacent tissue inflammation; this contrasts with coagulative necrosis, which involves groups of contiguous cells and evokes a secondary inflammatory response ([Am J Dermatopathol 1979;1:133](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/44963))
* Apoptosis is mediated by lymphocytes, which attach to basal keratinocytes in skin conditions resulting from immunologic mechanisms (such as lichen planus, graft versus host disease and fixed drug reaction) ([Clin Exp Dermatol 1980;5:425](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/6973424))
* First step in apoptosis is the formation of membrane bound cell fragments, i.e., apoptotic bodies, which involves chromatin margination, nuclear fragmentation, cytoplasmic condensation and budding
  + Apoptotic bodies that have accumulated tonofilaments in the cytoplasm may be referred to as dyskeratotic keratinocytes
  + Apoptotic bodies that have been extruded into the papillary dermis are referred to as colloid bodies
  + Apoptotic bodies trap immunoglobulins nonspecifically, especially the large IgM multimer ([Am J Dermatopathol 2002;24:130](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/11979072))
* Second step in apoptosis is the phagolysosomal digestion of these bodies by adjacent keratinocytes and macrophages ([Am J Dermatopathol 1979;1:133](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/44963))
* However, the presence of abundant cytoplasmic tonofilaments may prevent phagocytosis ([Am J Dermatopathol 1979;1:133](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/44963))

Vacuolar versus lichenoid interface dermatitis pattern

Sample pathology report

**Vacuolar**

* Skin, right arm, punch biopsy:
  + Vacuolar interface dermatitis (see comment)
  + Comment: Microscopic examination shows foci of vacuolar interface alteration in the basal keratinocytes with associated dyskeratotic keratinocytes. There is an accompanying superficial, sparse to mild lymphocytic inflammatory infiltrate. Melanophages are scattered in the papillary dermis. The differential diagnosis includes erythema multiforme / SJS / TEN spectrum of disorders, acute graft versus host disease, drug eruption and connective tissue diseases. Clinical correlation is recommended for further classification.

**Lichenoid**

* Skin, right inner wrist, punch biopsy:
  + Lichenoid interface dermatitis (see comment)
  + Comment: Microscopic examination shows a dense, superficial, band-like lymphocytic infiltrate, closely approximating the epidermis, with numerous dyskeratotic keratinocytes in the epidermal basal layer. The differential diagnosis includes lichen planus, lichenoid drug eruption and lichen planus-like keratosis. Clinical correlation is recommended for further classification.

Differential diagnosis

**Interface**

* Lymphocytes predominate
  + Erythema multiforme / SJS / TEN spectrum
    - **Subepidermal vesiculobullous disease**:
      * Clean subepidermal split with no dyskeratotic keratinocytes forming the roof of the blister
      * Suprabasal acantholysis
      * Direct immunofluorescence will have a characteristic staining pattern, such as **C3** and [IgG](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsIgA.html) at the basement membrane zone (seen in bullous pemphigoid)
    - [Fixed drug eruption](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/skinnontumordrugreaction.html):
      * Patchy parakeratosis
      * Classically associated with superficial and deep perivascular inflammation ([Dermatol Pract Concept 2011;1:33](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/24396718))
      * Predominantly neutrophilic or eosinophilic inflammatory infiltrate but this depends on the age of lesion
      * Acanthosis is more pronounced than EM
    - [PLEVA](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/skinnontumorPLC.html):
      * Parakeratosis, with or without neutrophils
      * Red cell extravasation, lymphocyte exocytosis
  + Pityriasis lichenoides
    - [PLEVA](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/skinnontumorPLC.html):
      * [Erythema multiforme](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/skinnontumorerythemamultiforme.html):
        + May also have vacuolar interface alteration and an interface obscuring infiltrate
        + Usually no parakeratosis
        + Less prominent lymphocyte exocytosis
        + No wedge shaped inflammatory infiltrate
        + No lymphocytic vasculitis (endothelial swelling, erythrocyte exocytosis, fibrinoid necrosis)
    - [PLC](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/skinnontumorPLC.html):
      * **Chronic superficial dermatitis**:
        + Spongiotic reaction pattern
        + May also have parakeratosis and lymphocyte exocytosis
        + No lymphocytic vasculitis
  + Graft versus host disease
    - **Subacute radiation dermatitis**:
      * Develops weeks to months after radiation
      * Both entities may have interface dermatitis with satellite cell necrosis ([J Am Acad Dermatol 1989;20:236](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/2915058))
      * Cytologically atypical keratinocytes
      * Effacement of dermal papillae and rete ridges
      * Large, atypical fibroblast nuclei (i.e., radiation fibroblasts in dermal collagen) ([J Am Acad Dermatol 1989;20:236](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/2915058))
    - **Eruption of lymphocyte recovery**:
      * Develops 2 - 3 weeks after chemotherapy
      * Both entities may have dyskeratotic keratinocytes and satellite cell necrosis ([Arch Dermatol 1993;129:855](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/8323305))
      * Erythematous maculopapular rash during bone marrow aplasia induced by chemotherapy
      * Epidermal spongiosis, keratinocyte atypia secondary to chemotherapy effect, vacuolar interface alteration, mild superficial perivascular lymphocytic inflammation
      * Too few dyskeratotic keratinocytes to suggest a diagnosis of GVHD ([Arch Dermatol 1989;125:1512](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/2684020))
      * May need clinical correlation to distinguish from grade 2 GVHD ([Arch Dermatol 1989;125:1512](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/2684020), [Arch Dermatol 1993;129:855](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/8323305))
    - **Engraftment syndrome**:
      * Usually occurs 3 - 10 days after HSCT, as this is the period of neutrophil recovery
      * Risk factors include less intense chemotherapy regimens, use of peripheral blood stem cells, use of granulocyte colony stimulating factor (G-CSF) ([Ann Transplant 2024;29:e944043](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/39039776), [Med Oncol 2022;40:36](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/36460884))
      * Noninfectious fever, diarrhea, skin rash, pulmonary edema, weight gain, deranged renal function tests and liver function tests ([Med Oncol 2022;40:36](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/36460884))
      * Lower numbers of epidermal [CD8](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/cdmarkerscd1.html)+ cells and [CD1a](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/cdmarkerscd1a.html)+ cells in engraftment syndrome than in GVHD ([Histol Histopathol 2012;27:235](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/22207558))
    - [Drug hypersensitivity reaction](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/lymphnodesdrughypersensitivity.html):
      * Both may have vacuolar interface changes
      * More spongiosis and dermal eosinophils than in GVHD ([J Cutan Pathol 2015;42:39](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/25393781))
    - **Viral exanthem**:
      * In immunocompromised adults, diffuse morbilliform rashes can mimic GVHD ([J Am Acad Dermatol 2017;76:538](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/28413059), [Allergol Int 2006;55:1](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/17075280))
      * Causative agents may include influenza virus, enteroviruses (coxsackie, echovirus), herpes simplex, HHV6 / 7, parvovirus B19, measles, cytomegalovirus (CMV), EBV, HIV and others ([J Am Acad Dermatol 2017;76:538](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/28413059), [Future Microbiol 2017;12:171](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/27838923))
      * Clinically, vesicles and petechiae favor viral etiology ([J Am Acad Dermatol 2012;67:1282](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/22627037))
      * Serology, polymerase chain reaction (PCR) or viral antigen detection methods can be used
* Eosinophils and neutrophils prominent
  + Fixed drug eruption
    - [Erythema multiforme](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/skinnontumorerythemamultiforme.html):
      * Superficial inflammatory infiltrate only
      * Inflammatory infiltrate is more sparse and does not contain neutrophils and eosinophils
      * Lack of pigment incontinence and melanophages
    - [Stevens-Johnson syndrome / toxic epidermal necrolysis](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/skinnontumorstevensjohnsonsyndrome.html):
      * Has a longer latency period (order of weeks versus hours), more mucosal involvement, more severe constitutional symptoms
    - [Herpes simplex / zoster](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/skinnontumorherpeszoster.html):
      * Considered when the fixed drug has abundant intraepidermal vesiculation
      * Features include multinucleation, molding and nuclear margination with intranuclear inclusions
      * [HSV](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsHSV.html) immunohistochemical stain is positive
    - **Other intraepidermal pustular conditions**:
      * [Acute generalized exanthematous pustulosis](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/skinnontumoragep.html):
        + Clinically, innumerable minute pustules on a background of intense erythema with desquamation, over the trunk and intertriginous areas, instead of a recurrent single round to oval lesion
      * [Dermatitis herpetiformis](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/skinnontumordermatitisherpetiformis.html) and the [pemphigus](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/skinnontumorpemphigus.html) group of conditions would show characteristic intercellular [IgG](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsIgA.html), [IgA](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainsIgA.html) or **C3** deposition on direct immunofluorescence

**Lichenoid**

* Lymphocytes predominate
  + Lichen planus
    - [Secondary syphilis](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/skinnontumorsyphilis.html):
      * Acral involvement in an immunocompromised patient
      * Plasma cells are abundant
      * Apoptotic keratinocytes are usually absent or far fewer than expected, given the density of the lymphocytic infiltrate
      * [Treponemal](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/stainstreponemaihc.html) immunostain is positive for spirochetal organisms in the basal epidermis / superficial dermis
    - [Lupus erythematous](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/skinnontumorlupussle.html):
      * Vacuolar interface pattern
      * Basement membrane zone thickening
      * Interstitial dermal mucin
      * Inflammation in discoid lupus erythematosus (DLE) is more widespread than in LP and can involve the entire hair follicle, the sweat glands, deep perivascular plexus and the subcutaneous fat
      * Panniculitis may manifest as mild patchy lymphocytic infiltrates, mucin deposition or lipoatrophy
    - **Lichen planus-like keratosis** ([DermNet: Lichenoid Keratosis [Accessed 5 February 2025]](https://dermnetnz.org/topics/lichenoid-keratosis)):
      * Parakeratosis
      * Plasma cells present
      * Adjacent solar lentigo or seborrheic keratosis
    - Early [lichen sclerosus](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/skinnontumorlichensclerosus.html):
      * Early homogenization of the papillary collagen
      * Dermal edema
    - **Idiopathic scarring alopecia**:
      * Late stage lesions of scalp involvement by lichen planopilaris can be identical, with replacement of hair follicles and sebaceous glands by fibrous tracts
      * Fibrous tracts contain elastic fibers, in contrast to late stage lichen planopilaris ([J Cutan Pathol 2000;27:147](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/10728818))
  + Lichenoid drug eruption
    - [Lichen planus](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/skinnontumorlichenplanus.html):
      * Orthokeratosis only
      * No eosinophils
      * More pronounced band-like inflammatory infiltrate
      * No deep involvement (in contrast to photolichenoid eruptions)
  + Lichen striatus
    - [Lichen planus](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/skinnontumorlichenplanus.html):
      * No inflammation surrounding eccrine coils
      * No parakeratosis
  + Lichen planus-like keratosis
    - [Lichenoid actinic keratosis](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/skintumornonmelanocyticactinickeratosis.html):
      * Large atypical basal layer keratinocyte nuclei with a loss of polarity, loss of granular layer
      * Atypical basal keratinocytes extend beyond the zone of most dense lymphocytic inflammation
      * Focal parakeratosis
      * Prominent solar elastosis
    - [Lichen planus](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/skinnontumorlichenplanus.html):
      * No plasma cells
      * No parakeratosis
    - [Lichenoid drug eruption](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/skinnontumordrugreaction.html):
      * Clinically, usually does not present as a discrete ~1 cm lesion on the chest
      * No adjacent solar lentigo or seborrheic keratosis
* Histiocytes predominate
  + Lichen nitidus
    - [Arthropod reactions](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/skinnontumorarthropod.html):
      * Inflammation is more widely distributed (not limited to 1 or 2 adjacent dermal papillae)
      * Eosinophils and neutrophils may be prominent
    - [Disseminated granuloma annulare](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/skinnontumorgranulomaannulare.html):
      * Granulomatous foci can form a dense ball in the superficial dermis, in disseminated granuloma annulare
      * However, there are no finger-like epidermal rete on the periphery of the inflammatory focus (no claw)
      * Necrobiosis may be present, along with increased dermal mucin

Board review style question #1

[![](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/imgau/skinnontumorinterfacedermatitisPelkey14.jpg)](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/imgau/skinnontumorinterfacedermatitisPelkey14.jpg)

A 41 year old woman presented with alopecia. What features, if present, would favor a diagnosis of lichen planopilaris?

1. Deep dermal mucin
2. Follicular epithelial interface alteration
3. Replacement of hair follicles by fibrous tracts containing elastic fibers
4. Syringotropic lymphocytes

Board review style answer #1

**B**. Follicular epithelial interface alteration is correct because the lymphocytic inflammatory infiltrate primarily targets the perifollicular (bulge) region of the hair follicle in lichen planopilaris, resulting in epithelial interface alteration. This is in contrast to lupus erythematosus, in which there is a vacuolar interface dermatitis involving the entire hair follicle, its appendages and the deep vascular plexus ([J Cutan Pathol 2005;32:675](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/16293179)).

Answer D is incorrect because syringotropic lymphocytes infiltrating eccrine coils and ducts may be seen in syringotropic mycosis fungoides and some inflammatory conditions but follicular involvement and follicular epithelial interface alteration will be absent ([J Am Acad Dermatol 2003;49:1177](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/14639413)).
Answer A is incorrect because deep and interfollicular (interstitial) dermal mucin is seen in lupus erythematosus. Perifollicular mucin may be present in lichen planopilaris but it is only focal ([Lupus Sci Med 2018;5:e000291](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/30397497)).
Answer C is incorrect because the replacement of hair follicles by fibrous tracts containing elastic fibers is a feature of idiopathic scarring alopecia ([Int J Dermatol 1982;21:316](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/6749712)). Idiopathic scarring alopecia is a noninflammatory, end stage scarring alopecia, in which hair follicles are replaced by fibrous tracts containing elastic fibers ([Arch Dermatol 1960;82:336](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/14438658), [Int J Dermatol 2002;41:8](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/11895507)). In the late fibrotic stages of lichen planopilaris and lupus, there are similar findings but there is a loss of elastic fiber staining in the replacement fibrous tracts ([J Cutan Pathol 2000;27:147](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/10728818)).

Board review style question #2

[![](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/imgau/skinnontumorinterfacedermatitisPelkey08.jpg)](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/imgau/skinnontumorinterfacedermatitisPelkey08.jpg)

A 70 year old man 26 days post-bone marrow transplant for acute myeloid leukemia developed a tender, diffuse rash over his bilateral lower extremities, trunk and scalp. He has gastrointestinal upset and elevated liver enzymes. A skin biopsy is performed. What is the most likely diagnosis, in correlation with the clinical findings?

1. Acute graft versus host disease
2. Drug hypersensitivity syndrome
3. Engraftment syndrome
4. Morphea
5. Scabies

Board review style answer #2

**A**. Acute graft versus host disease (GVHD) develops within 100 days posttransplant and the classic organs affected are the gastrointestinal tract, liver and skin. Histologically, cutaneous acute GVHD classically manifests as keratinocyte apoptosis, diffuse basal vacuolization and mild dermal inflammation. Keratinocyte apoptosis may be diffuse, involving the entire epidermis or may be confined to the basilar and lower spinous layers. Interface change can involve the hair follicles and acrosyringium.

Answer E is incorrect because scabies typically arises within skin creases (i.e., between the fingers and toes) and is intensely pruritic. Histologic diagnosis requires visualizing cross sections of a mite, which may be nested within the stratum corneum.

Answer D is incorrect because the clinical features of morphea may develop in lesions of chronic GVHD (> 100 days posttransplant). Histologically, there are homogenized thickened collagen bundles that replace the mid to deep reticular dermis.

Answer B is incorrect because drug hypersensitivity syndrome (DRESS syndrome) (or drug rash with eosinophilia and systemic symptoms) is a rare reaction that classically occurs within 1 - 8 weeks after the intake of an anticonvulsant ([Arch Intern Med 1995;155:2285](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/7487252)). DRESS has systemic (hematologic, visceral) and cutaneous manifestations (maculopapular eruption, SJS / TEN, angioedema). Histologically, the findings in DRESS are heterogeneous and nonspecific (vacuolar and lichenoid interface alteration, spongiosis, subcorneal pustules). Small granulomas and eosinophils may be present in the dermis and SJS / TEN must be considered in the clinicopathologic diagnosis. Clinically, the patient in the vignette is not presenting with the typical findings expected for DRESS syndrome.

Answer C is incorrect because engraftment syndrome occurs 3 - 10 days after hematopoietic stem cell transplant / engraftment, as this is the time of neutrophil recovery. Typical clinical presentation includes fever related to cytokine release, maculopapular skin rash mimicking acute GVHD, noncardiogenic pulmonary edema and diarrhea. Histologically, the findings may be indistinguishable from acute GVHD; however the patient in the vignette presented later in the posttransplant period, without pulmonary involvement, making this answer choice unlikely.
