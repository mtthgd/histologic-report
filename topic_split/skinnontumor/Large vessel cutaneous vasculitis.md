Definition / general

* Vasculitides are inflammatory processes that can affect capillaries, venules and small, medium and large sized vessels ([Curr Opin Rheumatol 2019;31:46](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/30394939),[An Bras Dermatol 2020;95:355](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/32307202))
* Large vessel vasculitis (LVV) involves inflammation of medium to large size arteries and veins

Essential features

* Large vessel vasculitis affects larger vessels and can present with more systemic involvement, sometimes requiring complex treatment
* Similar to small vessel vasculitis, it can result from immune mediated hypersensitivity reactions but may involve more extensive vessel wall damage
* Diagnosis is confirmed through clinical evaluation, laboratory tests, imaging and biopsy
* Many classifications have been proposed; however, the most accepted one is the Chapel Hill consensus conference (CHCC) which integrates the clinical presentation, histopathology and lab findings
  + It also classifies vasculitis based on the size of the affected vessel in small, medium and large vessel vasculitis ([Arthritis Rheum 2013;65:1](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/23045170))
* 2012 CHCC guidelines define and categorize large vessel vasculitis primarily to include
  + Giant cell arteritis (GCA)
  + Takayasu arteritis (TA)

Terminology

* Vasculitides

ICD coding

* ICD-10
  + [M31.5](https://www.icd10data.com/ICD10CM/Codes/M00-M99/M30-M36/M31-/M31.5) - giant cell arteritis with polymyalgia rheumatica
  + [M31.6](https://www.icd10data.com/ICD10CM/Codes/M00-M99/M30-M36/M31-/M31.6) - other giant cell arteritis
  + [M31.4](https://www.icd10data.com/ICD10CM/Codes/M00-M99/M30-M36/M31-/M31.4) - aortic arch syndrome [Takayasu]

Epidemiology

* Giant cell arteritis (GCA): incidence varies widely but is roughly 18 - 29 cases per 100,000 people over 50 years of age in the U.S. and Europe ([Semin Arthritis Rheum 2021;51:1193](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/34644662))
  + More common among individuals of Northern European descent
* Takayasu arteritis (TA): incidence is lower, estimated at 0.4 - 3.4 cases per million annually and it is more common in Asia ([Front Med (Lausanne) 2022;9:952972](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/36045929))
  + More prevalent in younger individuals (ages 10 - 40) and has a higher incidence in women, especially in Asia
* Kawasaki disease is a medium sized vessel vasculitis and primarily affects young children, with higher incidence rates in Asian populations, especially in Japan

Sites

* Most common sites are the aorta and major branches of the aorta such as the subclavian, carotid and renal arteries ([Pediatr Nephrol 2010;25:1037](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/%2019844748))
* Giant cell arteritis often affects the temporal arteries, while Takayasu arteritis involves the aortic branches (e.g., subclavian and carotid arteries) ([Front Immunol 2021;12:623716](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/33717128))

Pathophysiology / etiology

* Large vessel vasculitis primarily involves inflammation of the aorta and its major branches ([Nat Rev Dis Primers 2022;7:93](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/34992251))
* In both GCA and TA, T cells recognize unknown antigens in the vessel wall and release proinflammatory cytokines to recruit other immune cells ([Arthritis Rheum 2009;61:1454](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/19790127))
* Macrophages are attracted to the site of inflammation and differentiate into giant cells which cause destruction of elastic lamina
* Over time, the weakening of vessel walls may lead to aneurysm formation

Clinical features

* Large vessel vasculitis
  + Giant cell arteritis
    - New onset headaches, scalp tenderness, jaw claudication, fever, fatigue, polymyalgia and vision loss ([Neurol Clin 2019;37:335](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/30952412))
  + Takayasu arteritis
    - Arterial stenosis, aneurysms and occlusion involving the ascending and descending aorta, renovascular hypertension, lower extremity claudication, arthralgia, myalgia and skin nodules ([Heart 2018;104:558](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/29175979), [Dermatologica 1990;181:266](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/1981565))
  + Behçet disease
    - Recurrent aphthous stomatitis, genital aphthous ulcers, erythema nodosum-like lesions, papulopustular lesions, ocular manifestations, thrombophlebitis, pericarditis, myocarditis, arthralgia, fibromyalgia, neurologic and gastrointestinal manifestations ([Clin Dermatol 2017;35:421](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/28916023))
  + Cogan syndrome
    - Constitutional symptoms, ataxia, nystagmus and interstitial keratitis ([Curr Allergy Asthma Rep 2020;20:46](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/32548646))
  + Vasculitis in systemic lupus erythematosus
    - Petechiae, palpable purpura, papulonodular lesions, livedo reticularis and ulcers are associated with myocarditis, serositis and Raynaud phenomenon ([Clin Dermatol 2004;22:148](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/15234016))
  + Rheumatoid arthritis associated vasculitis
    - Purpura, ulcers, gangrene, mononeuritis multiplex, episcleritis, scleritis, myocarditis, pericarditis, lung, kidney, gastrointestinal and central nervous system involvement ([Curr Opin Rheumatol 2015;27:63](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/25405822))
  + Sarcoid vasculitis
    - Constitutional symptoms, peripheral adenopathy, rash, scleritis, iridocyclitis, musculoskeletal and pulmonary parenchymal involvement ([Semin Arthritis Rheum 2000;30:33](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/10966211))

Diagnosis

* Proper diagnosis requires a combination of clinical history and physical examination to assess organ involvement (systemic disease); large vessel vasculitis often has systemic effects that require a broader clinical assessment
* In cases of systemic vasculitis, a kidney or nerve biopsy may be helpful; while large vessel vasculitis primarily affects large vessels, systemic forms may involve other organs
* Obtain a complete blood count, complete metabolic panel and urinalysis
* Complementary laboratory exams, including workup for infectious, autoimmune disorders or malignancy; may need to rule out other conditions or identify systemic involvement ([An Bras Dermatol 2020;95:355](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/32307202))

Laboratory

* Complete blood count with differential, urinalysis, blood urea nitrogen / creatinine, liver function panel, hepatitis B and C serology, ANA, rheumatoid factor, ANCA, cryoglobulins, total complement and its fractions should be performed
* Results will vary and depend on the underlying etiology ([J Am Acad Dermatol 2003;48:311](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/12637912), [Actas Dermosifiliogr 2012;103:179](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/21839977), [Curr Rheumatol Rep 2021;23:49](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/34196889))

Prognostic factors

* Patients with longstanding localized vasculitis (such as cutaneous polyarteritis nodosa [PAN]) can progress to systemic disease; systemic PAN can involve larger vessels ([Semin Diagn Pathol 2001;18:59](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/11296994), [J Dermatol 1989;16:429](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/2576427))
* In vasculitides associated with autoimmune disease like giant cell arteritis, the likelihood of progression is thought to be related to high serologic evidence of connective tissue disease (rheumatoid factor, antinuclear antibodies) ([Nephrology (Carlton) 2008;13:S17](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/18713120), [Front Immunol 2020;11:2053](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/33013868))

Case reports

* 26 year old woman with Takayau arteritis presenting with thickened aortic walls ([JNMA J Nepal Med Assoc 2022;60:1041](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/36705112))
* 59 year old man with graft versus host disease after stem cell transplantation that later progressed to large vessel vasculitis ([J Med Case Rep 2021:15:478](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/34579779))
* 71 year old woman with large vessel vasculitis and myelodysplastic syndrome ([Intern Med 2014;53:63](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/24390531))

Treatment

* Typically involves immunosuppressive medications like methotrexate and tocilizumab (IL6 receptor antagonist) to reduce inflammation and prevent complications ([J Clin Med 2022;11:1588](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/35329914))
* Steroids like prednisone to control inflammation
* Vascular surgery or angioplasty may be necessary to restore blood flow

Microscopic (histologic) description

* Medium vessel vasculitis
  + Polyarteritis nodosa (PAN)
    - Nongranulomatous, necrotizing inflammation of medium sized and small arteries without glomerulonephritis
    - Prominent neutrophilic and lymphocytic infiltration which leads to fibrosis and thrombosis
    - Vasculitis is located in the deep dermis or subcutis ([Am J Dermatopathol 2005;27:504](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/16314707), [Rheum Dis Clin North Am 2015;41:33](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/25399938), [Semin Cutan Med Surg 2007;26:77](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/17544958))
  + Kawasaki disease
    - Acute process is expressed as nongranulomatous, necrotizing arteritis with neutrophilic infiltration
    - Subacute process presents with asynchronous infiltration of lymphocytes, plasma cells and eosinophils
    - Chronic process comprises luminal myofibroblast proliferation associated with chronic inflammation
    - Progressive arterial stenosis ([Circulation 2017;135:e927](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/28356445))
  + Cogan syndrome
    - Histopathologic examination of cochlear and corneal tissue shows lymphocytic and plasma cell infiltration ([Autoimmun Rev 2013;12:396](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/22846458))
* Large vessel vasculitis
  + Large vessel vasculitis
    - Affects the aorta and branches of major vessels
    - Segmental granulomatous involvement with prominent inflammation of the intima and media
    - Fibrosis of the adventitia ([Vasc Med 2019;24:79](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/30355272))
  + Giant cell arteritis
    - Granulomatous inflammation with giant cells, elastic lamina fragmentation and intimal thickening
    - Infiltration of T lymphocytes and macrophages between the intima and the media ([Am J Dermatopathol 2005;27:504](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/16314707), [Neurol Clin 2019;37:335](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/30952412))
  + Takayasu arteritis
    - Granulomatous inflammation of the media and adventitia
    - Infiltration of lymphocytes, macrophages, plasma cells and giant cells
    - Fibrosis with scarring leading to stenosis ([Heart 2018;104:558](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/29175979))

Sample pathology report

* Temporal artery, left, biopsy:
  + Consistent with giant cell arteritis (see comment)
  + Comment: The specimen shows transmural inflammation of the artery wall, characterized by a predominance of lymphocytes and macrophages, with multinucleated giant cells seen at the intima media junction. There is marked intimal hyperplasia leading to luminal narrowing, fragmentation of the internal elastic lamina and focal areas of medial necrosis. No evidence of neutrophilic infiltration is present. Findings are consistent with large vessel vasculitis, specifically giant cell arteritis.

Differential diagnosis

* [Atherosclerosis](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/heartatheroscleroticcad.html):
  + Can cause vessel narrowing, claudication and ischemic symptoms similar to LVV ([J Pers Med 2021;11:236](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/33806941))
  + Typically affects multiple types of vessels and lacks the inflammatory features seen in vasculitis
  + Imaging often shows calcifications and inflammatory markers are generally normal
* [Fibromuscular dysplasia](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/heartfibromusculardysplasia.html):
  + Causes vessel stenosis and can lead to ischemic symptoms in the limbs, brain or other organs like the kidneys
  + Primarily affects medium sized arteries, particularly in young women and does not involve systemic inflammation ([Rev Bras Reumatol 2012;52:70](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/22286647))
* [Infectious aortitis (mycotic aortitis)](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/heartcoronaryarteritis.html):
  + Inflammation of large vessels, often involving the aorta, can mimic LVV symptoms
  + Caused by infections (e.g., *Salmonella, Staphylococcus*), usually with fever and identified by blood cultures or biopsy ([Curr Treat Options Cardiovasc Med 2005;7:89)](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/15935117)
* [Radiation induced vasculopathy](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/cnsradiationinjury.html):
  + Vessel stenosis and ischemia can occur years after radiation therapy to large vessels ([Front Cardiovasc Med 2021;8:652761](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/33860001))
  + Typically localized to the radiation field and lacks systemic inflammatory signs
* **Thromboangiitis obliterans (Buerger disease)**:
  + Inflammatory disease affecting medium to large vessels, leading to ischemic symptoms ([Int J Inflam 2013;2013:156905](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/24102033))
  + Strongly associated with smoking and typically affects younger individuals
  + Primarily involves peripheral vessels rather than the aorta or carotid arteries
* **Marfan syndrome** and other connective tissue disorders (e.g., [Ehlers-Danlos syndrome](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/colonehlers.html)):
  + Can cause aneurysms, dissections and vessel wall weakness in large arteries ([Mol Syndromol 2012;3:47](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/23326250))
  + Genetic basis, often without systemic inflammatory markers
  + Presents with characteristic features like hyperflexible joints or skeletal abnormalities

Board review style question #1

Which of the following is true regarding large vessel vasculitis?

1. Giant cell arteritis affects medium sized vessels but rarely involves large arteries like the aorta
2. Large vessel vasculitis can present with aneurysms and vessel wall thickening, often detected via imaging studies
3. Large vessel vasculitis typically presents with prominent mucocutaneous symptoms and skin manifestations
4. Takayasu arteritis primarily affects elderly patients and typically involves the carotid and vertebral arteries

Board review style answer #1

**B**. Large vessel vasculitis can present with aneurysms and vessel wall thickening, often detected via imaging studies. These conditions can cause vessel wall thickening, stenosis and aneurysms, which are typically identified through imaging. Answer A is incorrect because giant cell arteritis primarily involves large arteries, especially the temporal arteries and aorta, although it can affect medium sized arteries as well. Answer C is incorrect because large vessel vasculitis does not typically present with mucocutaneous or skin manifestations, which are more common in small and medium vessel vasculitides (e.g., cutaneous vasculitis, Behçet disease). Answer D is incorrect because Takayasu arteritis primarily affects young women under the age of 40, not elderly patients. It typically involves large vessels like the aorta and its primary branches, including the subclavian and carotid arteries.

Board review style question #2

[![](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/imgau/skinnontumorvasculitisWirth01.jpeg)](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/imgau/skinnontumorvasculitisWirth01.jpeg)

Which of the following statements accurately describes a feature of giant cell arteritis (GCA) as classified under large vessel vasculitis?

1. GCA commonly affects young adults, with a high prevalence in Asia
2. GCA is diagnosed mainly through infectious serology and imaging of peripheral vessels
3. GCA is often associated with temporal artery involvement, leading to symptoms like headaches and jaw claudication
4. GCA primarily affects medium sized vessels, such as the renal and carotid arteries

Board review style answer #2

**C**. GCA is often associated with temporal artery involvement, leading to symptoms like headaches and jaw claudication. GCA frequently involves the temporal arteries and presents with clinical symptoms such as new onset headaches, jaw claudication and scalp tenderness. Answer A is incorrect because GCA primarily affects individuals over the age of 50, particularly in Northern Europe and the U.S., rather than young adults. Answer B is incorrect because GCA is not typically diagnosed through infectious serology but rather through clinical evaluation, imaging and often, temporal artery biopsy to identify the characteristic inflammatory changes. Answer D is incorrect because GCA is a large vessel vasculitis affecting larger arteries more often than medium sized vessels; it commonly involves arteries like the temporal and sometimes aortic branches.
