Definition / general
  • Insufficiency of macronutrients (carbohydrates, proteins and fats) or micronutrients (vitamins and minerals)
Essential features
  • Nutritional deficiencies rarely occur in isolation, so the workup should comprehensively assess the levels of multiple nutrients and overall nutritional status
  • At risk individuals demonstrate behaviors or have conditions that cause inadequate oral intake, absorption or metabolism of nutrients
  • Although overlap exists, specific nutritional deficiencies can manifest with distinct clinical features in dermatoses
  • Common histologic features include spongiosis, psoriasiform epidermal hyperplasia and pallor of the upper epidermis
  • Treatment involves repletion of deficient nutrients

  • Vitamin K deficiency
  • Zinc deficiency (acrodermatitis enteropathica)
  • Copper deficiency
  • Thiamine (vitamin B1) deficiency (beriberi)

Epidemiology
  • M = F
  • Infants / children or elderly adults
  • Alcohol use disorder (Cureus 2023;15:e35011)
  • Developing countries with limited nutritional resources
  • Restrictive eating behaviors (anorexia nervosa, bulimia nervosa, autism spectrum disorder) (Proc (Bayl Univ Med Cent) 2021;34:742)
  • Bariatric / bowel bypass surgery (Cureus 2023;15:e37056)
Sites
  • Skin, hair, oral mucosa, nails
  • Extracutaneous manifestations variable
Pathophysiology
  • Micronutrients required for enzymatic function and homeostasis in a wide array of cutaneous processes, including but not limited to immune functions, metabolic functions and tissue renewal processes
  • Nutritional deficiencies lead to malfunction of various cutaneous processes, manifesting as various dermatologic conditions
Etiology
  • Proper nutrition depends on ingestion, digestion, absorption and metabolism; disruption of any step can cause deficiency in any macro or micronutrient
  • Risk factors common to most nutrient deficiencies
    • Infants / children or elderly adults
    • Restrictive eating practices (anorexia nervosa, bulimia nervosa)
    • Bariatric / bowel bypass surgery
    • Malabsorption or inflammatory diseases of gastrointestinal (GI) tract
    • Alcohol use disorder (Cureus 2024;16:e57652)
    • Chronic liver disease
    • Developing countries with limited nutritional resources
    • Low socioeconomic status
    • Long term parenteral nutrition
  • Risk factors for fat soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K)
    • Fat malabsorption (gastric bypass surgery, pancreatic insufficiency, cystic fibrosis) decreases absorption of lipid soluble vitamins (Nutrients 2019;11:1876)
  • Risk factors for zinc deficiency
    • Hereditary: autosomal recessive mutation in SLC39A4 gene causes impaired intestinal zinc absorption
    • Acquired: transient neonatal zinc deficiency (low zinc levels in maternal breast milk due to impaired zinc transporter in breast tissue); penicillamine promotes zinc elimination through renal tubules (Pediatr Rep 2021;13:444)
  • Risk factors for copper deficiency
    • Menkes disease: X linked defect in ATP7A gene, causing impaired copper transport and metabolism
    • Excess zinc can impair copper absorption (Clin Case Rep 2020;8:1666)
  • Risk factors for niacin deficiency (vitamin B3)
    • Hartnup disease: autosomal recessive gene defect in neutral amino acid transporter in kidneys and intestines that causes severe niacin deficiency
    • Carcinoid tumors synthesize serotonin from tryptophan, shifting tryptophan supply away from niacin production
  • Risk factors for biotin deficiency (vitamin B7)
    • Inherited: autosomal recessive defects in carboxylase and biotinidase enzymes involved in biotin metabolism
    • Acquired: excess consumption of raw egg whites creates excess levels of avidin protein that binds biotin and decreases its absorption
  • Risk factors for cyanocobalamin deficiency (vitamin B12)
    • Pernicious anemia
    • Associated autoimmune endocrinopathies
    • Recreational nitrous oxide use (J Am Acad Orthop Surg Glob Res Rev 2024;8:e23.00104)
Clinical features
  • Vitamin A deficiency
    • Phrynoderma: follicular papules with central scaly plug; favors extensor surfaces
    • Ocular manifestations (conjunctival and corneal dryness, night blindness, vision loss) (Cureus 2022;14:e31683)
  • Vitamin C deficiency (scurvy)
    • Perifollicular erythema or hemorrhage
    • Corkscrew hairs
    • Bruising (Clin Case Rep 2023;11:e7418)
    • Bleeding gums, gum swelling (Tunis Med 2021;99:1093)
  • Vitamin K deficiency
    • Mucocutaneous bleeding, petechiae / purpura / ecchymoses
  • Zinc deficiency (acrodermatitis enteropathica)
    • Scaly erosive skin changes in perioral, anogenital and acral distribution
    • Diarrhea
  • Copper deficiency
    • Generalized hypo or depigmentation of skin
    • Pili torti (twisting of hair), hypopigmented, kinky, friable hair (Skin Appendage Disord 2022;8:57)
    • Microcytic anemia due to impaired iron uptake
    • Peripheral neuropathy (motor weakness, abnormal gait)
  • Thiamine (vitamin B1) deficiency
    • Glossitis, cheilitis, waxy, edematous skin
    • Wet beriberi: heart failure with resulting volume overload
    • Dry beriberi: peripheral neuropathy
    • Wernicke-Korsakoff syndrome: impaired cognition and memory, coordination problems
  • Riboflavin (vitamin B2) deficiency
    • Scaly, erythematous rash in anogenital region
    • Seborrheic dermatitis-like rash
    • Cheilitis, stomatitis, tongue atrophy, glossitis
  • Niacin (vitamin B3) deficiency
    • 4 D's of pellagra: diarrhea, dermatitis (scaly or fissuring rash in sun exposed areas), dementia and death
  • Pyridoxine (vitamin B6) deficiency
    • Periorificial seborrheic dermatitis-like rash
    • Intertrigo
    • Angular cheilitis, atrophic glossitis
  • Biotin (vitamin B7) deficiency
    • Hair loss
    • Seborrheic dermatitis of face, scalp and flexures
    • Acrodermatitis enteropathica-like rash
    • Blepharitis, conjunctivitis
    • Lethargy, paresthesias, hyperesthesias, hypotonia, seizures, ataxia
  • Folate (vitamin B9) deficiency
    • Diffuse hyperpigmentation in sun exposed areas
    • Cheilitis, glossitis, mucosal erosions / ulcerations
    • Megaloblastic anemia, hypersegmented neutrophils
    • Neuropsychiatric symptoms
  • Cyanocobalamin (vitamin B12) deficiency
    • Hyperpigmentation of palms, soles, nails, oral cavity, dorsal hands and feet, flexural areas
    • Glossitis
    • Alopecia
    • Megaloblastic anemia
    • Peripheral neuropathy, ataxia
Diagnosis
  • Diagnosis usually made via blood and serum based laboratory values that demonstrate deficient levels (J Prev Med Hyg 2022;63:E93)
  • Nutritional deficiencies rarely occur in isolation so workup should investigate levels of several micronutrients
  • Cutaneous biopsy can demonstrate findings that suggest the presence of nutritional deficiency (see Microscopic description)
Laboratory
  • Vitamin A deficiency: retinol levels, retinol binding protein levels
  • Vitamin C deficiency: ascorbate level
  • Vitamin K deficiency: elevated prothrombin time (PT) / international normalized ratio (INR), vitamin K1 levels
  • Zinc deficiency: plasma zinc levels, alkaline phosphatase (zinc dependent enzyme) levels (J Am Acad Dermatol 2022;86:267)
  • Copper deficiency: serum copper or ceruloplasmin, 24 hour urine copper excretion
  • Thiamine (vitamin B1) deficiency: thiamine levels (J Am Acad Dermatol 2022;86:281)
  • Riboflavin (vitamin B2): erythrocyte glutathione reductase activation coefficient (EGRac) assay
  • Niacin (vitamin B3): whole blood niacin, urinary N-methyl-nicotinamide, niacin number (whole blood NAD:NADP x 100)
  • Pyridoxine (vitamin B6) deficiency: plasma pyridoxal phosphate (PLP)
  • Biotin (vitamin B7) deficiency: serum biotin levels
  • Folate (vitamin B9) deficiency: serum or RBC folate level
  • Cyanocobalamin (vitamin B12) deficiency: serum B12 level
Radiology description
  • Vitamin C deficiency
    • Xray
      • Osteopenia, cortical thinning
      • Frankel line: dense metaphyseal bands (Cureus 2023;15:e39369)
      • Wimberger ring sign: a circular, opaque radiologic shadow surrounding epiphyseal centers of ossification
      • Pelkin spur: metaphyseal spurs that result in a cupping of the metaphysis
      • Trummerfeld zone: lucent metaphyseal band underlying the Frankel line
    • Magnetic resonance imaging (MRI): soft tissue edema, periosteal reaction (Front Endocrinol (Lausanne) 2022;13:968015)

Microscopic (histologic) description
  • Variable histologic changes nonspecific to any one nutritional deficiency
    • Hyperkeratosis, parakeratosis
    • Keratinocyte pallor and ballooning of upper half of epidermis
  • Vitamin A deficiency (phrynoderma)
    • Hyperkeratosis
    • Keratotic follicular plugging (Indian J Dermatol 2011;56:389)
    • Atrophic sweat glands; squamous metaplasia in severe cases
  • Vitamin C deficiency
    • Extravasated erythrocytes surrounding vessels in upper dermis; may also see perifollicularly (Pediatr Rheumatol Online J 2024;22:56)
    • Coiled, corkscrew-like hairs within keratotic follicular material

Sample pathology report
  • Skin, punch biopsy, right back:
    • Spongiotic psoriasiform dermatitis with marked epidermal pallor (see comment)
    • Comment: These findings are suggestive of a dermatitis associated with an underlying nutritional deficiency, such as acrodermatitis enteropathica, pellagra or necrolytic migratory erythema. Clinical correlation is recommended.

  • Skin, punch biopsy, forearm:
    • Spongiotic psoriasiform dermatitis with marked epidermal pallor (see comment)
    • Comment: In the correct clinical context, these findings are compatible with a nutritional dermatosis. The differential diagnosis includes psoriasis and less likely, an eczematous process.

  • Skin, L arm, shave biopsy:
    • Mild follicular hyperkeratosis with features suggestive of corkscrew hairs and perifollicular extravasated erythrocytes (see comment)
    • Comment: The findings raise the possibility of vitamin C deficiency. The patient should be evaluated for scurvy and other forms of nutritional deficiency.
Differential diagnosis
  • Psoriasis:
    • Lacks spongiosis and epidermal pallor
    • Presence of neutrophilic collections in the stratum corneum (Munro microabscesses) and stratum spinosum (spongiform pustules of Kogoj)
    • Dilated vessels in the papillary dermis
    • Thinned suprapapillary plates
  • Pityriasis rubra pilaris:
    • Presence of hyperorthokeratosis alternating with parakeratosis horizontally and vertically with stratum corneum
    • Irregular psoriasiform dermatitis
    • Absence of spongiosis and epidermal pallor
  • Eczematous dermatitis:
    • Lacks epidermal pallor
    • Superficial perivascular infiltrate with eosinophils
  • Dermatophytosis:
    • Lacks epidermal pallor
    • Presence of hyphal organisms in the stratum corneum; can be highlighted by PAS stain
Board review style question #1
A 43 year old man with anorexia nervosa presents with extensive bruising, swelling of his gums and coiled hairs on his arms and legs. What is the most likely diagnosis?

  1. Acrodermatitis enteropathica
  2. Pellagra
  3. Scurvy
  4. Vitamin B1 deficiency
Board review style answer #1
C. Scurvy. This man is presenting with clinical features characteristic of vitamin C deficiency, also known as scurvy. The features include signs of bruising or petechiae, swelling or hypertrophy of gums with or without bleeding and hair associated changes, such as characteristic corkscrew hairs or perifollicular erythema. Additionally, this patient demonstrates a risk factor (anorexia nervosa) for nutritional deficiency due to restricted oral intake.

Answer A is incorrect because zinc deficiency classically presents with scaly erosive dermatitis in perioral and anogenital areas. Bruising, gum swelling and corkscrew hairs are not commonly associated with this diagnosis. Answer B is incorrect because pellagra usually presents with a photo distributed dermatitis as well as symptoms of diarrhea and cognitive impairment. Answer D is incorrect because vitamin B1 deficiency (or beriberi) usually presents with symptoms of cardiac or neurological impairment.

Board review style question #2


An exclusively breastfed 4 month old girl is brought into the clinic for a rash on the face, arms and anogenital areas. On exam, the rash appears as scaly, erosive plaques most prominent around the mouth, the vulvar region and the buttocks. Punch biopsy of the rash shows the histology findings in the photo. Which nutrient is most likely deficient?

  1. Copper
  2. Thiamine
  3. Vitamin C
  4. Zinc
Board review style answer #2
D. Zinc. This infant's rash, consisting of scaly, erosive plaques affecting the perioral and anogenital regions, is a classic description for acrodermatitis enteropathica. Given that the infant has been exclusively breastfed, this most likely represents a case of transient neonatal zinc deficiency due to inadequate levels of zinc in maternal breastmilk.

Answer B is incorrect because thiamine deficiency usually presents with waxy, edematous skin along with glossitis and cheilitis. Additionally, the lack of cardiac and neurological dysfunction also makes thiamine deficiency unlikely. Answer C is incorrect because vitamin C deficiency usually presents with signs of vessel fragility, such as bruising and petechiae, along with corkscrew hairs and hypertrophied or bleeding gums. Answer A is incorrect because copper deficiency usually presents with diffuse or patchy hypopigmentation of the skin and pili torti.
