Definition / general

* Viral infection that typically causes mild rash in children

Terminology

* Clinical syndrome is called "Fifth disease" or erythema infectiosum

Etiology

* Parvovirus B19 is spread by respiratory droplets, and has an incubation period of 5 - 14 days
* Viral receptor is the erythrocyte P antigen, which is expressed on endothelial cells
* Parvovirus attacks erythroblasts; may cause acute anemic crisis in patients with accelerated erythropoiesis, immunocompromised or minimal reserve (chronic anemia, hemolytic anemia, sickle cell, fetuses), although usually recipients have no symptoms ([Transfusion 2005;45:1811](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/16271109))
* Pregnant women may pass virus to fetus, where it may cause marked fetal anemia and hydrops
* Rash may be due to delayed type hypersensitivity, antibody dependent cellular immunity against microbial antigens in epidermis or endothelium, or circulating immune complexes

Clinical features

* Causes "Fifth disease" in children, a mild illness with a "slapped cheek" facial rash
* Characteristic skin manifestations are petechial eruption in a glove and stocking distribution, reticular truncal erythema and "slapped cheek" sign ([Hum Pathol 2000;31:488](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/10821497))
* May produce influenza-like illness, miscarriage, fetal hydrops and neonatal angioedema
* Atypical presentations include generalized petechial rash ([Pediatrics 2010;125:e787](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/20194277)), asymptomatic papular eruption or lower extremity palpable purpura, red baby syndrome ([Indian J Pediatr 2009;76:309](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/19347671)), papular purpuric gloves and socks syndrome ([Med Oral Patol Oral Cir Bucal 2007;12:E4](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/17195827)); may resemble Sweet syndrome, myopathic dermatomyosis or lupus erythematosus ([Intern Med 1998;37:708](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/9745861))
* Immune responses usually clear infection and provide lifelong protection but virus may persist in blood or tissue ([Transfus Med 2007;17:263](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/17680952))
* Parvovirus B19 has been associated with some cases of Rosai-Dorfman disease ([J Clin Pathol 2006;59:1320](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/17142576))

Case reports

* Parvovirus and acute hepatic failure in two children ([Am J Transplant 2014;14:2645](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/25179206))
* Parvovirus B19 induced transient anemia in an immunocompetent child ([Turk Patoloji Derg 2015;31:158](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/24994616))
* Parvovirus B19 in children with sickle cell anemia ([Exp Biol Med (Maywood) 2016;241:749](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/26940953))
* Sudden cardiac death from parvovirus in a young man with Brugada syndrome ([J Forensic Leg Med 2014;25:8](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/24931854))

Treatment

* Usually self resolving
* Can treat symptoms of fever, joint pain, swelling and itching
* Children and healthy adults have complete recovery

Microscopic (histologic) description

* Interstitial histiocytic infiltrate with piecemeal fragmentation of collagen and mononuclear cell-predominant vascular injury pattern
* Extravasated red blood cells
* Erythrocyte precursors may have intranuclear inclusions
* Eosinophils and occasional neutrophils may be present
* Perineuritis may also be present
* Also interface dermatitis, eczematous alterations, papillary dermal edema; occasionally mesenchymal mucinosis, leukocytoclastic vasculitis

Molecular / cytogenetics description

* Also identified in keratinocytes
