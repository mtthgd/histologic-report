Definition / general
  • Benign proliferation of melanocytes following the removal of a prior nevus (Mod Pathol 2009;22:611)
Essential features
  • Benign melanocyte proliferation following a prior benign nevus removal
  • Can clinically and histologically resemble melanoma in situ or potentially invasive melanoma with regression
  • If available, clinical history pertaining to a prior biopsy, previous procedure or recent trauma is extremely useful to avoid misdiagnosing melanoma
  • It is diagnostically essential to identify the presence of an area consistent with scar right beneath the melanocytic lesion
Terminology
  • Recurrent nevus / nevi phenomenon
  • Pseudomelanoma (Arch Pathol Lab Med 2011;135:842)
  • Persistent nevus
ICD coding
  • ICD-10: I78.1 - nevus, nonneoplastic
  • ICD-11: 2F20.Y - other specific types of melanocytic nevus
Epidemiology
  • F > M, with predilection for young adult females
  • Most commonly seen in patients 20 - 30 year of age (Arch Pathol Lab Med 2011;135:842)
  • Lesions are observed relatively soon after the removal of a prior benign melanocytic nevus (Mod Pathol 2009;22:611)
Sites
  • Most common site is the back (Mod Pathol 2009;22:611)
  • Can potentially present on any body site following removal of a benign nevus
Pathophysiology
  • Not well understood and remains to be further characterized, though several theories have been proposed including
    • Repopulation of melanocytes from an incomplete excision of a prior nevus (Mod Pathol 2009;22:611, Arch Pathol Lab Med 2011;135:842)
    • Repopulation of melanocytes from adjacent epidermis or from nearby adnexal structures (Mod Pathol 2009;22:611, Arch Pathol Lab Med 2011;135:842)
Etiology
  • Not well understood and not currently known

Clinical features
  • Recurrence is usually several months after removal of a benign nevus (Mod Pathol 2009;22:611)
  • Often presents as a pigmented lesion that can have irregular borders and discoloration
  • Can resemble melanoma clinically and histologically (Dermatol Pract Concept 2013;3:29, Mod Pathol 2009;22:611)
Diagnosis
  • Diagnosis is based on both histologic and clinicopathologic correlation
  • Clinical history of a prior biopsy or procedure is nearly essential
  • Dermoscopy may be of clinical use for evaluating recurrent lesions (JAMA Dermatol 2014;150:138)

Prognostic factors
  • Recurrent nevi are benign melanocytic lesions
Case reports
  • 6 year old girl with a melanocytic lesion on the right lower limb (Dermatol Pract Concept 2019;9:165)
  • 18 year old woman with 2 pigmented back lesions post-shave excision (JAAD Case Rep 2023;49:132)
  • 42 year old woman with a hyperpigmented and raised lesion on the left ear (Dermatopathology (Basel) 2023;10:201)
  • 64 year old woman with a pigmented left forehead lesion (Dermatopathology (Basel) 2022;9:361)
Treatment
  • No currently accepted consensus treatment or management guidelines (Dermatopathology (Basel) 2023;10:201)
  • Monitoring or complete excision may be warranted if lesions are clinically concerning (Arch Pathol Lab Med 2011;135:842)
  • Clinicians may choose to inform patients of possible recurrence of removed nevi (Arch Pathol Lab Med 2011;135:842)
  • Knowledge of a prior biopsy or procedure, if available, is useful to reduce the chances of a potential melanoma diagnosis (Mod Pathol 2009;22:611)

Microscopic (histologic) description
  • Prototypically described as having junctional nevic components overlying a scar (Arch Pathol Lab Med 2011;135:842)
  • Melanocytes can exhibit cytologic atypia (Mod Pathol 2009;22:611)
  • Mitotic activity is rarely observed (An Bras Dermatol 2017;92:531, Mod Pathol 2009;22:611, Arch Pathol Lab Med 2011;135:842)
  • Has been observed to display architectural asymmetry and confluent growth (J Cutan Pathol 2001;28:400)

Positive stains
  • Melanocytic markers such as SOX10, HMB45, S100, MART1 and MelanA
Negative stains
  • PRAME: usually highly expressed in melanocytes seen in melanoma while negative or of low expression in benign melanocytic lesions (Dermatopathology (Basel) 2023;10:201)

Recurrent nevus

Sample pathology report
  • Skin, right medial chest, excision:
    • Dermal scar with overlying recurrent or residual junctional melanocytic nevus, completely excised (see comment)
    • Comment: The most recent prior shave biopsy is noted. The residual lesion seen in this specimen is restricted to an area of scarring and shows prominent recurrent nevus changes. Immunohistochemical stains performed with appropriate controls show that the lesional cells are positive for SOX10 and demonstrate negative PRAME expression, supporting the diagnosis of a recurrent nevus.
Differential diagnosis
  • Melanoma:
    • Malignant melanocytes can display a variety of concerning features such as significant cytologic atypia, asymmetry, pagetoid spread and irregularly distributed melanocytic nests
    • Stains positive for melanoma markers but usually is diffusely PRAME positive compared to benign melanocytic lesions (Int J Mol Sci 2023;24:15881)
    • Except for desmoplastic melanoma, melanocytes in recurrent nevi are usually associated with an underlying scar (J Cutan Pathol 2001;28:400)
  • Dysplastic nevus:
    • Can show concerning architectural, histological and cytological features
    • Not associated with an underlying scar, as compared with recurrent nevi
    • More commonly found on sun exposed skin and the trunk (Am Fam Physician 2015;91:762)
  • Lentigo (solar lentigo):
    • Classically described as having a clinical appearance of a moth eaten border
    • Usually seen in the setting of sun damaged skin and solar elastosis
    • Not associated with an underlying scar or prior biopsy / procedure

Board review style question #1
A 32 year old woman presents for an evaluation of a 2.3 x 1.8 cm dichromatic pigmented lesion on the left upper back that is subsequently excised for histopathological evaluation. Clinically, the lesion has ill defined borders and variegated pigmentation. There is no clinical documentation of a prior biopsy or procedure performed on the site of excision. In addition to ordering melanocytic markers and PRAME, which of the following histological findings if identified, may be useful in supporting a diagnosis of a recurrent nevus?

  1. Evidence of a scar underlying observed pigmented lesional cells
  2. Melanocytes arranged in dyscohesive nests with cytological atypia
  3. Melanocytes with subtle pleomorphism, impaired depth of maturation and numerous mitoses
  4. Paucicellular spindle cell proliferations in the background of scar tissue
  5. Presence of full thickness epithelial atypia that is confined to the basal layer of the epidermis
Board review style answer #1
A. Evidence of a scar underlying observed pigmented lesional cells. The presence of a scar underlying lesional cells in the evaluation of a pigmented lesion supports the possibility of a recurrent nevus, assuming there are no other histological or IHC findings that are suggestive of malignancy. Answer D is incorrect because it describes findings that can be seen in desmoplastic melanomas. Answer E is incorrect because it describes findings seen in squamous cell carcinoma in situ. Answer B is incorrect because it describes nonspecific findings that can be seen in special site nevi or melanoma. Answer C is incorrect because it describes histological findings that can be seen in nevoid melanoma.

Board review style question #2


A colleague decides to consult you for an opinion regarding a case of theirs involving a melanocytic lesion. The lesion was a 2.3 x 3.4 cm pigmented lesion from the left lower back of a 35 year old woman. Clinically, the lesion was characterized as having irregular borders with uneven pigmentation. The referring clinician gives a differential diagnosis of melanoma on the laboratory requisition. Histologically, representative microscopic sections show melanocytes with mild atypia that are irregularly nested. The lesion is positive for SOX10 and HMB45. Additionally, the lesional cells seen demonstrate low but positive PRAME expression. What advice might you give to your colleague to help them narrow the diagnosis in this case?

  1. Ask whether they have any clinical history about prior biopsies for this patient
  2. Ask whether this patient has any history of autoimmune disorders
  3. Ask whether this patient has had any prior radiation exposure
  4. Suggest signing the case out as melanoma
Board review style answer #2
A. Ask whether they have any clinical history about prior biopsies for this patient. Asking for potential clinical history or knowledge about prior biopsies or procedures can be useful as recurrent nevi can clinically resemble melanoma. Though one could consider a diagnosis of melanoma, answer D is incorrect because the differential diagnosis of a recurrent nevus or other benign melanocytic lesion should be considered as a potential misdiagnosis of melanoma may result in a patient receiving unnecessary treatment. Answer B is incorrect because, although autoimmune disorders can be associated with hypopigmentation states such as vitiligo that may increase the risk of skin cancers, inquiring about such information in this particular situation is of questionable yield. Answer C is incorrect because obtaining information pertaining to prior radiation exposure is also of questionable utility.

Board review style question #3


A 28 year old woman presents to her dermatologist for an evaluation of a 3.5 x 2.5 cm pigmented lesion on her right shoulder that she has had since she was a teenager. The patient mentions that she has noticed this lesion grow over the last several months and is concerned about the possibility of melanoma. Clinical evaluation shows that the lesion is circular with well defined borders and is uniform in color. Further dermoscopy evaluation did not reveal any features that are concerning for malignancy. For cosmetic reasons, the patient elects to have this lesion removed. What anticipatory advice might the dermatologist want to give prior to removal of this lesion?

  1. Removal of this lesion may increase the risk of developing a subsequent basal cell carcinoma
  2. Removal of this lesion may increase the risk of developing a subsequent melanoma
  3. The lesion is not resectable
  4. There may be a recurrence of another pigmented lesion after removal of this initial lesion
Board review style answer #3
D. There may be a recurrence of another pigmented lesion after removal of this initial lesion. Removal of pigmented lesions, especially benign nevi, may result in the occurrence of new pigmented lesions (also known as the recurrent nevus phenomenon). Answer C is incorrect because there is not sufficient clinical information to comment on whether this particular lesion is resectable. Answers A and B are incorrect because there is no evidence to substantiate them.
