Definition / general

* WHO classification of pediatric melanocytic tumors highlights benign and malignant pediatric melanocytic neoplasms focusing on their developmental and genetic origins, such as neural crest migration in congenital melanocytic nevi; these neoplasms are among the most frequent and diagnostically challenging pediatric skin tumors ([Cancer Discov 2022;12:331](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/34921008), [Curr Opin Pediatr 1996;8:372](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/8954269), [Parham: Pediatric Malignancies - Pathology and Imaging, 1st Edition, 2015](https://www.amazon.com/exec/obidos/ASIN/149394214X/pathologyoutl-20))

Major updates

* WHO Classification of Pediatric Tumors (5th edition) is the inaugural pediatric volume in their series, which covers certain pediatric melanocytic neoplasms
* Melanocytic lesions not discussed in this volume are covered more comprehensively in the 5th edition of the WHO Classification of Skin Tumors (see [Skin melanocytic tumor - WHO classification](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/skintumormelanocyticWHO.html))

Board review style question #1

[![](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/imgau/skintumormelanocyticpediatricWHOSamieiDehner008.jpg)](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/imgau/skintumormelanocyticpediatricWHOSamieiDehner008.jpg)

A 14 year old girl presents with a pigmented macule on the dorsum of her hand. A histologic image of this lesion is shown above. What is the diagnosis?

1. Cellular blue nevus
2. Congenital nevus
3. Spindle cell nevus (Reed nevus)
4. Superficial spreading melanoma

Board review style answer #1

**A**. Cellular blue nevus. The lesion demonstrates fascicles of spindled to ovoid melanocytes dissecting through collagen bundles and forming cellular lobules in some areas, which are hallmark features of a cellular blue nevus.
Answer B is incorrect because congenital nevi feature a maturing compound nevus with a deep dermal component and periadnexal extension, which are absent in this lesion.
Answer C is incorrect because spindle cell nevus (Reed nevus) typically shows bland pigmented spindled cells forming vertically and horizontally oriented lobulated nests, which are absent in this lesion.
Answer D is incorrect because the lesion lacks features of melanoma, such as atypia, confluent growth and pagetoid spread.

Board review style question #2

[![](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/imgau/skintumormelanocyticpediatricWHOSamieiDehner04.jpg)](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/imgau/skintumormelanocyticpediatricWHOSamieiDehner04.jpg)

According to the WHO, how would this lesion be best classified?

1. Blue nevus
2. Congenital nevus
3. Junctional nevus
4. Superficial spreading melanoma

Board review style answer #2

**B**. Congenital nevus. Note the relatively large size of the nevus, with a typical papillomatous epidermis overlying a dense, bland, maturing melanocytic proliferation primarily concentrated in the papillary dermis. The proliferation grows downward, tracking along the adnexa into
the reticular dermis and occasionally the subcutis.
Answer A is incorrect because cellular blue nevi are usually composed of predominantly dermal, oval to spindle shaped melanocytes with dendritic processes growing within desmoplastic stroma.
Answer D is incorrect because the lesion lacks the architectural and cytologic features of melanoma, such as confluent junctional growth, lack of maturation and cytologic atypia.
Answer C is incorrect because the nevus shown in the image is a predominantly intradermal melanocytic proliferation.
