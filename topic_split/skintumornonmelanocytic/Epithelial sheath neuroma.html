Definition / general
  • Nonneoplastic proliferation of enlarged nerve fibers surrounded by squamous epithelium in the dermis
Essential features
  • Recently characterized and rare skin lesion that typically occurs on the upper back of women
  • This benign lesion consists of enlarged nerve fibers surrounded by mature squamous epithelium in the superficial dermis
  • Differential diagnosis includes reactive neuroepithelial aggregates, re-excision perineural invasion and traumatic neuroma
  • Excision is typically curative
Terminology
  • None
ICD coding
  • ICD-O: 9570/0 - neuroma, NOS
  • ICD-10: D36.1 - benign neoplasm of peripheral nerves and autonomic nervous system
  • ICD-11: 2F24 - benign cutaneous neoplasms of neural or nerve sheath origin
Epidemiology
  • Rare, with only 14 cases described in the literature
  • Reported cases are in individuals > 40 years old (JAAD Case Rep 2020;6:240)
  • Predominantly in females
Sites
  • Reported cases have occurred on the back, typically the upper back
Pathophysiology
  • Reactive hyperplasia of nerves and epithelium due to external stimuli like rubbing leading to the production of growth factors and cytokines secreted during the inflammatory process (Am J Surg Pathol 2001;25:696)
  • Overexpression of IL6 may mediate development, as it has been shown to be expressed in the epithelial sheath surrounding nerve bundles (Am J Dermatopathol 2017;39:49)
  • Metaplasia of the perineurium forms a squamous epithelial sheath in response to inflammation (J Cutan Pathol 2009;36:937)
  • Neural crest elements remaining in the dermis post development that differentiate into neural and squamous epithelial components (J Cutan Pathol 2009;36:937)
Etiology
  • Hypothesis that prurigo, chronic rubbing and inflammation may contribute (Am J Surg Pathol 2001;25:696)

Microscopic (histologic) description
  • Architecture
    • Ill defined, located in the papillary dermis, superficial reticular dermis and around follicles (Am J Surg Pathol 2001;25:696, J Cutan Pathol 2009;36:937)
    • Plaque-like growth pattern (Am J Surg Pathol 2001;25:696)
  • Cytological features
    • Multiple enlarged peripheral nerve fibers surrounded by mature squamous epithelium (Arch Toxicol 2011;85:1441)
    • Nerve bundles contain axons, Schwann cells and perineural cells (Am J Surg Pathol 2001;25:696)
    • Squamous sheath contains mature squamous epithelium and may have dyskeratotic keratinocytes
    • Keratinocytes typically do not show atypia, pleomorphism or mitoses (Clin Neuropathol 2020;39:232)
    • Neuroepithelial bundles may be surrounded by myxoid stroma and lymphocytic infiltrates

Positive stains
  • S100, neurofilament, CD57, nerve growth factor receptor (NGFR) (positive immunostaining of the neural component) (Am J Surg Pathol 2000;24:190)
  • Cytokeratins, with specifically MNF116 cytokeratin showing positivity in the epithelial component
Negative stains
  • CEA, EMA (Am J Surg Pathol 2000;24:190)
  • Some cytokeratin cocktails including CAM5.2 have shown weak or negative expression (Am J Surg Pathol 2000;24:190)

Sample pathology report
  • Skin, shave, punch, incisional or excisional biopsy:
    • Epithelial sheath neuroma (see comment)
    • Comment: In the dermis, there are multiple enlarged neural fibers ensheathed by mature squamous epithelium with surrounding myxoid stroma. Though this is a recently characterized benign neoplasm, complete re-excision could be considered to prevent recurrence.
Differential diagnosis
  • Reactive neuroepithelial aggregates:
    • Contain fewer neuroepithelial bundles (1 - 2), no enlarged nerves, only reported on the face and neck, show connection to overlying epidermis (Arch Toxicol 2011;85:1441, Clin Neuropathol 2020;39:232)
  • Re-excision perineural invasion:
    • Limited to previous biopsy site (Arch Toxicol 2011;85:1441)
  • Traumatic and idiopathic neuromas:
    • Lack an epithelial sheath (Am J Surg Pathol 2000;24:190)
  • Regression of neoplasms:
    • Would have features of regression such as fibrillary collagen and vertically oriented vessels (Clin Neuropathol 2020;39:232)
  • Cutaneous hamartomas (including striated muscle hamartoma, neurofollicular hamartoma and congenital neural hamartoma):
    • Nerve fibers are not surrounded by an epithelial sheath (Am J Surg Pathol 2000;24:190)
Additional references
  • Am J Dermatopathol 2006;28:216, Am J Dermatopathol 2015;37:176, J Cutan Pathol 2009;36:570, Am J Dermatopathol 2006;28:423
Board review style question #1


A 65 year old woman with no history of skin cancer presents with a tender papule on her upper back. A skin biopsy is performed and is notable for a proliferation of enlarged nerve fibers in the superficial dermis surrounded by squamous epithelium without atypia. She has no history of prior skin biopsy or trauma to this area. Which of the following is the most likely diagnosis?

  1. Epithelial sheath neuroma
  2. Reactive neuroepithelial aggregates
  3. Re-excision perineural invasion
  4. Traumatic neuroma
Board review style answer #1
A. Epithelial sheath neuroma. Answer B is incorrect because reactive neuroepithelial aggregates contain neuroepithelial bundles without enlargement of the nerves and are reported to occur on the face and neck. Answer C is incorrect because the patient in the question has no prior history of skin cancer or skin biopsy in this area. Answer D is incorrect because traumatic neuroma lacks an epithelial sheath.
