Definition / general
  • This topic describes how to gross specimens obtained from various nail biopsy techniques
  • Essential clinical history
    • When a nail biopsy is submitted for histological examination, the biopsy site, laterality and technique must be provided
    • Adequate information of the clinical findings, such as nail pigmentation, discoloration, onycholysis or nail bed tumor, must be obtained in order to guide proper gross sampling and sectioning
    • Clinical impression and differential diagnosis provided by the submitting physician is of utmost importance to guide the pathologist to a good gross examination and ultimately a correct diagnosis
    • Rarely, lack of adequate clinical history or clinical indications may be encountered; in such a case, searching the patient's medical record or a phone call to the physician must be attempted
Techniques
  • There are many types and techniques of nail biopsy; the most frequently performed and encountered types of nail biopsies in the routine practice of dermatopathology are described below
    • Nail plate biopsy
      • This is the easiest nail biopsy technique and is often indicated for cases with onycholysis suspected for onychomycosis
      • This is performed by nail clipping to be sent for histopathological examination, including PAS staining
      • Indications other than onychomycosis include subungual wart and psoriasis
      • Onycholyzed nail plate is clipped (at least 3 - 4 mm in size) along with as much adequate subungual keratosis as possible (Indian Dermatol Online J 2018;9:3)
    • Nail bed biopsy
      • This is one of the most commonly performed biopsy techniques and often indicated to confirm the suspected clinical diagnosis or in cases in which the clinical presentation is atypical (e.g., onychomycosis, lichen planus and psoriasis)
      • It is also recommended for other conditions, such as nail discoloration, painful nail bed or tumors
      • Can be performed either by excision or punch biopsy
      • Excision requires avulsion of the nail plate while punch biopsy has the advantage of preserving the plate and therefore saving the nail bed epithelium for proper histological evaluation (Indian Dermatol Online J 2018;9:3)
    • Nail matrix biopsy
      • This is considered the most difficult technique of nail biopsy due to the risk of permanent scarring; it is commonly indicated for longitudinal nail pigmentation, such as nail lichen planus (Indian Dermatol Online J 2018;9:3)
      • Punch biopsy is the most common technique performed to sample the nail matrix (Calonje: McKee's Pathology of the Skin, 5th Edition, 2019)
      • Other indications include tumors arising from the nail matrix, such as onychopapilloma and glomus tumor, in which this technique can be therapeutic as well (Indian Dermatol Online J 2018;9:3)
    • Lateral longitudinal nail biopsy
      • This biopsy technique offers examination of the whole nail apparatus and therefore provides valuable information to the pathologist (Calonje: McKee's Pathology of the Skin, 5th Edition, 2019)
      • Technique begins from the distal interphalangeal joint all the way to the hyponychium; involving the proximal nail fold and nail plate (Haneke: Histopathology of the Nail - Onychopathology, 1st Edition, 2017)
    • Proximal nail fold biopsy
      • This biopsy may be indicated in certain diseases, such as collagen vascular disease, tumors and other systemic diseases
      • Nail fold sampling can be performed via punch biopsy; for superficial lesions, a biopsy including the epidermis and the superficial aspect of the dermis is sufficient
      • Full thickness biopsy of the nail fold to include the nail plate can be performed by 2 mm punch (Haneke: Histopathology of the Nail - Onychopathology, 1st Edition, 2017)
Procedure
  • Biopsy handling
    • Nail biopsies are fragile and careful handling is required
    • Immediate transfer of the biopsy into formalin fixative is encouraged to prevent dry out
    • Nail clippings and subungual hyperkeratosis should not be placed in formalin as it hardens the nail and therefore makes it difficult to cut
    • Soft tissue biopsies of the nail apparatus without nail plate are placed routinely in formalin; however, if both the soft tissue and nail plate are sampled, consider using nail softener
    • Appropriate inking of the biopsy and placement on a paper template are recommended to maintain orientation
  • Softening of the nail
    • Cedarwood oil is by far the best material used to soften the nail, thereby enabling smooth cutting
    • Nail thereafter can be processed as usual with microtome and H&E staining
  • Mounting nail sections
    • If the nail biopsy includes nail plate, it often gets detached from the glass slide when dry; therefore, pretreated charged slide is needed to keep the tissue adherent (Haneke: Histopathology of the Nail - Onychopathology, 1st Edition, 2017)
  • Staining
    • H&E stain is sufficient in most instances; special stains such as PAS and GMS may be applied upon the request of the physician or if the pathologist suspects fungal infections (onychomycosis)
    • Immunofluorescence and immunostains can be performed in certain cases
Sections to obtain
  • Most nail specimens submitted to the lab are small and therefore entirely submitted in one cassette
  • Nail biopsies < 5 mm in diameter are placed as such in a cassette while those > 5 mm in diameter should be bisected (one cassette)
  • Nail biopsies, such as nail plate with subungual hyperkeratosis, must be placed on edge (1 - 3 cassettes)
  • Large nail specimens, including digit amputation, must be thoroughly sampled and require total submission most of the time, especially in melanoma
  • Nail amputation specimens are serially sectioned along the longitudinal axis, with the necessary application of ink on resection margins (bone and soft tissue margins) and decalcification of bone
  • For large tumor specimens where total submission is not possible, submit representative sections as follows: margins and 1 section per each 1 cm of the tumor
  • It is recommended in amputation specimens to draw a diagram of the digit along with the nail apparatus to perform mapping of sections as this greatly helps in correlating the microscopic interpretation (Semin Cutan Med Surg 2010;29:148, Lester: Manual of Surgical Pathology, 3rd Edition, 2010)
Tips
  • Diagram of the nail unit showing where sections were taken from (section mapping) is an important step
  • Provide detailed description of the lesion / tumor, including size, color, consistency, border description, symmetry and distance to margins

Gross description
  • Nail psoriasis: extensive paronychia, pulpits, subungual hyperkeratosis, marked distal nail plate thinning and splitting (Dermatol Ther 2007;20:11)
  • Melanoma: black and brown longitudinal melanonychia that may partially or totally involve the nail plate (Dermatol Ther 2007;20:11)
  • Squamous cell carcinoma: subungual keratotic growth that may encroach on lateral folds; there may be a longitudinal pigmentation of the plate (Dermatol Ther 2007;20:11)
  • Onychomatricoma: longitudinal yellowish thickening of the nail plate (Skin Appendage Disord 2019;5:165)

Board review style question #1


A 3 mm nail clipping from the right big toe of a 64 year old man is received fresh to the pathology laboratory from the dermatology clinic. The pathology technical staff called you to confirm how to gently handle and process the sample appropriately. The requisition sheet indicates onycholysis suspected for fungal infections. As a dermatopathologist, which one of the following steps would you like to convey to the technical team to ensure an appropriate and gentle handling of the nail clipping sample?

  1. Bisect the sample, put it in formalin for a few hours and then use nail softener
  2. Don't place it in formalin but instead put it on a paper template in a cassette followed by the use of nail softener, routine sectioning and staining on a pretreated charged slide
  3. Immediately put it in formalin for few hours followed by nail softener, sectioning and staining
  4. Place it on a paper template in a cassette then put it in formalin; use nail softener and pretreated charged slide
  5. Serially section the sample then place in formalin followed by using nail softener
Board review style answer #1
B. Don't place it in formalin but instead put it on a paper template in a cassette followed by the use of nail softener, routine sectioning and staining on a pretreated charged slide. These are the correct handling and processing steps as the nail clipping requires careful and gentle handling. Answers A, C, D and E are incorrect because the nail clipping should not be placed in formalin, which hardens the tissue and makes the cutting more difficult. Additionally, a nail clipping or biopsy < 5 mm must not be bisected or sectioned.
