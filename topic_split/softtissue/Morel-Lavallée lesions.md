Definition / general

* Morel-Lavallée lesions (MLL) are trauma induced soft tissue injuries that most commonly arise from motor vehicle accidents or blunt force trauma and often present with concomitant fractures
* They result when shear forces separate subcutaneous tissue from underlying fascia, allowing fat and fluid to accumulate

Essential features

* Closed degloving injury with a history of trauma
* Commonly misdiagnosed due to distracting injuries or mistaken for other benign and neoplastic entities
* Asymptomatic or painful and fluctuant soft tissue lesions that contain blood and fat debris and may have overlying skin necrosis or discoloration, loss of cutaneous sensation and skin hypermobility
* Most common pathologic features are reactive fibroconnective tissue, fat necrosis, fibrin deposition and pseudocyst or cystic components
* Treatment varies on size and severity of the lesion, though chronic lesions with fibrous capsules will persist until treated surgically

Terminology

* Morel-Lavallée seroma or effusion
* Posttraumatic soft tissue cyst
* Posttraumatic extravasation
* Closed degloving soft tissue lesion
* Chronic expanding hematoma
* Reference: [Orthop Surg 2023;15:2485](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/37526135)

ICD coding

* ICD-10: [T79.2XXA](https://www.icd10data.com/ICD10CM/Codes/S00-T88/T79-T79/T79-/T79.2XXA) - traumatic secondary and recurrent hemorrhage and seroma

Epidemiology

* M = F
* Unilateral
* No specific age predilection, rare in children
* Polytrauma patients or those with pelvic or lower extremity fractures
* References: [Emerg Radiol 2014;21:35](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/23949106), [J Trauma Acute Care Surg 2014;76:493](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/24458056), [Chin Med J (Engl) 2014;127:1351](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/24709193)

Sites

* Most common within soft tissue of the pelvis and lower extremities
* Less commonly along the lumbosacral or cervicothoracic spine, upper extremities and abdominal wall
* Reference: [Orthop Surg 2023;15:2485](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/37526135)

Pathophysiology

* MLL occur in areas where the overlying skin is mobile and the underlying tissue is fixed
* Traumatic shearing forces separate these soft tissue planes and create a potential space for blood and fat debris to accumulate
* As the body attempts to resorb the contents, a chronic inflammatory reaction occurs within surrounding tissues and may lead to the formation of a fibrous capsule
* Fibrous capsule prevents any additional resorption of the cavitary contents and indicates a mature chronic lesion
* These lesions are often misdiagnosed as benign and neoplastic processes or missed due to distracting injuries
* Misdiagnosis and delayed presentation can lead to morbidity due to infection, expansion, fibrous capsule formation and recurrence
* References: [J Clin Diagn Res 2017;11:TE01](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/28571232), [Emerg Radiol 2014;21:35](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/23949106)

Etiology

* History of high impact trauma, most commonly motor vehicle accidents or blunt force trauma
* Blunt force trauma etiologies are typically sports related or due to mechanical falls
* Rare case reports without a history of trauma have been reported ([Ir J Med Sci 2011;180:265](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/19618237))

Clinical features

* Typically present within hours to days of inciting event, though 33% of cases may present months to years later
* Often seen with concomitant fractures that may be distracting injuries
* Area of ecchymosis, swelling, skin fluctuance and hypermobility
* Decreased cutaneous sensation of skin overlying injury due to nerve shearing
* Rarely seen with mammoplasty or liposuction procedures ([Br J Plast Surg 2005;58:849](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/16086993))
* References: [Emerg Radiol 2014;21:35](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/23949106), [Orthop Surg 2023;15:2485](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/37526135)

Prognostic factors

* Concomitant fractures and distracting injuries at initial presentation can result in delayed MLL diagnosis and long term morbidity due to wound expansion, infection, capsule formation and recurrence
* Most lesions, especially those that are large or chronic, will require surgical intervention to remove any capsular components and close the dead space

Case reports

* 12 month old girl with fluctuant mass of back and buttocks ([Int J Surg Case Rep 2018;49:180](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/30025335))

* 57 year old woman with breast mass ([Cureus 2023;15:e42979](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/37671210))

Treatment

* Surgical debridement and excision are the preferred methods for definitive treatment, especially in large and chronic cases
* Other interventional measures like aspiration, sclerodesis, fibrin sealant and low suction drains have been used with limited success
* Conservative management including observation and compression are often used in acute or misdiagnosed cases but are not recommended due to high risk of lesion recurrence and complications
* References: [Orthop Surg 2023;15:2485](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/37526135), [Arch Orthop Trauma Surg 2013;133:635](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/23443527)

Microscopic (histologic) description

* Prominent reactive fibroconnective tissue with reactive myofibroblasts
* Fat necrosis and prominent fibrin deposition
* Pseudocystic or cystic component
* Fibrous capsule
* Calcifications, hemosiderin deposition, macrophages
* Mellado and Bencardino classification into 6 subtypes ([Magn Reson Imaging Clin N Am 2005;13:775](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/16275583))
  + Type I is a serohematic effusion (seroma)
  + Type II is a subacute hematoma
  + Type III is a chronic organizing hematoma
  + Type IV represents perifascial dissection with closed fatty laceration
  + Type V is a perifascial pseudonodular lesion
  + Type VI represents an infected lesion with variable sinus tract formation, internal septations and thick enhancing capsule
* Reference: [Pathology 2018;50:693](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/30145069)

Clinicopathologic description of Morel-Lavallée lesions

Sample pathology report

* Soft tissue, left leg, excision:
  + Soft tissue with necrosis, fibrosis and chronic inflammation consistent with Morel-Lavallée lesion
* Soft tissue, right leg mass, excision:
  + Reactive fibroadipose tissue with associated pseudocyst formation (see comment)
  + Comment: Sections show a reactive fibroadipose tissue with associated fat necrosis and pseudocyst formation between the dense scarring fibrous tissue and deep subcutaneous tissue. This space contains fibrin bands, hemorrhage and has granulation tissue and reactive myofibroblasts at the periphery. The overall histologic findings in the proper clinical setting are compatible with Morel-Lavallée lesion.

Differential diagnosis

* [Subcutaneous hematoma](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/softtissuehematoma.html):
  + Rapid appearance after trauma
  + Occurs anywhere where trauma occurs, especially over bony prominences
  + Usually no skin hypermobility
  + No capsule formation, predominantly blood and fibrin on histology
* [Fat necrosis](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/skinnontumorencapsulatedfatnecrosis.html):
  + Firm, irregular, nontender mass
  + No fibrous capsule
* [Lipoma](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/softtissueadiposelipoma.html):
  + Soft, mobile, nontender, slow growing homogenous mass
  + Most often occurs in the absence of trauma
  + Solid, no fat fluid level on imaging
  + Mature benign adipose tissue on histology
* [Abscess](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/skinnontumorabscess.html):
  + Painful, tender, erythematous, fluctuant mass
  + Systemic symptoms may be present
  + Rim enhancing fluid collection, hyperemic walls on ultrasound Doppler
  + Collection of acute and chronic inflammatory cells on histology
* **Soft tissue sarcoma**:
  + Rapidly growing, firm, deep mass
  + Heterogeneous mass with poorly defined margins and irregular enhancement
  + Malignant features on histology

Board review style question #1

A 36 year old man presents to the emergency department following a motorcycle accident in which he was thrown off the vehicle and landed on his left hip. Physical examination reveals significant swelling, skin hypermobility and tenderness over the lateral aspect of his hip. An MRI shows a fat fluid collection between the subcutaneous tissue and underlying fascia. Which of the following best describes the pathophysiology of his condition?

1. Avulsion of subcutaneous tissue from underlying fascia, with lymphovascular and adipose tissue disruption
2. Degenerative changes in the subcutaneous fat with fibrous tissue deposition
3. Hematoma formation within underlying muscle fibers due to blunt force trauma
4. Rupture of fascia with herniation of underlying muscle into the subcutaneous space

Board review style answer #1

**A.** Avulsion of subcutaneous tissue from underlying fascia, with lymphovascular and adipose tissue disruption. Morel-Lavallée lesions (MLLs) are closed degloving injuries following high impact trauma as described in this case, liberating fat and fluid into the potential space. Answers B, C and D are other nonspecific findings that do not support the pathophysiology of MLLs.

Board review style question #2

[![BRQ image](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/imgau/softtissuemorellavalleehrubydehner08.jpg)](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/imgau/softtissuemorellavalleehrubydehner08.jpg)

A 50 year old woman presents to the clinic 4 months following a traumatic accident where he fell from a ladder and fractured his femur. He reports persistent swelling in this area and physical examination reveals a firm, fluctuant mass with discoloration of the overlying skin. MRI demonstrates a subcutaneous fluid collection with evidence of a fibrous capsule, as shown on the histologic image above. What is the most appropriate treatment option for this chronic lesion?

1. Aspiration and subcutaneous steroid injections
2. Conservative management with compression and close follow up
3. Debridement and excision
4. Incision and drainage with placement of surgical drain

Board review style answer #2

**C.** Debridement and excision. Chronic Morel-Lavallée lesions (MLLs) are associated with capsule formations due to prolonged fluid collection and fibrosis and often recur or cause significant morbidity when treated conservatively. Debridement and complete excision of the fibrous capsule is the best definitive treatment option. Answers A, B and D are other treatment modalities that are not recommended for definitive management of chronic MLLs.
