Definition / general
  • Nuchal type fibromas are benign fibrous tumors of undetermined etiology frequently occurring in the posterior neck (70% of cases) of males (M:F = 4:1), which are associated with diabetes mellitus (50% of cases) and repetitive trauma
  • These tumors are histologically characterized by poorly circumscribed hypocellular aggregates of dense, thick, haphazard collagen fibers with a cracked mud appearance which entrap native structures
Essential features
  • Thickened bundles of collagen separated by cleft-like spaces (Head Neck Pathol 2020;14:43)
  • Entrapment of native structures (adipose tissue, nerve, muscle)
  • Characteristically occurs in posterior neck of adult men
    • If present in a child / adolescent, must rule out Gardner fibroma, a sentinel lesion of Gardner syndrome
Terminology
  • Nuchal type fibroma
  • Nuchal fibroma
  • Collagenosis nuchae (not recommended; antiquated) (Clin Exp Dermatol 1988;13:263)
  • Historical note: as recent as the year 2000, the term nuchal type fibroma encompassed both Gardner fibroma and nuchal type fibroma; these are now recognized by the WHO as distinct entities (Am J Surg Pathol 2000;24:1563)
ICD coding
  • ICD-O: 8810/0 - nuchal fibroma
  • ICD-10: D21 - other benign neoplasms of connective and other soft tissue
    • Subcategorized by anatomic location
    • Examples
      • D21.0 - benign neoplasm of connective and other soft tissue of head, face and neck
      • D21.9 - benign neoplasm of connective and other soft tissue, unspecified
  • ICD-11: EE6Y & XH0XH6 - other specified fibromatous disorders of skin and soft tissue & nuchal fibroma
Epidemiology
  • Male predominance (Cancer 1999;85:156)
    • M:F = 4:1
  • Adults
    • Peak incidence: 40 - 50 years (Head Neck Pathol 2020;14:43, Cancer 1999;85:156)
  • Up to 50% occur in patients with diabetes mellitus (Cancer 1999;85:156)
Sites
  • Dermis and subcutis
    • Posterior neck soft tissues (70%) (Cancer 1999;85:156, Head Neck Pathol 2020;14:43, Am J Surg Pathol 1995;19:313)
    • Uncommon extranuchal sites
      • Upper back
      • Shoulder
      • Head (scalp, face, oral cavity)
      • Extremities
      • Buttocks
Pathophysiology
  • Undetermined
Etiology
  • Undetermined
    • Up to 50% associated with diabetes mellitus but no proven causative relationship (Cancer 1999;85:156)
    • Reported association with recurrent trauma (Trauma Case Rep 2016;4:16, Plast Reconstr Surg Glob Open 2024;12:e5517)
Clinical features
  • Long history of a slow growing lesion
  • Painless
  • Solitary
    • Rare exceptions are multifocal (Ann Dermatol 2015;27:194)
Diagnosis
  • Histological diagnosis: hypocellular collagenous lesion, often with small nerve bundles
  • Bland CD34 positive spindle cells
Radiology description
  • Computed tomography (CT) (Int J Dermatol 2005;44:703)
    • High density mass
    • Ill definded borders
    • Adherent to deep dermis or fascia
  • Magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) (Jpn J Radiol 2010;28:538)
    • T1: low signal intensity
    • T2: low (or mixed) signal intensity
    • STIR: low signal intensity
    • T1 C+ (Gd): avid enhancement
  • Ultrasound (J Ultrasound 2024 Jan 16 [Epub ahead of print], In Vivo 2020;34:2217)
    • Hypoechoic ill defined lesion situated in dermis - hypodermis (B mode)
    • Increased vascularization at the periphery of the lesion (color and power Doppler)

Prognostic factors
  • Benign neoplasm
    • Overall favorable prognosis
  • Reported local recurrence rates: 13 - 20% (Cancer 1999;85:156, In Vivo 2020;34:2217)
Case reports
  • Nuchal site
    • 23 year old woman and 50 year old man with posterior neck mass (In Vivo 2020;34:2217)
    • 25 year old man with posterior midline neck mass related to repetitive trauma from weight lifting (Plast Reconstr Surg Glob Open 2024;12:e5517)
  • Extranuchal sites
    • 21 - 32 year old men concurrently developed shoulder masses related to repetitive trauma from bearing a palanquin (Trauma Case Rep 2016;4:16)
    • 32 year old man with multiple (6) scalp masses and no history of Gardner syndrome or diabetes mellitus (Ann Dermatol 2015;27:194)
    • 80 year old woman with intraoral (floor of mouth) mass and diabetes mellitus (J Stomatol Oral Maxillofac Surg 2019;120:75)
Treatment
  • Complete excision (In Vivo 2020;34:2217)
  • Monitor for recurrence

Microscopic (histologic) description
  • Markedly hypocellular
    • Bland spindled fibroblastic cells lining dense cords of collagen
  • Thick haphazard collagen bundles
    • Cracked mud pattern: clefts separate bundles
  • Entrapment of native structures
    • Adipose tissue
    • Skeletal muscle
    • Nerves
      • Proliferative nerve twigs are sometimes present
      • Similar appearance to peripheral traumatic neuroma
    • Deep skin adnexa
  • Unencapsulated
    • Irregular or infiltrative border
  • Scant elastic fibers on elastic stain (EVG) (Skeletal Radiol 2022;51:681)
  • Scattered lymphocytes (variably present)

Negative stains

Molecular / cytogenetics description
  • Unknown
  • Lesions found to contain APC mutations (germline) are most likely Gardner fibromas, not nuchal type fibromas (Transl Pediatr 2023;12:1715)
    • Pathologists must be aware that Gardner fibroma may be the sentinel lesion in Gardner syndrome, a subtype of familial adenomatosis polyposis (FAP) that has extracolonic manifestations (Eur J Hum Genet 2015;23:715)

Gardner fibroma versus nuchal type fibroma

Sample pathology report
  • Soft tissue, posterior neck mass, excision:
    • Nuchal type fibroma (see comment)
    • Comment: Hypocellular haphazard bundles of dense collagen are seen entrapping native adipose and nerves. Proliferative nerve sprouts (similar to those found in a traumatic neuroma) are present. Supported by the location of the mass, these features are diagnostic of nuchal type fibroma. While Gardner fibroma is histologically very similar and may be a sentinel lesion of Gardner syndrome, it lacks the nerve sprouting seen in this mass and generally occurs in children.
Differential diagnosis
  • Gardner fibroma:
    • Histologically identical, except lacks traumatic neuroma-like nerve proliferation
      • Nerve sprouting is not always present in nuchal type fibroma but when present is helpful in excluding Gardner fibroma
    • Most common site: paraspinal trunk (only 15% in head and neck) (Am J Surg Pathol 2007;31:410)
    • Most occur in children
    • Associated with Gardner syndrome, a subtype of FAP (Eur J Hum Genet 2015;23:715)
    • Beta catenin+ (nuclear) in 66% of cases (Am J Surg Pathol 2007;31:410, J Pediatr Hematol Oncol 2016;38:e154)
  • Spindle cell / pleomorphic lipoma:
    • More cellular with parallel arrays of fibroblast-like cells (school of fish pattern)
    • Floret pattern nuclei
    • Lipoblasts present in 40% of cases
    • Frequent mast cells
    • RB1 loss of nuclear staining
  • Desmoid fibromatosis:
    • More cellular with long sweeping fascicles of parallel spindle cells
    • Homogenous stroma, usually lacking dense collagen bundles
      • Exception: hyalinized keloidal collagen is sometimes present (especially in intraabdominal tumors)
    • Uncommon in subcutaneous tissues
    • CD34-, beta catenin+ (nuclear)
  • Keloid scar:
    • Hyalinized collagen bundles lacking cracked mud pattern (clefts)
    • Situated in the dermis
    • Regions of dense collagen transition to peripheral hypertrophic scar tissue
    • Clinical history of injury or surgical incision
  • Desmoplastic fibroblastoma:
    • Homogenous, delicate collagen
    • Well circumscribed
    • Most common site: upper extremity (rare in head and neck)
    • CD34-
  • Elastofibroma:
    • Thick, fragmented, globular elastic fibers (highlighted by elastic stain)
    • Most common site: scapular border
  • Solitary fibrous tumor:
    • More cellular with patternless spindle cells
    • Usually lacks dense collagen bundles
    • Staghorn (hemangiopericytoma-like) vessels
    • STAT6+
  • Low grade fibromyxosarcoma:
    • Myxoid regions present
    • Abrupt transitions between collagenous and myxoid regions
    • Lacks diffusely thickened collagen bundles
    • Collagen rosettes may be present
    • MUC4+

Board review style question #1


A 46 year old man was found to have the lesion shown in the image above in the subcutaneous soft tissues of the lateral neck. Which of the following is most likely?

  1. Lesion is locally aggressive and may be treated by wide local excision, radiotherapy or small molecular inhibitors
  2. Lesion occurred as a result of chromosomal translocation
  3. Lesion occurred at a previous surgical incision site
  4. Patient has an elevated HgbA1c
  5. Patient has innumerable colonic polyps
Board review style answer #1
D. Patient has an elevated HgbA1c. The image shows dense haphazard bundles of collagen and bland spindle cells entrapping adipose tissue and a proliferation of small entrapped nerves. These features, the patient's demographics and the location of the lesion are compatible with nuchal type fibroma. 50% of nuchal type fibromas occur in patients with diabetes mellitus. Answer B is incorrect because the abrupt transition between collagenous and myxoid areas and collagen rosettes typical of low grade fibromyxosarcoma are not seen. Low grade fibromyxosarcoma is most often caused by a (7;17)(q34;p11) translocation resulting in a FUS::CREB3L2 fusion. Answer C is incorrect because keloid scar is associated with growth at sites of prior trauma and is characterized by dense hyalinized collagen bundles lacking cracked mud cleft spaces, which do not entrap surrounding tissues. Answer E is incorrect because Gardner fibroma is an extracolonic manifestation of Gardner syndrome, a subtype of familial adenomatous polyposis (FAP), wherein innumerable colonic polyps may be present. While histopathological features of these are very similar, nuchal type fibroma is distinguished from Gardner fibroma by entrapped small nerve bundles in a pattern similar to peripheral traumatic neuroma. Answer A is incorrect because the long fascicles of parallel spindle cells within a collagenous stroma typical of desmoid fibromatosis are not seen. Unlike nuchal type fibroma, desmoid fibromatosis is locally aggressive and treated with radiotherapy and oral small molecule inhibitors (e.g., gamma secretase inhibitor [GSI], nirogacestat).

Board review style question #2


In which of the following soft tissue sites and patient demographics does this lesion most frequently occur?

  1. Site: distal phalanx; demographic: infant
  2. Site: ear pinna; demographic: Black woman
  3. Site: neck; demographic: adult male
  4. Site: palm; demographic: White European man
  5. Site: paraspinal trunk; demographic: child
Board review style answer #2
C. Site: neck; demographic: adult male. The image shows dense haphazard bundles of collagen and bland spindle cells infiltrating adipose tissue and a proliferation of small entrapped nerves. These features are typical of nuchal type fibroma. This neoplasm most frequently occurs in the soft tissues of the neck (70%) of adult men (M:F = 4:1). Answer A is incorrect because this site and demographic is typical of inclusion body fibromatosis, which has interlacing short fascicles and eosinophilic intracytoplasmic inclusion bodies. Answer E is incorrect because this site and demographic is typical of Gardner fibroma, which has similar histological features to nuchal type fibroma but lacks the entrapped proliferative small nerve bundles visible in the image. Answer B is incorrect because this site and demographic is typical of keloid scar (typically related to ear piercing), which has dense hyalinized collagen bundles lacking the cracked mud pattern clefts visible in the image. Answer D is incorrect because this site and demographic is typical of palmar fibromatosis (Dupuytren contracture), which has moderately cellular proliferative parallel fascicles of spindle cells forming nodules in a background of homogenous collagenous stroma.
