Definition / general

* Polyarteritis nodosa (PAN) is the most common form of testicular vasculitis, predominantly seen in the fourth to sixth decades of life; often associated with hepatitis B virus and human immunodeficiency virus; although 60% to 86% of patients with PAN show testicular involvement at autopsy ([ScientificWorldJournal 2010;10:1915](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/20890580)), testicular symptoms are present in less than 20% of patients ([Hum Pathol 1988;19:186](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/2893767))
* Necrotizing vasclulitis: systemic disease which manifests as inflammation of arteries with fibrinoid necrosis and fibrosis of the vascular wall
  + May be associated with polyarteritis nodosa or an isolated finding; can be associated with a variety of other entities or may be idiopathic ([Cardiovascular Pathology 1994;3:197](http://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/1054880794900302))
  + While necrotizing vasculitis can involve any organ, isolated testicular involvement is rare and can pose diagnostic difficulties, especially in the absence of clinical and laboratory evidence of systemic involvement ([World J Surg Oncol 2011;9:63](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/21672251))
* Other vasculitides that may involve the testes include [Granulomatosis with polyangiitis (Wegener's)](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/lungnontumorgranulomatosiswithpolyangiitis.html), [Henoch-Schönlein purpura](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/kidneyhsp.html), [giant cell arteritis](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/breastgiantcellarteritis.html) and various autoimmune connective tissue disorders such as [systemic lupus erythematosis](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/kidneysle.html) ([J Urol 2006;176:2682](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/17085194))

Clinical features

* Can present with acute onset of pain and swelling, presumably related to intratesticular hemorrhage ([Ir Med J 2006;99:27](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/16506690))
* Systemic disease is frequently already present at the time of initial presentation of testicular vasculitis or develops shortly thereafter
* Some cases, however, remain isolated to the testes with no evidence of progression to systemic disease ([J Clin Pathol 1994;47:1121](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/7876390))
* It is, therefore, unclear whether isolated testicular vasculitis represents an early site of involvement by systemic disease or a separate entity ([World J Surg Oncol 2011;9:63](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/21672251))
* Only several cases of isolated testicular vasculitis were reported in the literature and a presentation suggestive of a testicular neoplasm is even less common ([J Clin Pathol 1994;47:1121](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/7876390))
* Since testicular biopsy is contraindicated when malignancy is suspected, the diagnosis is usually reported postorchiectomy in these cases ([ScientificWorldJournal 2010;10:1915](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/20890580), [Urology 2003;61:1035](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/12736037))
* Biopsy most likely diagnostic in patients with testicular symptoms (pain, enlargement or shrinking)
* Wedge biopsy should contain capsule, tunica vasculosa and testicular parenchyma

Treatment

* Treatment options are still debated and frequently hinge on individual factors which need to be carefully considered in each patient
* Immunosuppressive therapy has been administered to some of the patients
* In other cases, careful follow up to exclude progression to systemic disease was preferred ([J Clin Pathol 1994;47:1121)](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/7876390)
* Some authors have even postulated that the excision of the affected organ might be curative, obviating the need for pharmacologic therapy ([ScientificWorldJournal 2010;10:1915](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/20890580))
* Close follow up, including serology, is recommended since the risk of progression to systemic disease is unknown ([J Clin Pathol 1994;47:1121](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/7876390))

* Testis was bivalved to show a hemorrhagic, well defined, soft, dark red lesion ([Case of the Week #328](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/caseofweek/case328.htm))

Differential diagnosis

* Although uncommon, **isolated necrotizing testicular vasculitis** remains in the differential diagnosis of hypovascular testicular lesions; long term follow up and additional research is needed in order to further elucidate the pathogenesis of isolated organ vasculitis and the subsequent risk of disease progression
