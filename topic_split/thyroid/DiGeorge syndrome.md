Definition / general

* DiGeorge syndrome is a chromosomal disorder due to 22q11.2 deletion, characterized by failure of development of the third to fourth pharyngeal pouches and fourth branchial arch, which leads to a combination of congenital heart disease, parathyroid abnormalities (hypocalcemia) and thymic abnormalities (immunodeficiency)
* In 1965 DiGeorge described a group of infants with congenital absence of the thymus and parathyroid glands ([J Pediatr 1965;67:907](http://www.jpeds.com/article/S0022-3476%2865%2981796-6/abstract)); however a constellation of clinical signs was known at least since 1829 ([London Medical Gazette 1829;3:314](https://archive.org/details/londonmedicalgaz03londuoft))
* See also [Thymic dysplasia](https://www.pathologyoutlines.com/topic/mediastinumothernonneoplastic.html#thymicdysplasia)

Terminology

* There are numerous synonyms, which were used historically to describe various manifestations of chromosome 22q11.2 deletion ([OMIM #188400](http://www.omim.org/entry/188400))
* Most common synonyms: DiGeorge syndrome / anomaly, velocardiofacial syndrome (Shprintzen syndrome), conotruncal anomaly face syndrome (Takao syndrome)
* Other synonyms: hypoplasia of thymus and parathyroids, III - IV pharyngeal pouch syndrome, Opitz G / BBB syndrome, CATCH 22 syndrome, Sedlackova syndrome, Cayler cardiofacial syndrome, Strong syndrome, congenital thymic aplasia
* It is now recommended that patients with the classic chromosome 22q11.2 deletion be described according to the genetic nomenclature, i.e. "22q11.2 deletion syndrome", and patients with a clinical phenotype but a distinct cause or no known cause be described using syndromic nomenclature, i.e. "DiGeorge syndrome" ([Medicine (Baltimore) 2011;90:1](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/21200182))

Epidemiology

* Estimated prevalence is 1:4,000 births, range 1:3,000 - 1:6,000 ([Lancet 2007;370:1443](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/17950858))
* M = F
* Spontaneous : familial = 10:1 ([Genet Med 2001;3:23](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/11339373))
* 22q11.2 deletion syndrome is one of the most common genetic abnormalities (slightly less frequent than Down syndrome), and the most common chromosomal deletion syndrome in humans
* 5% of congenital cardiac defects and 2% - 3% of childhood onset schizophrenia are due to 22q11.2 deletion ([J Med Genet 1993;30:852](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/8230162))
* Infant mortality in 22q11 deletion syndrome is now relatively low (4%) due to adequate cardiac care, however the overall mortality rate compared to general population is elevated, especially in adults ([J Pediatr 2011;159:332](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/21570089))

Sites

* The syndrome crosses all organ systems, and is classically defined as polytopic developmental field defect ([Am J Med Genet Suppl 1986;2:113](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/3146281))
* Major targets are the organs derived from branchial apparatus; see details in **Clinical Features**
* Thyroid is one of the organs involved, however clinical impact in the thyroid is low compared to other manifestations

Pathophysiology / etiology

* Due to the hemizygous (only one of the chromosome pair) 22q11.2 deletion
* The occurrence of 22q11.2 deletions is related to the genomic architecture of the chromosome 22q11.2 region with several common rearrangement breakpoints
  ([J Pediatr 2011;159:332](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/21570089))
* The characteristic deletion of chromosome 22q11.2 is at least 10 times more common than the next most frequent human deletion syndrome, suggesting that
  this region is inherently unstable ([Dev Disabil Res Rev 2008;14:11](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/18636632))
* The deletion typically arises via unequal meiotic crossover / exchange, facilitated by asynchronous replication at the site of the deletion
  ([J Med Genet 2004;41:413](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/15173225))
* Alternative mechanism is 22q11.2 microduplication ([Eur J Med Genet 2009;52:88](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/19254783))
* Approximately 85% - 90% of individuals with the syndrome have 3 Mb deletion, 8% - 10% have 1.5 Mb deletion with mild phenotype, and individuals with atypical deletions (so called central and distal) have also been reported ([Cytogenet Genome Res 2015;146:89](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/26278718))
* There are approximately 40 genes within the commonly deleted region of chromosome 22q11.2
  + TBX1 is the main gene responsible for DiGeorge phenotype
  + TBX1 is a transcription factor normally expressed in the pharyngeal pouches, which give rise to the face, neck (parathyroids and thymus) and upper thorax
    ([Medicine (Baltimore) 2011;90:1](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/21200182))
  + TBX1 is involved in retinoic acid signaling ([Circ Res 2010;106:630](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/20203312))
  + TBX1 is not expressed in the thyroid primordium, but in surrounding mesoderm, and determines thyroid size and positioning ([Hum Mol Genet 2007;16:276](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/17164259))
  + Other candidate genes are CRKL, COMT, DCGR8 ([Cytogenet Genome Res 2015;146:89](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/26278718))
* Over 90% of the deletions occur de novo, while 6% - 10% of new cases are inherited from an affected parent ([Hum Mol Genet 2004;13:417](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/14681306))
* Risk of recurrence in the offspring is up to 50% in each pregnancy

Clinical features

* Classic phenotype is a triad of heart defects (usually involving the aorta and the part of the heart from which the aorta develops), thymus gland abnormalities with related immunodeficiency / autoimmunity, and parathyroid gland abnormalities with hypocalcemia
* However, it is widely acclaimed today that DiGeorge / 22q11.2 deletion syndrome has many more manifestations beyond the classical triad
* Phenotypic features grouped by frequency and clinical significance ([Medicine (Baltimore) 2011;90:1](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/21200182))
  + Major:
    - Cardiac anomalies mainly involving outflow tract, i.e. conotruncal (80%): tetralogy of Fallot, pulmonary atresia, truncus arteriosus, interrupted aortic arch, ventricular septal defect
    - Immune deficiency (> 75%): thymic hypoplasia, rarely aplasia, with T cell lymphopenia
    - Palatal anomalies (70%): velopharyngeal insufficiency and cleft palate, both contributing to poor feeding and speech
    - Developmental delay with low IQ (50%)
  + Intermediate: hypocalcemia, various structural anomalies (renal, GI, CNS, etc.), and psychiatric illnesses
  + Minor: facial dysmorphism (bulbous nasal tip, hypertelorism, hooded eyelid, micrognathia, low set ears)
* Depending on whether thymic hypoplasia or aplasia is present (in addition to heart defects and hypoparathyroidism), DiGeorge syndrome can be classified as partial or complete ([Eur J Pediatr 1989;149:96](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/2512165)):
  + Complete phenotype (< 1%) is used to describe patients who are athymic and have no circulating T cells
  + Patients with partial or incomplete phenotype have thymic hypoplasia with circulating T cells
* Thyroid issues:
  + Up to 10% of patients have overt thyroid disease, with hypo- or hyperthyroidism (4:1, [Am J Med Genet A 2015;167:1560](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/25944702))
  + Subclinical thyroid dysfunction often converts to apparent disease
  + Recommended to monitor TSH and thyroxine levels in all 22q11.2 deletion syndrome patients ([J Pediatr 2011;159:332](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/21570089))
  + Rarely presents with Graves disease (16× risk compared to general population, [Hormones (Athens) 2005;4:200](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/16613818))
  + Structural defects are detailed in **Gross Description**

Diagnosis

* Most patients are identified shortly after birth due to the presence of cardiac anomaly, hypocalcemia or recurrent infections
* Genetic testing (see **Molecular / Cytogenetics Descriptions / Images**):
  + May be warranted if affected parent/sibling or suspicious prenatal US findings;
  + Advised in children with certain combinations: cardiac anomaly + any major / intermediate phenotypic feature; speech delay + any
    major / intermediate phenotypic feature; hypocalcemia alone or with any major / intermediate phenotypic feature ([J Allergy Clin Immunol Pract 2013;1:589](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/24565705))
* DiGeorge syndrome needs to be considered for some pediatric autopsies; for example, thymus and parathyroid should be located in cases of congenital heart disease, and in turn, absent parathyroids / thymus should prompt a detailed heart examination ([J Pediatr 1979;94:883](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/448529))

Treatment

* Varies by age and phenotype
* May include cardiac and palatal surgery, thymus transplantation, calcium supplementation, etc.

Facial dysmorphism

Gross description

* Unremarkable thyroid in half of the cases
* Reported anomalies include ([J Pediatr 1979;94:883](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/448529), [Pediatr Pathol 1993;13:463](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/8372031)):
  + Thyroid hypoplasia, with a left lobe predilection ([Clin Endocrinol (Oxf) 2010;72:839](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/19863572))
  + Absence of the isthmus of thyroid
  + Agenesis of lobe of thyroid
  + Retrocarotid or retroesophageal extension ([Laryngoscope 2009;119:1495](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/19507237))
* Hypoplasia and persistent fetal shape of thyroid cartilage ([Pediatr Pathol 1986;6:209](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/3822935))

Microscopic (histologic) description

* Consistent with normal thyroid histology (most specimens come from pediatric autopsies)
* Rarely, pathological changes has been described:
  + Predominant fetal follicles
  + Hyperplasia
  + Follicular adenoma and nodular goiter ([Case Rep Med 2013;2013:923129](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/23762078))
  + Thyroid carcinoma ([Am J Med Genet A 2006;140:906](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/16532473))
* C cells can be absent or significantly reduced in number, consistently with their pharyngeal pouch origin ([Hum Pathol 1987;18:355](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/3557439), [Am J Med Genet 1993;46:641](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/8362905), [Pediatr Pathol 1993;13:463](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/8372031))

Molecular / cytogenetics description

* FISH is usually performed with a chromosome 22 specific probe that identifies the chromosome and a second probe that hybridizes to the commonly deleted region; if the second probe is absent on a "tagged" chromosome, then the diagnosis is established ([Medicine (Baltimore) 2011;90:1](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/21200182))
  + Turnaround time is 3 - 14 days
  + Cost of the commercial test is $750
* Alternative genetic tests
  + Multiplex PCR (MLPA): rapid and cost effective
  + CGH array: identifies atypical deletions and duplications not recognized by FISH

Differential diagnosis

* Some genetic syndromes have overlapping features with 22q11 deletion syndrome (resolved by genetic testing):
  + **CHARGE syndrome**: coloboma, heart, atresia, retarded growth / development (or both), genital, ear ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CHARGE_syndrome))
  + **Smith-Lemli-Opitz syndrome** ([Genetics Home Reference](https://ghr.nlm.nih.gov/condition/smith-lemli-opitz-syndrome))
  + **Kabuki syndrome** ([Genetics Home Reference](https://ghr.nlm.nih.gov/condition/kabuki-syndrome))
  + **Goldenhar syndrome** ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Goldenhar_syndrome))
